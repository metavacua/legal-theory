  

# **Structuring a California Cooperative with Tradable Membership Securities: A Legal and Strategic Blueprint**

  
  

### **Executive Summary**

  

This report provides a comprehensive legal and strategic blueprint for the formation of a novel California cooperative corporation. The central innovation of this proposed entity is the creation of two distinct classes of tradable membership interests designed to qualify as securities under both California and federal law. This structure intentionally departs from traditional cooperative models that seek to avoid securities classification.

The first class of security, designated "Class S" or "Safe," is structured to rigorously protect the cooperative's qualification for favorable tax treatment under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code. It incorporates multiple protective mechanisms within its governing documents to ensure stability and adherence to cooperative principles.

The second class, "Class U" or "Unsafe," is a high-risk instrument explicitly designed with embedded triggers that, if activated by specific trading patterns or ownership concentrations, could cause the cooperative to lose its Subchapter T status and involuntarily transmute into a standard C-corporation. These triggers and their consequences will be clearly defined in the cooperative's formation documents and disclosed in its offering prospectus.

This dual-class structure is made possible by a strategic synthesis of three legal domains: the flexibility of the California Cooperative Corporation Law, which permits varied classes of membership; the breadth of California's "risk capital" test for defining a security, which provides a robust foundation for this model; and the specific requirements of the IRS's Subchapter T, the vulnerabilities of which form the basis for the "Unsafe" security's design. This report details the legal architecture, governance model, security design, and operational plan necessary to implement this complex but legally plausible hybrid entity.

## **Part I: Foundational Legal & Tax Frameworks**

  

The viability of this proposed cooperative structure rests upon the precise interplay of three distinct but interconnected legal frameworks. A nuanced understanding of California cooperative law, federal and state securities law, and federal tax law under Subchapter T is essential, as the structure is designed to operate at the intersection of their respective requirements and tensions.

  

### **Section 1: The California Cooperative Corporation Law**

  

A California cooperative is fundamentally defined as a democratically controlled entity organized not for its own profit, but for the mutual benefit of its members as patrons.1 This core principle, which typically manifests as a "one member, one vote" governance structure, presents the primary conceptual challenge to creating an entity with tradable securities, where ownership and control can become concentrated.2 However, the California Cooperative Corporation Law provides a surprisingly flexible toolkit that, when used strategically, can accommodate this hybrid model.

The formation process is standard for a corporation, requiring the filing of Articles of Incorporation with the California Secretary of State, the creation of detailed bylaws, and the issuance of membership applications or disclosure documents to prospective members.4 The cooperative's legal name must contain the word "Cooperative" or an abbreviation like "Co-op".1

The critical feature of California law is its allowance for significant flexibility in capital and membership structure. A cooperative is expressly permitted to establish different classes of membership, each with its own unique set of rights, preferences, and restrictions.2 The statute also allows for the issuance of non-voting preferred shares, a mechanism typically used for raising capital that can be adapted for the purposes of this model.9 Furthermore, while membership in a cooperative is not inherently transferable, the articles or bylaws may explicitly permit transferability.2 This provision is the legal gateway for creating a tradable instrument.

The combination of these statutory tools makes a hybrid entity possible. The law allows for a deliberate decoupling of economic rights from voting rights. By authorizing multiple membership classes with variable rights and permitting transferability, the statute provides the necessary components. The strategic task, detailed in this report, is to assemble these components in a novel configuration that allows for a liquid market in the cooperative's securities while preserving the legally mandated democratic core.

  

### **Section 2: The Definition of a "Security" in a Cooperative Context**

  

The objective here is a direct inversion of legal precedent. Historically, cooperatives have meticulously structured their membership interests to *avoid* being classified as securities. They rely on the U.S. Supreme Court's landmark decision in *United Housing Foundation, Inc. v. Forman*, which held that when a person's primary motivation for acquiring a co-op share is to use or consume the co-op's services (like housing), and not to earn a profit, the share is not a security.10 This report outlines a strategy to do the opposite: to intentionally and unambiguously create membership interests that qualify as securities.

The federal standard for an "investment contract," a type of security, is the *Howey* test, which requires: (1) an investment of money, (2) in a common enterprise, (3) with an expectation of profits, (4) derived solely from the efforts of a promoter or third party.10 The offering documents for the proposed cooperative will be drafted to explicitly meet this test. The purchase of membership shares will be framed not as a prerequisite for patronage but as an investment in a business enterprise with the potential for financial return through patronage distributions and, crucially, through the potential appreciation in the trading value of the security itself.

However, the more powerful legal foundation for this structure lies in California's unique securities law. California employs the broader **Risk Capital Test**, established in *Silver Hills Country Club v. Sobieski*. This test is more accommodating to this venture because it focuses on whether investors are contributing "risk capital" to a business enterprise where their funds are inadequately secured and where they are substantially powerless as individuals to affect the enterprise's success.10 Significantly, this test does not require a strict "expectation of profit" in the same way the

*Howey* test does.14

This legal distinction is paramount. The "profits" of a cooperative are distributed as patronage dividends, which are technically rebates based on use. Arguing that these constitute "profits" under the *Howey* test could be legally complex. The *Risk Capital* test bypasses this complexity by shifting the focus. Are members' capital contributions at risk? In any new business venture, unequivocally yes. Are the investors passive in terms of managerial control? Yes; day-to-day control is vested in an elected board, and the "one member, one vote" principle ensures that even a large security holder remains a single voice among many voting members. Therefore, by emphasizing in the offering documents that member capital is at risk and is being used to capitalize and grow the enterprise, the membership interests will almost certainly be deemed securities under California law, providing a more direct and defensible legal basis for the entire structure.14

### **Section 3: The Architecture of Subchapter T**

  

The primary economic engine of the cooperative model is found in Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code (§§ 1381-1388). This legislation codifies the "single tax" principle, which allows a qualifying cooperative to deduct patronage dividends from its corporate income.16 This effectively allows profits from patron business to pass through the entity untaxed at the corporate level; the tax is instead paid by the patron at their individual income tax rate upon receipt of the distribution.4 Preserving this tax treatment is the central goal of the "Safe" security class, while placing it at risk is the defining feature of the "Unsafe" class.

To qualify for Subchapter T treatment, a cooperative must satisfy several core requirements:

1.  **Operate on a Cooperative Basis:** This is not rigidly defined in the code but is determined by the facts and circumstances. IRS rulings and court cases have established three key characteristics: democratic control by patrons, subordination of capital (meaning capital providers do not control the entity or receive the primary benefits), and the allocation of earnings to patrons on the basis of their patronage (business volume).16
2.  **Have a Pre-existing Obligation:** The cooperative's bylaws must contain a binding, pre-existing obligation to distribute net earnings from patronage business back to its patrons.16 This obligation must be in place  
    *before* the income is earned.
3.  **Distribute Patronage-Sourced Income:** The tax deduction applies *only* to income generated from business conducted "with or for patrons".17 Any income derived from non-patronage sources—such as investments, rent from property, or business conducted with the general public—is fully taxable at standard corporate rates, just as it would be in a C-corporation.19

Should the cooperative fail to meet these requirements—for instance, if it ceases to be democratically controlled by its patrons or if it operates primarily for the benefit of non-patron investors—it would lose its qualification under Subchapter T. The consequence would be the loss of the patronage dividend deduction, and the entity would be taxed as a standard C-corporation on all of its net income.19

This strict dichotomy between patronage and non-patronage activity is the central vulnerability that can be leveraged to design the "Unsafe" security class. A public market for securities will inevitably attract non-patron investors whose primary goal is capital appreciation or dividends paid from general corporate profits, not patronage rebates. If a significant portion of the cooperative's securities come to be held by these non-patrons, they will exert pressure on the board to maximize total corporate profit, which may involve increasing business with non-members or pursuing non-patronage investment activities. This operational shift would generate more non-patronage income, which is taxable, and could ultimately undermine the entity's claim to be "operating on a cooperative basis." This provides a logical and defensible basis for a bylaw provision that explicitly triggers a transmutation of the cooperative's status based on non-patron ownership levels or non-patronage revenue thresholds.

## **Part II: Designing the Cooperative Entity and its Governance**

  

Moving from legal theory to practical application, this section outlines the specific architectural choices required to construct the cooperative's legal and governance framework. The design must embed the dual-class security structure while adhering to the foundational principles of California cooperative law.

  

### **Section 4: Articles of Incorporation and Bylaw Architecture**

  

The cooperative's legal DNA will be encoded in its Articles of Incorporation and, more extensively, in its bylaws.

The **Articles of Incorporation** will be filed with the California Secretary of State and will contain all statutorily required information.4 Critically, the Articles will also explicitly state:

  - The corporation's purpose includes operating on a cooperative basis in accordance with Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code.
  - The corporation is authorized to issue two distinct classes of tradable membership securities, designated herein as "Class S" and "Class U."
  - A clear statement that the specific rights, preferences, restrictions, and conditions of each security class—including any conditions that could trigger a change in the corporation's tax status—are set forth in detail in the corporation's bylaws.9

The **Bylaws** will serve as the master operational document. They will be far more comprehensive than those of a typical cooperative, functioning as a hybrid of cooperative rules, a traditional corporate bylaw set, and a detailed shareholder agreement. This document will contain the intricate mechanics governing both security classes, membership, patronage, and the triggers for transmutation.4

  

### **Section 5: Reconciling Democratic Control with Tradable Ownership**

  

The central governance challenge is to accommodate a liquid, public market for ownership stakes without violating the legal requirement of democratic patron control. The solution lies in a carefully constructed decoupling of economic rights from voting rights.

The bylaws will establish a clear and legally significant distinction between a **"Security Holder"** and a **"Voting Member."**

  - A **Security Holder** is any person or entity that owns one or more Class S or Class U securities. This status confers economic rights, primarily the right to receive patronage distributions (if the holder is a patron) and the right to trade the security on the open market.
  - A **Voting Member** is a Security Holder who has met a second, distinct set of requirements specified in the bylaws. These requirements will include being a natural person, formally applying for and being accepted into voting membership by the board, and, most importantly, being an active patron of the cooperative.25

The bylaws will then firmly establish the **"one member, one vote"** principle, stipulating that each *Voting Member* is entitled to exactly one vote in the affairs of the cooperative, regardless of the quantity of securities they own.1

This structure effectively uses the governance rules as a filter. It allows ownership (the securities) to be fluid and traded on a market, but it makes control (the vote) a stable privilege that must be earned and maintained through active participation in the cooperative's primary business. A passive, non-patron investor, such as a hedge fund, could acquire a substantial economic stake by purchasing a large number of securities but would be unable to acquire a vote. This architecture satisfies the need for access to capital markets while preserving the democratic governance structure required by both California law and Subchapter T. It creates a stable governing body of active patrons that is insulated from the potential volatility and differing motivations of purely financial investors.

## **Part III: Structuring the "Safe" and "Unsafe" Membership Securities**

  

This section provides the detailed design of the two distinct security classes, which lie at the heart of this proposal. Each class is engineered to serve a different purpose and appeal to a different type of investor.

  

### **Section 7: The "Safe" Membership Class (Class S)**

  

The Class S security is designed for stability, long-term investment, and the preservation of the cooperative's core identity and tax advantages. It represents a proprietary interest in the cooperative, entitling the holder to a pro-rata share of patronage distributions based on their own patronage activity. The architecture of Class S securities is fortified by several protective mechanisms embedded within the bylaws to safeguard the cooperative's Subchapter T status.

1.  **Mandatory Patronage for Voting Rights:** As previously detailed, the bylaws will unequivocally state that only holders of Class S securities who are also active patrons, and who have been formally accepted by the board, can become Voting Members. This ensures that the governance of the cooperative remains in the hands of those who actively use its services.
2.  **Corporate Right of First Refusal (ROFR):** The cooperative will retain a ROFR on any proposed transfer of Class S securities to a third party.29 This is a powerful control mechanism. If a Class S holder wishes to sell their securities, they must first offer them to the cooperative on the same terms as the third-party offer. This allows the cooperative to prevent large blocks of securities from accumulating in the hands of non-patrons or potentially hostile entities, thereby preserving the patron-focused ownership structure.
3.  **Active Patron Requirement for Continued Membership:** Voting rights are not permanent. The bylaws will stipulate that to maintain their status as a Voting Member, an individual must remain an active patron. Failure to meet a defined minimum patronage threshold over a specified period (e.g., two consecutive fiscal years) will automatically revert their status from a "Voting Member" to a non-voting "Security Holder".27 This ensures that the voting body remains composed of current, engaged users.
4.  **Supermajority Vote for Fundamental Changes:** Any proposed amendment to the Articles of Incorporation or bylaws that could endanger the cooperative's Subchapter T status will require an exceptionally high supermajority vote (e.g., 75% or 80%) of all Voting Members. This would apply to changes to the "one member, one vote" rule, the definition of patronage, or the formula for patronage distribution.

The offering documents for Class S securities will prominently feature these protective measures as a key benefit, appealing to investors—including member-patrons and impact investors—who value the stability, democratic principles, and tax advantages of the cooperative model.

  

### **Section 8: The "Unsafe" Membership Class (Class U)**

  

The Class U security is a novel, high-risk, high-reward financial instrument. It is designed to be freely tradable on the open market without a corporate ROFR and can be acquired and held by any person or entity, regardless of their patronage status. Holders of Class U securities are entitled to the same economic rights to patronage dividends as Class S holders (if they are patrons), but the security itself is intentionally exposed to risks that could trigger a fundamental change in the cooperative's corporate and tax structure.

These risks are not unforeseen liabilities; they are designed features of the security that will be explicitly defined in the bylaws and prominently disclosed in all offering documents.

Defining the Trigger Events for Transmutation:

The bylaws will define specific, measurable events related to the trading and ownership of Class U securities that will constitute a "Cooperative Status Review Event."

  - **Trigger 1: Non-Patron Ownership Concentration:** "If, at the close of any two consecutive fiscal quarters, the total number of outstanding Class U securities held by non-patron Security Holders exceeds sixty percent (60%) of all outstanding Class U securities, this shall constitute a 'Cooperative Status Review Event'."
  - **Trigger 2: Shift to Non-Patronage Income:** "If, for any single fiscal year, the cooperative's gross revenue derived from non-patronage sources exceeds fifty percent (50%) of its total gross revenue, this shall constitute a 'Cooperative Status Review Event'." This trigger directly links the trading of the security to the operational requirements of Subchapter T.17
  - **Trigger 3: High Trading Velocity (Optional advanced trigger):** "If the average daily trading volume of Class U securities over any ninety (90) day period exceeds five percent (5%) of the total outstanding Class U securities, indicating a prevalence of short-term speculation over long-term investment, this shall constitute a 'Cooperative Status Review Event'."

The Consequence of a Trigger Event:

The bylaws will clearly state the non-discretionary outcome of a "Cooperative Status Review Event." The most direct approach is Automatic Transmutation: upon the official certification of a trigger event by the board, the pre-existing obligation to distribute patronage dividends under Subchapter T is automatically terminated for the subsequent fiscal year. The cooperative would then be obligated to file its federal and state income taxes as a standard C-corporation, losing the patronage dividend deduction. A secondary, less automatic approach would be a Mandatory Membership Vote, where a trigger event requires the board to call a special meeting to vote on formally converting the entity to a general stock corporation.

Disclosure and Risk Factors:

The prospectus for Class U securities will contain an extensive and "plain English" risk factors section.33 It will state, in no uncertain terms, that the security is designed with these features. A sample disclosure might read:

"This security is intentionally designed with mechanisms that may lead to the termination of the company's status as a cooperative and its favorable tax treatment under Subchapter T. Investors must be aware that certain trading patterns and ownership concentrations, including those of other investors over whom they have no control, can trigger this transmutation. AN INVESTMENT IN CLASS U SECURITIES CARRIES THE INHERENT AND INTENTIONAL RISK OF CORPORATE TRANSMUTATION, WHICH WOULD FUNDAMENTALLY ALTER THE NATURE OF THIS INVESTMENT.".35

This "Unsafe" security is more than just a risky stock; it is a tool for financial engineering. It would likely appeal to sophisticated investors, such as hedge funds or activist investors, who understand and may even seek to profit from the transmutation event. For example, a C-corporation may be a more attractive acquisition target than a cooperative, potentially allowing investors to realize a return through a sale of the company at a premium. The Class U security thus creates a pre-defined pathway for the cooperative's evolution, driven by market forces that are explicitly acknowledged in its legal DNA from inception.

## **Part IV: Operational and Compliance Blueprint**

  

The successful launch and operation of this hybrid cooperative demand a rigorous approach to regulatory qualification, accounting, and ongoing compliance.

  

### **Section 9: The Path to Qualification**

  

To establish the membership interests as fully tradable securities, the cooperative must undergo a formal qualification process, intentionally forgoing available exemptions.

  - **State and Federal Registration:** While California Corporations Code § 25100(r) provides an exemption from qualification for cooperative securities sales up to $1,000 per member, this structure will deliberately *avoid* this exemption.37 The goal is not to avoid regulation but to embrace it to create a legitimate, publicly tradable instrument. This will involve a formal qualification of the securities with the California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI).40 Concurrently, if the securities are to be offered to the public across state lines, the cooperative must register the offering with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). This typically involves filing a Form S-1 registration statement, which includes a detailed prospectus.41 Given the novelty of this structure, extensive dialogue with both state and federal regulators will be essential.
  - **Prospectus Contents:** The prospectus is the primary disclosure document for investors. It must provide full, true, and plain disclosure of all material facts.33 For this cooperative, it must include detailed audited financial statements, a comprehensive description of the business model, and a clear explanation of the rights, preferences, and restrictions of both the Class S and Class U securities. The "Risk Factors" section, particularly for the Class U shares, must be exceptionally clear and prominent, detailing the transmutation triggers and their consequences.34

  

### **Section 10: Managing Patronage and Non-Patronage Income Streams**

  

The integrity of the cooperative's tax status and the functionality of the Class U triggers depend on the meticulous separation and tracking of income sources.

  - **Meticulous Accounting:** The cooperative must implement a robust accounting system capable of clearly segregating patronage-sourced income from all forms of non-patronage-sourced income.19 This is not merely a best practice; it is a fundamental requirement for Subchapter T compliance and is necessary to monitor the revenue-based trigger for the Class U securities.
  - **Patronage Tracking:** A reliable system must be established to accurately track the volume or value of business conducted with each individual patron.16 This data is the basis for the correct allocation of patronage dividends and is essential for demonstrating that the cooperative is operating for the benefit of its patrons.

  

### **Section 11: Ongoing Compliance and Reporting**

  

Once public, the cooperative will be subject to continuous reporting obligations at both the federal tax and securities levels.

  - **Tax Filings:** Annually, the cooperative will file IRS Form 1120-C, the U.S. Income Tax Return for Cooperative Associations.16 It will also be required to issue a Form 1099-PATR to each patron who receives $10 or more in patronage distributions during the tax year.45
  - **Securities Filings:** As a publicly reporting company, the cooperative must comply with the ongoing disclosure requirements of the SEC. This includes filing annual reports on Form 10-K, quarterly reports on Form 10-Q, and current reports on Form 8-K to disclose material events as they occur.46 These filings ensure that the public market has continuous access to current information about the cooperative's financial condition and operations.

## **Part V: Strategic Recommendations and Risk Mitigation**

  

This final section offers high-level strategic advice for managing the inherent complexities of this structure and provides concluding recommendations.

  

### **Section 12: Addressing Inherent Structural Tensions**

  

The dual nature of this entity—part cooperative, part publicly traded company—creates inherent tensions that must be actively managed.

  - **Investor Relations Strategy:** The cooperative will require a sophisticated and transparent investor relations strategy. Communication must be tailored to two distinct audiences: member-patrons, who are primarily interested in the quality and cost of services, and public investors, who are focused on financial returns. The cooperative's dual mandate must be communicated clearly and consistently to manage the potentially conflicting expectations of these groups.
  - **Board Composition:** To maintain the cooperative's character, the bylaws must ensure that the board of directors is elected by and composed predominantly of active Voting Members. However, to give public security holders a voice and demonstrate a commitment to standard corporate governance, the bylaws could allow for one or two board seats to be elected by all security holders (both Class S and Class U) on a one-share, one-vote basis. This would provide representation without ceding control.

  

### **Section 13: A Pathway for Voluntary Termination of Subchapter T Status**

  

Beyond the involuntary transmutation triggers associated with the Class U securities, the bylaws should provide a mechanism for an orderly, member-driven conversion. This provision would empower the Voting Members to voluntarily terminate the cooperative structure and convert the entity into a standard C-corporation if they determine the model is no longer serving the business's best interests. Such a fundamental change should require a very high supermajority vote (e.g., 85% or 90%) of the Voting Members, ensuring that the decision is the result of a broad consensus among active patrons. This provision affirms the principle of democratic control by giving the ultimate decision over the entity's long-term fate to its members.49

  

### **Section 14: Concluding Analysis and Final Recommendations**

  

The proposed structure for a California cooperative with tradable "Safe" and "Unsafe" membership securities is legally plausible but operationally complex and represents a significant departure from any existing model in the public markets. Its ultimate success will depend on meticulous legal execution, unwavering transparency in its disclosures, and sophisticated management of its dual constituencies.

The primary risks are threefold: **regulatory risk**, as state and federal securities regulators may apply intense scrutiny to such a novel structure; **market risk**, as investors may misunderstand the complex nature of the securities, particularly the intentional transmutation triggers of the Class U shares; and **governance risk**, stemming from the inherent conflict between cooperative principles of service-at-cost and the profit-maximization pressures of public capital markets.

To mitigate these risks and move forward, the following actions are recommended:

1.  **Engage Expert Counsel:** It is imperative to retain legal counsel with deep, integrated expertise in California cooperative law, federal and state securities law, and IRS Subchapter T tax law at the earliest possible stage.
2.  **Initiate Pre-Filing Regulatory Dialogue:** Proactively engage in pre-filing discussions with both the California DFPI and the SEC. This will allow the cooperative to gauge regulatory appetite, identify potential concerns, and collaboratively address them before a formal filing is made.
3.  **Develop a Robust Financial Model:** A comprehensive financial model should be developed to project the cooperative's performance under various scenarios. This model must include projections for the business both as a cooperative and as a C-corporation, allowing stakeholders to understand the financial implications of a transmutation event triggered by the Class U securities.
4.  **Assemble an Experienced Leadership Team:** The cooperative's success will require a management team and a board of directors with a rare combination of skills, including experience in cooperative governance, public company management, securities compliance, and sophisticated investor relations.
