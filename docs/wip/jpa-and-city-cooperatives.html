JPA and City Cooperatives

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Your hypothesis that an incorporated city could become a member of a larger cooperative entity is sound. In California, cities are legally recognized as "municipal corporations," which grants them the status of a legal person capable of entering into contracts and joining other organizations.1

This concept of a "cooperative of cities" has a powerful real-world parallel in the Joint Powers Authority (JPA) model, which is widely used in California and other states.3 A JPA is a distinct legal entity created when two or more public agencies, such as cities or counties, sign an agreement to jointly exercise powers that they all share.5 This structure allows municipalities to:

  • Pool resources to achieve economies of scale.3

  • Jointly provide services such as water, waste management, or transportation more efficiently.5

  • Obtain financing for large-scale public projects that might be too large for a single city to undertake.5

Functionally, a JPA operates as a cooperative of its member municipalities. The cities are the members, and their "patronage" is their use of the shared services provided by the JPA. The "benefit" they receive is not a cash dividend, but rather cost savings, increased efficiency, and access to services they could not afford alone.8

Crucially, this model directly addresses your point about securitization. JPAs are explicitly authorized to issue bonds on behalf of their member agencies.5 This is a common and well-established practice. For example, under California's Marks-Roos Local Bond Pooling Act of 1985, a JPA can issue a larger, single bond issuance and then lend the proceeds to its various member cities to finance their respective local projects.3

This structure offers several advantages:

  • Securitization of Municipal Need: It effectively securitizes the collective financing needs of the member cities. Instead of each city issuing its own small, potentially less attractive municipal bond, the JPA pools these needs into a larger, more marketable security.10

  • Access to Capital Markets: This pooling mechanism can provide smaller cities with better access to capital markets and more favorable interest rates than they could achieve on their own.12

  • Maintained Tax Status: The bonds issued by the JPA on behalf of its city-members are still considered municipal bonds. As long as the proceeds are used for a qualifying "public purpose," the interest paid to bondholders remains tax-exempt, which is the key feature that makes municipal bonds an efficient financing tool.13

Therefore, your vision of a central organization that has cities as members and is itself securitized for the benefit of those city-members is not only plausible but is actively practiced through the Joint Powers Authority framework. It is a powerful mechanism for inter-municipal cooperation that leverages the principles of both cooperative enterprise and public finance.
