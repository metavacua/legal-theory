Coop Membership Investment — Phase 1: The "Risk Capital" Security

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Phase 1: The "Risk Capital" Security (Class B Membership)

The initial sale of a membership to finance the development of a housing unit on a vacant lot is a textbook example of a transaction that would be governed by California's "risk capital" test.1 This test, established in

Silver Hills Country Club v. Sobieski, was designed for exactly this type of scenario: raising funds from passive individuals to create a business enterprise where the promised benefit depends on the promoter's success.2

Here is how your proposed Class B membership fits the key factors of the risk capital test:

  • Raising Funds for a Business Venture: The cooperative is selling memberships to raise the necessary capital to develop the land and construct the housing.2 This is the definition of providing risk capital to an enterprise.

  • Passive Investor: The purchaser of the Class B membership is in a passive position. They are providing funds and waiting for the developer (the cooperative) to successfully build the promised domicile. Their ability to realize the benefit of their investment is entirely dependent on the efforts of others.1

  • Substantial Risk: The investor's money is substantially at risk because it is inadequately secured.1 If the development fails, the lot remains vacant, and the capital is lost. The value is contingent on future construction, not on an existing, tangible asset.4

  • Expectation of a "Benefit": Crucially, the risk capital test does not require an expectation of monetary "profit" in the traditional sense. The expected benefit can simply be the right to use the facilities—in this case, the right to occupy the finished domicile.1

Under this analysis, the initial sale of the Class B membership is unequivocally the sale of a security under California law. It is an investment contract where capital is risked for a future benefit.

Phase 2: The Transformation to a "Use" Instrument (Class A Membership)

The truly innovative part of your model is what happens upon the successful construction of the housing unit. At the moment the Class B membership converts to a Class A membership and the owner takes occupancy, the "economic reality" of the instrument fundamentally changes.

This is where the analysis shifts from California's risk capital test to the federal standard established in United Housing Foundation, Inc. v. Forman.5 The Supreme Court in

Forman held that when the primary motivation for purchasing a share in a cooperative is for personal use and consumption—in that case, to acquire an apartment to live in—it is not a securities transaction.5

  • Shift from Investment to Consumption: Once the building exists, the member is no longer a passive investor risking capital on a future enterprise. They are now a resident using the asset for its intended purpose: housing.1

  • Elimination of Risk: The primary "risk" has been eliminated. The member's capital is no longer funding a speculative development; it is now tied to a tangible, existing asset that they occupy.

  • Transformation of the Asset: The instrument transforms from a security (a claim on a future benefit dependent on the efforts of others) into a non-security (a right of use and occupancy).

Your proposed structure creates a dynamic life cycle for the cooperative share. It begins its life as a publicly-tradable "risk capital" security (Class B) to finance development, and upon success, it matures into a private, non-tradable "use" instrument (Class A) for the resident-member. This is a powerful model that leverages the specific nuances of securities law to facilitate the financing and creation of new cooperative housing.
