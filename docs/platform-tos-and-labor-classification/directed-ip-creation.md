### **The Directed Hand: An Analysis of Externally-Guided IP Creation as a Service**

#### **Introduction: The Locus of Control**

The proposition that "all externally directed intellectual property creation is an act of service" sharpens the focus of the debate from the general nature of creativity to the specific legal and economic character of a relationship. When the impetus for creation comes not from the creator's internal drive but from an external party's request, guidance, or control, the act is fundamentally recontextualized. This report will argue that this refined hypothesis is overwhelmingly correct, particularly when analyzed under the stringent labor and contract laws of jurisdictions like California.

The moment an external entity—be it a client, a community, a corporation, or even a paying "patron"—directs the creation of intellectual property (IP), a legal and economic nexus is formed. This relationship, regardless of the contractual language used to describe it, is almost invariably classified as one of "labor or services for remuneration".1 This classification has profound and often retroactive consequences, triggering a legal framework that presumes an employment relationship and reorders the ownership of the resulting IP. By examining the legal tests that define a service contract and applying them to various models of directed creation—from explicit commissions to gamified platforms—it becomes clear that the act of external direction is the defining characteristic that transforms creation into a service.

#### **The Legal Framework: Defining a Service Contract in California**

In California, the determination of whether an act constitutes a service begins with the "Predominant Factor Test." This test is used to discern the "essence of the agreement" in hybrid transactions that involve both labor and the creation of a good, such as IP.1 When creation is externally directed, the value for the directing party lies not in the resulting digital file or artifact as a standalone "good," but in the specialized skill, creative effort, and intellectual labor invested by the creator to generate that IP.1 The predominant factor is overwhelmingly the user's service, which triggers the application of California's labor laws.1

Once this threshold is met, the relationship is scrutinized under the state's rigid, three-pronged "ABC test," which was established in *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court* and applies retroactively.1 The test presumes a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can prove all three of the following:

  - **(A)** The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity.
  - **(B)** The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
  - **(C)** The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.1

The concept of "external direction" directly challenges Prong A. If an outside party is directing the work, the creator is, by definition, not entirely free from control. This legal framework looks past contractual labels to the functional reality of the relationship, making the act of direction a key determinant of a service relationship.1

#### **Models of External Direction and Their Legal Classification**

Different forms of external direction all tend to lead to the same legal conclusion: the creation of IP is a service.

1\. Explicit Commissions and Contracts

This is the most straightforward model. When a business hires a graphic designer to create a logo or a production company hires a writer for a script, the relationship is an explicit contract for services.4 The external direction is clear and specific. In many such cases, the "work made for hire" doctrine under U.S. copyright law applies, stipulating that the employer or commissioning party—not the creator—is the legal author and owner of the IP from the moment of creation. This legal outcome solidifies the nature of the act: the creator was remunerated to perform a service that resulted in IP owned by the client.

2\. Algorithmic and Gamified Direction

Platforms like Google Maps direct a vast, distributed workforce through gamified systems. Users who act as "Local Guides" are incentivized with points, badges, and perks to perform specific creative labor: writing reviews, uploading photos, and correcting data.1 This "algorithmic management" is a form of control that directs the user's labor to meet the platform's data needs, failing Prong A of the ABC test.1 The user-generated content is the core of the platform's business, failing Prong B.1 This model is intentionally designed to attract "hobbyists" who are not operating independent businesses, thus failing Prong C.1 Even though the direction is indirect and the remuneration is non-monetary, the law views this as a service relationship due to the control exercised and the value generated for the platform.1

3\. Crowdfunded Commissions

The patronage model on platforms like Patreon is often framed as support for a creator's independent vision. However, this relationship is legally transformed the moment a creator offers a tier where a patron can specify the creation of a piece of IP. The patron's payment ceases to be a gift or general support and becomes remuneration for a specific, directed service.1

Under California law, the platform itself is the likely "hiring entity" in such a transaction, as it controls the ecosystem and processes payments.1 The platform would fail the ABC test because the patron exerts control over the work (failing Prong A), the creative labor is the core of the platform's business (failing Prong B), and many creators are hobbyists, not independent businesses (failing Prong C).1 The external direction provided by the patron, facilitated by the platform, legally defines the creative act as a service.

4\. Directed Community Labor and Moderation

Social media platforms frequently deputize users as volunteer moderators, directing them to enforce the platform's terms of service.7 These moderators, in turn, direct the creative output of other users by approving or removing content and disciplining users.7 This creates a de facto managerial class performing an uncompensated service for the platform. Their labor is externally directed by the platform's policies and for the platform's benefit. This creates a "cascading misclassification" crisis, where the moderators are misclassified supervisors managing a misclassified workforce.7 The entire structure is built on externally directed labor that functions as a service to the platform.

#### **The Inescapable Consequences: IP Ownership and Wage Theft**

The legal reclassification of externally directed creation as a service has two profound consequences that reinforce the hypothesis.

First, under the "work made for hire" doctrine, a finding of an employment relationship—the default presumption under the ABC test—means the IP is automatically owned by the employer/directing entity from its inception.1 The legal framework itself validates the service model: the creator's labor was performed in service to the entity that now owns the resulting IP.

Second, this reclassification exposes the hiring entity to massive liability for wage theft under the California Labor Code. The creator becomes entitled to back pay for *all hours worked*, including minimum wage and overtime, plus significant penalties.1 This potential liability underscores the economic reality that the law does not view this directed labor as a gift or a hobby, but as compensable work that was performed as a service.

#### **Conclusion**

While purely self-motivated creation can exist outside the service paradigm, the hypothesis that "all externally directed IP creation is an act of service" holds true under rigorous legal and economic scrutiny. The act of external direction—whether through a formal contract, an algorithmic nudge, or a patron's request—is legally interpreted as an exercise of control. When this control is coupled with any form of remuneration, it establishes a service relationship.

In jurisdictions like California, this relationship is presumptively one of employment, with transformative consequences for IP ownership and financial liability. The law looks past the labels of "user," "patron," or "contributor" to the functional reality of the exchange. In that reality, the creator who follows an external party's direction is providing a service, and the resulting IP is the deliverable of that service.
