### **The Unilateral Mandate: Social Media Platforms as Service Contracts for IP Creation**

  

#### **Introduction: The Illusion of the Neutral Platform**

  

Social media platforms like Meta (Facebook) and X (formerly Twitter) present themselves as neutral conduits for user expression, governed by a simple bargain: in exchange for access to a "free" service, users grant the platform a license to host their content. However, a deeper legal analysis reveals a far more complex and directive relationship. The platform's architecture—from its User Experience (UX) design to its extensive content moderation policies—functions as a powerful mechanism of external control, shaping and directing the creation of user-generated intellectual property (IP).

This report argues that the relationship between a social media platform and its content-creating user is best understood as a **unilateral contract for services**. The platform makes a standing, public offer, and the user accepts not with a promise, but by performing the specific act of creating and uploading content that conforms to the platform's meticulously defined and ever-changing rules. This act of creation is not one of pure, unfettered expression; it is a service rendered to the platform, which in turn monetizes the resulting IP.

  

#### **1. The Contractual Framework: A Unilateral Offer to the World**

  

The governing agreement for any social media platform is its Terms of Service (ToS), a classic contract of adhesion that users must accept to participate.1 While some legal scholars have analyzed these as complex bilateral or "networked" contracts, the core transaction of content creation aligns more precisely with the structure of a unilateral contract: a promise made in exchange for the performance of an act.3

  - **The Offer:** The platform's offer is, in essence: "If you create content that adheres to our rules (as defined by our ToS, Community Guidelines, and moderation policies) and upload it using our interface, we will host it and make it available to others." This is a one-sided promise that becomes active only when the other party completes the specified task.6
  - **Acceptance by Performance:** The user does not enter into a bilateral negotiation or exchange mutual promises to create specific content. Instead, they accept the platform's standing offer each time they perform the act of creating and uploading a post, photo, or video.7 The contract is formed only upon the completion of this performance.6
  - **Consideration:** The consideration exchanged is multifaceted. The user receives access to the platform and potential visibility. The platform, in turn, receives a broad, perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free, and sublicensable license to the user's IP, which is the essential raw material for its advertising-based business model.9

This unilateral framework is crucial because it defines the relationship not as a partnership, but as a task-based exchange. The platform sets the terms, and the user performs the task to receive the benefit.

  

#### **2. The Mechanisms of External Direction**

  

The assertion that this relationship constitutes a service rests on the degree of control the platform exerts over the user's creative labor. This control is not wielded through direct orders, but through a sophisticated and pervasive system of architectural and policy-based direction.

  - **User Experience (UX) as "Soft" Control:** A platform's UX is not a neutral canvas; it is an intentional design that guides and constrains user expression to elicit specific, monetizable behaviors.11 Character limits, content formats (e.g., Reels vs. static images), engagement-driven algorithms, and even the design of buttons and menus all serve to channel creativity into forms that maximize platform engagement.12 The entire interface is engineered to influence user behavior and shape the nature of the IP being created.15
  - **Content Moderation as "Hard" Control:** If the UX provides the "rules of the road," the platform's moderation and policy enforcement systems are the traffic police. These systems, governed by extensive Community Standards, give the platform broad discretion to remove content, suspend accounts, or "shadowban" (algorithmically suppress) content that, while not illegal, violates its subjective and often vaguely defined policies.10 This active management of content transforms the platform from a passive host into an active publisher that is directing the "manner and means" of user expression.18
  - **Algorithmic Amplification as Editorial Choice:** The platform's algorithms do not merely display content; they make editorial decisions at a massive scale. By prioritizing and amplifying content that drives engagement, the platform creates powerful incentives for users to produce IP that conforms to the algorithm's preferences.20 This algorithmic curation is a form of indirect control that is foreseeable, quantifiable, and essential to the platform's business model.20

  

#### **3. The Legal Implication: User-Generated Content as a Service**

  

When a user creates IP within this highly controlled environment, their creative act is legally re-characterized. It is no longer a purely independent act of expression but a service performed for the benefit of the platform.

  - **The ToS and the Illusion of Ownership:** Platform ToS are meticulously drafted to state that users retain ownership of their IP.9 However, the simultaneous granting of a perpetual, royalty-free, and sublicensable license to the platform effectively transfers the most commercially valuable rights. This legal structure allows the platform to acquire the benefits of the user's creative labor without assuming the responsibilities of an employer or client.24
  - **The Unilateral Contract as a Service Agreement:** The platform's unilateral offer is not simply for "content," but for "content that meets our specifications." The user's performance is the service of creating this compliant IP. This dynamic is particularly clear in California, where the provision of "labor or services for remuneration" triggers a statutory presumption of employment under the state's retroactive "ABC test".24 Access to a service is a valid form of remuneration, and the creation of content is a form of labor.25 Under the ABC test, a platform would almost certainly fail to prove that its users are independent contractors, as content creation is the core of the platform's business (failing Prong B) and most users are not operating independent content-creation businesses (failing Prong C).24
  - **Moderation as Managerial Function:** When platforms deputize certain users as moderators with the power to enforce the ToS, they create a de facto managerial class. These moderators are performing a supervisory service, directing the work of other users by approving, denying, or removing their content.18 This creates a "cascading misclassification" crisis, where the platform acts as a joint employer, liable for the actions of its uncompensated managers who are, in turn, supervising an uncompensated workforce.18

  

#### **Conclusion**

  

The architecture of modern social media platforms is a testament to a new form of labor management. Through a unilateral contract offered to billions, these platforms direct a global workforce to create the intellectual property that constitutes the entire value of their enterprise. The control they exert is not overt, but is embedded in the very code of the user experience and enforced through opaque moderation policies.

While the ToS may frame this relationship as a simple license-for-access, the functional and economic reality is one of service. Users are providing the valuable service of content creation, tailored to the platform's specifications and for the platform's ultimate commercial benefit. This re-framing has profound implications, particularly under California law, suggesting that the foundational business model of the social media economy may be built upon a systemic misclassification of its most essential workers.
