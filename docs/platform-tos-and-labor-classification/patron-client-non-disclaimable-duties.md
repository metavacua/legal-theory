# **Beyond Employment: Non-Disclaimable Duties in Patron, Client, and Professional Relationships Under California Law**

  
  

## **Executive Summary**

  

While Terms of Service agreements for digital platforms and services are drafted to minimize liability, they are not impervious to the foundational principles of California law. Standard agreements often contain broad disclaimers of responsibility, but these disclaimers can be rendered unenforceable when they conflict with statutory duties or public policy. This report explores a different prong of misclassification, moving beyond the employee/independent contractor dichotomy to examine how user relationships with a service provider can be legally characterized as "patron" or "client" relationships. These classifications are not merely semantic; they trigger a hierarchy of non-disclaimable duties under California law.

The analysis finds that California law establishes a baseline, non-disclaimable "duty of care" for all individuals and businesses to prevent foreseeable harm.1 Beyond this general obligation, specific statutes impose heightened duties on businesses toward their "patrons" in various contexts, from ensuring physical safety in public venues to protecting the confidentiality of library records.2 These duties are often codified and cannot be waived through a standard user agreement.

Furthermore, where a relationship is built on a foundation of trust and confidence—where a user entrusts their assets, data, or interests to a platform—a fiduciary relationship may be established. This "client" relationship imposes the highest standard of care known to law, including uncompromising duties of loyalty, care, and confidentiality.5 Such duties are fundamental and cannot be disclaimed. The doctrine of unconscionability provides California courts with a powerful tool to invalidate contractual terms that are overly harsh or one-sided, particularly in agreements where there is a significant imbalance of bargaining power.8 Consequently, a platform's attempt to disclaim these fundamental duties through its Terms of Service is vulnerable to legal challenge.

  

## **Part I: The Foundation of Non-Disclaimable Duties in California**

  

The enforceability of any contract in California is subject to overarching legal principles that prevent agreements from violating public policy or fundamental standards of fairness. These principles form the basis for duties that cannot be simply waived by a clause in a Terms of Service agreement.

  

### **1.1 The General Duty of Care (California Civil Code § 1714)**

  

The cornerstone of liability in California is established in Civil Code § 1714(a), which states: "Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person".1 This statute codifies a universal duty of care that all persons and entities owe to one another.

This is not an abstract principle; it creates a direct, non-disclaimable obligation for business owners to their patrons.1 A business must exercise reasonable care to keep its premises and operations safe for visitors.11 While a contract may attempt to limit liability, it cannot fully absolve a business of this fundamental duty to act with "ordinary care or skill" to prevent foreseeable harm.12

  

### **1.2 The Doctrine of Unconscionability**

  

California law provides a powerful tool for courts to police unfair contracts: the doctrine of unconscionability. A court can refuse to enforce a contract or any of its terms if it finds the agreement to be unconscionable, meaning it is so unfair that it "shocks the conscience".8

The analysis involves two components 9:

  - **Procedural Unconscionability**: This focuses on the process of contract formation and the presence of "oppression" or "surprise." It often arises from unequal bargaining power, such as in a "take it or leave it" contract of adhesion where the user has no ability to negotiate terms.9
  - **Substantive Unconscionability**: This examines the fairness of the terms themselves, looking for provisions that are "overly harsh" or "so one-sided" as to be unreasonable.9 Examples include severe limitations on the seller's liability or on a consumer's right to seek legal relief.8

California courts use a "sliding scale" approach: the more procedurally unconscionable the contract formation is, the less evidence of substantive unconscionability is required to invalidate a term, and vice versa.9 This doctrine is particularly relevant for challenging broad liability disclaimers in standard Terms of Service, as these agreements are almost always procedurally unconscionable.

  

## **Part II: The "Patron" Relationship and Statutory Duties**

  

California law frequently uses the term "patron" to define a relationship that carries specific, codified duties that go beyond the general duty of care. These duties are imposed by statute and reflect a legislative determination that patrons of certain businesses require enhanced protection.

  

### **2.1 Defining the "Patron"**

  

The term "patron" appears across the California Codes to describe individuals interacting with a wide range of entities, including cooperative corporations, public transit, commercial venues, libraries, and public utilities.2 This legal classification signifies a recognized relationship between an individual and an establishment that gives rise to specific rights and responsibilities. For example, in a cooperative, "patrons" are the members who use the co-op's services and are entitled to an equitable distribution of its benefits.2 In public venues like bars or amusement parks, "patrons" are the customers whose safety and well-being are subject to specific regulations.14

  

### **2.2 Specific, Non-Disclaimable Duties to Patrons**

  

Many duties owed to patrons are explicitly defined by statute and cannot be waived through a private agreement.

  - **Duty to Protect from Foreseeable Harm**: Business owners have a duty to take reasonable steps to protect patrons from foreseeable risks, which can include the criminal or tortious conduct of third parties.12 A proprietor of a bar, for instance, has a duty to exercise reasonable care to protect patrons from injury at the hands of other guests.12 This duty arises from the special relationship between the venue and its customers.15
  - **Premises Liability**: Property owners owe the highest duty of care to "invitees"—a legal category that includes patrons of a business. This duty requires the owner to keep the premises reasonably free of dangerous conditions by performing regular inspections and either fixing hazards or posting clear warnings.11
  - **Industry-Specific Statutory Duties**: The law imposes numerous specific duties on certain industries. For example, "dram shop" laws can hold an establishment liable for injuries caused by a patron who was served alcohol while obviously intoxicated.14 Amusement parks must report patron injuries and carry liability insurance, and public swimming pools must have specific safety equipment.2
  - **Duty of Confidentiality**: In certain contexts, the patron relationship includes a duty of confidentiality. The most prominent example is Government Code § 7927.105, which makes all "patron use records" at a public library confidential.2

  

## **Part III: The "Client" Relationship and Fiduciary Duties**

  

The most stringent standard of care recognized by California law is a fiduciary duty. This duty arises in relationships where one party (the "client" or beneficiary) places special trust and confidence in another (the fiduciary). While often associated with professionals like attorneys and trustees, the duty can be imposed in any relationship where such confidence is reposed and accepted.5

  

### **3.1 The Core Fiduciary Duties**

  

A fiduciary relationship imposes several fundamental duties that are non-disclaimable as a matter of public policy 5:

  - **Duty of Loyalty**: This is the paramount duty, requiring the fiduciary to act solely in the best interest of the client. The fiduciary must avoid any conflicts of interest or acts of self-dealing.6
  - **Duty of Care**: A fiduciary must manage the client's affairs with the reasonable care, skill, and caution that a prudent person would exercise in a similar situation.6
  - **Duty of Confidentiality**: This duty requires the fiduciary to preserve the client's secrets "at every peril to himself or herself".6 It is a far more rigorous standard than in a typical commercial relationship.

  

### **3.2 Application to Digital Platforms and Services**

  

The concept of a fiduciary duty is increasingly being applied to the digital realm. A platform that acts as a "custodian" of a user's digital assets may be seen as taking on fiduciary-like responsibilities. California's Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act defines a "user" as a person with an account with a "custodian," and imposes duties of care, loyalty, and confidentiality on fiduciaries charged with managing such assets.18

An argument can be made that when a platform encourages users to entrust it with vast amounts of personal data, creative content, and other digital assets, and then uses those assets for its own commercial benefit, it has voluntarily accepted a position of trust and confidence. In such a scenario, a court could find that the platform owes its users fiduciary duties. An explicit disclaimer of these duties in the Terms of Service would be highly vulnerable to being struck down as unconscionable, as it would represent an attempt to waive the very essence of the trust-based relationship the platform has cultivated.

  

## **Conclusion**

  

The written agreements governing the use of digital services do not exist in a legal vacuum. Under California law, the nature of the relationship between a service provider and a user can give rise to duties that cannot be contractually disclaimed. A user may be legally classified as a "patron," triggering a host of specific, non-waivable statutory protections related to safety, fairness, and privacy. In cases where a platform acts as a custodian for a user's data and digital life, it may even assume the role of a "fiduciary," imposing the highest duties of loyalty and care recognized by law. Any attempt to disclaim these foundational duties through one-sided Terms of Service is subject to invalidation under the doctrine of unconscionability, leaving the service provider exposed to liability for breaches of duties they may have believed they had successfully waived.
