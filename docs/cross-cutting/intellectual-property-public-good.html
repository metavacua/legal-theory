Intellectual Property as a Public Good: An Analysis of Non-Commercial Creation and Contribution

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Introduction: Beyond the Service Model

While intellectual property (IP) is often created within a commercial, service-based framework, a significant portion of creative and innovative work is intended for the benefit of a community, government, or other organization without a traditional client-provider relationship. These scenarios, which often involve mutual aid, material support, or the advancement of a public good, rely on legal and economic arguments that distinguish the act of creation from a compensable service.

This report examines the material frameworks that enable the creation and transfer of IP in a manner that is potentially tax-free, unencumbered by significant administrative burdens, and rooted in a relationship of support rather than commerce. The analysis focuses on three primary models: the treatment of IP as a donatable asset, the use of collaborative licensing frameworks like Open Source and Creative Commons, and the specific legal status of IP created for or by government entities. These models demonstrate that the "property" aspect of IP is the key mechanism that allows creators to contribute their work to the public good outside of a service-for-hire context.

1. The Constitutional Shield: IP as a Donatable Asset

The foundational argument for non-service creation rests on the legal nature of intellectual property in the United States. The U.S. Constitution empowers Congress to grant creators exclusive rights to their works, establishing IP as a form of property.1 This property right is fundamentally a right to

exclude others from using the creation, which is the philosophical opposite of an obligation to provide a service.3 An inventor or author who holds a patent or copyright can legally refuse to license or sell it to anyone.3

This principle of ownership is what enables a creator to voluntarily contribute their IP. Instead of selling their work as a service, they can create it and then transfer ownership or grant permissions for its use as a gift or contribution.

Legal and Economic Framework

A creator can transfer their IP to a qualifying nonprofit organization, community group, or even a government entity through a simple assignment or license agreement.5 In this model, the creation is not a service rendered

for the organization but an asset transferred to the organization. The transaction is one of material support, not a fee for service.

Tax and Regulatory Implications

The tax implications of donating IP are complex and hinge on the distinction between donating services and donating property.

  • Donation of Services vs. Property: The value of donated time or services is not tax-deductible.7 A graphic designer who volunteers to create a logo for a charity cannot deduct the value of their labor. However, intellectual property is a recognized asset, and its donation can qualify as a tax-deductible charitable contribution.10

  • Limitations on Deductions: The American Jobs Creation Act of 2004 significantly altered the tax benefits for donating IP. The donor's initial deduction is limited to the lesser of the property's fair market value or their adjusted basis (cost) in the property.12 The law allows for potential additional deductions in subsequent years based on the net income the charity earns from the donated IP.12

  • Achieving a "Tax-Free" and "Low-Burden" Scenario: A truly tax-free and low-burden scenario for the creator is achieved when they donate the IP without seeking a tax deduction. In this case, the transfer is a gift, generating no income and thus no tax liability for the creator.13 The administrative burden can be minimal, requiring only a written agreement to transfer the rights. The process becomes administratively complex only when the donor seeks to claim a tax deduction, which may require formal appraisals and specific IRS reporting by both the donor and the recipient organization.12

Therefore, the "Constitutional Shield" argument aligns with a mutual aid scenario where a creator formally transfers their property rights to an organization without the expectation of payment or a significant tax benefit, thereby avoiding both service classification and regulatory complexity.

2. Collaborative Frameworks: Open Source and Creative Commons

The "Independent Vision" argument, which posits that creators often seek to protect their work from market pressures, finds its most robust legal and economic expression in collaborative licensing models. Open Source Software (OSS) and Creative Commons (CC) are sophisticated legal frameworks designed to facilitate the creation and sharing of IP as a community resource or public good.

Open Source Software (OSS)

The OSS model is built on the principle of collaborative, decentralized development.16 While the underlying code is protected by copyright, OSS licenses use those copyright protections to enforce norms of openness rather than exclusion.17

  • Legal and Economic Framework: OSS licenses grant users the right to use, study, modify, and distribute the software and its source code for any purpose.18 This creates a "commons" where contributions are a form of mutual aid, benefiting all users and co-developers.18 The legal structure is a standardized, pre-written license that removes the need for individual negotiation. While developers can charge for OSS, the licenses permit free redistribution, which severely constrains the ability to sell the code itself.17 Monetization typically comes from selling related services and support rather than the IP itself.19

  • Tax and Regulatory Implications: For an individual contributing to an OSS project without direct payment, the act is generally tax-neutral—it generates no income and offers no deduction.20 If a developer receives payments through crowdfunding or sponsorship platforms, this is typically treated as taxable self-employment income, not a tax-free gift, because it is compensation for their work.13 This introduces a tax burden. The administrative overhead for participating in OSS is extremely low, often involving nothing more than choosing a standard license for a project.19

Creative Commons (CC)

Similarly, Creative Commons provides a suite of standardized licenses that allow creators of artistic and literary works to grant the public permission to share and use their work under specific conditions.21

  • Legal and Economic Framework: CC licenses are legal tools that modify the default "all rights reserved" nature of copyright to "some rights reserved".21 A creator can choose a license that allows for reuse and modification, creating a framework for mutual aid and cultural sharing. Critically, licenses can include a NonCommercial (NC) clause, which explicitly permits use for non-commercial purposes only, directly aligning with the user's scenario.21 These licenses are irrevocable, ensuring that once a work is contributed to the commons, it remains there.23

  • Tax and Regulatory Implications: Applying a CC license is a simple, no-cost action with no direct tax consequences. It is a declaration of permissions, not a financial transaction. It allows a creator to contribute their work to a community or the public at large with minimal administrative burden while retaining ownership of the copyright.

Both OSS and CC models provide robust, legally sound frameworks for creating and distributing IP in a system of mutual support, largely free from the tax and administrative burdens of commercial transactions.

3. Creation for the Public Domain: Government Works

A distinct scenario arises when IP is created for a government entity. The legal and economic outcome in these cases can represent the most complete form of contribution to the public good.

  • Works by Federal Employees: Under U.S. copyright law, a "work of the United States Government" is defined as a work prepared by a federal officer or employee as part of their official duties.25 Such works are not entitled to copyright protection in the U.S. and are automatically placed in the public domain.26 This is a clear-cut case where IP is created for society, is entirely free of IP restrictions, and involves no additional tax or regulatory burden beyond the creator's normal employment.

  • Works from Federally Funded Research: The Bayh-Dole Act governs IP created through federally funded research. It allows universities and small businesses to retain the title to their inventions but grants the federal government a "nonexclusive, nontransferable, irrevocable, paid-up license" to use the invention.28 This arrangement ensures that the public, having funded the research, retains a material interest and right to use the resulting IP. While the creator can commercialize the IP, the public's contribution is secured through this permanent, royalty-free license, reflecting a form of societal mutual aid. This framework, however, does involve significant administrative and reporting requirements.28

Conclusion

The arguments against a universal service model for IP creation provide a strong basis for scenarios involving mutual aid and the contribution of IP for the public good. The key is the legal status of IP as a form of property, which can be owned, licensed, and transferred by choice.

  • A creator can donate their IP as a tangible asset, a transaction that can be structured to be tax-free and low-burden, provided no tax deduction is sought.

  • Creators can use established legal frameworks like Open Source and Creative Commons licenses to release their work into a collaborative commons, creating a system of mutual aid with minimal administrative overhead.

  • IP created for or by the U.S. federal government can directly enter the public domain or be subject to broad, royalty-free government use rights, ensuring a return to the public that supported its creation.

These models demonstrate that IP creation is not inherently an act of service. Through deliberate legal and economic choices, creators can structure the creation and dissemination of their work to function as a contribution, a gift, or a shared resource, aligning with principles of community support and the advancement of the public good.
