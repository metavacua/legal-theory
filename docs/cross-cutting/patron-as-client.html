The Patron as Client: Analyzing Crowdfunded Commissions Under California Labor Law

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Introduction: The Blurring Line Between Support and Service

Crowdfunding and patronage platforms like Patreon and Kickstarter have created powerful new models for sustainable artistic creation, built on a foundation of mutual aid between creators and their communities. However, a significant legal question arises when this relationship shifts from general support to specific performance. When a creator invites patrons to specify the creation of intellectual property (IP) in exchange for their financial contribution, the arrangement begins to resemble a traditional commission.

Under California's stringent and retroactive worker classification laws, this distinction is paramount. This report examines the specific scenario where a patron's financial support is directly tied to the creation of a requested piece of IP. It analyzes how this "patronage-commission" hybrid is viewed through the lens of California's "ABC test," the legal standard for determining employment status. The analysis concludes that once a patron's payment is exchanged for the right to direct creative work, the relationship is highly likely to be legally defined as a contract for services, exposing the facilitating platform to systemic misclassification risk.

The Legal Framework: California's Retroactive ABC Test

The analysis of any labor relationship in California begins with the "ABC test," a rigid, three-pronged standard established in the landmark Dynamex decision and applied retroactively.1 This test presumes a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can prove

all three of the following conditions:

  • (A) The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity.

  • (B) The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.

  • (C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.1

Before the ABC test is applied, a court must first determine that the relationship involves "labor or services for remuneration".2 In the case of a patron paying for a specific creative work, this threshold is easily met. The creator provides skilled labor, and the patronage is the remuneration. Under California's "Predominant Factor Test," the essence of this specific agreement is the service of creating IP, not merely the sale of a finished good.1

The Patron-as-Client Scenario: Applying the ABC Test

When a creator on a platform like Patreon offers a tier where patrons can request and receive a specific piece of art, or promises to fulfill all such requests from a pool of patrons, the legal nature of the relationship is fundamentally altered. It shifts from a model of general support for an independent vision to a specific, transactional exchange.

1. Identifying the "Hiring Entity"

In a legal challenge, the platform itself (Patreon, Kickstarter, etc.) is the most likely target to be designated the "hiring entity." The platform is not a passive intermediary; it controls the entire ecosystem, processes payments, sets the terms of service, and provides the tools that define the relationship.1 The platform's business model is predicated on facilitating this exact type of transaction between creators and their audiences.

2. Analyzing the Three Prongs of the Test

When the platform is viewed as the hiring entity, its ability to satisfy the ABC test in a patronage-commission scenario becomes exceptionally difficult.

  • Prong A (Control): Failure. The creator is subject to two layers of control. First, the platform exercises significant control through its terms of service, payment processing requirements, and "algorithmic management," which guides creator and patron behavior.1 Second, and more directly in this scenario, the patron exerts control over the "manner and means" of the work by specifying the IP to be created. The creator is no longer fully autonomous; they are fulfilling a client's (the patron's) directive. This dual control makes it highly unlikely that the creator would be considered "free from control and direction".1

  • Prong B (Course of Business): Failure. This prong requires the creator's work to be outside the platform's usual course of business. This is a clear failure. The core business of platforms like Patreon and Kickstarter is to facilitate the creation and funding of content. The intellectual labor provided by creators is not ancillary to the platform's business; it is the business. The platform's value is derived entirely from the creative work it hosts and monetizes.1

  • Prong C (Independent Business): The "Hobbyist Dilemma." This is the most critical and systemic failure point for platforms. To satisfy Prong C, the platform must prove the creator is "customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business." While many full-time, professional creators on these platforms would meet this standard (often operating as LLCs or sole proprietors), the platforms' business models are built on a massive scale that relies on millions of casual, non-professional "hobbyist" creators.1 A hobbyist, by legal and tax definitions, is not engaged in an independent business with an intent to profit.3 Therefore, for this substantial segment of their user base, platforms cannot satisfy Prong C. This structural conflict between the business model and the legal requirement is what is termed the "Hobbyist Dilemma".1

Distinctions Between Crowdfunding Models

While the legal risk applies broadly, the structure of different platforms can heighten the vulnerability.

  • Patreon and Subscription Models: In its purest form, Patreon is a subscription for ongoing support, which offers a stronger argument for a non-service relationship. However, the moment a creator introduces a "commission" tier—"pay this amount and I will create X for you"—that specific transaction becomes a contract for services. The IRS already treats Patreon income as self-employment income, not as a gift, because there is an expectation of receiving something in return (content, benefits, etc.).7 Adding a specific, patron-directed deliverable solidifies this transactional nature.

  • Kickstarter and Project-Based Models: Kickstarter's model is even more closely aligned with a service contract. Its Terms of Use explicitly state that a contract is formed between the creator and the backer, obligating the creator to fulfill promised rewards.10 While Kickstarter frames this as "helping to create something new" rather than buying from a store, the legal reality is an exchange of money for a future good or service.14 If a backer's reward is a custom piece of IP based on their pledge, the service relationship is unambiguous.

Conclusion: Remuneration for Directed Labor is a Service

When a creator uses a crowdfunding platform to solicit and accept payment in exchange for producing IP specified by the paying patron, the "against" arguments (personal passion, independent vision) are legally superseded by the economic reality of the transaction. Under California's retroactive ABC test, this arrangement constitutes "labor or services for remuneration."

The facilitating platform, as the likely hiring entity, would almost certainly fail Prongs B and C of the test for the vast majority of its casual creators. The work is central to the platform's business, and the creators are often hobbyists, not independent businesses. This creates a systemic risk of worker misclassification, where these creators could be legally deemed employees of the platform. Consequently, while the patronage-commission model can provide vital material support, under California law it transforms the relationship into a service contract, moving it squarely into the category of employment and undermining the argument that the creation is not an act of service.
