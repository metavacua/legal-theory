Google User Contracts — Implicit Employment Obligations

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Here is an analysis of the two scenarios you presented, which highlight the tension between the transactional nature of a unilateral contract and the continuous obligations of an employment relationship.

Scenario 1: "If I perform a specific act, and they never pay out?"

You complete the performance required by the unilateral offer (e.g., find and report a qualifying vulnerability according to the strict rules of a VRP), but the reward is denied.

In theory, acceptance by performance completes the contract, and the offeror is obligated to provide the consideration. However, the analysis hinges on the enforceability of the contract terms.

The Challenge of "Sole Discretion"

Companies universally include language in VRPs stating that the decision to issue a reward, the amount, and the validity of the submission are at their "sole discretion." This is intended to manage the program and avoid disputes.

Arguments to Compel Payment

If payment is denied despite a valid submission, several legal arguments could be utilized:

  1. Breach of Contract and Good Faith: Contract law generally implies a covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Even "sole discretion" cannot typically be exercised arbitrarily, irrationally, or in bad faith. If a researcher can demonstrate they objectively met all published criteria, denying the reward could be argued as a bad-faith breach.

  2. Promissory Estoppel: As you noted, this can substitute for consideration or overcome a weak contract. If Google makes a promise (the VRP structure), and a researcher reasonably relies on that promise to their detriment (expending significant time, skill, and resources), the promise may be enforced to avoid injustice. Google receives a significant benefit (valuable IP and improved security) by inducing this labor.

  3. Illusory Promise: If the discretion is so absolute that the company is never actually bound to pay, the promise of a reward might be deemed "illusory," meaning no contract existed in the first place. In such a case, the researcher might seek compensation based on the fair market value of the IP transferred (quantum meruit).

Scenario 2: The Employee Argument ("I am and have been this entire time an Employee")

This is the more fundamental challenge. If the totality of the relationship—the continuous provision of services (UGC, feedback, QA, AI training), the degree of control exerted by Google (via rules, algorithms, and gamification), and the integral nature of the work—establishes an employment relationship, the VRP structure becomes secondary.

If you are legally deemed a misclassified employee, the analysis shifts entirely from contract law to employment law.

The Employer's Burdens

Yes, if an employment relationship exists, Google assumes all the "burdens" of an employer. This fundamentally changes how compensation and IP are treated:

  1. Wages vs. Prizes: If you are an employee, the work you perform is labor. You are entitled to wages (including minimum wage and overtime) for all hours worked, regardless of whether a specific task was successful. The VRP structure, which only pays for results, is generally incompatible with standard employment wage requirements. The "reward" is recharacterized as a wage, commission, or bonus. Failure to pay wages for labor performed constitutes wage theft.

  2. Benefits and Protections: Employers are responsible for payroll taxes, workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, and potentially other benefits mandated by local labor laws.

  3. IP Ownership (Work Made for Hire): In an employment context, intellectual property created by an employee within the scope of their employment automatically belongs to the employer. You would not "own" the vulnerability discovery to sell it back to Google via the VRP; Google owns it the moment you discover it.

The Implications of Performing the Act but Not Reporting It

If you find a vulnerability (perform the act) but do not report it, and you are deemed an employee:

  1. Compensation: You are still entitled to wages for the time spent performing the skilled labor required to find it.

  2. Employee Duties: However, employees have a duty of loyalty to their employer. If your implied job function includes improving product security or quality, discovering a vulnerability (which is the employer's IP) and intentionally failing to report it would likely constitute a serious breach of your duties as an employee.

Synthesis and Damages

The argument you are constructing is that the various unilateral contracts (VRPs, Local Guides, UXR) are merely components of a broader, misclassified employment relationship.

If the "Employee" argument prevails:

  • The focus shifts from unpaid "rewards" for specific acts to systemic unpaid wages for the entirety of the services rendered (all feedback, all testing, all content creation) over the duration of the "employment."

  • The discovery of high-value IP (like a vulnerability or sophisticated AI feedback) serves as evidence of the skilled nature of the labor provided, justifying the claim for fair compensation, rather than being a standalone transaction.
