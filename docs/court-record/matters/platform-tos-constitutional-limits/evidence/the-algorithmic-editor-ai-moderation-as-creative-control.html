The Algorithmic Editor: AI Moderation as Creative Control in the Platform Economy

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Introduction: From Passive Host to Active Publisher

Social media platforms have long maintained the legal fiction that they are neutral intermediaries, passive hosts for user-generated content (UGC). This position, historically shielded by Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, is becoming increasingly untenable in the face of two realities: the economic necessity of content moderation and the evolving legal understanding of authorship in the age of artificial intelligence.

A platform's content moderation system—governed by extensive Community Standards and executed by a combination of human reviewers and automated AI—is not merely a janitorial function for removing illegal content. It is an active, pervasive, and powerful form of editorial and creative control.1 This report argues that this system of mechanized control, when analyzed through the lens of recent U.S. Copyright Office (USCO) guidance on AI-assisted creation, functions as a form of creative direction. This direction is so profound that it transforms the user's act of creation into a service rendered to the platform, with significant implications under California's stringent labor laws.

1. Moderation as an Act of Creative Direction

A platform's value is derived from user engagement, which is driven by advertising revenue. To maximize this value, platforms must curate a user experience that is both engaging and "brand-safe" for advertisers. Content moderation is the primary tool for achieving this balance.

This is not a passive process. It involves a cascade of discretionary decisions that actively shape the creative output of users:

  • Architectural Control: The User Experience (UX) itself is a form of "soft" control, guiding users toward creating specific types of monetizable content through character limits, algorithmic preferences, and interface design.

  • Policy-Based Control: Vague and subjective Community Standards give platforms broad discretion to remove, suppress, or "shadowban" content that is not illegal but is deemed "objectionable". This forces creators to self-censor and conform to the platform's preferred aesthetic and ideological boundaries.

  • Mechanized Enforcement: This control is executed at scale by AI systems. These automated tools use techniques like perceptual hashing to identify and block or monetize content, and are increasingly using Large Language Models (LLMs) to determine if content violates platform policies.

This system functions as a set of creative constraints. The user is not free to express themselves in any manner; they must create a "work" that can survive the platform's automated editorial review. This is a form of creative direction, where the platform acts as an executive producer, setting the parameters for acceptable contributions to its overall media product.

2. The AI Authorship Analogy: Who Holds Creative Control?

The U.S. Copyright Office (USCO) has recently issued guidance on the copyrightability of works created with the assistance of generative AI. The central principle of this guidance is that copyright protection depends on the degree of human creative control over the AI's output.3 A mere text prompt is considered an unprotectable "idea." For a human to be the "author" of an AI-generated image, they must demonstrate that they exercised sufficient creative control over the expressive elements of the final work.3

This framework provides a powerful analogy for the social media context, but with the roles inverted.

  • The User's Contribution: The human user provides the initial, original work of authorship—the text, photo, or video. This is the core expressive act.

  • The Platform's Contribution: The platform's AI (its moderation and algorithmic amplification systems) then exercises the final, decisive "creative control" over that work. It determines the final form of the work that is actually "published" to the public, whether it is displayed prominently, suppressed, or removed entirely.

If the USCO's standard for authorship is "intellectual domination" over the final expressive output, then it can be argued that the platform, through its AI, is asserting a form of co-authorship or "executive authorship" over the collective work of its user base.3 The platform is not merely hosting content; its AI is actively arranging, transforming, and curating a vast compilation of user submissions into a monetizable commercial product. This mechanized curation is a creative act of selection and arrangement, which is itself a copyrightable form of expression.

3. The Legal Consequence: A Service Relationship Under California Law

This dynamic—where a platform uses AI to exert creative control over user-generated IP—has profound implications under California's worker classification laws. The relationship, framed by the platform as a simple license-for-access, is vulnerable to re-characterization as a contract for services, triggering the state's retroactive "ABC test".5

  • Prong A (Control): The platform's moderation and algorithmic systems are the ultimate tools of control and direction. The argument that a user is "free from control" is severely weakened when their work is subject to constant, automated scrutiny and potential suppression based on the platform's subjective and commercial interests.1 The AI functions as an "algorithm as boss," dictating the "manner and means" of acceptable creative expression.5

  • Prong B (Course of Business): The platform's business is not merely providing hosting; it is curating and monetizing a stream of user-generated content. The creative labor of users is not outside the platform's usual course of business; it is the business.5

  • Prong C (Independent Business): This remains the most significant failure point. The platform's business model relies on the contributions of millions of casual "hobbyists," who are, by legal definition, not "customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business".5

This legal analysis is further complicated by the influence of third parties. Payment processors, for example, often impose their own Acceptable Use Policies, creating another layer of content restriction that creators must adhere to in order to monetize their work. This functions as a form of indirect control, potentially implicating the payment processor as a joint employer under California's broad definition.5

Conclusion

The act of content moderation on social media is far more than a simple filtering mechanism. It is a sophisticated system of mechanized creative control that directs and shapes the IP created by users to serve the platform's commercial interests. When viewed through the copyright principles of AI-assisted authorship, the platform emerges not as a passive host but as an active "executive author," transforming the raw creative output of its users into a curated, monetizable product.

This level of creative control and transformation of value fundamentally alters the legal nature of the relationship. Under California law, it provides powerful evidence that the user is not a mere licensor, but a service provider in a misclassified employment relationship. The very AI systems designed to protect the platform's business model may, in fact, be creating its single greatest legal liability.
