# **Cooperative Securities in the Global Marketplace: A Multi-Jurisdictional Analysis**

  
  

## **Executive Summary**

  

The prospect of a cooperative enterprise accessing public capital markets requires navigating a complex and overlapping web of state, federal, and international regulations, as well as the specific rules of securities exchanges. This report provides a comprehensive analysis of this intersection, examining the legal frameworks that govern the creation, taxation, and trading of cooperative securities.

The analysis begins with the foundational legal layers in the United States. At the state level, California's Cooperative Corporation Law provides the essential statutory exceptions that permit a hybrid governance model, allowing for a dual-class share structure that can accommodate both member-patrons and public investors.1 At the federal level, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has, through a series of no-action letters, established a critical precedent: cooperative shares that primarily serve a membership function and lack the traditional characteristics of an investment (such as profit-based dividends, transferability, and appreciation potential) are generally not considered "securities" under federal law.2 This distinction is paramount, as it provides the legal basis for creating a non-traded class of member shares exempt from SEC registration. Concurrently, the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) provides a distinct tax framework under Subchapter T, which treats the cooperative as a pass-through entity for patronage-sourced income, a significant departure from standard corporate taxation.5

The report then provides a detailed examination of the listing and governance requirements of the three primary U.S. securities trading venues: the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), the NASDAQ Stock Market, and the OTC Markets Group. Each exchange has specific financial, public float, and corporate governance standards that a cooperative's publicly-traded security class must meet.7 Crucially, both the NYSE and NASDAQ permit dual-class voting structures, which is the linchpin that makes the hybrid cooperative model viable for a public listing.10 The OTC Markets offer a more accessible, tiered alternative with less stringent, though still significant, requirements.12

Finally, the report addresses the international dimension. It clarifies that there is no single "international court law" governing corporate securities. Instead, international law manifests as a patchwork of national regulations that a cooperative must navigate if it seeks to cross-list on foreign exchanges. While principles from bodies like the International Co-operative Alliance inform the cooperative's ethos, legal compliance remains a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction and exchange-by-exchange undertaking.1

The conclusion is that a pathway for a publicly-traded cooperative exists but requires meticulous legal architecture. The entity must be structured to satisfy California cooperative law, federal SEC and IRS regulations, and the specific listing and governance rules of its chosen trading venue.

  

## **Part I: The U.S. Legal & Regulatory Framework**

  
  

### **Section 1.1: California Cooperative Corporation Law: The Enabling Framework**

  

The legal foundation for a publicly-traded cooperative in the United States begins at the state level. California's Cooperative Corporation Law provides a uniquely flexible framework for this endeavor.1 The chosen entity is a "General Stock Cooperative Corporation," which is explicitly authorized to issue stock.1

The primary obstacle within cooperative law is the principle of "one-member, one-vote," which is the default rule under California Corporations Code § 12404.1 This democratic governance standard is incompatible with public capital markets, where investors demand voting power proportional to their investment. The viability of a hybrid model hinges on statutory exceptions within the law itself. Corp. Code § 12401(b)(7) mandates that if voting power is unequal, this must be clearly disclosed, implying that unequal voting is permissible if properly structured.1 This statutory gateway allows for the creation of a dual-class share structure:

  - **Class A Member Shares:** Adhere to cooperative principles with one-member, one-vote rights, transfer restrictions, and economic returns via patronage. These are designed to fall outside the SEC's definition of a security.1
  - **Class B Investor Shares:** Designed as traditional equity with per-share voting rights and profit-based dividends, intended to be listed and traded on a public exchange.1

Recent legislative changes, such as the California Worker Cooperative Act (AB 816) and AB 283, have further signaled the state's intent to make the cooperative model more flexible and capital-friendly, reducing the regulatory risk of such an innovative structure.1

  

### **Section 1.2: Federal Securities Law: The SEC's View on Cooperative Shares**

  

A cooperative seeking public investment must navigate the federal securities laws administered by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). The central question is whether the cooperative's membership shares are considered "securities" that require registration under the Securities Act of 1933.

While there is no blanket statutory exemption for all cooperative shares, the SEC has provided crucial guidance through a series of no-action letters.2 In these letters, the SEC staff has consistently taken the position that it would not recommend enforcement action against cooperatives that issue membership shares without registration, provided those shares function as an instrument of membership rather than as a financial investment.4

The analysis relies on the Supreme Court's test in *United Housing Foundation, Inc. v. Forman*, which established that the name "stock" is not dispositive. Instead, one must look at the economic realities of the transaction.15 The key factors that indicate a membership interest is

*not* a security include 2:

1.  **No Dividends:** The shares do not pay dividends contingent on profits. Economic returns come in the form of patronage refunds, which are based on the member's use of the cooperative, not their capital investment.15
2.  **Non-Transferable:** The shares are not freely negotiable and can only be transferred to other eligible members with the cooperative's approval, preventing speculative trading.4
3.  **No Appreciation:** The shares cannot appreciate in value and are typically redeemable only at their original par value.4
4.  **Democratic Control:** Each member has one vote, regardless of the number of shares owned, reinforcing that the shares' purpose is participation, not financial control.2

The SEC has issued favorable no-action letters to various cooperatives that structured their membership shares with these characteristics, including Entheos Audiology Cooperative, National Consumer Cooperative Bank, American Crystal Sugar Company, and Minn-Dak Farmers Cooperative.4 This body of interpretation provides a strong legal basis for the hybrid model's non-traded Class A Member Shares to be issued without SEC registration, while the Class B Investor Shares, which

*are* designed as investments, would be subject to full registration requirements for an IPO.1

  

### **Section 1.3: Federal Tax Law: The IRS and Subchapter T**

  

The Internal Revenue Code (IRC) provides a distinct tax regime for cooperatives under Subchapter T (§§ 1381-1388).5 This treatment is available to any corporation "operating on a cooperative basis," which is defined by principles such as subordination of capital, democratic control, and the proportionate allocation of profits to patrons.5

The core of Subchapter T is its treatment of **patronage dividends** (also called patronage refunds). A cooperative is allowed to deduct qualified patronage dividends from its gross income.5 This means the income generated from member business is taxed only once, at the member level, when the dividend is received.6 This pass-through treatment is a significant advantage over standard C corporations, where profits are taxed at the corporate level and again when distributed to shareholders as dividends.5

Key aspects of Subchapter T include:

  - **Patronage-Sourced Income:** The deduction applies only to income derived from business done with or for patrons.5 Income from non-patronage sources (e.g., investments or business with non-members) is taxed at the cooperative level at standard corporate rates.5
  - **Qualified Distributions:** To be deductible in the current year, patronage dividends must be "qualified." This generally requires that at least 20% of the distribution be paid in cash, with the remainder potentially paid in "qualified written notices of allocation" (a form of equity).6 The member must consent to take the full amount of the distribution into their taxable income for that year.6
  - **Form 1099-PATR:** Cooperatives report these distributions to members and the IRS on Form 1099-PATR.20

This tax structure reinforces the cooperative's purpose of providing economic benefit to its members based on their participation, aligning with the legal structure of the Class A Member Shares.

  

## **Part II: The Public Markets: Listing & Trading Rules**

  

A cooperative seeking to list its investor-class securities must comply with the specific rules of its chosen trading venue. The requirements of the three main U.S. markets—the NYSE, NASDAQ, and OTC Markets—vary significantly in terms of financial thresholds and corporate governance mandates.

  

### **Section 2.1: New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)**

  

The NYSE is the world's largest stock exchange and has the most stringent listing requirements.

  - **Listing Standards:** To list on the NYSE, a company must meet demanding standards for financial size and public share distribution. This includes minimums for the number of public shareholders, the market value of publicly-held shares, and at least one of several financial tests related to earnings, cash flow, or revenue.24
  - **Corporate Governance:** The NYSE Listed Company Manual, particularly Section 303A, outlines extensive corporate governance rules. These include requirements for a majority-independent board of directors, fully independent audit, compensation, and nominating committees, and the adoption of comprehensive corporate governance guidelines and a code of conduct.9 Companies listing in conjunction with an IPO are granted transition periods to phase in these requirements.9
  - **Dual-Class Shares:** Historically, the NYSE banned dual-class voting structures but reversed this policy in the 1980s due to competition from other exchanges.10 Today, the NYSE permits companies to list with dual-class share structures, which is essential for the hybrid cooperative model.10 However, once listed, a company cannot take actions that would disparately reduce the voting rights of an existing class of stock.27

  

### **Section 2.2: The NASDAQ Stock Market**

  

The NASDAQ is the second-largest exchange and is known for its technology-focused listings. It offers three tiers with escalating requirements: the NASDAQ Capital Market, the NASDAQ Global Market, and the NASDAQ Global Select Market.

  - **Listing Standards:** Each NASDAQ tier has its own set of initial and continued listing requirements, including minimum bid price, number of publicly held shares, market value of public float, and shareholder equity.7 The Global Select Market has the most rigorous standards, comparable to the NYSE.7
  - **Corporate Governance:** The NASDAQ Rule 5600 Series governs corporate governance.30 Like the NYSE, it mandates a majority-independent board and independent audit, compensation, and nomination committees.31 It also requires shareholder approval for certain stock issuances and the adoption of a code of conduct.28
  - **Dual-Class Shares and Cooperatives:** NASDAQ's Voting Rights Rule (Rule 5640) generally permits dual-class structures at the time of an IPO.11 This makes it a viable venue for a hybrid cooperative. Significantly, NASDAQ Listing Rule 5615(a)(2) provides an explicit exemption for "Cooperatives" from the corporate governance rules related to board independence and independent committees, recognizing their unique member-driven governance structure.33 This exemption could provide substantial flexibility for a listed hybrid cooperative.

  

### **Section 2.3: OTC Markets Group**

  

The OTC Markets provide an alternative to a formal exchange listing and are organized into three tiers based on the quality and quantity of information the companies provide.

  - **Market Tiers:**

  - **OTCQX® Best Market:** This is the highest tier. Companies must meet stringent financial standards, be current in their disclosure, and undergo a qualitative review. They are explicitly forbidden from being penny stocks or shell companies.12
  - **OTCQB® Venture Market:** This is the middle tier for entrepreneurial and development-stage companies. Companies must be current in their reporting, have a minimum bid price of $0.01, and undergo an annual verification and management certification process.35
  - **Pink® Open Market:** This tier has no financial standards or disclosure requirements. It is an open marketplace for brokers to trade a wide spectrum of securities.12

  - **Corporate Governance:** The OTCQX and OTCQB markets have corporate governance requirements for companies using their "Alternative Reporting Standard." These rules require at least two independent directors and an audit committee with a majority of independent directors, though these are less prescriptive than NYSE or NASDAQ rules.12
  - **Suitability for Cooperatives:** The OTC markets, particularly OTCQX or OTCQB, could serve as a more accessible entry point for a hybrid cooperative's Class B shares, with lower financial thresholds and less burdensome governance rules than the major exchanges.8

  

### **Section 2.4: FINRA's Role**

  

The Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) is a self-regulatory organization that oversees broker-dealers in the United States. Its role is not to approve the issuance of securities but to regulate their trading and the conduct of its member firms.38 For a cooperative security trading on the OTC markets, FINRA rules would govern how brokers handle and report trades (Rule 6600 series) and would prohibit manipulative practices like trading ahead of customer orders (Rule 5320).39 FINRA also has the authority to halt trading in an OTC security under extraordinary circumstances to protect investors.41

  

## **Part III: The International Dimension**

  

The query's reference to "international court law" touches on a common misconception. There is no single international court or body of law that governs the issuance and trading of corporate securities globally. Governance is a function of national and exchange-specific rules.

For a cooperative enterprise, the international dimension manifests in two primary ways:

1.  **International Principles:** The global cooperative movement adheres to a set of shared values, most notably the seven Rochdale Principles articulated by the International Co-operative Alliance (ICA).1 These principles—including Democratic Member Control and Member Economic Participation—are foundational to the cooperative's identity and inform the structure of its governing documents.1 However, they are ethical guidelines, not a legally binding framework with standing in a U.S. court or before the SEC.
2.  **Cross-Border Listings:** If a U.S.-based hybrid cooperative wishes to be truly global and trade on an exchange outside the United States (e.g., the London Stock Exchange or the Hong Kong Stock Exchange), it must undergo a separate listing process in that jurisdiction.1 This would require full compliance with that country's securities laws and that exchange's specific listing and governance rules.1 This adds significant legal and administrative complexity, as the company would be subject to multiple, and potentially conflicting, regulatory regimes. For example, a foreign private issuer listing on a U.S. exchange is subject to SEC rules but may be permitted to follow its home country's governance practices in lieu of certain exchange rules, provided it discloses the differences.25

  

## **Conclusion: A Synthesized Path to Market**

  

The analysis of these intersecting legal and regulatory frameworks reveals that a path for a cooperative to access public capital markets is legally and structurally feasible, though it demands a highly sophisticated and precisely engineered approach. The strategy rests on a careful bifurcation of the cooperative's legal and economic identity.

The enterprise must be built on the foundation of California's flexible cooperative statutes, which allow for the creation of a dual-class share structure. The Class A Member Shares must be meticulously designed to lack the economic characteristics of a traditional investment, thereby aligning with SEC no-action letter guidance and avoiding securities registration. These shares embody the cooperative's mission, are governed by democratic principles, and receive economic benefits through the tax-advantaged patronage system under IRS Subchapter T.

In parallel, the Class B Investor Shares must be designed as conventional, publicly-traded securities. They must be fully registered with the SEC via an S-1 filing and must be structured to meet all the quantitative and qualitative listing requirements of the chosen trading venue, whether it be the NYSE, NASDAQ, or an OTC Market tier. The viability of the entire enterprise depends on the exchange's acceptance of a dual-class voting structure, a feature that both the NYSE and NASDAQ currently permit. While there is no overarching international law, a successful U.S. listing based on this hybrid model would create a strong foundation for any future cross-border listing ambitions.
