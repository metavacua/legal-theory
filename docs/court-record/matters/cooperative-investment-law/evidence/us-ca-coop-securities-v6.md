### **A Unified Framework for Analyzing a Cooperative Security**

**Preamble:** This document provides a formal, unified framework for analyzing any proposed cooperative security. It integrates the Supreme Court's primary tests for defining a security (*Reves* and *Howey*) with the core operational pillars of Subchapter T. This allows for a robust, step-by-step analysis of whether a cooperative instrument should be treated as a security and, if so, whether its design is compliant and safe for the cooperative.

### **Part 1: Initial Characterization – What is the Nature of the Instrument?**

The first and most critical step is to determine the fundamental nature of the instrument in question. The path of legal analysis depends entirely on this initial characterization.

  - **Is it a "Note" or Debt-Like Instrument?**
      
      - **Characteristics:** Does it involve a loan of money? Is there a promise to repay a principal amount? Is there a stated interest rate (even if it's 0%)? Is the instrument actually called a "note"?
      - **If YES, proceed to Part 2, Path A: The** ***Reves*** **Test.**
  - **Is it an "Investment Contract" or Equity-Like Instrument?**
      
      - **Characteristics:** Does it represent a stake in the cooperative's assets or profits? Is the return for the holder entirely dependent on the success and profitability of the cooperative's ventures? Does it involve pooling capital with others in a common enterprise?
      - **If YES, proceed to Part 2, Path B: The** ***Howey*** **Test.**

### **Part 2: The Threshold Analysis – Is the Instrument a Security?**

Based on the characterization above, apply the appropriate Supreme Court test.

#### **Path A: The** ***Reves*** **"Family Resemblance" Test (for Notes)**

The *Reves* test determines if a note bears a "family resemblance" to an investment, thus classifying it as a security.

1.  **Motivations of Seller/Buyer:** Is the cooperative raising money for general business use (leans toward security) or to facilitate a specific patron's transaction (leans away)? Is the buyer motivated by profit (leans toward security) or acquiring a good/service (leans away)?
2.  **Plan of Distribution:** Is the note offered on a public market (leans toward security) or is the distribution narrowly limited to patrons for whom it facilitates a transaction (leans away)?
3.  **Reasonable Expectations of the Public:** Is the instrument marketed as an "investment" (leans toward security) or as "patronage capital" or a "membership deposit" (leans away)?
4.  **Presence of an Alternative Regulatory Scheme:** Does the framework of cooperative law and Subchapter T itself provide sufficient protection, making securities law unnecessary (leans away from security)?

#### **Path B: The** ***Howey*** **Test (for Investment Contracts)**

The *Howey* test defines an investment contract as a security if these four conditions are met:

1.  **An Investment of Value:** Has the holder given up something of value (money, assets, labor) to acquire the instrument?
2.  **In a Common Enterprise:** Is the holder's fortune interwoven with the fortune of the cooperative and other members?
3.  **With an Expectation of Benefit/Profit:** Does the holder expect to receive a valuable benefit, such as appreciation, dividends, or access to services?
4.  **Derived from the Efforts of Others:** Does the realization of the benefit depend primarily on the managerial or operational efforts of the cooperative's leadership?

### **Part 3: The Cooperative Compliance Test**

If an instrument is determined to be a security under either *Reves* or *Howey*, it must then be tested for its compliance with the core principles of cooperative operation. *Puget Sound Plywood, Inc. v. Commissioner*, 44 T.C. 305 (1965)

1.  **Subordination of Capital as Defense of the Commons:** This pillar tests a fundamental question: Is the security a tool for the private capture of cooperatively-created value (the "enclosure of the commons"), or is it a defense against this malappropriation? This reframes "subordination of capital" from a simple limit on returns to a core principle of protecting the shared value generated by the cooperative and its patrons.
      
      - **No Cooperative Elements (Private Capture):** The security is structured like traditional equity, designed to extract value *from* the enterprise for an investor's private benefit. The investor's gain is theirs alone. This embodies the "enclosure of the commons" and is fundamentally at odds with cooperative principles.
      - **Partial/Hybrid Cooperative Elements:** The security has a mixed design. It may place some limits on returns, but it still contains mechanisms for private value capture, creating a risk that the cooperative's commons can be eroded over time.
      - **Full Subchapter T Alignment (Cooperative Socialization):** The security is a tool for injecting capital *into* the enterprise to serve a collective purpose. It is engineered to ensure that any subsequent gain or appreciation is captured *by the cooperative* and distributed to the entire patronage according to cooperative principles. This structure actively defends the commons by socializing gains rather than privatizing them.
2.  **Democratic Member Control:** The core principle is preventing the concentration of governance power. The analysis must examine how voting rights are structured:
      
      - **Single Voting Security Model:** This is the traditional cooperative structure. A member holds a single security that grants them one vote in all general matters. This model clearly preserves democratic control.
      - **Multiple Non-Duplicative Voting Securities Model:** A member may hold multiple distinct securities, but their voting power is not duplicative; the security must provide at least one vote while satisfying the other two conditions jointly. For example, one security grants a vote in the general election, while a second grants a vote *only* in a specific subcommittee or regional board. As long as this structure prevents any member from having disproportionate power in any single decision, it can be consistent with democratic control.
      - **Non-Compliant Model:** Any structure where a member can acquire more voting power in a single election or decision-making body by purchasing more securities fails this test. This is the C-Corp "one share, one vote" model and is fundamentally at odds with cooperative principles.
3.  **Patronage-Source Income Integrity (The "Actually Facilitates" Test):** The core test is whether the instrument—regardless of its form—is engineered so its function "actually facilitates" the cooperative's core mission, ensuring income generated is defensibly patronage-sourced. This is a key principle elaborated by both traditional and New Generation Cooperatives (NGCs).
      
      - **Compliant Models (NGC & Traditional):** Instruments like NGC non-voting delivery rights or traditional per-unit capital retains are compliant because they are directly and inextricably linked to a member's patronage. The capital raised is used to facilitate the cooperative's primary activities (e.g., building a processing plant, providing operating capital). The security's function is to enable and quantify patronage, making a strong case that it "actually facilitates" the cooperative's purpose. Even a voting security can be compliant if its primary purpose is tied to patronage.
      - **Non-Compliant Model:** An instrument fails this test if the capital it raises is used for purposes unrelated to the cooperative's direct business with its patrons. For example, if a cooperative issues securities to the public to fund a speculative investment portfolio in an unrelated industry, the link to patronage is severed. The income generated would be non-patronage, and the instrument would fail this pillar of the compliance test.

### **Conclusion: Classifying the Instrument**

  - **Non-Security Instrument:** An instrument that fails to be classified as a security under the appropriate *Reves* or *Howey* test.
  - **Non-Subchapter-T Security:** An instrument classified as a security that fails the Cooperative Compliance Test.
  - **Subchapter T Security:** An instrument that is legally a security but is so deeply integrated with the principles of cooperative compliance that it functions within the Subchapter T framework. These can be further broken down:
      
      - **Singular Subchapter T Security:** This represents a structure where a single security encompasses the member's entire relationship with the cooperative, typical of traditional models.
      - **Non-Singular Subchapter T Security:** This represents a structure where a member may hold multiple, distinct Subchapter T securities (e.g., non-voting delivery rights or non-duplicative voting shares). Each instrument is independently compliant, and their aggregation in the hands of a single member does not violate the principles of democratic control or subordination of capital.
