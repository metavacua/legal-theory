# **The Membership Security: Structuring a New Asset Class from the Principles of Cooperative Law**

  
  

## **Part I: The Legal and Structural Foundation of California Cooperatives**

  

The exploration of a new class of financial instruments derived from cooperative memberships necessitates a foundational understanding of the legal architecture governing these unique entities. While the cooperative model is ideologically rooted in democratic principles, the California Corporations Code provides a surprisingly nuanced and flexible framework. This legal landscape, shaped by a series of deliberate legislative modernizations, contains specific statutory gateways that permit governance structures deviating from the traditional "one-member, one-vote" paradigm. It is within these exceptions that the potential for financial innovation resides. The analysis begins by deconstructing the default rule and then systematically mapping the statutory pathways that allow for the asymmetric governance and sophisticated capital structures required to create a tradable cooperative security.

  

### **1.1 The Democratic Mandate: Deconstructing Cal. Corp. Code § 12404**

  

The cornerstone of cooperative governance in California is codified in Corporations Code § 12404, which states, "Except as permitted in Sections 12314 and 12404.5, the voting power of members having voting rights shall be equal".1 This provision establishes the "one-member, one-vote" principle as the default standard for cooperatives organized under this part of the code. The legislative intent behind this rule is to embody the democratic ethos that distinguishes cooperatives from conventional corporations.4 In a traditional corporate structure, voting power is proportional to capital investment (one share, one vote). In a cooperative, by contrast, control is divorced from capital contribution; it is tied to membership status itself. This democratic organization is a core tenet of the cooperative movement, appealing to smaller participants by ensuring that no single member can dominate decision-making through greater financial investment.4

However, this very principle has historically been perceived as a significant impediment to capital formation. Larger investors may be disinclined to contribute substantial capital if it does not translate into greater control, creating a "capital conundrum" for cooperatives seeking to grow and compete.4 The text of § 12404 is therefore critical not for its mandate, but for its explicit exceptions. It signals that the rule of equal voting power is not absolute but is instead a default setting from which the law provides specific, sanctioned deviations.

It is essential at the outset to clarify a point of potential confusion arising from statutory numbering. California *Insurance Code* § 12404 deals with unlawful inducements and rebates in the title insurance industry and is entirely unrelated to the governance of cooperative corporations.9 All analysis herein pertains exclusively to the

*Corporations Code*.

  

### **1.2 Pathways to Asymmetric Governance: Statutory Exceptions to Equal Voting**

  

The California legislature has intentionally created specific exceptions to the one-member, one-vote rule, recognizing the need for more sophisticated governance and capitalization models, particularly for larger and more complex cooperative enterprises. These statutory carve-outs provide the legal foundation for designing financial instruments based on the differential value of membership rights.

  

#### **1.2.1 The Central Organization Exception: Cal. Corp. Code § 12314**

  

The most significant exception for the purposes of this analysis is found in Cal. Corp. Code § 12314. This statute provides that, "The articles of incorporation of a central organization...may provide for unequal voting power of its members based upon the number of its members' members, the patronage of its members, or both. In no event shall any member have less than one vote".11

This exception is exclusively available to a "central organization," which is defined under § 12256 as "a corporation whose membership is composed, in whole or in part, of other corporations organized under this part".13 In essence, a central organization is a "cooperative of cooperatives" or a cooperative whose members are other corporate entities.

The implications of § 12314 are profound. It explicitly authorizes a departure from democratic purity and links voting power directly to metrics of scale and economic participation—either the size of the member corporation (measured by its own membership) or the volume of business it conducts with the central organization (patronage). This creates a quasi-corporate governance model within the cooperative framework, where a member's vote is not merely a symbolic representation of membership but an instrument whose weight can reflect its economic significance to the enterprise. This statutory provision is the primary legal gateway for conceptualizing a membership "slot" as an asset with a quantifiable and variable value, a necessary precondition for securitization.

  

#### **1.2.2 The Worker Cooperative Precedent: Cal. Corp. Code § 12404.5 and AB 816 (2015)**

  

The passage of Assembly Bill 816 in 2015 marked a significant modernization of California's cooperative statutes, renaming the law from the "Consumer Cooperative Corporation Law" to the more general "Cooperative Corporation Law".14 This legislation created a specific framework for worker cooperatives and, in doing so, introduced another crucial exception to equal voting via the new § 12404.5.16

This section, cross-referencing § 12253, establishes a bifurcated capital and governance structure. It allows worker cooperatives to have "community investors"—persons who are not worker-members but who provide capital.15 The voting rights of these community investors are strictly limited to approval rights over fundamental corporate changes such as a merger, sale of major assets, or dissolution, and they are explicitly denied the right to propose any such actions.14

While the worker cooperative model itself is distinct from the central organization model, the precedent set by AB 816 is powerful. It demonstrates clear legislative intent to facilitate innovative capital structures by formally separating capital provision from day-to-day control. The creation of a non-voting or limited-voting investor class within a cooperative structure validates a core principle required for the development of a Cooperative Membership Security (CMS). Furthermore, AB 816 raised the exemption from securities qualification for cooperative membership sales from $300 to $1,000, signaling a legislative desire to make it easier for cooperatives to raise capital from their communities.18

  

#### **1.2.3 General Flexibility: AB 1255 (2013) and Classes of Membership**

  

Further evidence of the legislature's intent to provide cooperatives with modern corporate finance tools can be found in Assembly Bill 1255, passed in 2013.19 This bill amended the Cooperative Corporation Law to explicitly allow for the creation of different classes and series of memberships.20 This includes the authorization of "preferred memberships," which can have preferential rights regarding distributions or assets upon liquidation.20 The law now permits a cooperative's board of directors, if authorized by the articles or bylaws, to fix the specific rights, privileges, preferences, restrictions, and conditions of any wholly unissued class or series of memberships.20

This legislation provides the essential corporate machinery for a central organization to create sophisticated capital structures. For instance, a cooperative could authorize a specific class of non-voting preferred shares designed to be held by outside investors, including its publicly traded corporate members. The rights associated with these shares (e.g., dividend rates, redemption clauses) could be tailored by contract to attract capital without diluting the voting control of the primary membership class. This statutory flexibility is a critical enabler for the financial engineering concepts explored later in this report.

The legislative activity in 2013 and 2015 does not represent isolated adjustments but rather a clear and deliberate trend. The California legislature has been actively modernizing cooperative law to address the model's inherent challenges in capital formation.4 By creating these pathways for flexible governance and multi-tiered capital structures, lawmakers are responding to the practical needs of cooperatives operating in a competitive market, thereby creating a legal environment that is increasingly receptive to the type of innovation proposed here.

  

### **1.3 Deep Dive: The Central Organization as a Gateway for Financial Innovation**

  

The central organization is the ideal legal chassis for developing a CMS. By definition, these entities are structured as "cooperatives of cooperatives" or, more broadly, cooperatives of other corporations.13 This tiered structure is most prevalent and sophisticated in the agricultural sector, where large-scale processing and marketing operations require immense capital and coordination.

Prominent California-based agricultural cooperatives such as Sunkist Growers, Inc., and Blue Diamond Growers serve as prime examples of this model.21 These are not small, local enterprises; they are multi-billion-dollar global businesses. Their membership is not composed of thousands of individual farmers directly, but rather of other entities—local packing associations (which are themselves cooperatives) and, in some cases, large incorporated farming operations.23 This structure allows them to legally adopt unequal voting rights under § 12314, tying voting power to the economic scale of their corporate members. It is this specific combination—a cooperative structure that permits unequal voting and includes corporate members, some of which may be publicly traded—that creates the direct nexus to public capital markets.

  

### **1.4 Proof-of-Concept Case Study: The Sunkist-Limoneira Nexus**

  

The theoretical potential of this legal structure is made concrete by the recent agreement between Sunkist Growers, Inc., and Limoneira Company. This arrangement serves as a living proof-of-concept for the core thesis of this report.

  - **The Parties:** Sunkist is a quintessential central agricultural cooperative, a marketing association owned by and operated for thousands of citrus growers in California and Arizona, who are organized through local packing houses and district exchanges.23 Limoneira Company is a 132-year-old international agribusiness, publicly traded on the Nasdaq stock exchange under the ticker LMNR.26
  - **The Transaction:** In June 2025, Limoneira, which was one of Sunkist's founding members, announced it would rejoin the Sunkist cooperative, with the agreement becoming effective on November 1, 2025.26
  - **The Structure:** Crucially, this is not a merger or acquisition. Limoneira remains a fully independent, publicly traded corporation.26 The relationship is codified in a "Commercial Packinghouse License Agreement" filed with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).30 Under this agreement, Limoneira transfers its fresh citrus sales and marketing responsibilities to Sunkist. In return, Limoneira becomes a member of Sunkist and its affiliate, the Fruit Growers Supply Company, and agrees to be bound by their respective bylaws.32

The significance of this transaction cannot be overstated. It establishes a direct, tangible, and legally defined link between a publicly traded company and a membership slot in a major central cooperative. The value of this membership to Limoneira's public shareholders is quantifiable; the company projects the arrangement will generate $5 million in annual cost savings and EBITDA improvement through streamlined operations and access to Sunkist's formidable global marketing and distribution network.34

This arrangement demonstrates that "membership" in a modern central cooperative is not a monolithic concept of total integration. Instead, it can be a highly negotiated, contractual relationship that involves the unbundling of specific rights and obligations. Limoneira trades its marketing autonomy for the economic benefits of the Sunkist system. This contractual and unbundled nature transforms the membership "slot" from an abstract status into a discrete and definable asset. An asset that is a bundle of legally defined rights (access to markets, use of trademarks) and obligations (supply of product, adherence to quality standards) is precisely the type of asset that can be valued, analyzed, and ultimately, securitized. It is analogous to other intangible assets, such as intellectual property licenses or franchise rights, that are routinely used as the basis for sophisticated financial products.

## **Part II: The Securities Law Gauntlet: Navigating Federal and State Regulation**

  

Having established a viable legal pathway under California cooperative law, the proposed Cooperative Membership Security (CMS) must be subjected to the rigorous scrutiny of federal and state securities law. The foundational nature of a cooperative membership is for patronage and use, which generally places it outside the scope of securities regulation. However, the CMS is conceived as an instrument for investment, acquired by third parties with a clear expectation of financial return. This deliberate shift from consumption to investment fundamentally alters the legal analysis. The central question is not *if* a CMS is a security, but rather *how* it will be classified and which regulatory frameworks will govern its issuance and trading. Successfully navigating this gauntlet is paramount to the instrument's viability.

  

### **2.1 The Threshold Question: Consumption vs. Investment**

  

The analysis of any instrument related to a cooperative must begin with the landmark U.S. Supreme Court case, *United Housing Foundation, Inc. v. Forman*.36 In

*Forman*, the Court held that shares of "stock" in a state-subsidized housing cooperative, which entitled the purchaser to lease an apartment, were not securities under federal law. The Court's reasoning was grounded in the "economic reality" of the transaction.36 The purchasers were motivated by a desire to acquire affordable housing for personal use—a consumptive purpose—not by an expectation of profit derived from the entrepreneurial or managerial efforts of others.38 The Court concluded that "people who intend to acquire only a residential apartment... are not likely to believe that, in reality they are purchasing investment securities simply because the transaction is evidenced by something called a share of stock".38

The *Forman* doctrine is critical because it defines the boundary that a CMS is designed to cross. A traditional cooperative membership, like the one in *Forman*, is purchased for its utility—the right to market crops, purchase supplies, or obtain housing. The CMS, by contrast, would be an instrument purchased by a passive, third-party financial investor. Its sole purpose would be to generate a financial return based on the economic value of the underlying membership slot. Therefore, the "economic reality" of the CMS transaction is one of investment, not consumption. The *Forman* analysis does not provide a shield from regulation; rather, it confirms that the CMS falls squarely within the territory that securities laws were designed to govern.

  

### **2.2 Federal Scrutiny: Applying the** ***Howey*** **and** ***Reves*** **Tests**

  

Given that a CMS is an investment instrument, it must be analyzed under the primary tests established by the Supreme Court for defining a security.

  

#### **2.2.1 The** ***Howey*** **Test for Investment Contracts**

  

The seminal test for determining if a transaction constitutes an "investment contract," and therefore a security, was established in *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.*.39 The

*Howey* test has four prongs: (1) an investment of money, (2) in a common enterprise, (3) with a reasonable expectation of profits, (4) to be derived solely from the efforts of the promoter or a third party.40

Applying this framework to a hypothetical CMS:

1.  **Investment of Money:** This prong is unequivocally met. An investor would purchase the CMS for cash or other valuable consideration.41
2.  **Common Enterprise:** This is also clearly met. The value of the CMS is inextricably linked to the financial performance and success of the underlying cooperative and its corporate member, whose activities generate the economic rights (patronage) that give the CMS its value.
3.  **Reasonable Expectation of Profits:** This is the core of the analysis. The "profit" for a CMS investor is not a traditional dividend on stock but rather the financial return generated by the instrument. This could be through appreciation in the market value of the CMS itself (driven by the increasing value of the underlying membership's economic rights) or through direct cash flows, as in the case of a patronage swap. The expectation of this financial gain is the primary motivation for the investor.40
4.  **Efforts of Others:** This prong is also met. The CMS investor is, by design, a passive capital provider. The profitability of the instrument depends entirely on the managerial efforts of others: the board and management of the cooperative who run the business, the corporate member whose patronage generates the economic returns, and the managers of any fund or market where the CMS is created and traded.38

Based on this analysis, a CMS would almost certainly be classified as an "investment contract" and thus a security under the *Howey* test.

  

#### **2.2.2 The** ***Reves*** **Test for Notes**

  

Should the CMS be structured as a debt instrument—for example, a note whose interest payments are linked to the patronage stream of a member—the analysis would fall under the "family resemblance" test articulated in *Reves v. Ernst & Young*.42 This case is particularly relevant as it involved demand notes issued by a farmer's cooperative to raise capital from members and nonmembers. The Supreme Court found these notes

*to be* securities.43

The *Reves* test begins with the presumption that any note is a security. This presumption can be rebutted only if the note in question bears a strong "family resemblance" to a judicially recognized list of non-security instruments, such as a note secured by a home mortgage or a short-term note for a commercial purpose.42 The resemblance is evaluated using four factors:

1.  **Motivations of the Parties:** A CMS note would be sold by the issuer to raise capital and purchased by the investor to earn a profit (interest), aligning it with an investment, not a commercial transaction.
2.  **Plan of Distribution:** The CMS would be offered to a class of investors, indicating a plan for "common trading for speculation or investment," as was the case in *Reves*.
3.  **Reasonable Expectations of the Public:** The instrument would be marketed as an investment, creating a reasonable expectation that it is a security.
4.  **Existence of Other Risk-Reducing Factors:** A CMS would be an uncollateralized instrument whose risk is not mitigated by another regulatory scheme like federal deposit insurance.

Under all four factors of the *Reves* test, a CMS structured as a note would be deemed a security.

  

### **2.3 The California Anomaly: The "Risk Capital" Test**

  

While a CMS would be considered a security under federal law, the analysis under California law is even more conclusive due to the state's uniquely broad "risk capital" test. This standard, established by the California Supreme Court in *Silver Hills Country Club v. Sobieski*, is a critical component of the state's Corporate Securities Law of 1968 and represents the strictest regulatory hurdle.38

The risk capital test finds a security to exist where investors provide risk capital to a business enterprise, even if the investors do not have an expectation of "profit" in the traditional sense.46 The test was famously applied to find that

*memberships* in a new country club were securities, because the members' funds were at risk to finance the development of the enterprise, and the benefit they expected (the use of the club) depended on the promoters' success.38 The key factors are whether: (1) funds are being raised for a business venture; (2) the offering is made indiscriminately to the public; (3) the investors are passive and powerless to affect the success of the enterprise; and (4) the investors' money is substantially at risk because it is inadequately secured.46

The CMS fits this framework perfectly. An investor provides capital that is at risk, the funds are used for a business enterprise, the investor is passive, and the expected benefit (appreciation or cash flow) is dependent on the efforts of the cooperative's and corporate member's management. Because the risk capital test is broader and does not require a traditional profit motive, it is the controlling standard for any offering conducted in or from California. This is not a barrier but a critical planning assumption: the CMS project must proceed from the outset with the understanding that the instruments are securities under California law and must either be qualified with the Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) or sold pursuant to a valid exemption. The legal and compliance strategy must be "California-first," as satisfying the state's more stringent test will likely satisfy the federal tests as well.

  

### **2.4 Navigating the Labyrinth: Exemptions and Potential Safe Harbors**

  

Acknowledging that the CMS is a security, the strategic focus shifts from classification to compliance. A full public registration with the SEC and qualification in every state is a prohibitively expensive and time-consuming process for a new asset class. Therefore, the viability of the CMS hinges on the availability of exemptions from these requirements.

California law provides a specific exemption for cooperative securities under Cal. Corp. Code § 25100(m), but this is limited to sales of memberships or shares of $1,000 or less per person.18 While this exemption is useful for facilitating community-based capitalization of local cooperatives, it is wholly inadequate for the institutional-grade market envisioned for the CMS.

Consequently, the only viable path is to utilize transactional exemptions available under both federal law (Regulation D) and California law. The initial offerings of any CMS would need to be structured as private placements, limited to "accredited investors" and "qualified institutional buyers" (QIBs). This would exempt the offering from the full registration requirements, allowing for a more controlled and cost-effective launch of the asset class to a small group of sophisticated financial institutions, such as hedge funds, family offices, and specialized impact investment funds. This approach contains the legal risk while allowing for a pilot program to establish proof of concept and begin building a market.

The legal ambiguity surrounding an instrument as novel as the CMS creates a significant "first-mover advantage." The first entity to successfully structure, place, and navigate the regulatory landscape for a CMS will not only capture a new market but will also play a crucial role in shaping the future regulatory interpretation of such instruments. A proactive strategy of engagement with regulators at both the SEC and the California DFPI, presenting a well-reasoned legal framework grounded in the logic of *Forman*, *Howey*, *Reves*, and *Silver Hills*, could result in favorable no-action letters or interpretive guidance. Such guidance would create a substantial "regulatory moat," establishing a clear path for the innovator and a significant barrier to entry for subsequent competitors.

## **Part III: Architecting the Cooperative Membership Security (CMS): A New Asset Class**

  

Transitioning from legal theory to financial engineering, this section outlines the practical architecture of the Cooperative Membership Security (CMS). The central innovation is to treat the cooperative membership "slot" not as an indivisible right of participation, but as a distinct, valuable, and securitizable asset. This requires a conceptual shift and the application of established derivative and securitization principles to this novel underlying asset.

  

### **3.1 Conceptual Framework: Securitizing the Membership Slot**

  

The underlying asset for a CMS is not a traditional share of stock, which represents a fractional ownership claim on an entire enterprise. Instead, the asset is the **membership slot** itself. This slot is a unique, non-divisible bundle of legally defined rights and obligations.6 For a corporate member in a central organization, this bundle typically includes:

  - **Governance Rights:** The right to vote in the cooperative's affairs. Under the default rule of § 12404, this is one vote. However, under the critical § 12314 exception for central organizations, this vote can be weighted based on patronage or the member's own scale, giving it tangible influence.11
  - **Economic Rights:** This is the primary source of financial value. It is the right to receive patronage distributions (also known as patronage dividends or refunds) from the cooperative's net earnings. These distributions are allocated not based on capital invested, but in proportion to the volume of business the member transacts with the cooperative.4 For a member like Limoneira in Sunkist, this means its share of the profits is based on the volume of citrus it markets through the Sunkist system.
  - **Access Rights:** The right to use the cooperative's services and infrastructure. This can include access to processing facilities, supply chains, and, most importantly, established marketing channels and brand recognition, as in the Sunkist-Limoneira case.29

The key to creating a CMS is the well-established financial principle of separating voting rights from economic rights.50 While the corporate member retains the governance and access rights integral to its operational relationship with the cooperative, a CMS can be designed to transfer the economic rights—the stream of future patronage dividends—to a third-party investor. This allows an outside investor to gain direct financial exposure to the economic performance of the cooperative relationship without participating in its governance.

  

### **3.2 Structural Mechanics: Derivatives and Synthetic Instruments**

  

The most effective way to structure a CMS is not as a direct sale of the membership, which could violate cooperative bylaws restricting transfer, but as a derivative instrument whose value is based on the underlying membership slot. Drawing from the standard toolkit of financial securitization 53, several structures are possible:

  - **Patronage Swap:** This is the most promising and cleanest initial structure. It is a derivative contract in which the owner of the membership slot (e.g., Limoneira) agrees to pay the variable cash flows from its future patronage dividends to a financial investor. In return, the investor pays the corporate member a fixed, recurring payment or an upfront lump sum. This structure allows the corporate member to monetize its membership, hedge against fluctuations in patronage income, and receive immediate liquidity. The investor, in turn, receives a pure-play synthetic exposure to the economic performance of the cooperative without any of the complexities of membership. This structure requires no change in the legal ownership of the membership slot, thus bypassing most bylaw restrictions on transfer.
  - **Membership Options:** A contract giving a buyer the right, but not the obligation, to acquire a specific membership slot from the seller at a predetermined price (the strike price) by a certain date. This would allow for speculation on the future value of the membership slot, which would be a function of the expected growth in patronage dividends and any control premium.
  - **Membership Forwards:** A contract obligating a buyer to purchase a membership slot from a seller at a predetermined future date and price. This could be used by a corporate member to lock in an exit price for its membership, or by an aspiring member to lock in an entry price.
  - **Analogy to Tracking Stocks:** A CMS based on the membership of a major corporate member like Limoneira can be conceptually compared to a tracking stock.56 A tracking stock is a separate class of shares issued by a parent company that is designed to track the financial performance of a specific subsidiary or division, without conferring direct ownership or full voting rights in the parent company.59 Similarly, a CMS would allow investors to invest in the performance of the specific economic relationship between the corporate member and the cooperative, isolating it from the broader performance of the corporate member's other business lines.

The "one-member, one-vote" principle, initially perceived as a structural limitation, reveals itself here as a powerful feature for financial engineering. Traditional corporate stock is infinitely divisible, leading to complex capitalization tables and calculations of percentage ownership. The cooperative membership slot, by its very nature, is a single, indivisible, standardized unit of account. One is either a member or not. This indivisibility, a direct consequence of the "one-member" rule, makes the membership slot a perfect, standardized building block for creating derivative contracts. One can write an option on "one membership slot" with the same conceptual clarity as writing an option on "100 shares of stock." The ostensible obstacle of non-divisibility becomes the crucial feature of standardization, which is essential for creating a transparent and potentially liquid market.

  

### **3.3 Target Entities: Profiling Ideal Cooperative Counterparties**

  

The universe of potential cooperatives for this model is specific. An ideal target for the creation of a CMS would exhibit the following characteristics:

  - **Legal Structure:** The cooperative must be a "central organization" as defined in Cal. Corp. Code § 12256, to legally permit unequal voting under § 12314. This is a non-negotiable prerequisite.
  - **Membership Composition:** The cooperative must have at least one (and preferably several) large, publicly traded corporation as a member. The existence of a public member provides a direct nexus to capital markets, ensures a high level of financial transparency through SEC filings, and creates a membership slot of significant and publicly analyzable economic value.
  - **Economic Scale:** The cooperative must be a large-scale enterprise with substantial economic throughput. This ensures that the patronage dividends generated are significant enough to be attractive to institutional investors. Major agricultural marketing cooperatives, purchasing cooperatives, and some financial cooperatives (like CoBank) are the most likely candidates.21
  - **Bylaw Analysis:** The cooperative's articles of incorporation and bylaws must be meticulously reviewed for any provisions restricting the transfer, assignment, or encumbrance of membership or economic rights. While a structure like a patronage swap can circumvent outright transfer prohibitions, the legal documents must be analyzed to ensure no clauses would prevent such a contractual arrangement.63

  

### **3.4 Valuation Models for a One-Vote Asset**

  

Valuing a CMS presents a novel challenge that deviates from standard equity valuation. Since the CMS does not represent a pro-rata claim on all of a company's earnings, its valuation must be tied directly to the specific bundle of rights inherent in the membership slot.

  - **Patronage-Based Discounted Cash Flow (DCF):** The primary valuation methodology would be a DCF analysis of the expected future patronage dividends attributable to the specific membership slot being securitized. This would require detailed financial modeling, forecasting the cooperative's overall profitability, the specific member's anticipated volume of business (patronage) with the cooperative, and the cooperative's policy on patronage distribution. The recent Sunkist-Limoneira agreement provides a real-world data point, with Limoneira's public statements on expected cost savings and synergies offering a basis for such a model.34
  - **Control Premium:** In a central organization with unequal voting under § 12314, the vote itself has value. While a single membership's vote may not confer outright control, it contributes to a voting bloc and provides influence over the cooperative's governance. A modest control premium can be added to the DCF valuation to account for this governance right, especially if the member is one of the larger patrons with a correspondingly weighted vote.
  - **Optionality Value:** For CMS structured as options, established option pricing models like the Black-Scholes model could be adapted. The key inputs would be the strike price (the price to acquire the membership), the time to expiration, the risk-free rate, and the volatility of the underlying asset. In this case, the "volatility" would be the volatility of the expected patronage dividend stream, which could be derived from the historical performance of the cooperative and the member's business sector.

  

## **Part IV: Market Analysis and Strategic Recommendations**

  

The successful creation of a new asset class requires not only a sound legal and financial architecture but also a receptive market and a clear strategic path to implementation. This final section assesses the real-world viability of the Cooperative Membership Security (CMS), identifies the target investor base, and provides an actionable roadmap for moving from concept to execution.

  

### **4.1 Market Potential and Investor Appetite**

  

The market environment for an instrument like the CMS is exceptionally favorable, driven by the powerful and accelerating trend of impact investing.

  - **The Impact Investing Market:** The impact investing market is no longer a niche segment but a major force in global finance. Market size estimates vary, but all point toward explosive growth. The Global Impact Investing Network (GIIN) reported assets under management at over $1.5 trillion in 2024.64 Other market reports project growth from a base of approximately $550-630 billion in 2024/2025 to over $1.2 trillion by 2029, with some forecasts reaching nearly $293 billion by 2030 for certain segments.65 This rapid expansion reflects a fundamental shift in investor priorities.
  - **Investor Preferences for 2025 and Beyond:** There is overwhelming evidence of growing investor demand for strategies that generate positive social and environmental outcomes alongside financial returns. This is particularly pronounced among Millennial and Gen Z investors, who are set to inherit trillions in wealth and show a clear preference for ESG-aligned portfolios.65 Surveys indicate that retail investors want their funds to address critical issues like climate change, nature protection, and economic inequality.69 Key themes for 2025 include climate solutions, renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, and building wealth through more equitable structures like worker-owned businesses.69
  - **The CMS Narrative:** The CMS is perfectly positioned to meet this demand. It offers a compelling and authentic ESG narrative. A CMS is not merely an investment with an ESG screen applied; it is an instrument that derives its value directly from a more equitable and community-focused form of enterprise. Investing in a CMS allows capital to flow toward supporting farmer-owned agricultural cooperatives, worker-owned businesses, or community-owned purchasing groups. This directly aligns with investor preferences for promoting sustainable agriculture, empowering workers, fostering community development, and addressing economic inequality.69

  

### **4.2 The Coop Exchange Precedent: A Market in the Making**

  

The theoretical market potential for cooperative securities is being validated in real time by the emergence of new, specialized financial platforms. The most prominent of these is Coop Exchange.

  - **Business Model:** Coop Exchange is a London-based fintech company building a global network of regulated stock exchanges designed specifically for cooperative and mutual securities.72 Its core business model is to enable cooperatives to raise capital by listing tradable, restricted-voting investor shares. This structure is intended to provide cooperatives with access to the liquidity and depth of public capital markets while preserving the foundational principle of member control.73
  - **Market Validation:** The viability of this concept has been affirmed by the market. Coop Exchange successfully closed a pre-seed funding round at a £10 million valuation, with a significant lead investment of over $2 million (CAD) from Co-operators, a major Canadian insurance and financial services cooperative.75 The CEO of Co-operators explicitly stated that the investment was driven by polling that showed over 60% of their market is interested in aligning investments with their values but see limited opportunities.72

The existence and institutional backing of Coop Exchange provide powerful validation for the thesis of this report. It demonstrates that sophisticated institutional capital recognizes the untapped potential of the cooperative sector. It confirms that there is both a supply of cooperatives in need of innovative capital solutions and a demand from investors for instruments that provide exposure to these enterprises. Coop Exchange could represent a future venue for the listing and secondary trading of a standardized CMS, providing the liquidity necessary for a robust market.

  

### **4.3 Comprehensive Risk Analysis**

  

Despite the promising legal foundation and market environment, the development of a CMS involves significant and novel risks that must be rigorously managed.

  - **Legal and Regulatory Risk:** This is the primary risk. As a novel instrument, the CMS exists in a "gray area" of securities law. While the analysis indicates it would be treated as a security, a regulator could take a hostile stance, attempt to prohibit its issuance, or impose such onerous compliance burdens as to make it unviable. The lack of direct precedent creates inherent uncertainty.
  - **Market and Liquidity Risk:** Any new asset class is, by definition, initially illiquid. There will be no established market, making price discovery difficult and bid-ask spreads wide. A sustainable market can only develop if a critical mass of CMS instruments is created across a diverse range of underlying cooperatives, which will take considerable time and effort.
  - **Counterparty Risk:** The value of a CMS is doubly exposed. It is dependent on the ongoing financial health and operational success of *both* the underlying cooperative *and* the specific corporate member whose slot is being securitized. A bankruptcy, operational failure, or significant decline in business by either party could render the CMS worthless.
  - **Cultural and Reputational Risk:** The cooperative movement is historically and culturally skeptical of financialization.7 There is a significant risk that this innovation could be perceived as an attempt by Wall Street to exploit the cooperative model for extractive profit. Overcoming this cultural barrier will require a carefully crafted narrative and a genuine commitment to structuring the CMS in a way that benefits, rather than harms, the cooperative and its members. The success of this venture depends as much on cultural translation as it does on financial engineering. The project's champions must be fluent in the languages of both finance and cooperation, framing the CMS not as a tool for extraction but as a bridge providing cooperatives with access to capital and liquidity, thereby strengthening the model for future generations. The framing of the Sunkist-Limoneira deal, which emphasized "shared history, values, and vision," provides a template for this essential communication strategy.26

### **Conclusion**

  

The foundational premise of this investigation—that the "one-member, one-vote" principle of cooperative law can be transformed from a perceived obstacle into a core design constraint for a new class of securities—is not only theoretically sound but is supported by a confluence of legal precedent, legislative trends, and real-world market developments.

The analysis confirms that California's legal framework, through specific and intentional exceptions for central organizations and other modern cooperative forms, provides the necessary legal architecture to support instruments with asymmetric voting and economic rights. The Sunkist-Limoneira agreement serves as a powerful, tangible proof-of-concept, demonstrating that a publicly traded corporation can hold a membership slot in a major cooperative, creating a direct bridge between the cooperative sector and public capital markets. This membership slot, as a distinct bundle of governance, economic, and access rights, is a definable and thus securitizable asset.

While such an instrument, the Cooperative Membership Security (CMS), would undoubtedly be classified as a "security" under the broad "risk capital" test of California law and the established federal tests, this is a matter for compliance, not a barrier to creation. By structuring initial offerings as private placements to sophisticated institutional investors, the significant regulatory burdens of public registration can be deferred.

The most viable initial product is a **Patronage Swap**, which cleanly separates the economic rights of membership from the governance rights, minimizing disruption to the cooperative's internal structure and bylaws. The valuation of such an instrument, while novel, can be achieved through established financial modeling techniques, primarily a discounted cash flow analysis of the expected patronage stream.

The market timing for such an innovation is highly opportune. The explosive growth of the impact investing market reveals a deep and expanding investor appetite for financial products that offer not only returns but also measurable social and environmental benefits. The CMS, by channeling capital to support member-owned enterprises, possesses a powerful and authentic ESG narrative that aligns perfectly with these preferences.

The primary challenges are not legal or financial but cultural and executional. Success will hinge on the ability to translate the value proposition of the CMS into terms that are acceptable to the historically risk-averse and finance-skeptical cooperative community. It requires a strategic, phased approach, beginning with a well-structured pilot program with a willing cooperative-corporate pair.

The potential reward for overcoming these challenges is immense. The creation of a liquid market for cooperative-based securities could unlock vast amounts of capital for a sector that has traditionally struggled with financing, fundamentally altering its growth trajectory. It represents an opportunity to pioneer a new, hybrid "public-cooperative" asset class, offering investors a unique vehicle for participating in a more equitable and sustainable form of capitalism. The path is complex and fraught with novel challenges, but the legal foundation is firm, the market demand is evident, and the potential impact is transformative.
