## **Part I: The Philosophical and Economic Premise: Formalizing the Price of Voice**

  
  

### **Section 1: Introduction - Beyond Puritanical Cooperativism**

  

The modern cooperative movement, while rooted in principles of democratic control and mutual self-help, often finds itself constrained by an ideological puritanism that can hinder its financial viability and adaptive capacity in competitive capitalistic markets.1 Many traditional cooperative models, built on variations of 19th-century ideals, struggle with capitalization, leading to a "capital conundrum" where the very structure designed to empower members limits the enterprise's ability to grow, innovate, and secure necessary resources.2 This structural tension is particularly acute in the face of practical challenges, such as the withdrawable nature of membership shares, which financial regulators often do not recognize as core equity capital, and the general economic unattractiveness of non-appreciating, non-tradable equity to potential investors.3 The result is a history littered with cooperatives that fail to live up to their own standards, often behaving more like investor-owned firms (IOFs) to survive or facing financial instability.1

This report proceeds from a critical re-examination of these foundational premises. It explores the design and implications of a novel class of cooperative securities that are both tradable and appreciable, directly confronting the traditional subordination of capital. The central objective is to architect a financial instrument that can provide liquidity and generate capital for its holders without sacrificing the core cooperative principle of democratic control. This exploration is not merely a financial engineering exercise; it is grounded in a deeper philosophical inquiry into the nature of freedom, democracy, and power in a capitalist society.

A core concept animating this analysis is that of "democratic exit." The freedom to "vote with your feet"—to leave a society, community, or system that one finds coercive or unjust—is a fundamental, yet often overlooked, democratic right. However, exercising this right is not a matter of mere will; it is a capital-intensive act. The ability to relocate, establish a new life, and achieve genuine autonomy from a coercive system requires substantial financial resources. Traditional models of social organization, including puritanical cooperatives, not only fail to provide these resources but often perpetuate a form of coercive poverty through an ideological aversion to the monetization of assets, including the asset of one's own opinion and influence.

This leads to the report's central and most provocative hypothesis: that a transparent, formalized market for political influence, structured within a new type of cooperative framework, could serve as a powerful mechanism for generating the very capital necessary for this democratic exit. In many contemporary democracies, votes and political influence already have a price, whether through formalized lobbying, campaign finance, or other, less transparent means. Rather than living in denial of this reality, this report investigates the potential of a system that ritualizes and formalizes this process. By creating a structure where political voice can be valued, pooled, and monetized, it may be possible to create a vehicle for economic liberation, providing ordinary individuals with the capital required to achieve true freedom from systems they no longer wish to support. This report, therefore, will construct the legal, financial, and political architecture for such an entity—the Political Cooperative—and analyze its potential to revolutionize both cooperative finance and the landscape of political engagement.

  

### **Section 2: The Political Economy of Influence**

  

To construct a viable model for a political cooperative, it is essential to first understand the existing, often informal, market for political influence. The notion that political outcomes can be bought or sold is not a cynical assertion but a subject of rigorous economic and political analysis. This section establishes the theoretical groundwork by examining the economics of vote-buying, the mechanisms of lobbying and regulatory capture, and the philosophical critiques of the majoritarian "one person, one vote" principle that underpins many democratic systems.

  

#### **The Market for Votes and Influence**

  

The act of vote-buying, while often viewed as simple corruption, can be understood as a complex socio-economic transaction. From a purely economic perspective, it is a contract or an auction where a voter sells their vote to the highest bidder.4 Economic models of this process treat political competition as a contest between profit-maximizing parties seeking to acquire the asset of government control at the lowest possible cost.5 In these models, parties may compete through campaign promises (payments contingent on the election outcome) or through up-front vote-buying (payments contingent on the individual's vote).5

However, a purely economic lens is insufficient. Vote-buying is also a social ritual, embedded in networks of patronage and clientelism. In some contexts, elections are seen as moments when patrons (candidates) fulfill their financial obligations to their clients (voters), temporarily achieving a form of justice or equality.4 These transactions are not always simple quid pro quos. Givers may hope to produce not just instrumental compliance (a vote in exchange for a reward) but also normative compliance, where the offer convinces the recipient of the candidate's worthiness or creates a feeling of obligation.4 This nuanced understanding is critical; a political cooperative would not be creating a market for influence from scratch but rather formalizing and restructuring an existing, deeply embedded set of social and economic practices. The challenge lies in the secrecy of the ballot, which makes it impossible for a buyer to perfectly verify a seller's vote, creating a classic enforcement problem that rational choice theories struggle to explain and that any formalized system would need to address.6

  

#### **Lobbying and Regulatory Capture**

  

Beyond direct vote-buying, the market for influence operates at a more sophisticated level through lobbying and campaign finance. Lobbying can be conceptualized as "buying access" to policymakers, while campaign contributions function as "buying influence" over their decisions.7 This influence is not always an explicit quid pro quo; it is often a subtle exchange where a special interest group (SIG) makes its support for sympathetic politicians known through its actions, and policymakers understand what is expected of them.7

This dynamic is explained by several key theories in political economy. Public choice theory posits that political actors, like economic ones, are driven by self-interest, and their decisions can be modeled as such.8 Elite theory argues that a small subset of the population with significant economic power exerts disproportionate influence on policy, challenging the notion of a pluralistic democracy.8 Perhaps most relevant is the theory of regulatory capture, which describes how regulatory agencies, intended to serve the public interest, can become dominated by the very industries they are meant to regulate.8 This occurs as powerful firms leverage their resources to shape regulations in their favor, creating a system where economic power directly translates into political and policy outcomes.

This translation of market power into political power creates a significant feedback loop. A recent study analyzing U.S. mergers found that corporate consolidation is consistently followed by a significant and persistent increase in political activity, such as lobbying and campaign contributions.10 The underlying logic is that in a highly competitive market, any benefits (or "rents") a firm gains from successful lobbying might be competed away through price competition with rivals. However, when an industry consolidates, the merged, more powerful entities can stop competing on prices and thus retain the full benefit of their political investments, strengthening their incentive to lobby for even more favorable regulations.10 This creates a cycle where market power fuels political power, which in turn reinforces market dominance, harming consumers twice: first through higher prices and second through regulations that favor the powerful.

  

#### **Critique of Majoritarian Democracy**

  

The philosophical underpinnings of Western-style democracies, particularly the "one person, one vote" principle, are not beyond critique.11 This principle, enshrined in U.S. jurisprudence in cases like

*Gray v. Sanders* and *Reynolds v. Sims*, holds that the concept of political equality requires that each citizen's vote carries equal weight.12 However, critics argue that this is a form of dogma that ignores the complexities of modern governance. It is questionable whether a simple majority of voters, who are often busy with their daily lives, can possess the specialized knowledge required to make informed, rational decisions on complex issues like climate change, geopolitical strategy, or macroeconomic policy.11

No successful private corporation, for instance, is run as a direct democracy; they are managed by teams of experts.11 The argument is that as long as "one person, one vote" is the ultimate rule, popular but unsound policies may overrule the advice of experts. This critique opens the door to considering alternative governance models that might incorporate other factors, such as the intensity of preference. A vote-buying mechanism, for example, allows agents to express a level of support for an alternative at an increasing cost, effectively measuring their willingness to pay to affect the outcome.14 This suggests a system where the collective choice is influenced not just by the number of supporters but by the aggregate intensity of their support, as measured by their financial commitment.

  

#### **The Paradox of Formalization**

  

The user's premise—that formalizing the price of political influence could be a liberating tool—collides with a significant paradox revealed by this analysis. The very mechanism designed to generate capital for the masses to "exit" a coercive system could inadvertently strengthen that system's coercive power.

The chain of reasoning is as follows. First, the proposal is to create a market mechanism to monetize political influence, allowing citizens to pool resources and generate capital. Second, the economic theory of regulatory capture and political influence demonstrates a powerful feedback loop where market power begets political power.8 Firms with greater economic resources can invest more in lobbying and campaign finance, shaping regulations to their advantage and further increasing their market power. Third, creating a new, more efficient, and liquid market for political influence—as the political cooperative aims to do—could supercharge this feedback loop. It would likely attract large amounts of capital from already powerful entities seeking to leverage this new tool. Fourth, this would accelerate the concentration of political power in the hands of the wealthy, leading to policies that further benefit capital holders at the expense of ordinary citizens. Consequently, the socio-economic system could become

*more* coercive, not less. The capital required for a meaningful "democratic exit" would increase, while the ability of those without initial capital to generate it through this mechanism would diminish relative to the power of large players. The report must therefore proceed with a clear understanding of this fundamental tension: the proposed solution, if not carefully structured and governed, risks exacerbating the very problem it seeks to solve.

  

## **Part II: Deconstructing the Components: Legal and Financial Frameworks**

  

To engineer a viable political cooperative security, one must navigate and synthesize four distinct and complex regulatory and structural domains: cooperative law, federal tax law, the mechanics of financial securitization, and the intricate web of campaign finance and political law. Each domain presents both significant constraints and potential opportunities. This part deconstructs these components to build a foundational understanding for the synthetic models proposed later in this report.

  

### **Section 3: The Cooperative Legal & Tax Structure**

  

The cooperative is a unique business entity, defined by a set of principles and a specific tax treatment that distinguish it from standard investor-owned firms. Understanding these foundational elements is critical, as they form the primary legal and financial chassis upon which any new model must be built.

  

#### **Foundational Cooperative Principles**

  

The modern cooperative movement is guided by seven core principles, as articulated by the International Cooperative Alliance, which trace their roots to the Rochdale Pioneers of 1844.15 These principles are:

1.  **Voluntary and Open Membership:** Cooperatives are voluntary organizations, open to all able to use their services and willing to accept the responsibilities of membership, without discrimination.17
2.  **Democratic Member Control:** Cooperatives are democratic organizations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting policies and making decisions. In primary cooperatives, this is embodied by the "one member, one vote" rule.15
3.  **Member Economic Participation:** Members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of the cooperative. Benefits are distributed in proportion to members' transactions (patronage) with the cooperative, not on the capital invested.15
4.  **Autonomy and Independence:** Cooperatives are autonomous, self-help organizations. If they raise external capital, they must do so on terms that ensure continued democratic control by members.15
5.  **Education, Training, and Information:** Cooperatives provide education and training to enable effective member participation.15
6.  **Cooperation Among Cooperatives:** Cooperatives strengthen the movement by working together.15
7.  **Concern for Community:** Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their communities.18

Of these, the principles of democratic member control and member economic participation are most salient to this analysis, as they directly conflict with the logic of a tradable, investor-oriented security.

  

#### **The Principle of Subordination of Capital**

  

The most significant ideological and structural constraint within cooperative law is the principle of subordination of capital. This principle dictates that in a cooperative, "capital is the servant, not the master".3 Unlike a traditional corporation where the primary goal is to maximize return on investment for shareholders, a cooperative's purpose is to meet the needs of its members.3 Capital is merely a tool to achieve this end.

This principle manifests in several concrete ways. First, returns on equity investments are strictly limited. The IRS has affirmed that "a cooperative may not be operated for the purpose of paying a return on equity investments".3 Many state statutes and the Capper-Volstead Act explicitly limit dividends on equity to a low percentage, such as 8%.17 Second, control of the cooperative must remain with the member-patrons, not with non-patron equity investors.20 This is the essence of democratic control. Third, the pecuniary benefits of the cooperative's operations must primarily inure to the members based on their use (patronage), not to investors based on their capital contribution.20 This ensures the cooperative remains faithful to its purpose of providing services at cost, rather than generating profits for capital providers.20 This fundamental principle appears to create an insurmountable barrier to the creation of a security designed for capital appreciation and trade.

  

#### **Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code**

  

The tax treatment of cooperatives is governed by Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code (§§ 1381-1388).22 This regime is built around the "single-tax principle," which recognizes the unique relationship between a cooperative and its patrons. Instead of the double taxation faced by C corporations (where profits are taxed at the corporate level and again when distributed as dividends to shareholders), Subchapter T allows a cooperative to be treated as a conduit, passing its earnings through to its patrons to be taxed only once, at the patron level.24

The mechanism for this pass-through is the **patronage dividend** (also called a patronage refund). A cooperative can deduct patronage dividends from its gross income, effectively eliminating its own tax liability on that income.22 However, this favorable treatment is strictly limited to a specific type of income. This creates the central, and most critical, distinction in cooperative tax law: the separation of patronage-sourced income from non-patronage-sourced income.

  

##### **Patronage vs. Non-Patronage Income**

  

The ability to deduct patronage dividends applies *only* to income derived from patronage sources. This distinction is the linchpin of the entire Subchapter T framework and, as will be shown, the key to unlocking the political cooperative model.

  - **Patronage-Sourced Income:** The Code defines this as earnings derived from "business done with or for its patrons".24 To be deductible, distributions from this income must be paid to patrons based on the quantity or value of their business with the co-op, pursuant to a pre-existing legal obligation.24 This is the income stream subject to the principle of subordination of capital.
  - **Non-Patronage-Sourced Income:** This is any other income the cooperative earns. Treasury Regulation § 1.1382-3(c)(2) defines it as "incidental income derived from sources not directly related to marketing, purchasing, or service activities of the cooperative association".24 Examples given include income from the lease of premises, investments in securities, and the sale of capital assets.24 This income is  
    *not* eligible for the patronage dividend deduction. It is taxed at the cooperative level at regular corporate rates, just like any C corporation, and is not subject to the cooperative principles of distribution based on patronage or subordination of capital.23

  

##### **The "Directly Related/Actually Facilitates" Test**

  

For years, the IRS and cooperatives battled over how to classify income that wasn't explicitly listed in the regulations. The IRS often took a literal, *per se* view, arguing that any income from investments, for example, was automatically non-patronage. Cooperatives argued for a more functional approach.24 Over time, court decisions have solidified the cooperative position, establishing the "directly related/actually facilitates" test as the proper standard.24

This test is a fact-intensive inquiry into the economic reality of a transaction.24 It asks whether the income-generating activity is "integrally intertwined" with and "actually facilitates" the accomplishment of the cooperative's primary business of serving its patrons.24 For example, interest earned by a cooperative on a loan from a Bank for Cooperatives, where the loan was taken out to finance the acquisition of supplies for its members, was deemed patronage-sourced because borrowing the money "actually facilitates" the cooperative's purchasing activities.24 Conversely, if a transaction "merely enhances the overall profitability" but has no necessary linkage to the cooperative enterprise, the income is from non-patronage sources.24

  

#### **Subchapter T as an Enabling Framework**

  

At first glance, the rigid principles of cooperative law and the complex rules of Subchapter T appear to be formidable obstacles. The subordination of capital seems to forbid the creation of a profit-seeking security, and the democratic control principle appears to preclude the influence of outside investors. However, a deeper analysis reveals that the structure of Subchapter T, particularly its strict bifurcation of income, does not prohibit the proposed model but in fact provides a precise legal and tax blueprint for its creation.

The key is to recognize that the constraints of cooperative principles apply *only to patronage-sourced income*. The existence of a separate, legally distinct category of non-patronage income, which is treated for tax and distribution purposes like the income of any standard corporation, creates a firewalled space within the cooperative structure.

The logical progression is as follows:

1.  The goal is to create a tradable, appreciative security linked to a cooperative, which requires generating profits for capital investors.
2.  Cooperative principles like subordination of capital and distribution based on patronage make it impossible to distribute profits from the cooperative's primary, member-serving business to investors based on their capital contribution.3
3.  Subchapter T explicitly defines and segregates non-patronage income, which is *not* subject to these principles. This income is taxed at the corporate level, and any after-tax profits can be distributed as dividends to shareholders or retained, at the discretion of the board, without regard to patronage.23
4.  Therefore, if a political cooperative can structure its political influence activities in such a way that the resulting revenue is legally classified as non-patronage income, it creates a pool of profits that is legally "free" from the constraints of traditional cooperative ideology. This would require demonstrating that the political activity does not "actually facilitate" the primary patronage business of the members but is instead a separate, incidental, profit-making endeavor.
5.  This pool of non-patronage profits can then be allocated to a separate class of capital investors without violating Subchapter T or the foundational principles of cooperation as they apply to the patronage side of the business.

This realization transforms Subchapter T from a restrictive tax code into an architectural guide. It allows for the design of a hybrid entity that operates on a cooperative basis for its members while simultaneously operating a non-patronage business line for the benefit of its investors.

  

### **Section 4: The Architecture of Liquidity: Securitization and Secondary Markets**

  

To create a *tradable* cooperative security, it is not enough to simply generate a profit stream for investors. That profit stream must be packaged into a financial instrument that can be bought and sold with ease, providing the liquidity that investors demand. The field of structured finance, specifically the process of securitization, offers a powerful and well-established toolkit for transforming illiquid assets into marketable securities. This section outlines the mechanics of securitization and secondary markets, demonstrating how they can be adapted to serve the unique goals of the political cooperative.

  

#### **The Securitization Process**

  

Securitization is a financial process that pools various types of contractual debt or other income-producing assets and sells their related cash flows to third-party investors as securities. This process effectively transforms illiquid assets, such as mortgages or auto loans, into liquid, tradable instruments. While traditionally applied to debt, the principles can be applied to any asset with a stable and predictable future cash flow. The process typically involves several key steps:

1.  **Origination and Pooling:** The process begins with an "originator"—in this case, the political cooperative—which identifies an income-producing asset it wishes to securitize. This asset would be the future net income stream generated from the cooperative's non-patronage political activities. The originator pools these assets or receivables into a "reference portfolio". For this model, the "pool" would be the contractual right to receive all future profits from the designated non-patronage business line.
2.  **The Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV):** The originator then establishes a new, legally distinct entity known as a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) or Special Purpose Entity (SPE). This entity is created for the sole purpose of the securitization transaction. The originator sells the asset pool to the SPV in what is known as a "true sale". This is a critical legal step. A defining feature of the SPV is that it is structured to be "bankruptcy remote" from the originator. This means that if the originator (the cooperative) were to face financial distress or bankruptcy, the assets legally owned by the SPV are isolated and protected, available only to satisfy the claims of the investors who bought securities from the SPV. This legal insulation is a primary benefit, as it allows the securities to be rated based on the credit quality of the isolated assets, not the overall creditworthiness of the originator.
3.  **Issuance and Tranching:** The SPV finances its purchase of the assets by issuing tradable, interest-bearing securities to investors in the capital markets. To appeal to a broad range of investors with different risk appetites, these securities are often structured into different layers, or "tranches".

<!-- end list -->

  - **Senior Tranches:** These have the first claim on the cash flows from the underlying assets. They carry the lowest risk and offer the lowest yields, often receiving high credit ratings.
  - **Mezzanine Tranches:** These have a middle-level claim, absorbing losses only after the junior tranches are wiped out. They offer moderate risk and moderate yields.
  - **Junior/Equity Tranches:** These have the last claim on cash flows and are the first to absorb losses. They carry the highest risk and offer the highest potential returns, and are often unrated.

<!-- end list -->

1.  **Ongoing Servicing:** A "servicer," which is often the original lender or originator, continues to manage the underlying assets, collect payments, and pass them on (less a fee) to the SPV or a trustee, who then distributes the cash flows to the investors according to the tranche priority.

  

#### **Secondary Markets**

  

The value of a security is intrinsically linked to its liquidity—the ability of an investor to sell it for cash. While the initial issuance of securities occurs in the primary market, the vast majority of trading happens in secondary markets.27 These markets, which include centralized exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and over-the-counter (OTC) markets, allow investors to buy and sell previously issued securities from one another, rather than from the issuing company.

For a novel instrument like a political cooperative security, establishing a liquid secondary market would be a significant challenge. Private secondary markets exist for the shares of non-public companies, but they are often opaque, illiquid, and subject to transfer restrictions. Platforms like Coop Exchange are emerging with the goal of creating dedicated secondary markets for cooperative shares, aiming to provide the liquidity, transparency, and flexibility that have historically been absent in the sector.29 The success of a tradable political cooperative security would depend heavily on the development of such a marketplace.

  

#### **The SPV as a Legal and Political Firewall**

  

The architecture of securitization offers a solution that goes far beyond mere financial engineering; it provides a mechanism to resolve the central governance conflict of the political cooperative. The "bankruptcy remote" status of the SPV can be repurposed to create a robust *political and legal firewall* between the cooperative and its external investors.

This structure functions as follows:

1.  The core problem is how to provide investors with a tradable, profit-generating instrument without giving them ownership or control rights that would violate the cooperative principle of democratic member control.
2.  The securitization process, through the "true sale" of an asset to an SPV, legally severs the originator's connection to that asset.
3.  In this model, the political cooperative (the Originator) would sell its *future non-patronage income stream* (the asset) to a newly created SPV. At this point, the legal ownership of that future income transfers entirely to the SPV.
4.  The SPV, a separate legal person, then issues securities to investors. The investors' contract and legal relationship are exclusively with the SPV. Their claim is on the assets held by the SPV—the right to the future non-patronage income—not on the cooperative itself.
5.  This structure elegantly achieves the user's primary objectives. The cooperative receives a significant infusion of upfront capital from the sale of the asset to the SPV. The investors receive a tradable security whose value is tied to the performance of the cooperative's political activities. Crucially, the cooperative's governance remains untouched. The investors are not members of the cooperative; they hold no cooperative shares and have no voting rights in its affairs. Their legal recourse is limited to the SPV and its assets.

This use of the SPV as a firewall effectively insulates the cooperative's democratic core from the demands of external capital. It allows the cooperative to access public capital markets while preserving its fundamental identity, resolving the "capital conundrum" in a novel and powerful way.

  

### **Section 5: The Modern Political-Financial Complex**

  

The creation of a political cooperative cannot occur in a vacuum. It must be designed to operate within the specific legal and regulatory framework governing political activity by corporate entities in the United States. This framework has been fundamentally reshaped over the past two decades by landmark Supreme Court decisions that have expanded the concept of corporate rights and by the subsequent proliferation of new vehicles for political spending.

  

#### **Corporate Personhood and Constitutional Rights**

  

The legal landscape of corporate political speech rests on two intertwined doctrines: corporate personhood and the application of First Amendment rights to these "persons."

  - **Legal Foundation of Corporate Personhood:** The concept that a corporation is a "juridical person" with legal rights and responsibilities separate from its owners or managers is a long-standing principle in law.31 U.S. law has long recognized corporations as legal entities that can hold property, enter into contracts, and sue or be sued.32 While the Constitution does not explicitly mention corporations, the Supreme Court has, since the 19th century, recognized that they are holders of certain constitutional rights.32 This doctrine does not grant corporations all the rights of natural persons, but it establishes them as entities capable of asserting constitutional protections.32
  - ***Citizens United v. FEC*** **(2010):** This seminal Supreme Court case dramatically altered the landscape of campaign finance. In a 5-4 decision, the Court held that the government cannot restrict independent political spending by corporations and unions, as doing so violates the First Amendment's protection of free speech.34 The majority opinion, authored by Justice Kennedy, asserted that the government may not suppress political speech based on the speaker's corporate identity.35 The Court reasoned that "independent" spending—that which is not coordinated with a candidate's campaign—does not give rise to the quid pro quo corruption that would justify such a restriction.36 The decision overruled prior precedents that had allowed for such bans.38 The dissent, authored by Justice Stevens, argued passionately that the ruling "threatens to undermine the integrity of elected institutions" by allowing corporate domination of the electoral process, rejecting the notion that corporations are members of society in the same way as individuals.36
  - ***Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*** **(2014):** This decision further solidified the trend of ascribing rights to corporate entities. The Court ruled 5-4 that "closely held" for-profit corporations could be exempt from a federal mandate to provide contraceptive coverage to their employees if it violated the owners' sincere religious beliefs.40 The legal basis for this was the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA), which prohibits the government from substantially burdening a "person's" exercise of religion. The Court, citing the U.S. Code's "dictionary" definition, found that "person" includes corporations, thus extending RFRA's protections to these entities.42 This case reinforced the legal principle that corporations can be vehicles for the expression of their owners' rights and beliefs.

Together, these cases establish a legal environment where a corporate entity, including an incorporated cooperative, possesses a robust First Amendment right to engage in unlimited independent political spending.

  

#### **Mechanisms of Political Influence**

  

In the wake of *Citizens United*, a sophisticated ecosystem of political financing vehicles has emerged, each with distinct rules and capabilities.

  - **PACs vs. Super PACs:**

<!-- end list -->

  - **Political Action Committees (PACs):** Often called "connected PACs" or "separate segregated funds" (SSFs), these are established by corporations, labor unions, or trade groups.43 They are funded by voluntary contributions from a restricted class (e.g., managers and shareholders of a corporation) and are subject to strict contribution limits (e.g., $5,000 per candidate per election).43 They can, however, contribute directly to a candidate's campaign committee.44
  - **Super PACs (Independent Expenditure-Only Committees):** Created as a direct result of the *Citizens United* and *SpeechNow.org v. FEC* decisions, Super PACs can raise unlimited sums of money from individuals, corporations, and unions.37 They can then spend unlimited amounts to expressly advocate for or against candidates. The crucial legal restriction is that their spending must be truly "independent" and cannot be coordinated with candidates or political parties.45

<!-- end list -->

  - **501(c)(4) "Social Welfare" Organizations:** These are tax-exempt organizations that must be "operated exclusively for the promotion of social welfare".47 Under IRS rules, this means they must be "primarily engaged" in promoting the common good and general welfare of the community.48 The IRS has established a "primary activity test" to make this determination, but it is a vague "facts and circumstances" test with no clear numerical threshold, though a cautious guideline is that political campaign intervention should not exceed 30-40% of total expenditures.49 Unlike 501(c)(3) charities, 501(c)(4)s can engage in unlimited lobbying related to their mission and a certain amount of political campaign activity.51

  

#### **The Flow of "Dark Money"**

  

The most significant consequence of this regulatory structure is the rise of "dark money." This term refers to political spending by groups that are not required to disclose their donors.53 The primary vehicle for this is the 501(c)(4) organization.

The mechanism works as follows: A wealthy individual or corporation makes a large, anonymous donation to a 501(c)(4) organization. Because the 501(c)(4) is generally not required to publicly disclose its donors, the source of the funds remains secret.47 That 501(c)(4) can then make a large contribution to a Super PAC. The Super PAC must disclose that it received money from the 501(c)(4), but the trail ends there; the original donor remains hidden from public view.53 This creates a perfectly legal channel for anonymous, unlimited spending to influence elections, a practice that has been used extensively by groups on both sides of the political spectrum.55

  

#### **Legal Status of Cooperatives in Campaign Finance**

  

Federal Election Commission (FEC) regulations explicitly address the role of cooperatives. An incorporated cooperative is treated like any other corporation and is therefore prohibited from using its general treasury funds to make direct contributions to federal candidates.57 However, like other corporations, a cooperative is legally permitted to use its treasury funds to establish, administer, and solicit contributions for a separate segregated fund (SSF)—a traditional PAC.57 This PAC can then make limited contributions to candidates.

The *Citizens United* decision overlays this existing framework. It grants cooperatives, as corporate entities, the First Amendment right to make unlimited independent expenditures directly from their treasury funds. Therefore, a political cooperative could legally fund advertisements, get-out-the-vote efforts, or other communications expressly advocating for or against a candidate, provided such spending is not coordinated with any campaign.58 This gives the political cooperative a direct and powerful avenue for political engagement, funded by its operational revenues.

### **Section 6: Precedents and Pathfinders: New Generation and Multi-Stakeholder Models**

  

The concept of a tradable cooperative security, while novel, does not emerge from a vacuum. For decades, cooperatives have experimented with their structures to address the inherent tension between democratic principles and the need for capital. Examining these evolutionary steps provides a solid foundation and valuable precedents for the more radical models proposed in this report.

  

#### **New Generation Cooperatives (NGCs)**

  

Pioneered in the upper Midwest in the 1970s, the New Generation Cooperative (NGC) represents a significant departure from traditional cooperative structure, particularly in agricultural processing. NGCs are designed to handle the large, upfront capital requirements of value-added processing facilities, a challenge traditional models struggle to meet. Their key innovations are:

  - **Tradable Delivery Rights:** The cornerstone of the NGC is the creation of an equity instrument known as a "delivery right" or "delivery share". This is not merely a membership certificate; it is a tradable and appreciable asset that represents both the right and the *obligation* to deliver a specific quantity and quality of a commodity to the cooperative's processing facility. The value of these rights can fluctuate based on the cooperative's profitability and market conditions, making them analogous to shares of corporate stock. This establishes a crucial precedent: a cooperative instrument that is designed to appreciate and be traded, albeit often in a limited market with board approval required for transfers.
  - **Closed Membership and Upfront Equity:** Unlike traditional open-membership co-ops, NGCs have a closed or restricted membership. Membership is contingent upon purchasing a substantial number of delivery rights, which provides the cooperative with a significant upfront equity base to fund its capital-intensive projects. This structure directly links ownership and benefit not just to patronage, but to a significant initial capital investment, blurring the lines of traditional cooperative finance.59

  

#### **Multi-Stakeholder and Hybrid Cooperatives**

  

Another important evolutionary path is the development of multi-stakeholder cooperatives. These organizations formally include more than one class of stakeholder in their governance and economic structure. A common example is a food co-op that includes consumer-members, worker-members, and perhaps even producer-members or community supporters.

This model is significant because it provides a governance precedent for managing the potentially conflicting interests of different groups within a single cooperative entity. The bylaws of a multi-stakeholder co-op must explicitly define the rights, responsibilities, and economic participation of each class, including how board seats are allocated and how profits are distributed among the different groups.60 This demonstrates that a cooperative structure is flexible enough to accommodate heterogeneous member classes with divergent interests, a necessary feature for any model that seeks to integrate passive capital investors alongside active member-patrons.

  

#### **California's "Community Investor" Model**

  

A particularly relevant legal innovation is the California Worker Cooperative Act (formerly AB 816), which amended the state's Consumer Cooperative Corporation Law to create a specific legal framework for worker cooperatives. A key provision of this law was the creation of a new class of participant: the "community investor".

A community investor is defined as a person who is not a worker-member but who invests money in the cooperative. This law provides a direct statutory template for integrating outside capital while preserving democratic control:

  - **Limited Voting Rights:** Community investors can be granted limited voting rights, but these rights are explicitly restricted. Their voting power can only extend to approval rights on major existential decisions, such as a merger, sale of major assets, or dissolution. They have no right to elect directors or propose actions.
  - **Securities Exemption:** The law also raised the exemption from securities registration for cooperative memberships from $300 to $1,000 per member, making it easier for co-ops to raise community capital. Subsequent legislation (AB 283) further clarified that equity-related patronage distributions are also exempt from qualification requirements, removing a significant administrative burden.

The California model is a landmark. It provides a clear, state-sanctioned legal pathway for a cooperative to issue a separate class of shares to non-patron investors, grant them a financial return, and strictly limit their governance rights to protect the democratic control of the worker-members. This is a direct and powerful precedent for the "Dual-Class Influence Share" model proposed later in this report.

### **Section 7: The Howey Test Gauntlet: Is the Political Cooperative Share a Security?**

  

Any attempt to create a tradable financial instrument in the United States must confront the nation's securities laws, which are designed to protect investors through mandatory disclosure and registration. The foundational legal standard for determining whether a particular financial arrangement constitutes a "security" is the *Howey* test, established by the Supreme Court in the 1946 case *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.*. A rigorous analysis of how the proposed political cooperative securities would be treated under this test is not merely a compliance exercise; it is fundamental to their design.

  

#### **The Howey Test Defined**

  

The *Howey* test provides four prongs to identify an "investment contract," which is a type of security. A transaction is an investment contract if it involves:

1.  **An investment of money** (or other valuable assets);
2.  **In a common enterprise**;
3.  **With a reasonable expectation of profits**;
4.  **To be derived from the entrepreneurial or managerial efforts of others**.

The test is designed to be flexible, focusing on the economic reality and substance of a transaction rather than its form or name. If an instrument meets these four criteria, it is a security and is subject to regulation by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

  

#### **Application to Cooperative Interests**

  

Historically, the application of the *Howey* test to cooperatives has been fact-dependent and has evolved with the complexity of cooperative models.

  - **Traditional Cooperatives:** The equity interests in traditional cooperatives are generally **not** considered securities. In the landmark case *United Housing Foundation, Inc. v. Forman*, the Supreme Court analyzed shares in a housing cooperative and found they were not securities. The Court reasoned that there was no "investment" with an "expectation of profits"; rather, the residents were purchasing a place to live.62 The economic benefits were derived from the members' own use and participation, not "solely from the efforts of others".62 The SEC has consistently issued guidance that common shares in a cooperative are not securities, provided they do not pay dividends, are not freely transferable, and grant only one vote per member, thus lacking the typical features of an investment.63
  - **New Generation Cooperatives (NGCs):** The analysis for NGCs is far more complex and points toward their equity interests being classified as securities. The substantial upfront capital investment clearly satisfies the "investment of money" prong. The cooperative itself is a "common enterprise." The tradable and appreciable nature of the delivery rights creates a clear "expectation of profits" through capital gains, which is distinct from patronage refunds. Finally, while the member must deliver a commodity, the ultimate profitability of the enterprise depends heavily on the "essential managerial efforts" of the cooperative's management in processing and marketing the final product, satisfying the "efforts of others" prong.62 For these reasons, many legal analyses conclude that NGC delivery rights are likely securities, and cooperatives are often advised to operate under this assumption, typically by seeking exemptions from full registration.

  

#### **Analysis of Proposed Political Cooperative Securities**

  

Any of the proposed models for a political cooperative that involve issuing a tradable instrument to passive investors in exchange for capital with the promise of a financial return will almost certainly be classified as a security under the *Howey* test. The entire purpose of such an instrument is to function as an investment.

A prong-by-prong analysis confirms this conclusion:

1.  **Investment of Money:** Investors would purchase "Influence Shares" or asset-backed securities with cash or other assets. This prong is clearly met.
2.  **Common Enterprise:** Investors would be pooling their capital in a common venture—the political cooperative or its associated SPV—whose fortunes are tied together. This prong is clearly met.62
3.  **Expectation of Profits:** The instrument would be marketed to investors with the explicit or implicit promise of financial returns, either through dividends from non-patronage income, interest payments from an asset-backed security, or capital appreciation upon resale in a secondary market. This is the very definition of profit from an investment perspective and is distinct from the patronage-based savings of traditional members.62
4.  **From the Efforts of Others:** The investors in these models are passive capital providers. Their financial return depends entirely on the "essential managerial efforts" of the cooperative's leadership and staff to successfully execute its political strategy and generate non-patronage income. The investors themselves perform no labor for the cooperative.

Given that all four prongs are met, the conclusion is inescapable: the proposed instruments are investment contracts and therefore securities. This is not a barrier but a critical design parameter. It means the cooperative must embrace this status and structure its capital-raising activities in full compliance with federal and state securities laws, either through full registration or, more likely, by utilizing an available exemption.

### **Section 8: Proposed Models for a Political Cooperative**

  

Synthesizing the legal, financial, and political frameworks analyzed thus far, it is possible to construct specific, actionable models for a political cooperative. Each model offers a different approach to balancing democratic control, capital formation, and political efficacy. The following three models represent a spectrum from a more conventional, structured-finance approach to a radically decentralized, market-driven one.

  

#### **Model A: The Securitized Political Action Fund**

  

This model leverages the established architecture of securitization to create a clean separation between the cooperative's core functions and its investment vehicle. It prioritizes legal and financial insulation over structural simplicity.

  - **Structure:**

<!-- end list -->

1.  **Cooperative Formation:** A cooperative is formed for a primary purpose (e.g., a consumer co-op for its members, a worker co-op for its employees). This establishes the patronage-based side of the enterprise.
2.  **Non-Patronage Business Line:** The cooperative's board, exercising its business judgment, establishes political action as a distinct, non-patronage business activity. This could involve creating an in-house division or a wholly-owned subsidiary (a standard C-corp or LLC) dedicated to political consulting, lobbying, or managing independent expenditure campaigns. Under the "actually facilitates" test, this activity would be structured to "merely enhance overall profitability" rather than being "integrally intertwined" with the co-op's primary patronage function.24
3.  **True Sale to SPV:** The cooperative (the Originator) enters into a contract to sell the rights to the future net income stream from this political action subsidiary to a bankruptcy-remote Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV). This "true sale" legally transfers ownership of the future profits to the SPV in exchange for an upfront cash payment.
4.  **Securities Issuance:** The SPV, now owning the income stream, issues tradable, asset-backed securities to the public or to accredited investors. These securities can be tranched into senior, mezzanine, and equity layers to appeal to different investor risk profiles. The payments of principal and interest to investors are funded exclusively by the cash flows generated by the political action subsidiary and collected by the SPV.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Governance:** Governance is bifurcated. The member-patrons retain absolute one-member, one-vote control over the cooperative and its primary business. The investors who purchase the asset-backed securities have no membership, no voting rights, and no legal claim against the cooperative itself. Their legal relationship is solely with the SPV, and their claim is limited to the assets held by the SPV.
  - **Pros:** This model offers the strongest legal and financial separation between the democratic cooperative and the capital markets. It utilizes a well-understood and legally robust framework (securitization) that is designed precisely for this kind of asset isolation.
  - **Cons:** The structure is complex and carries significant transactional costs to set up. Its success hinges on the ability of the political action arm to generate a sufficiently predictable and substantial profit stream to make the securities attractive to investors, a significant challenge given the inherent uncertainty of political outcomes. Furthermore, the non-patronage income would be subject to corporate income tax at the cooperative/subsidiary level before being transferred to the SPV.

  

#### **Model B: The Dual-Class "Influence Share" Cooperative**

  

This model is structurally simpler and draws directly from precedents like the California "Community Investor" law. It integrates the investment mechanism directly into the cooperative's capital structure.

  - **Structure:**

<!-- end list -->

1.  **Cooperative Incorporation:** A cooperative corporation is formed under a state statute that explicitly allows for multiple classes of membership or shares, such as California's Cooperative Corporation Law.
2.  **Issuance of Two Share Classes:** The cooperative's articles and bylaws authorize two distinct classes of shares:

<!-- end list -->

  - **Class M (Member Shares):** These are the patronage shares. One share is issued per member, entitling the holder to one vote in all cooperative matters and to receive patronage dividends derived from the cooperative's primary business activities. These shares are non-tradable and redeemable only at their par value.15
  - **Class I (Influence Shares):** These are the investment shares. They are sold to the public (members or non-members) and are designed to be tradable on a secondary market. They carry no voting rights, or, following the California model, strictly limited approval rights on existential matters like dissolution or merger. Holders of Class I shares are entitled to receive dividends paid exclusively from the cooperative's *after-tax non-patronage income*.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Governance:** Democratic control is fully preserved within the Class M member-shareholders, who are the only ones with the power to elect the board and direct the cooperative's affairs.15 Class I investors are passive capital providers, legally firewalled from governance by the share structure.
  - **Pros:** This model is structurally simpler and more direct than securitization. It creates a tradable instrument that is part of the cooperative itself, potentially fostering a greater sense of alignment between investors and the organization's mission.
  - **Cons:** It creates a direct potential for agency conflict. The board, elected by Class M members, must manage the cooperative for their benefit while also generating non-patronage profits to satisfy Class I investors. This could lead to difficult decisions where the interests of the two groups diverge. This model also requires careful navigation of state-level cooperative laws and federal securities regulations.63

  

#### **Model C: The Futarchy-Governed Political Fund**

  

This model represents the most radical departure from traditional structures, embracing decentralized technology and prediction markets to formalize a market for effective political decision-making.

  - **Structure:**

<!-- end list -->

1.  **Entity Formation:** The organization is likely formed as a Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO), a digitally native entity governed by smart contracts on a blockchain, though it would still need a legal wrapper (like an LLC or unincorporated association) to interact with the traditional legal and financial world.
2.  **Governance by Futarchy:** The core of the model is futarchy, a governance system where participants "vote on values, but bet on beliefs".65

<!-- end list -->

  - **Voting on Values:** The members of the DAO (who could join via a token purchase) vote democratically on a clear, measurable, high-level goal. For example: "The objective for this fiscal year is to pass legislation that reduces carbon emissions in State X by 10%," or "The objective is to ensure Candidate Y wins the election."
  - **Betting on Beliefs:** Once the goal is set, specific proposals for action are put forth (e.g., "Fund a $1M ad campaign targeting these districts," "Lobby these specific legislators," "Fund a grassroots organizing effort"). For each proposal, a prediction market is created.66 Investors (who can be members or the general public) buy and sell tokens that represent the predicted success of that proposal in achieving the democratically chosen goal. The price of the "Pass" token for a given proposal reflects the market's collective belief in its efficacy.

<!-- end list -->

1.  **Automated Execution:** The proposal whose prediction market token reaches the highest price (or passes a certain threshold) is automatically funded and executed by the DAO's smart contracts.65 Investors are rewarded financially based on the accuracy of their predictions, not on the political outcome itself.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Governance:** This model redefines democratic control. The "demos" (members) sets the ultimate direction and defines success, while the "market" (investors) determines the most efficient strategy to get there. It directly incentivizes participants to make rational, informed decisions rather than emotional or biased ones, as they have "skin in the game".67
  - **Pros:** This is the purest formalization of a market for effective political influence. It has the potential to be radically more efficient and effective at allocating political capital than traditional top-down decision-making. It aligns financial incentives directly with achieving stated goals.
  - **Cons:** The model is highly experimental and technologically intensive. It faces enormous legal and regulatory uncertainty, as DAOs and futarchy exist in a legal gray area. The greatest challenge is defining and objectively measuring the "success" of a political goal, which is often far more complex than a simple token price metric used in other DAO futarchy experiments.66

  

### **Section 9: Governance, Agency Costs, and Compliance**

  

The viability of any political cooperative model, particularly the hybrid structures of Models A and B, hinges on its ability to manage the inherent conflicts of interest between its constituent groups and to navigate a labyrinth of regulatory requirements. This section analyzes these governance challenges through the lens of mechanism design and outlines the necessary compliance architecture.

  

#### **Agency Problems and Mechanism Design**

  

In corporate governance, an "agency problem" arises when the interests of an "agent" (e.g., a company's management) diverge from those of the "principal" (e.g., the shareholders).68 The political cooperative creates a complex, multi-layered agency problem:

  - **Vertical Conflict:** The classic conflict between owners (the cooperative as a whole) and management (the board and staff) exists, where managers might pursue their own interests.68
  - **Horizontal Conflict:** A new and more acute conflict arises between the two classes of principals: the member-patrons (Class M) and the capital investors (Class I). Member-patrons may prioritize long-term community benefits, lower-cost services, or ideological purity, while capital investors will exclusively prioritize the maximization of financial returns from the non-patronage political activities.71

Mechanism design theory, a field of economics that studies how to design institutions to achieve desired outcomes given actors' self-interest, provides a framework for addressing these conflicts.73 The goal is to design a set of rules—a mechanism—that aligns the incentives of all parties. For the political cooperative, this would involve:

  - **Governance Structure:** The dual-class share structure (Model B) or the SPV firewall (Model A) are themselves forms of mechanism design, intended to legally separate the control rights of members from the financial rights of investors. The bylaws must be meticulously drafted to reinforce this separation, for example, by establishing a super-majority vote of members to alter the profit-split formula between patronage and non-patronage activities, preventing it from becoming a recurring political issue.61
  - **Executive Compensation:** The compensation for the cooperative's management could be structured to align incentives. For example, bonuses could be tied to a balanced scorecard that measures both the delivery of benefits to member-patrons *and* the profitability of the non-patronage business line, forcing management to serve both masters.
  - **Information Disclosure:** Transparency is a key tool for mitigating agency costs. The cooperative would need to provide clear and separate financial reporting for its patronage and non-patronage activities, allowing both members and investors to monitor performance and hold management accountable.

  

#### **Compliance Architecture**

  

Operating a political cooperative would require simultaneous compliance with at least four distinct and demanding regulatory regimes. A failure in any one area could jeopardize the entire enterprise.

1.  **Entity Formation and Cooperative Law:** The organization must be properly incorporated, likely as a cooperative corporation or an LLC electing to be taxed as a C-Corporation, to access Subchapter T benefits. The choice of state for incorporation is critical; a state like California with explicit statutes for multi-stakeholder or community-investor models would be highly advantageous. The articles of incorporation and bylaws must be carefully drafted to define the rights of each class of shareholder and to establish the pre-existing obligation to pay patronage dividends, a requirement for Subchapter T.
2.  **Federal and State Campaign Finance Law:** As an entity engaged in political spending, the cooperative and/or its associated vehicles would fall under the jurisdiction of the Federal Election Commission (FEC) and state election boards.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Registration:** If the cooperative establishes a traditional PAC (SSF), it must register with the FEC.44 If it creates a Super PAC for its independent expenditures, that entity must also register.74 A 501(c)(4) must register with the IRS.47
  - **Disclosure:** All political spending—contributions from a PAC or independent expenditures from the cooperative's treasury or a Super PAC—would be subject to strict and periodic public disclosure requirements, detailing the amounts and recipients of the spending.58
  - **Coordination Rules:** The cooperative must implement a rigorous compliance program to ensure that any independent expenditures are not coordinated with candidate campaigns, as coordination would transform the spending into an illegal corporate contribution.58

<!-- end list -->

1.  **IRS Tax Law (Subchapter T):** The cooperative must meticulously track and separate its income into patronage and non-patronage sources to comply with Subchapter T.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Form 1120-C:** The cooperative would file its annual income tax return using Form 1120-C, U.S. Income Tax Return for Cooperative Associations.22
  - **Form 1099-PATR:** For every member receiving over $10 in patronage dividends, the cooperative must file Form 1099-PATR, Taxable Distributions Received From Cooperatives.22 This form details the amount of the dividend, distinguishing between cash and non-cash (equity) portions.

<!-- end list -->

1.  **SEC Securities Law:** As established by the *Howey* test analysis, the tradable "Influence Shares" would be considered securities. This triggers the jurisdiction of the SEC.

<!-- end list -->

  - **Registration or Exemption:** The offering of these shares must either be registered with the SEC—a costly and complex process—or qualify for an exemption. Common exemptions include Regulation D (for offerings to accredited investors), Regulation A+ (a "mini-IPO"), or Regulation Crowdfunding (for raising smaller amounts from the general public).
  - **State "Blue Sky" Laws:** In addition to federal law, the cooperative must comply with the securities laws (known as "blue sky" laws) of every state in which it offers shares. While most states have exemptions for cooperatives, these vary and must be carefully navigated.63
  - **Anti-Fraud Provisions:** Regardless of registration status, all securities offerings are subject to the anti-fraud provisions of the securities acts, which prohibit any material misstatements or omissions in connection with the sale of a security.

## **Part IV: Implications and Recommendations**

  

The construction of a tradable political cooperative security is more than a theoretical exercise; it carries profound implications for the nature of democratic participation, corporate governance, and economic power. This final part evaluates the viability of the models against their foundational premise, confronts the ethical dilemmas they raise, and offers concrete recommendations for moving from theory to practice.

  

### **Section 10: The Viability of Democratic Exit and the Ethics of Monetized Influence**

  

The core philosophical justification for this endeavor is the concept of "democratic exit"—the idea that true freedom requires the financial capacity to opt out of a coercive system. The proposed models aim to generate this capital by formalizing a market for political influence. We must now critically assess both the viability of this goal and the ethical consequences of the proposed means.

  

#### **Assessing the "Exit" Premise**

  

Can the proposed models realistically generate sufficient capital to enable a meaningful "democratic exit" for a significant number of people? The answer is likely no, at least not directly. The scale of capital required for an individual or family to achieve complete financial autonomy and relocate away from a national economic system is immense. It is improbable that dividends from an "Influence Share" or returns from a securitized political fund could, on their own, provide this level of capital to a large base of small investors.

However, the premise may be more viable when viewed indirectly. A successful political cooperative, by wielding influence effectively, could advocate for policies that lower the barriers to exit for everyone. This could include policies that support universal basic income, portable benefits, social housing, or public banking—all of which reduce an individual's dependence on the traditional wage-labor market and make the prospect of "voting with your feet" more attainable. In this sense, the capital generated is not for individual cash-outs but is a collective tool to reshape the economic environment itself, making exit less of a financial necessity and more of a practical possibility. The cooperative's success would be measured not by the exit of its members, but by the creation of a society from which they no longer feel a desperate need to escape.

  

#### **Ethical Implications of Monetized Influence**

  

The proposal to formalize a market for political influence forces a confrontation with deep ethical questions. Does this model democratize influence or does it further entrench plutocracy?

  - **The Argument for Democratization:** Proponents would argue that the political cooperative democratizes influence by allowing small actors to pool their resources.78 An individual's $100 investment has little political weight, but one hundred thousand such investments, aggregated and professionally managed by the cooperative, could create a formidable political force capable of challenging established interests. It brings the tools of sophisticated political finance, previously the domain of corporations and billionaires, to a broader membership base. It makes the "market for influence" transparent and accessible, rather than opaque and exclusive.79
  - **The Argument for Plutocracy:** Critics, however, would point to the paradox of formalization identified earlier. Creating a liquid, efficient, and tradable market for political influence would be an irresistible opportunity for the wealthiest actors in society.80 They could deploy vast sums of capital to purchase "Influence Shares" or the most senior tranches of a securitized fund, not to fund a democratic exit, but to further their own political and economic agendas. This could lead to an arms race where the political cooperative, despite its democratic internal governance, becomes an external tool of the very plutocracy it was designed to counter. The model could simply become a more efficient mechanism for converting economic power into political power, exacerbating the problems of big money in politics.10

The ultimate outcome would depend entirely on the governance and ownership structure. A model that caps the number of "Influence Shares" any single entity can own, or one that prioritizes member-investors over purely external ones, might mitigate this risk. Without such safeguards, the political cooperative could easily become a vehicle for reinforcing, rather than challenging, existing power structures.

  

#### **The Rochdale Principles Revisited: The Obsolescence of Neutrality**

  

One of the original Rochdale Principles was that of "political and religious neutrality".82 This principle, born in an era when overt partisanship could fracture a fledgling enterprise, may now be obsolete. In the post-

*Citizens United* world, corporate political speech and spending are not only legal but are increasingly seen as a standard part of corporate strategy.37 Major corporations, institutional investors, and their executives engage deeply in the political process to shape regulations, secure contracts, and advance their interests.10

The relevant question today is not *if* economic entities will be political, but *how* they will be governed when they are. A traditional corporation's political spending is directed by a small board and executive team, often with little to no input from shareholders or employees, creating significant agency costs.86 The political cooperative, by contrast, offers a model where political engagement is subject to the democratic control of its members. The decision to engage, the goals to pursue, and the values to uphold are determined by the one-member, one-vote principle. The political cooperative thus represents a direct answer to the challenge of corporate political action, replacing opaque, top-down influence with transparent, democratically governed engagement. It rejects neutrality not in favor of corporate whim, but in favor of a democratically determined political purpose.

  

### **Section 11: Recommendations and Avenues for Future Research**

  

The concept of a tradable political cooperative security, while fraught with complexity and ethical ambiguity, represents a significant potential innovation at the intersection of finance, law, and democratic theory. To advance this concept from a theoretical construct to a practical possibility, the following recommendations and research avenues are proposed.

  

#### **Actionable Recommendations**

  

1.  **For Legal Innovators and Policymakers:**

<!-- end list -->

  - **Promote State-Level Cooperative Law Reform:** The most viable near-term path for a political cooperative is in a jurisdiction with favorable statutes. Policymakers should be encouraged to study and adopt legislation similar to the California Worker Cooperative Act, which explicitly creates legal categories for non-patron, non-voting "community investors". Expanding such provisions beyond worker co-ops to all cooperative types would create a clear and stable legal foundation for hybrid capital structures.
  - **Clarify Federal Campaign Finance Rules for Cooperatives:** The FEC should be petitioned to issue clear guidance on how the *Citizens United* framework applies to cooperatives that issue multiple classes of equity. Specifically, guidance is needed on whether treasury-funded independent expenditures must benefit all shareholders (including non-voting investors) or only the voting member-patrons.
  - **Develop a "Political Cooperative" Safe Harbor:** Congress or regulatory agencies could develop a specific legal safe harbor for entities structured as political cooperatives. This could involve creating a new entity type or providing streamlined, cross-agency compliance procedures that recognize the unique nature of an entity subject to cooperative law, Subchapter T, SEC regulations, and FEC rules simultaneously.

<!-- end list -->

1.  **For Cooperative Organizers and Financial Innovators:**

<!-- end list -->

  - **Launch a Pilot Project (Model B):** The most practical first step would be to launch a pilot project based on Model B, the "Dual-Class 'Influence Share' Cooperative." This should be done in a jurisdiction with favorable laws, such as California. The pilot should focus on a clearly defined, measurable political goal to attract initial investors and test the governance model.
  - **Focus on Non-Patronage Income Generation:** The success of any model depends on the "actually facilitates" test.24 The pilot's organizers must meticulously structure their political activities as a business line that is legally and operationally distinct from the cooperative's primary patronage purpose, ensuring the resulting income is classified as non-patronage.
  - **Build a Secondary Market Ecosystem:** A tradable security is worthless without a market. Organizers should partner with emerging platforms focused on social or cooperative capital (e.g., Coop Exchange 30) to ensure that a secondary market for the "Influence Shares" can be established, providing the necessary liquidity to attract investors.

  

#### **Unresolved Questions and Avenues for Future Research**

  

This report lays the architectural groundwork, but many critical questions remain unanswered and require further investigation.

  - **Valuation and Market Dynamics:** How would a financial market price a novel security like an "Influence Share"? Its value is tied to the expected profitability of political action, which is notoriously difficult to predict. What valuation models would apply? Would the market be driven by rational analysis of political efficacy, or would it be subject to speculative bubbles based on partisan fervor?
  - **Managing Agency Costs Over the Long Term:** While mechanism design offers tools to align incentives, the long-term stability of a hybrid cooperative with two distinct classes of owners remains an open question. Can a board elected by member-patrons consistently make decisions that also satisfy profit-seeking investors, especially during periods of financial stress or intense political conflict? Empirical research on the performance and governance of existing multi-stakeholder cooperatives would be invaluable here.
  - **Societal Impact: Democratization or Plutocracy?** This is the ultimate unresolved question. Would a successful political cooperative movement empower citizen-led political action, or would it simply create a more efficient marketplace for the wealthy to purchase political outcomes? This question likely cannot be answered theoretically. It would require real-world experimentation and careful observation of how capital flows into these new entities and what political effects they produce. Research should focus on tracking the ownership of "Influence Shares" and correlating the cooperative's political spending with policy outcomes to determine who truly benefits from this new form of monetized influence.

In conclusion, the political cooperative is a radical concept born from a critique of both the limitations of traditional cooperativism and the inequities of modern capitalism. While its path to implementation is narrow and fraught with legal and ethical challenges, its potential to reconfigure the relationship between capital, governance, and political power makes it a field ripe for continued, rigorous exploration.
