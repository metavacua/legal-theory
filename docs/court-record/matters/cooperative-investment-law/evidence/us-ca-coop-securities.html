A Unified Framework for Analyzing a Cooperative Security

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

A Unified Framework for Analyzing a Cooperative Security

Preamble: This document provides a formal, unified framework for analyzing any proposed cooperative security. It integrates the Supreme Court's primary tests for defining a security (Reves and Howey) with the core operational pillars of Subchapter T. This allows for a robust, step-by-step analysis of whether a cooperative instrument should be treated as a security and, if so, whether its design is compliant and safe for the cooperative.

Part 1: Initial Characterization – What is the Nature of the Instrument?

The first and most critical step is to determine the fundamental nature of the instrument in question. The path of legal analysis depends entirely on this initial characterization.

  • Is it a "Note" or Debt-Like Instrument?

  • Characteristics: Does it involve a loan of money? Is there a promise to repay a principal amount? Is there a stated interest rate (even if it's 0%)? Is the instrument actually called a "note"?

  • If YES, proceed to Part 2, Path A: The Reves Test.

  • Is it an "Investment Contract" or Equity-Like Instrument?

  • Characteristics: Does it represent a stake in the cooperative's assets or profits? Is the return for the holder entirely dependent on the success and profitability of the cooperative's ventures? Does it involve pooling capital with others in a common enterprise?

  • If YES, proceed to Part 2, Path B: The Howey Test.

Part 2: The Threshold Analysis – Is the Instrument a Security?

Based on the characterization above, apply the appropriate Supreme Court test.

Path A: The Reves "Family Resemblance" Test (for Notes)

The Reves test determines if a note bears a "family resemblance" to an investment, thus classifying it as a security.

  1. Motivations of Seller/Buyer: Is the cooperative raising money for general business use (leans toward security) or to facilitate a specific patron's transaction (leans away)? Is the buyer motivated by profit (leans toward security) or acquiring a good/service (leans away)?

  2. Plan of Distribution: Is the note offered on a public market (leans toward security) or is the distribution narrowly limited to patrons for whom it facilitates a transaction (leans away)?

  3. Reasonable Expectations of the Public: Is the instrument marketed as an "investment" (leans toward security) or as "patronage capital" or a "membership deposit" (leans away)?

  4. Presence of an Alternative Regulatory Scheme: Does the framework of cooperative law and Subchapter T itself provide sufficient protection, making securities law unnecessary (leans away from security)?

Path B: The Howey Test (for Investment Contracts)

The Howey test defines an investment contract as a security if these four conditions are met:

  1. An Investment of Value: Has the holder given up something of value (money, assets, labor) to acquire the instrument?

  2. In a Common Enterprise: Is the holder's fortune interwoven with the fortune of the cooperative and other members?

  3. With an Expectation of Benefit/Profit: Does the holder expect to receive a valuable benefit, such as appreciation, dividends, or access to services?

  4. Derived from the Efforts of Others: Does the realization of the benefit depend primarily on the managerial or operational efforts of the cooperative's leadership?

Part 3: The Cooperative Compliance Test – Is it a "Safe" Cooperative Security?

If an instrument is determined to be a security under either Reves or Howey, it must then be tested for its compliance with the core principles of cooperative operation.

  1. Subordination of Capital as Defense of the Commons: Does the security's structure ensure that financial gains are socialized for the benefit of the entire patronage, rather than being privately captured by the instrument holder?

  2. Democratic Member Control: Is the instrument's ownership separated from governance, preserving the "one-member, one-vote" principle?

  3. Patronage-Source Income Integrity (The "Actually Facilitates" Test): Is the security engineered so its function "actually facilitates" the cooperative's core mission, ensuring income generated is defensibly patronage-sourced?

Conclusion: Classifying the Instrument

  • Non-Security Instrument: An instrument that fails to be classified as a security under the appropriate Reves or Howey test.

  • Non-Subchapter-T Security: An instrument classified as a security that fails the Cooperative Compliance Test, creating a conflict with the cooperative's legal and tax status.

  • Subchapter T Security: An instrument that is legally a security but is so deeply integrated with the principles of cooperative compliance that it functions safely within the Subchapter T framework.
