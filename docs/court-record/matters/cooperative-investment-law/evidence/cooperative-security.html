A Unified Framework for Analyzing a Cooperative Security

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

A Unified Framework for Analyzing a Cooperative Security

Preamble: This document provides a formal, unified framework for analyzing any proposed cooperative security, including tokenized versions. It synthesizes the key legal and operational tests discussed—tradability, consumption, federal securities law, California's "risk capital" standard, and the pillars of Subchapter T—into a comprehensive 9-point analytical checklist. This framework can be used to classify any instrument, assess its legal risks, and determine its viability as a "safe" cooperative security.

The 9-Point Analytical Checklist

To be considered a viable and compliant Cooperative Security, an instrument must be analyzed against the following nine criteria, grouped into four main categories.

Category 1: Market & Use Characteristics

1. Is the instrument tradable?

  • Analysis: This is the threshold question for market access. Does the cooperative's legal structure and the instrument's design permit its transfer on a secondary market? If not, it cannot be a publicly-traded security, though it may still be a security offered in a private placement.

2. Is the instrument's primary purpose consumption?

  • Analysis: This addresses the Forman test. Is the "economic reality" of the instrument for personal use and consumption (e.g., living in a house, entering a park)? If yes, it is likely not a security. If the purpose is to hold for a future financial return or benefit, it likely is.

Category 2: The Combined Securities Law Test

This category combines the federal Howey test with the broader California "risk capital" test to determine if the instrument legally qualifies as a security. All four prongs must be satisfied for an instrument to be a security.

3. Is there an investment of value?

  • Analysis: Was value—tangible (money, assets) or intangible (labor, data, a binding promise)—given up to acquire the instrument? This uses the broad contract law standard for consideration.

4. Is there a common enterprise?

  • Analysis: Is the fortune of the instrument's holder interwoven with the fortune of the cooperative enterprise and other members?

5. Is there an expectation of benefit?

  • Analysis: Does the holder expect to receive some valuable benefit? The California "risk capital" test is controlling here; the benefit need not be "profit" but can include access to services, use of facilities, or other valuable rights.

6. Is the benefit derived from the efforts of others?

  • Analysis: Does the realization of the benefit depend primarily on the managerial, operational, or entrepreneurial efforts of the cooperative's leadership and staff, rather than the holder's own actions? A benefit derived from passive holding points toward a security; a benefit derived from active consumption points away from it.

Category 3: The Subchapter T Operational Test

This category determines if the cooperative and the security's structure are compliant with the three pillars of "operating on a cooperative basis," which is essential for maintaining the cooperative's legal identity and favorable tax status.

7. Does the structure ensure the subordination of capital?

  • Analysis: Is capital treated as a servant to the enterprise, not its master? Are returns on capital (dividends) limited? Is the primary purpose of the enterprise to serve patrons, not to maximize returns for capital providers?

8. Does the structure ensure democratic member control?

  • Analysis: Is control of the cooperative vested in the members, typically on a "one-member, one-vote" basis? For any tradable security that confers membership, are its voting rights structured to prevent capital from dominating governance?

9. Does the structure operate on a cost/patronage basis?

  • Analysis: Is the cooperative's primary function to return net margins to its members in proportion to their use (patronage)? Does the security's existence interfere with or support this core function?

Conclusion: This 9-point checklist serves as a comprehensive diagnostic tool. A proposed instrument can be classified based on its answers:

  • A Consumer Membership Interest (non-security) would likely fail at least one prong of the securities test (typically #6).

  • An "Unsafe" Security would pass the securities test (prongs #3-6) but would fail one or more of the Subchapter T operational tests (prongs #7-9), creating an existential risk for the cooperative.

  • A "Safe" Cooperative Security is an instrument that successfully passes all relevant criteria: it is designed to be a security (satisfying #3-6) while its structure is meticulously engineered to uphold all three pillars of cooperative operation required by Subchapter T (satisfying #7-9).
