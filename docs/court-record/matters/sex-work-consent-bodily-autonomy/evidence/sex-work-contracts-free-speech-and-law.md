  

# **Pactum de Sexu: Re-Theorizing Sexual Service Contracts as Protected Economic and Expressive Association**

  
  

## **Section 1: Introduction: From Criminal Act to Commercial Contract**

  

The legal and social discourse surrounding the commercial exchange of sexual services is mired in a fundamental categorical error. The monolithic term "prostitution" is used to encompass a vast spectrum of activities, ranging from coercive exploitation and human trafficking to consensual, autonomous commercial transactions between adults. This terminological imprecision has led to a legal framework, particularly in jurisdictions like California, that relies on broad criminal prohibitions, failing to distinguish between acts that victimize and agreements that are the product of free will. This report posits that this legal paradigm is not only ineffective but also constitutionally suspect. It proposes a necessary re-theorization that bifurcates the concept into two distinct legal phenomena: the "prostitution contract," which is rightly prohibited as a matter of public policy due to elements of coercion, exploitation, or public harm; and the "sexual service contract," a legitimate form of commercial agreement that falls within the ambit of protected economic and expressive activity.

This reframing is not a mere semantic exercise; it is a crucial legal and constitutional undertaking designed to align the regulation of sexual commerce with foundational principles of economic liberty, freedom of contract, and expressive rights under the First Amendment. The current approach, which treats any exchange of compensation for sexual activity as a criminal act, fails to account for the complex realities of modern labor and commerce and overlooks the expressive dimensions inherent in the formation of any contract. To construct a more nuanced and constitutionally sound framework, this report will synthesize doctrines from disparate fields of law.

First, it will employ California's "ABC test" for worker classification not as a determinative rule, but as a powerful analytical lens.1 By examining the structure of regulated sex work, such as that in Nevada, through the criteria of control, course of business, and independent trade, the analysis will demonstrate that autonomous sex workers can be seen as the quintessential independent contractors, reinforcing the argument that their engagements are fundamentally commercial contracts rather than relationships of subordination or exploitation.2

Second, the report will draw upon the constitutional principles articulated in landmark Supreme Court cases. From *Citizens United v. FEC*, it will adapt the logic that the expenditure of money is a form of speech to argue that the offer and acceptance of compensation—the core of contract formation—is a protected act of commercial expression.3 From

*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, it will derive the principle that individuals and the associations they form do not forfeit fundamental rights and liberties when they engage in commerce, applying this to the economic liberty of individuals to form contracts for their services.5

Third, it will ground the analysis in the common law of contracts, specifically the public policy doctrine. This doctrine, which voids agreements that are injurious to the public good, will be presented not as a justification for a blanket ban, but as a precise legal tool for delineating the boundaries of impermissible conduct.7 By identifying the specific, compelling state interests at stake—such as preventing trafficking, protecting minors, and ensuring public health—the doctrine can be used to invalidate genuinely harmful "prostitution contracts" while leaving space for the legal recognition of consensual "sexual service contracts."

To illustrate these theoretical arguments, this report will use the starkly contrasting legal regimes of California and Nevada as a central case study. California's blanket prohibition under its Penal Code represents the traditional model of criminalization.9 Nevada's highly regulated, county-option system of licensed brothels, conversely, will be presented not as "legalized prostitution," but as a nascent, if imperfect, model for the state's recognition and formalization of the sexual service contract.11 This comparative analysis will illuminate the practical and theoretical failings of the former and the potential of the latter. Ultimately, this report will argue for a new jurisprudence of commercial intimacy—one that moves beyond moral condemnation and toward a framework of legal and economic rights, recognizing that the liberty to form a contract is a fundamental expressive act that should only be abridged when necessitated by a compelling and narrowly tailored public policy interest.

  

## **Section 2: The Dichotomy of Regulation: California's Prohibition vs. Nevada's Formalization**

  

The legal treatment of commercial sexual services in the United States is not monolithic; rather, it is characterized by a profound dichotomy, exemplified by the legal frameworks of California and Nevada. California represents the prevailing approach of absolute prohibition, where the law identifies the act of exchanging compensation for sex as a criminal offense, rendering any associated agreement void *ab initio*. Nevada, in stark contrast, has created a regulatory apparatus that, within specific and controlled circumstances, removes the taint of illegality and gives legal form to what can be understood as a new class of commercial agreement: the sexual service contract. Analyzing these two systems in parallel reveals that they are not merely different in degree, but different in kind. One is a system of criminal law aimed at suppressing an act; the other is a system of administrative and contract law aimed at regulating a service.

  

### **2.1. The Anatomy of a Prostitution Contract: An Unenforceable Agreement**

  

In California, the legal framework is designed to ensure that any agreement for the exchange of sexual services for compensation is legally nonexistent and unenforceable. The cornerstone of this regime is California Penal Code § 647(b), which defines the crime of prostitution broadly to include not only the commission of a sexual act for compensation but also the acts of soliciting or agreeing to such an act.10 This is a critical feature of the law: the crime is completed at the moment of contract formation. The verbal or non-verbal exchange that constitutes an offer and acceptance is, in itself, the prohibited conduct. The law makes no distinction between a coercive arrangement and a consensual one; the presence of "money, goods or services" as consideration for a "lewd act" or sexual intercourse is the sole determinant of criminality.10

This statutory construction has profound implications under contract law. The common law doctrine of legality, a foundational principle of contract jurisprudence, asserts that agreements which breach statutory laws or violate public policy are inherently unenforceable.7 Courts will not lend their authority to enforce contracts that are founded on illegal acts, as doing so would undermine the integrity of the legal system and condone conduct that society, through its laws, has deemed harmful.8 A contract to commit a crime is the archetypal example of an agreement that is void against public policy.17

Because California Penal Code § 647(b) criminalizes the very formation of the agreement, a "prostitution contract" in California is void from its inception. It is not merely voidable at the option of one party; it is a legal nullity. Neither party can seek legal recourse for a breach. For example, a client who pays but does not receive the agreed-upon service cannot sue for restitution, nor can a service provider who performs but is not paid sue for the fee. The parties are considered to be *in pari delicto*—in equal fault—and the courts will leave them where they are found.18

The public policy undergirding this prohibition is broad and historically rooted in concepts of public morality and decency.19 The state's interest is not framed in the narrow, targeted terms of preventing specific harms like trafficking or disease, but as a wholesale rejection of the commercialization of sex as an act that is injurious to societal welfare and moral standards.7 This approach treats the agreement itself as the social harm to be eradicated. Consequently, the California model does not regulate sexual commerce; it attempts to eliminate it by rendering the foundational contractual element illegal, thereby creating a legal vacuum in which no rights, obligations, or protections can exist for the parties involved.

  

### **2.2. The Architecture of a Sexual Service Contract: A Regulated Agreement**

  

In stark contrast to California's prohibitory model, Nevada has constructed a legal architecture that does not merely create an exception to prostitution laws but establishes an entirely different legal category: the regulated sexual service contract. This system operates by decriminalizing the act of exchanging sex for compensation under a strict set of conditions, thereby allowing for the formation of valid, albeit heavily regulated, commercial agreements. The Nevada framework is not an endorsement of "prostitution" as it is commonly understood; rather, it is a legislative recognition that, under certain controls, a contract for sexual services can be brought within the bounds of public policy.

The legality of these contracts is geographically and structurally circumscribed. Prostitution is only legal in licensed brothels, and these brothels are only permitted in counties with populations below a certain threshold (currently 700,000 residents), which excludes major urban centers like Las Vegas (Clark County) and Reno (Washoe County).11 This creates a "safe harbor" where the formation of a sexual service contract is not a criminal act and is therefore not automatically void against public policy.

Within this safe harbor, the state of Nevada imposes a comprehensive set of non-negotiable terms that are implicitly incorporated into every sexual service contract. These state-mandated terms function as a form of consumer and worker protection and public health regulation. Key provisions include:

  - **Health and Safety Mandates:** Workers must undergo weekly testing for gonorrhea and chlamydia and monthly testing for HIV and syphilis.11 Condom use is mandatory for most sexual acts.11 A positive test for certain infectious diseases requires the immediate cessation of work.22
  - **Licensing and Registration:** Both the brothel establishment and the individual sex worker must be licensed and registered with the county sheriff, which includes age verification to ensure all workers are adults (minimum age varies by county from 18 to 21).2
  - **Operational and Advertising Restrictions:** Brothels are subject to strict zoning laws, prohibiting them from operating near schools or places of worship or on principal streets.12 Furthermore, advertising is heavily restricted, particularly in counties where prostitution is illegal.12

This extensive regulatory scheme fundamentally transforms the nature of the transaction. It is no longer an illicit, hidden act but a formal, state-sanctioned commercial service. The state's role shifts from that of a punitive enforcer to a regulatory overseer. This legal distinction is analogous to the one between illegal prostitution and legal escort services, where the crucial difference lies in the intent and nature of the agreement; an escort contract is for companionship, while a prostitution contract is for sex.23 Nevada's framework effectively creates a new category—a contract for sexual services where the terms are predefined by the state to mitigate public policy concerns. By doing so, the state implicitly declares that a contract for sexual services is not

*inherently* contrary to public policy, but that an *unregulated* one is. The regulation itself is what redefines the act, moving it from the realm of criminal law into the domain of contract and administrative law.

The following table starkly illustrates the fundamental differences between the two legal constructs.

Instead, it has created the legal conditions for an entirely different type of transaction to exist: a formal, regulated, and enforceable sexual service contract, distinct in its legal character and public policy implications from the void and illegal prostitution contract.

  

## **Section 3: The Independent Contractor as a Paradigm for the Autonomous Service Provider**

  

The legal classification of a worker as either an employee or an independent contractor is a critical determinant of rights, responsibilities, and the fundamental nature of the work relationship. In the context of sexual services, this classification is paramount. It provides a powerful analytical framework for distinguishing between a subordinate, potentially exploitative labor relationship and an autonomous, business-to-business contractual engagement. By applying California's stringent ABC test—the modern legal standard for defining economic independence—to the structure of regulated sex work in Nevada, it becomes clear that these workers exemplify the ideal of the independent contractor. This classification reinforces the central thesis that their work is grounded in contract, not employment, and situates the discussion within the broader national debate over the future of work in the gig economy.

  

### **3.1. The ABC Test as an Analytical Lens**

  

The legal landscape of worker classification was fundamentally altered by the California Supreme Court's 2018 decision in *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court* and the subsequent legislative codification of its holding in Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5).28 This framework established the "ABC test" as the presumptive standard for determining worker status under California's wage and hour laws.31 The test is notable for its simplicity and its strong presumption of employee status; the burden falls entirely on the hiring entity to prove that a worker is an independent contractor by satisfying

*all three* of the following conditions:

  - **(A) The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity** in connection with the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact.
  - **(B) The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.**
  - **(C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business** of the same nature as the work performed. 1

If the hiring entity fails to establish even one of these prongs, the worker is legally considered an employee and is entitled to the full suite of protections that status confers, including minimum wage, overtime, workers' compensation, and unemployment insurance.27 The test was designed to be more predictable and worker-protective than the previous, more flexible multi-factor

*Borello* test, which had been the standard in California since 1989.28 AB 5's passage was highly consequential, sending ripples through numerous industries, most notably the gig economy, and prompting significant legal and political challenges.30 While the ABC test is a California standard, its clear, rigorous structure makes it an ideal analytical tool for assessing the degree of economic autonomy in any work relationship, regardless of jurisdiction.

  

### **3.2. Applying the ABC Test to Nevada's Regulated Sex Workers**

  

Nevada law and the operational model of its legal brothels classify sex workers as independent contractors, a status that has been the subject of some legal and administrative debate but remains the prevailing classification.2 Applying the rigorous criteria of the ABC test to this relationship illuminates the structural autonomy inherent in the role and strongly supports the independent contractor classification.

**Prong A: Freedom from Control and Direction.** This prong requires that the worker, not the hiring entity, controls the manner and means by which the work is performed.1 In the context of a Nevada brothel, while the establishment exercises significant control over the

*environment* to comply with state law, it exerts minimal control over the *performance* of the service itself. The brothel ensures security, manages client flow, and enforces state-mandated health regulations.12 However, the individual worker typically retains ultimate discretion over critical aspects of the work: which clients to accept or refuse, what specific services to offer, the price of those services (often negotiated directly with the client from a base rate), and the precise manner in which the services are performed.2 This is fundamentally different from the level of control a strip club might exert over a dancer's costume, music selection, or stage routine—factors that have been used to argue for employee status under AB 5 in California.27

A crucial distinction must be made regarding the state-mandated health and safety regulations. While the brothel is the entity that enforces rules like mandatory condom use and weekly health screenings, this control originates not from the brothel's business discretion but from the state's police power. The brothel acts as an agent of state compliance, enforcing regulations that apply to both the worker and the establishment as a condition of legality. This is distinct from an employer imposing rules to direct the work for its own commercial benefit. The worker's core autonomy in the performance of the service—the personal interaction with the client—remains intact. Therefore, these state-mandated controls should be viewed as part of the legal framework that *enables* the independent contract, rather than as evidence of an employment relationship that would negate it.

**Prong B: Work Outside the Usual Course of Business.** This prong is often the most difficult for businesses to satisfy, as it requires that the service provided by the worker is not central to the hiring entity's core business offering.1 A retail store hiring a plumber satisfies this prong easily; a clothing company hiring seamstresses does not.1 At first glance, it appears a brothel would fail this test, as its business is seemingly the provision of sexual services. However, a more nuanced analysis suggests otherwise. The brothel's core business can be defined as providing a safe, legal, and regulated

*venue* for independent service providers to meet clients. The brothel markets the facility, provides security, ensures legal and health compliance, and processes financial transactions. In this model, the brothel is a platform or intermediary, much like a hair salon that rents chairs to independent stylists or an art gallery that provides space for artists to sell their work.28 The brothel sells access, safety, and legality; the worker, as an independent entrepreneur, sells a personal, intimate service. The brothel's business is facility management and regulatory compliance, while the worker's business is the provision of sexual services. The two are related but not identical, arguably placing the worker's service "outside the usual course" of the brothel's primary business of venue operation.

**Prong C: Customarily Engaged in an Independent Trade.** This prong requires that the worker has an independently established business that exists apart from their relationship with the hiring entity.1 Sex workers in Nevada's legal brothels clearly meet this standard. They are required to register with the county and obtain a work card, which is a form of professional licensure.2 Their business is their personal service, which is portable and not dependent on any single brothel. Many workers move between different legal brothels over the course of their careers, demonstrating that their trade is independent of any one establishment. The critical question, as articulated in one analysis of the ABC test, is what would happen if the relationship ended: if being fired would cause the worker to "join the ranks of the unemployed," they are likely an employee. If they could continue their business by contracting with other entities, they are likely an independent contractor.40 A Nevada sex worker who leaves one brothel is not unemployed; she is an independent business owner seeking a new venue in which to contract her services.

  

### **3.3. Parallels with the Gig Economy**

  

The legal and economic debates surrounding sex worker classification are not occurring in a vacuum. They are a microcosm of the larger societal struggle to adapt 20th-century labor laws to the realities of the 21st-century "gig economy".30 Platform companies like Uber, Lyft, and DoorDash have built their business models on the classification of their workers as independent contractors, arguing that they are merely technology platforms connecting independent service providers with customers.30 This argument has been fiercely contested, with critics asserting that it is a smokescreen for misclassification designed to avoid the costs and responsibilities of employment, such as paying minimum wage, providing benefits, and contributing to social insurance programs.30

The legal brothel model can be viewed as an analog precursor to the digital platform economy. The brothel functions as a physical platform that aggregates providers, vets clients (to a degree), provides a safe space for the transaction, and processes payments. The arguments for classifying Nevada's sex workers as independent contractors—autonomy over the work, the brothel as an intermediary, the portability of the worker's trade—are strikingly similar to the arguments made by gig economy companies.

Situating the analysis of sex work within this broader context serves two purposes. First, it destigmatizes the discussion by framing it as a legitimate labor and contract issue, rather than a unique moral one. Second, it reinforces the contractual nature of the relationship. If the legal system is grappling with how to recognize and regulate the contractual relationships facilitated by digital platforms, it should be equally capable of analyzing the contractual relationships facilitated by physical platforms like brothels. The classification of Nevada's sex workers as independent contractors is not an anomaly but an early example of the type of autonomous, contract-based work that is becoming increasingly prevalent across the entire economy. This classification is the lynchpin that supports the conceptual shift from viewing the transaction as a criminal act to seeing it as a commercial contract between two independent economic actors.

  

## **Section 4: Constitutional Underpinnings of Economic Expression**

  

The argument for re-theorizing sexual service contracts is not merely one of labor or contract law; it is deeply rooted in constitutional principles of free expression and economic liberty. The Supreme Court's jurisprudence in recent decades, particularly in *Citizens United v. FEC* and *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, has expanded the scope of First Amendment and statutory protections for commercial actors. While these cases dealt with political speech and religious freedom, respectively, the logic undergirding them provides a powerful constitutional foundation for recognizing the expressive dimensions of forming a commercial contract. The principles they establish—that economic activity can be expressive, and that individuals do not forfeit fundamental rights when they engage in commerce—are directly applicable to the consensual exchange of sexual services for compensation.

  

### **4.1.** ***Citizens United*** **and the Expressive Nature of Compensation**

  

The Supreme Court's 2010 decision in *Citizens United v. FEC* is best known for holding that corporate independent expenditures in political campaigns are a form of protected speech under the First Amendment.45 The Court struck down provisions of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act that prohibited corporations and unions from using their general treasury funds for "electioneering communications".45 The core of the Court's reasoning was twofold: first, that the expenditure of money is a necessary predicate for much speech and is therefore itself a form of expressive activity; and second, that the First Amendment protects the speech itself, regardless of the identity of the speaker.3 As Justice Kennedy wrote for the majority, "the Government may not suppress political speech on the basis of the speaker's corporate identity".4

While *Citizens United* concerned political speech, which receives the highest level of constitutional protection, its foundational logic has profound implications for commercial speech. The Court affirmed that spending money is an essential mechanism for expression, "necessarily reduc\[ing\] the quantity of expression by restricting the number of issues discussed, the depth of their exploration, and the size of the audience reached" when limited.4 If the act of spending money to fund a political advertisement is protected speech, it follows that the act of offering or accepting money to form a commercial contract is also a form of expression.

The offer of compensation is the quintessential example of "speech that proposes a commercial transaction"—the very definition of commercial speech that the Supreme Court has afforded First Amendment protection since the 1970s.48 When one party offers payment and another accepts it, they are engaging in a communicative act that expresses their mutual intent to create a binding legal relationship. This exchange is not merely conduct; it is speech that has a performative effect.

The second key principle from *Citizens United*—that the identity of the speaker is irrelevant to the protection of the speech—can be powerfully redeployed in this context. Prostitution statutes like California's Penal Code § 647(b) function by suppressing commercial speech entirely based on the identity and intent of the speakers (a "prostitute" and a "john").10 The law criminalizes the offer and acceptance, silencing the communicative act that would form the contract.14 Applying the logic of

*Citizens United*, the state's justification for this suppression cannot rest merely on a societal disapproval of the speakers or the subject matter of their proposed transaction. It shifts the analytical focus away from the moral status of the participants and toward the legitimacy of the state's interest in banning the commercial expression itself. This creates a much higher constitutional bar for the state to clear, requiring it to demonstrate a compelling interest in prohibiting the speech act of contract formation, not just the subsequent conduct.

  

### **4.2.** ***Hobby Lobby*** **and the Rights of Commercial Actors**

  

The 2014 decision in *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc.* further solidified the principle that fundamental rights extend into the commercial sphere. In that case, the Supreme Court held that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA) required the government to provide an exemption from the Affordable Care Act's contraceptive mandate for closely held for-profit corporations whose owners had sincere religious objections.5 The Court's reasoning was that corporations are legal "persons" under RFRA and are comprised of individuals who use them to achieve desired ends.5 Therefore, protecting the corporation's free exercise of religion was a means of protecting the rights of the individuals who owned and operated it.55

The central takeaway from *Hobby Lobby* is that engaging in for-profit commerce is not a disqualifying condition for the exercise of fundamental rights. The Court rejected the government's argument that the profit-making purpose of a corporation rendered it incapable of exercising religion.5 By analogy, an individual who chooses to engage in the commercial provision of sexual services as an independent contractor or sole proprietor should not, by virtue of that commercial activity, be stripped of their fundamental economic liberties, including the liberty to contract.

The doctrine of corporate personhood, which long predates these recent cases, has historically been a vehicle for securing essential economic rights for business entities.57 Since the 19th century, this legal concept has enabled corporations to own property, sue and be sued, and, most importantly, enter into contracts as legal persons.57 This was never about granting corporations human consciousness, but about creating a legal entity through which individuals could efficiently exercise their own economic rights and liberties.60

When an independent sex worker forms a contract for her services, she is acting as a commercial entity (a sole proprietor). The *Hobby Lobby* principle suggests that her status as a commercial actor does not diminish her claim to fundamental rights, including the economic right to sell her labor and the expressive right to form a contract to that effect. Just as the Green family used their corporation as a vehicle for their religious expression, an autonomous sex worker uses her business as a vehicle for her economic self-determination. To deny her the right to contract based solely on the nature of the service offered is to penalize her for engaging in commerce, a position that runs counter to the logic of *Hobby Lobby* and the broader history of corporate and commercial law in the United States. Together, *Citizens United* and *Hobby Lobby* provide a robust constitutional framework for viewing the formation of a sexual service contract not as an illicit act, but as an exercise of protected expressive and economic rights by a commercial actor.

  

## **Section 5: The Speech Act of Contract Formation**

  

At the heart of this report's re-theorization of sexual services is a novel but legally coherent proposition: the act of forming a contract is a form of protected speech, legally and conceptually distinct from the act of performing the services stipulated in that contract. Prostitution statutes have historically conflated these two elements, creating a legal tautology where the agreement is illegal because the act is commercialized, and the commercial act is illegal because of the agreement. By disaggregating these components and analyzing the formation of the contract as an independent "speech act" or a form of "expressive conduct," it is possible to break this circular reasoning and subject the state's prohibition to rigorous First Amendment scrutiny. This approach allows for the regulation of the conduct (the service) to address legitimate public policy concerns without necessitating a complete ban on the speech (the contract).

  

### **5.1. The Liberty of Contract as a First Amendment Right**

  

The concept of "liberty of contract" is most famously associated with the *Lochner* era of the early 20th century, where the Supreme Court used the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to strike down economic regulations.62 While that substantive due process framework has since been abandoned, the underlying principle of contractual freedom can be re-grounded in the more solid footing of the First Amendment's Free Speech Clause.63

The formation of a contract is, fundamentally, a communicative process. It consists of an exchange of promises—an offer and an acceptance—that uses language to create a new legal reality and a set of binding obligations.62 This is not merely incidental conduct; it is a "speech act" in the philosophical sense, where the utterance of words performs an action. The First Amendment protects not only the dissemination of ideas but also "expressive conduct" or "symbolic speech"—actions designed to convey a message.64 The act of entering into a contract is a form of expressive association, where two or more parties join to pursue a shared economic purpose, a right closely related to the freedoms of speech and assembly.68

While no court has explicitly recognized a First Amendment right to contract formation *per se*, the Supreme Court's commercial speech jurisprudence provides a strong foundation. The Court has protected the right "to advertise commercial products and professional services" because of the informational value to the consumer and society.50 If the advertisement

*proposing* a transaction is protected speech, then the direct communication that *creates* the transaction—the offer and acceptance—must logically fall within the ambit of First Amendment protection as well. It is the culmination of the communicative process that commercial speech doctrine was designed to protect.

  

### **5.2. Disaggregating the Expression: The Contract vs. The Service**

  

The most significant analytical move in this framework is the legal separation of two distinct events: (1) the expressive act of forming the contract, and (2) the subsequent conduct of performing the service. Current prostitution laws fail to make this distinction. They operate on a tautological basis: the sexual act is illegal *because* it involves compensation, and the agreement to provide compensation is illegal *because* it is for a prohibited sexual act. This circular logic prevents any meaningful First Amendment analysis.

By disaggregating the two, a clearer legal picture emerges. The formation of the contract—the offer, negotiation, and acceptance of terms—is a set of speech acts that fall under the category of commercial speech. The performance of the contract—the provision of the sexual service—is a form of conduct. The state has significantly more leeway to regulate conduct than it does to regulate speech.66 The government can impose time, place, and manner restrictions on conduct, and can regulate it to advance substantial government interests, as it does with countless other professional services.

The primary exception to the protection of commercial speech is for speech that proposes an illegal transaction.64 However, this doctrine is typically applied to conduct that is illegal

*independent* of its commercial nature. For example, a contract to commit murder is illegal because murder is illegal, regardless of whether money is exchanged. A contract to sell cocaine is illegal because the possession and sale of cocaine are illegal in themselves. In the case of prostitution, the act of consensual sex between adults is not illegal. It is the *commercial element*—the contract—that renders the entire transaction criminal.10

This distinction is crucial. By separating the speech from the conduct, we force the state to justify why the commercial nature of the act is the specific harm it seeks to prevent. The state cannot simply rely on the illegality of the underlying act, because the act is only illegal due to the commercial speech that creates the contract. This breaks the tautology and requires the state to provide a compelling justification for banning the commercial speech itself, a much higher constitutional hurdle. It allows for a regulatory regime where the state can heavily regulate the *conduct* (e.g., mandating health checks, zoning, age limits as in Nevada) without imposing a complete prior restraint on the *speech* that forms the contractual basis for that conduct.

  

### **5.3. The Offer as Expressive Conduct**

  

The specific act of offering or soliciting a commercial sexual service can be analyzed under the Supreme Court's two-part test for expressive conduct, established in *Spence v. Washington*.65 For conduct to be considered expressive speech, it must meet two criteria: (1) there must be an intent to convey a particularized message, and (2) there must be a great likelihood that the message will be understood by those who view it.

An offer to provide a sexual service for a fee unequivocally meets both prongs of the *Spence* test:

1.  **Intent to Convey a Particularized Message:** The speaker's intent is not ambiguous. They intend to communicate a precise commercial proposal: a willingness to enter into a contract to perform specific services in exchange for specific compensation under a given set of terms. The message is highly particularized and transactional.
2.  **Likelihood of Understanding:** The likelihood that the recipient of this offer will understand its meaning is virtually 100%. The transactional nature of the communication is its defining characteristic and is universally understood within the cultural context.

Because the act of making a commercial offer for a sexual service meets the *Spence* test, it constitutes expressive conduct. As such, it is a form of speech protected by the First Amendment. This does not mean it is immune from regulation. As commercial speech, it is subject to a lower level of protection than political speech and can be regulated to prevent deception or to advance a substantial government interest.69 However, it cannot be banned outright without a compelling justification that is narrowly tailored to that interest. The analysis of the offer as expressive conduct provides a firm constitutional basis for challenging the facial validity of statutes that criminalize the mere act of solicitation, forcing the state to defend its prohibition under the standards of First Amendment jurisprudence rather than relying on broad appeals to public morality.

  

## **Section 6: Delineating the Impermissible: Public Policy as the Boundary of Legality**

  

The freedom of contract, whether grounded in due process or the First Amendment, has never been absolute. The common law has long recognized that private agreements must yield to the public good. The doctrine of public policy serves as the primary judicial mechanism for this limitation, empowering courts to refuse enforcement of contracts that are deemed injurious to the welfare of society.7 However, this doctrine should not be a blunt instrument for enforcing a particular moral code. In a modern legal system, it must function as a scalpel, precisely targeting specific, demonstrable harms that constitute the state's compelling interests. A blanket prohibition on all sexual service contracts represents an overly broad and archaic application of this doctrine. A properly calibrated legal framework, exemplified by Nevada's regulatory model, uses public policy not to prohibit an entire category of commerce, but to define the conditions under which that commerce may be conducted legally and safely, thereby representing a "least restrictive means" of achieving legitimate state goals.

  

### **6.1. The Public Policy Doctrine as a Limitation on Contractual Freedom**

  

The legality doctrine in contract law stipulates that an agreement is void and unenforceable if its object or performance is contrary to law or public policy.7 Public policy itself is an amorphous concept, generally understood to encompass the collective moral, social, and economic standards of a community.20 Courts invoke this doctrine to invalidate a wide range of agreements, including contracts to commit a crime or tort, agreements in unreasonable restraint of trade, contracts that promote litigation, and those that are grossly unfair or unconscionable.16

Historically, the doctrine was applied broadly, with courts invalidating contracts contrary to "the rules and claims of decency and the dictates of morality".19 However, modern jurisprudence has grown more cautious, recognizing that an expansive use of the doctrine risks judicial overreach and undermines the stability and predictability that are central to contract law. Contemporary courts are more likely to require that an agreement "expressly and unquestionably contravene public policy" and be "injurious, beyond all doubt, to the interests of the State" before declaring it void.19 There is a clear trend toward grounding public policy defenses in specific statutes or well-established legal principles rather than free-floating notions of morality.75 A blanket ban on all consensual commercial sex between adults relies on the older, more expansive, morality-based conception of public policy. A more modern and defensible approach requires identifying the specific, articulable harms that such a ban is meant to prevent.

  

### **6.2. Identifying the State's Compelling Interests in Regulating Sexual Commerce**

  

When the public policy doctrine is used to justify the infringement of a fundamental right, such as the freedom of expression inherent in contract formation, the state must demonstrate that its policy serves a compelling governmental interest. A vague appeal to public morality is insufficient. Instead, the state's interest must be concrete and focused on preventing specific, tangible harms. In the context of sexual commerce, these compelling interests are well-documented and include:

  - **Combating Human Trafficking and Coercion:** The most significant state interest is the prevention of modern-day slavery and the exploitation of individuals forced into the sex trade against their will.14
  - **Protecting Minors:** The state has an undeniable and paramount interest in preventing the sexual exploitation of children.14
  - **Ensuring Public Health:** The state has a compelling interest in controlling the spread of sexually transmitted infections (STIs) and other communicable diseases.12
  - **Preventing Public Nuisance and Disorder:** The state has a legitimate interest in regulating commercial activities to prevent them from disrupting public order, creating unsafe neighborhoods, or infringing on the rights of other citizens.

These specific harms—not the act of consensual sex for money itself—are the proper subjects of the state's public policy concerns. Any contract that involves trafficking, minors, or an intentional disregard for public health would be unequivocally void against public policy, and the associated conduct could and should be severely criminalized. However, a blanket prohibition on *all* sexual service contracts, including those between consenting, autonomous adults that do not involve these harms, is an overly broad means of addressing these interests. It is a legislative sledgehammer used where a surgical scalpel is required.

  

### **6.3. The Nevada Model as a "Least Restrictive Means"**

  

When a government regulation infringes upon a constitutionally protected right, it must not only serve a compelling interest but must also be narrowly tailored to achieve that interest. This principle, often referred to as the "least restrictive means" test, is central to strict scrutiny analysis in First Amendment cases and is also a key component of the RFRA analysis used in *Hobby Lobby*.5 A total ban on sexual service contracts, as seen in California, fails this test because a less restrictive alternative exists that achieves the state's compelling interests more effectively. That alternative is a comprehensive regulatory framework, as modeled by Nevada.

Nevada's system is a highly tailored solution that directly addresses each of the state's compelling interests:

  - To combat **trafficking and coercion**, the system requires the licensing of establishments and the registration of workers, creating a paper trail and a point of contact with law enforcement that helps ensure voluntariness.2
  - To **protect minors**, strict age verification (minimum 18 or 21) is a non-negotiable condition of licensure and registration.12
  - To protect **public health**, the state mandates frequent STI testing and compulsory condom use, directly mitigating the risk of disease transmission in a way that prohibition cannot.11
  - To prevent **public nuisance**, strict zoning regulations control the location of brothels, keeping them away from residential areas, schools, and places of worship.12

This regulatory approach allows the state to use the public policy doctrine with precision. It does not declare the entire category of sexual service contracts void. Instead, it uses specific statutes and administrative rules to define the boundaries of legality. A contract formed *outside* this regulatory framework (e.g., on the street, involving a minor, or at an unlicensed location) remains void against public policy and criminal. A contract formed *within* the framework is legally cognizable.

This model demonstrates that the state can achieve its public policy goals far more effectively through regulation than through prohibition. A ban drives the industry underground, severing contact with health and law enforcement officials and exacerbating the very dangers of disease, exploitation, and violence that the state seeks to prevent. Regulation, by contrast, brings the activity into the open, where it can be monitored, taxed, and controlled. Therefore, a regulatory system like Nevada's represents a less restrictive and more effective means of furthering the state's compelling interests, making a total ban on the underlying economic and expressive activity of contract formation constitutionally suspect.

  

## **Section 7: Conclusion: Toward a New Jurisprudence of Commercial Intimacy**

  

The prevailing legal framework governing commercial sexual services in most of the United States is predicated on a categorical failure to distinguish between exploitation and enterprise. By grouping all exchanges of sex for compensation under the single, pejorative heading of "prostitution," the law employs the blunt instrument of criminalization to address a complex social and economic phenomenon, with demonstrably poor results. This report has constructed a multi-layered legal and constitutional argument for a fundamental paradigm shift. This new framework moves beyond the moralistic and prohibitory status quo toward a jurisprudence that recognizes the legitimacy of consensual "sexual service contracts" while retaining robust legal tools to combat the true harms of exploitation and coercion.

The argument is built upon a synthesis of distinct but complementary legal doctrines. First, by bifurcating the monolithic concept of prostitution, we can legally separate harmful, coercive "prostitution contracts"—which are rightly void against public policy—from consensual, commercial "sexual service contracts." The comparative analysis of California's prohibitory regime and Nevada's regulatory system illustrates this distinction in practice. California's law criminalizes the agreement itself, creating a legal nullity. Nevada's framework, conversely, provides the legal architecture for a valid, state-sanctioned commercial agreement, demonstrating that regulation can redefine a transaction's legal character.

Second, by applying the analytical lens of modern labor law, specifically California's ABC test, this report has shown that autonomous sex workers in a regulated environment function as quintessential independent contractors. This classification is crucial, as it reinforces the contractual, business-to-business nature of their engagements and aligns the analysis of their work with the broader legal challenges of the gig economy. It frames the worker as an autonomous economic actor, not a subordinate employee or a passive victim.

Third, and most critically, this report has grounded the right to form a sexual service contract in the First Amendment. Drawing on the principles of *Citizens United* and *Hobby Lobby*, it argues that the offer and acceptance of compensation is a form of protected commercial speech and that individuals do not forfeit their fundamental economic liberties when they act in a commercial capacity. The act of contract formation is an independent expressive act, a "speech act" that is legally distinct from the subsequent performance of the service. Prohibiting this speech act based on the identity of the speakers or the subject matter of their transaction requires the state to meet the high standards of First Amendment scrutiny.

Finally, the public policy doctrine, when applied with precision, serves not as a justification for a blanket ban but as the proper mechanism for delineating the boundaries of legality. The state's compelling interests—preventing trafficking, protecting minors, and ensuring public health—are the legitimate basis for regulation. A comprehensive regulatory scheme, as modeled by Nevada, is a "least restrictive means" of achieving these interests, directly addressing specific harms in a way that total prohibition cannot. This targeted approach is more effective as a matter of policy and more sound as a matter of constitutional law.

In conclusion, a constitutionally and commercially coherent legal framework can and should distinguish between prohibited prostitution and regulated sexual service contracts. This report provides the theoretical foundation for such a distinction. It recommends that policymakers and legal scholars move beyond the failed paradigm of criminalization and consider a contractual and regulatory framework as a more nuanced, effective, and constitutionally defensible alternative. Such a shift would allow the state to focus its resources on combating the real and devastating harms of sexual exploitation and trafficking, while respecting the principles of economic liberty, freedom of contract, and expressive association for consensual commercial activity among adults. It reframes the debate from one of intractable moral conflict to one of sound and sophisticated regulatory policy.
