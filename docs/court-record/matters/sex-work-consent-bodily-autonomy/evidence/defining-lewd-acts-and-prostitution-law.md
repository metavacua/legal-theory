# **Re-Characterizing Prostitution: A Legal Analysis of Lewd Acts, Sexual Service Contracts, and Illicit Labor in California**

  
  

## **Section 1: The Statutory and Judicial Contours of Prostitution in California**

  

To construct a nuanced understanding of prostitution, it is imperative to move beyond generalized moral or social definitions and engage in a forensic analysis of its statutory and judicial framework. Under California law, prostitution is not a broad prohibition on compensated sexual activity but a specific, narrowly defined misdemeanor. Its legal architecture, encompassing the core offense, the technical definition of a "lewd act," a penumbra of related business-oriented felonies, and a recent evolution in legislative philosophy, reveals a legal construct focused more on public order and the regulation of illicit commerce than on the policing of private morality. Deconstructing this framework reveals that the offense is rooted in a specific transactional moment, providing a foundation for re-characterizing it through the lenses of contract, expression, and labor law.

  

### **1.1 The Core Offense: A Forensic Analysis of Penal Code § 647(b)**

  

The statutory heart of California's prostitution law is Penal Code § 647, which defines various forms of "disorderly conduct." Within this statute, subdivision (b) specifically addresses prostitution. A close reading of the text reveals that it criminalizes not a single act, but a transactional process involving three distinct forms of conduct: soliciting an act of prostitution, agreeing to engage in an act of prostitution, or actually engaging in an act of prostitution.1 The law is explicitly bilateral, applying with equal force to the person offering the sexual service and the customer, often referred to as a "john," who solicits it.4

For a prosecutor to secure a conviction under PC § 647(b), a series of specific elements must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt. First, the defendant must have possessed the *specific intent* to engage in an act of prostitution.6 This is a critical element, as it distinguishes criminal conduct from ambiguous social interactions, jokes, or misunderstandings. An individual who seeks a sexual encounter without the intent to exchange compensation, or who does not know the other party is a prostitute, lacks the requisite criminal state of mind.4 Second, there must be an

*agreement* or an offer to perform a sexual act for compensation.6 This agreement need not be a formal, written contract; it can be, and almost always is, communicated through conversation and conduct from which an agreement can be inferred.6

Crucially, for the crime of "agreeing to engage" in prostitution, the law requires a third element: an *overt act* in furtherance of the commission of the act of prostitution.1 This overt act must be something more than the mere verbal manifestation of acceptance.1 While the statute does not enumerate specific overt acts, courts have recognized actions such as driving to an agreed-upon location, withdrawing money from an ATM for the purpose of the transaction, or beginning to disrobe as sufficient.4 This requirement is legally significant because it establishes a clear line between criminal conduct and mere discussion or fantasy. The law does not punish a private thought or a conversation that goes no further; it punishes the initiation of a commercial transaction that has been deemed illegal.

This statutory focus on the transactional moment—the solicitation, the agreement, and the overt act of furtherance—is a foundational point. The legal harm is not situated exclusively in the physical act of sex itself, but in the formation and advancement of an illicit commercial arrangement. This transactional emphasis provides a critical bridge to the subsequent analysis of prostitution as a form of illicit contract, shifting the legal inquiry from the bedroom to the marketplace. The classification of the offense as a misdemeanor, albeit a "priorable" one with mandatory minimum jail sentences for repeat offenses (45 days for a second offense, 90 for a third), further situates it within the realm of public order offenses rather than grave moral crimes.4

  

### **1.2 Defining the "Lewd Act": The Nexus of Conduct, Intent, and Compensation**

  

The statutory definition of prostitution in California hinges on the concept of a "lewd act." PC § 647(b) states that "prostitution includes any lewd act between persons for money or other consideration".1 This term is not left to subjective interpretation but has been given a precise legal definition through judicial precedent. A "lewd act" for the purposes of prostitution is the physical touching of the genitals, buttocks, or female breast of either the prostitute or the customer with some part of the other person's body.4

This definition is further qualified by two critical elements. First, the touching must be done for a specific purpose: the sexual arousal or gratification of either person.5 This intent requirement distinguishes a "lewd act" from incidental or accidental contact. Second, the act must be performed in exchange for "money or other consideration," which can include anything of value, such as drugs.7 This element of compensation is what separates the crime of prostitution from other offenses involving lewd acts. For instance, the crime of "lewd conduct in public" under PC § 647(a) requires that the act occur in a public place where another person who might be offended is present, but it does not require compensation.11

California case law has added another layer of specificity. In *Wooten v. Superior Court*, the court held that the definition of prostitution requires actual physical contact *between* the prostitute and the customer.16 This means that a performance, such as a striptease or a live sex show where the performers touch only each other but not the paying customer, does not constitute the underlying crime of prostitution, even if it is done for the customer's sexual gratification.16

The legal definition of a "lewd act" within the context of prostitution is therefore not coextensive with the entire universe of possible "sexual acts" or "sexual services." It is a specific, conduct-based definition that requires a particular type of physical contact between parties, a specific intent related to sexual arousal, and the element of compensation. This technical precision is fundamental, as it supports the foundational assumption that a "lewd act" is not legally synonymous with a "sexual act" in all circumstances. This distinction creates a legal space for compensated activities that may be sexual in nature but do not involve a "lewd act" as statutorily defined. For example, a contract for services that involve watching a sexual performance without physical contact, or non-arousal-focused touching in a therapeutic or educational context, would fall outside the narrow definition of prostitution under PC § 647(b). This creates the critical legal distinction that underpins the broader inquiry into the potential legality of certain sexual service contracts.

  

### **1.3 The Penumbra of Related Offenses: Pimping, Pandering, and the Business of Prostitution**

  

Surrounding the core misdemeanor offense of prostitution is a penumbra of more serious felony offenses that target not the individual transaction, but the organization and enterprise of the sex trade. These statutes, when analyzed collectively, reveal a legislative intent to dismantle the business structure of prostitution. They are, in essence, statutes that criminalize illicit labor recruitment, management, and financing.

The crime of pimping, defined in Penal Code § 266h, makes it a felony for any person, knowing another person is a prostitute, to live or derive support or maintenance, in whole or in part, from the earnings or proceeds of that person's prostitution.17 The statute is fundamentally an economic one; its focus is on the illicit revenue stream generated by the labor of the prostitute. It criminalizes the act of profiting from this labor, framing the pimp as an illegal beneficiary of an illicit enterprise.2

The crime of pandering, under Penal Code § 266i, targets the recruitment and retention of labor for the prostitution industry. A person is guilty of pandering if they procure another person for the purpose of prostitution; use promises, threats, or violence to persuade someone to become a prostitute; or encourage an existing prostitute to remain in the trade.17 The statutory language employs verbs of labor acquisition: "procures," "causes," "induces," "persuades," and "encourages".17 These actions are analogous to illegal and coercive recruitment practices, positioning pandering as a crime of illicit labor trafficking.

Further statutes criminalize the physical infrastructure of the prostitution business. Penal Code § 316 makes it a misdemeanor to keep a "disorderly house" or any house for the purpose of assignation or prostitution.17 This statute effectively outlaws the establishment of a workplace for the performance of this illicit labor. Relatedly, statutes like PC § 309 and PC § 273f impose criminal liability on those who allow minors to be present in such establishments, recognizing the heightened vulnerability of children in these environments.17

Viewed together, this statutory scheme is not merely a collection of ancillary offenses. It represents a systematic legislative effort to criminalize the entire business operation of prostitution. The misdemeanor offense in PC § 647(b) targets the point-of-sale transaction between the worker and the customer. The felonies of pimping and pandering target the management and labor supply chain. The disorderly house statutes target the physical plant. This comprehensive approach demonstrates that the state's interest extends far beyond the morality of the individual sex act. The legislative framework is designed to attack an unregulated, underground commercial enterprise. This perspective reframes these felonies from being simply about "immorality" to being about the regulation of an illicit business, providing a direct statutory basis for the hypothesis that the state's true compelling interest lies in the domain of labor and commerce. These laws can be seen as precursors to modern statutes against human trafficking and criminal enterprises, reinforcing the argument that the state's primary concern is the regulation of dangerous and exploitative commercial conditions.

  

### **1.4 Legislative Evolution: The Impact of SB 357 and SB 233 on Modern Enforcement**

  

In recent years, the California Legislature has enacted significant reforms that signal a clear philosophical shift in the state's approach to prostitution. This evolution moves away from a purely punitive, morality-based enforcement model and toward a paradigm rooted in public health, harm reduction, and the protection of vulnerable individuals. This legislative trend provides strong support for the hypothesis that the state's compelling interest is not in punishing the act of prostitution itself, but in mitigating the dangerous conditions and violent crimes associated with its illicit status.

Senate Bill 357, which took effect in 2022, repealed Penal Code § 653.22, the statute that criminalized loitering in a public place with the intent to commit prostitution.9 This former law was a powerful tool for law enforcement, allowing for arrests based on subjective assessments of a person's appearance, location, and behavior, even without any evidence of solicitation or agreement.21 Critics argued that it led to discriminatory enforcement, particularly against transgender women and women of color. By repealing this statute, the legislature has narrowed the scope of criminalization to focus on the actual transactional conduct defined in PC § 647(b), rather than preparatory or ambiguous acts. This change implicitly recognizes the harm of preemptive enforcement and prioritizes concrete evidence of a criminal transaction over suspicion.

Senate Bill 233, enacted in 2020, introduced two critical protections. First, it amended Evidence Code § 782.1 to make the possession of condoms inadmissible as evidence in a prosecution for prostitution-related offenses.9 This reform addressed a significant public health paradox: individuals engaging in sex work were discouraged from carrying condoms for fear they would be used as evidence against them. The law now aligns criminal procedure with public health goals. Second, SB 233 amended Evidence Code § 1162 to grant immunity from arrest and prosecution for prostitution to individuals who are victims or witnesses of serious felonies and report those crimes to law enforcement.9 This provision is a profound acknowledgment that people involved in the sex trade are disproportionately victims of violent crime and that their status as sex workers should not be a barrier to seeking justice and ensuring public safety.

This legislative posture is further reflected in programs like Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion (LEAD), authorized under Penal Code § 1001.87. The LEAD program allows law enforcement officers to divert individuals suspected of low-level prostitution offenses into case management and social services as an alternative to arrest and prosecution.17 This approach treats the underlying issues that may lead to sex work—such as poverty, addiction, or homelessness—as public health and social problems rather than purely criminal ones.

Collectively, these reforms paint a clear picture. The state is actively moving to protect people engaged in sex work from discriminatory enforcement (SB 357), to encourage safe sex practices (SB 233), to empower them to report violent crime without fear of self-incrimination (SB 233), and to offer pathways out of the justice system and into supportive services (LEAD). This legislative trajectory is fundamentally inconsistent with a state interest primarily focused on punishing immorality. Instead, it is perfectly consistent with a state interest in mitigating the harms of a dangerous, unregulated, and underground industry. These harms—violence, exploitation, and public health risks—are precisely the types of harms that fall within the purview of labor and safety regulations, lending significant weight to the labor-law framework as the most accurate lens through which to view the state's modern interest in this area.

  

## **Section 2: The Contractual Dimension:** ***Marvin v. Marvin*** **and the Enforceability of Agreements for Sexual Services**

  

The assertion that any contract for sexual services is inherently illegal and criminal represents an oversimplification of a complex area of contract law. While agreements that are explicitly for the commission of a crime are indeed void, the California Supreme Court's landmark decision in *Marvin v. Marvin* established a sophisticated legal framework for analyzing agreements between unmarried partners. This framework, which hinges on the doctrine of severable consideration, provides the necessary tools to deconstruct the relationship between contracts and sexual services, creating a pathway to identify categories of agreements that may be licit and enforceable. By applying these principles, it becomes clear that the law distinguishes between a contract *for* prohibited sexual acts and a contract *between* individuals who also engage in sexual acts, a distinction that is central to this entire analysis.

  

### **2.1 The** ***Marvin*** **Doctrine: Severing Meretricious Consideration from Lawful Agreements**

  

The foundational case for understanding the enforceability of agreements between nonmarital partners is *Marvin v. Marvin* (1976) 18 Cal. 3d 660. Prior to this decision, courts often refused to enforce any agreement between cohabitating partners, viewing such arrangements as "tainted" by the illicit nature of the relationship itself.22 The

*Marvin* court decisively rejected this approach, establishing a new, more precise standard. The court held that adults who voluntarily live together are as competent as any other individuals to contract with respect to their earnings and property rights.23

The core of the *Marvin* doctrine is that a contract between nonmarital partners is unenforceable *only to the extent that it explicitly rests upon the immoral and illicit consideration of meretricious sexual services*.23 This principle introduces the critical concept of severability. A court must examine the consideration underlying the agreement. If the agreement is not based on a promise of sexual services in exchange for financial support or property sharing, it can be enforced.26 For example, if an agreement is based on consideration such as one partner's services as a homemaker, companion, and business assistant, it is not invalidated merely because the parties also had a sexual relationship.26 The sexual relationship is seen as independent of the contractual obligations.

This legal reasoning creates a fundamental dichotomy: a contract *for* sex versus a contract *between people who also have sex*. The former, where sexual services form the basis of the bargain, is void as against public policy. The latter, where the consideration is severable and lawful in its own right, is potentially valid and enforceable, either as an express or an implied contract.23 The court in

*Marvin* mandated a granular, fact-based inquiry into the nature of the agreement, moving legal analysis away from a blanket prohibition based on the parties' relationship status. This forces a distinction between the relationship itself and any economic agreements made within it. This judicial approach provides the central pillar supporting the inquiry into potentially licit sexual service contracts. It requires a rigorous analysis of what, precisely, is being exchanged as consideration, rather than allowing the mere presence of a sexual component to invalidate an entire economic arrangement.

  

### **2.2 From** ***Marvin*** **to Modern Cohabitation Agreements: A Framework for Permissible Contracts**

  

The principles articulated in *Marvin* have been operationalized and normalized in modern legal practice through the use of cohabitation agreements. These are legally binding contracts entered into by unmarried couples who live together, designed to define their respective property rights and financial responsibilities both during the relationship and in the event of a separation.29 These agreements are a direct and practical application of the

*Marvin* doctrine, demonstrating that the law has a robust mechanism for recognizing and enforcing complex financial arrangements that exist alongside a nonmarital sexual relationship.

For a cohabitation agreement to be valid and enforceable in California, it must meet standard contractual requirements. It should be in writing, involve a full and fair disclosure of assets and liabilities by both parties, and be entered into voluntarily, preferably with each party having the benefit of independent legal counsel.29 Crucially, like any agreement between nonmarital partners, it cannot be based on meretricious consideration.27 The agreement can delineate ownership of property brought into the relationship, specify how jointly acquired assets will be treated, and outline responsibilities for shared expenses.29

The widespread acceptance and enforceability of cohabitation agreements provide a powerful analogy for the present analysis. These agreements prove that the legal system is capable of severing the economic aspects of a relationship from the personal and sexual ones for the purpose of legal enforcement. If the law can uphold a comprehensive agreement governing all financial aspects of a couple's life together, it logically follows that it possesses the analytical tools to evaluate other, more limited contracts for services between individuals who might also engage in sexual activity. The existence of this legal framework normalizes the concept of analyzing economic exchanges independently of the sexual dynamics of a relationship, reinforcing the idea that not all financial arrangements between sexually intimate individuals are reducible to a prohibited contract for sex.

  

### **2.3 The Illegality of "Naive" Prostitution Contracts: Void as Against Public Policy**

  

While the *Marvin* doctrine opens the door to a wide range of enforceable agreements, it also implicitly clarifies why what might be termed a "naive prostitution contract" is a legal nullity. Any express contract where the consideration is explicitly defined as the performance of a "lewd act" for money, as proscribed by Penal Code § 647(b), is void on its face. Such an agreement is a contract to commit a criminal act, rendering it unenforceable as a matter of fundamental public policy.34 The attempt to cloak an illegal transaction in the language of contract law is legally futile.

The very act of trying to formalize an agreement for prostitution highlights the fundamental distinction between a legally cognizable contract and a criminal conspiracy. A contract requires a lawful object; an agreement to commit a crime has an unlawful object by definition. Therefore, the "cover up" of attempting to legitimize an illegal act through contractual formalities not only fails to make the agreement enforceable but can also create additional legal liabilities, such as fraud or conspiracy. This legal absurdity underscores the point that prostitution, as defined by the penal code, occupies a narrow and specific category of prohibited conduct. It is not part of the broader universe of "contracts for sexual services" that might be subject to analysis under principles of contract law. Instead, it is an illegal act, and an agreement to perform it is simply evidence of a conspiracy to commit a crime, not a contract to be interpreted or enforced. This reinforces the clean separation between the narrow criminal act of prostitution and the much broader, and legally complex, category of compensated sexual services.

  

### **2.4 A Proposed Taxonomy of Compensated Sexual Service Contracts**

  

By synthesizing the narrow definition of criminal prostitution from the Penal Code with the principles of severable consideration from *Marvin v. Marvin*, it is possible to construct a taxonomy of compensated sexual service contracts, categorizing them based on their potential legality and enforceability. This framework moves beyond a simple legal/illegal binary and provides a more nuanced understanding of the legal landscape.

1.  **Category 1: Illicit and Unenforceable (Criminal Prostitution).** This category includes any agreement, express or implied, where the bargained-for consideration is the performance of a "lewd act" (the touching of genitals, buttocks, or female breast for sexual arousal) in exchange for money or other compensation. This is the precise conduct criminalized by PC § 647(b). Such agreements are not merely unenforceable; they constitute the crime of soliciting or agreeing to engage in prostitution and are void *ab initio*.
2.  **Category 2: Licit but Unenforceable (Gratuitous Exchange).** This category covers arrangements within an intimate relationship where services, including sexual intimacy, are exchanged without the intent to create a legally binding contract. Courts often presume that services rendered between cohabitating partners are done out of "love and affection" and are therefore gratuitous gifts, not part of a bargained-for exchange that would give rise to contractual rights.35 While the relationship is licit, there is no enforceable contract for the services.
3.  **Category 3: Licit and Enforceable (Severable Consideration).** This is the classic *Marvin* agreement. It involves an express or implied contract between nonmarital partners where the consideration is for lawful services that are severable from the sexual aspect of the relationship. Examples include compensation or property sharing in exchange for one partner's contributions as a homemaker, business partner, financial manager, or companion in a non-sexual sense.23 The sexual relationship exists alongside the contract but is not the consideration for it.
4.  **Category 4: Potentially Licit and Enforceable (Non-Proscribed Sexual Services).** This theoretical category represents the frontier of the analysis. It encompasses contracts for compensated sexual services that do not fall within the narrow definition of criminal prostitution. The legality and enforceability of these agreements would depend on a detailed analysis of public policy and their specific nature, particularly through the lens of First Amendment protections for expressive conduct. This category could include contracts for services such as:

  - **Performance Art:** Hiring an actor to perform in a non-obscene but sexually explicit work, as protected by the *Freeman* decision.
  - **Sexual Surrogacy:** Engaging a surrogate partner for therapeutic purposes under the guidance of a licensed therapist, where the goal is treatment, not prurient gratification.
  - **Educational Modeling:** Paying a model to participate in the creation of materials for sex education or medical training.

The existence of this fourth category, however theoretical, demonstrates that the statement "contracting for sexual services is illegal" is imprecise. The law, in fact, requires a highly specific inquiry into the nature of the act, the intent of the parties, and the context of the transaction to determine legality.

  

## **Section 3: Prostitution as Impermissible Expression: A First Amendment Analysis**

  

The regulation of prostitution intersects with the constitutional guarantees of freedom of expression. While the First Amendment provides broad protection for speech and expressive conduct, this protection is not absolute. Certain categories of speech, such as obscenity, are deemed unprotected. This section will utilize the legal framework of obscenity law, not to argue that prostitution is obscene, but as a powerful analytical tool to delineate the boundary between compensated sexual conduct that is protected expression and conduct that constitutes an unprotected criminal act. The California Supreme Court's decision in *People v. Freeman* serves as a crucial precedent, demonstrating that the purpose and context of a compensated sexual act can transform it from a crime into a constitutionally protected artistic performance.

  

### **3.1 The** ***Miller*** **Test as an Analytical Framework for Permissible Sexual Expression**

  

The controlling standard for determining whether sexually explicit material is obscene, and therefore unprotected by the First Amendment, was established in *Miller v. California* (1973) 413 U.S. 15. The *Miller* test sets forth a three-prong standard, all of which must be met for material to be judged obscene:

1.  Whether "the average person, applying contemporary community standards" would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest.36 The "prurient interest" is defined not as a healthy interest in sex, but as a shameful or morbid interest.36
2.  Whether the work depicts or describes, in a patently offensive way, sexual conduct specifically defined by the applicable state law.41 This prong targets "hard core" sexual conduct.
3.  Whether the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value (the "SLAPS" test).37

It is critical to recognize that sexually explicit content that is *not* legally obscene receives First Amendment protection.39 The

*Miller* test, therefore, provides a structured methodology for evaluating expressive content on a spectrum from protected to unprotected.

For the purposes of this analysis, the *Miller* test is not used to determine if an act of prostitution is obscene. Rather, its structure is employed by analogy to categorize different forms of compensated sexual services. Instead of asking, "Is this act obscene?", the inquiry becomes, "Does this compensated sexual service possess qualities that would cause it to fail one of the prongs of the *Miller* test, thereby suggesting it is a form of protected expression rather than the crime of prostitution?" This reframes the test from a tool of censorship into a tool of legal categorization. Any compensated act that qualifies as "expression" (e.g., a performance, a film, a work of art) and is not obscene—most notably because it possesses "serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value"—is presumptively protected speech. This creates a direct constitutional tension with any overly broad interpretation of prostitution statutes, suggesting that such statutes must be construed narrowly to avoid infringing on protected rights.

  

### **3.2** ***People v. Freeman*** **and the "Serious Work" Exemption: Differentiating Artistic Expression from Prostitution**

  

The cornerstone of this First Amendment analysis is the California Supreme Court's decision in *People v. Freeman* (1988) 46 Cal.3d 419. In this landmark case, a film producer, Harold Freeman, was convicted of pandering (procuring a person for the purpose of prostitution) for hiring and paying actors to engage in explicit sexual acts, including sexual intercourse and oral copulation, for a commercial adult film.50 The film itself was not alleged or found to be obscene.50

The California Supreme Court reversed the conviction, drawing a sharp constitutional line between artistic expression and prostitution. The court's reasoning hinged on the *purpose* of the financial transaction. It held that the payments made to the actors were not for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification, either for the producer or the actors themselves.51 Instead, the actors were paid fees for their services in creating a work of expression—a non-obscene film.50 The court concluded that applying the pandering statute to the hiring of actors for a non-obscene film would unlawfully impinge upon protected First Amendment rights.50

The *Freeman* decision is dispositive because it establishes in California that the *dominant motivation* and *context* of a compensated sexual act are the determinative factors in its legal classification. The exact same physical conduct can be either the crime of prostitution or a constitutionally protected performance, depending entirely on the purpose for which the compensation is paid. When the dominant motivation is artistic creation, the act is shielded by the First Amendment. This is, in effect, a direct application of the third prong of the *Miller* test as a defense against a prostitution-related charge. The creation of a non-obscene film is inherently an activity with "serious artistic value," thus removing it from the realm of unprotected conduct. This precedent provides a powerful legal basis for the argument that prostitution is a much narrower category of conduct than the broad class of all contracts for sexual services, and that non-obscenity, understood as the presence of "serious work," is a critical exemption.

  

### **3.3 A Theoretical Model for Categorizing Compensated Sexual Services by** ***Miller*** **Prongs**

  

Building on the principles of *Freeman* and using the *Miller* test as an analytical framework, it is possible to construct the theoretical model of compensated sexual services hypothesized in the initial query. This model categorizes services based on which prong of the obscenity test they would fail, thereby distinguishing them from the conduct proscribed as prostitution.

  - **Category A: Fails Prong 3 (Lacks SLAPS Value).** This category encompasses compensated sexual conduct that is devoid of any serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value. The sole purpose of the transaction is sexual gratification. This is the domain of conduct that can be properly regulated as prostitution under PC § 647(b), as it lacks the "serious work" protection established in *Freeman* and has no claim to First Amendment protection. The conduct is non-expressive in a constitutional sense.
  - **Category B: Passes Prong 3 (Possesses SLAPS Value).** This is the *Freeman* category. It includes the production of non-obscene pornography, erotic performance art, and other forms of expression that, while sexually explicit, are deemed to have serious artistic or other value. The compensation is for the expressive labor of the performer, not for the act of sexual gratification itself. Such activities are constitutionally protected and cannot be prosecuted as prostitution.
  - **Category C: Fails Prong 1 (Not for Prurient Interest).** This is a significant theoretical category for services that are sexual in nature but are not intended to appeal to a "shameful or morbid interest in sex." The dominant motivation is not prurient. This could include:

  - **Sexual Surrogacy:** A compensated partner works with a client under the supervision of a licensed therapist to address sexual dysfunction. The purpose is therapeutic, not prurient.
  - Medical or Scientific Modeling: An individual is paid to engage in or simulate sexual acts for the purpose of medical research or the creation of educational materials. The purpose is scientific or educational.  
    These activities, because their purpose is not to appeal to a prurient interest, would fall outside the rationale for both obscenity and prostitution laws.

  - **Category D: Fails Prong 2 (Not Patently Offensive).** This category could include compensated services that are sexual in nature but do not depict or describe sexual conduct in a "patently offensive way" as defined by state law. This might encompass certain forms of erotic entertainment, companionship, or services that involve sexual titillation but fall short of the "hard core" conduct targeted by obscenity and prostitution statutes. The line here is determined by "contemporary community standards," but it provides another potential avenue for distinguishing licit services from the specific "lewd act" required for a prostitution conviction.

  

### **3.4 Beyond Obscenity: When Non-Obscene Conduct Becomes Impermissible and Isomorphic to Prostitution**

  

The question of how non-obscene expression or conduct can still be deemed "impermissible" and legally equivalent to prostitution is answered by distinguishing between the regulation of content and the regulation of conduct. The state is not required to prosecute prostitution as an obscenity crime. Instead, California prosecutes prostitution under PC § 647(b) as a form of "disorderly conduct".8

The First Amendment does not protect "speech integral to illegal conduct".53 When an individual solicits prostitution, their speech—the offer to exchange money for a lewd act—is not being punished for its expressive content in the abstract. It is being punished because it is the verbal act that initiates a specific, prohibited criminal transaction. The expression is inseparable from the commission of the crime itself. This is how non-obscene conduct becomes isomorphic to prostitution: when the act is stripped of any overriding expressive purpose (as in

*Freeman*) and is reduced to a simple commercial transaction for a statutorily defined "lewd act," it loses its First Amendment protection as expressive conduct. It becomes merely the conduct of forming an illicit contract.

Therefore, the state can regulate this conduct without needing to prove that it is obscene. The legal framework targets the *commercial transaction*—the agreement and overt act—rather than the *content* of the speech or the potential offensiveness of the sexual act. This approach allows the state to prohibit the specific conduct of prostitution while leaving a wide berth for the protection of sexually explicit but non-obscene expression, as mandated by the First Amendment and affirmed in *Freeman*.

  

## **Section 4: A New Paradigm: Prostitution as an Illicit Labor Arrangement**

  

The most robust and constitutionally sound framework for analyzing the state's regulation of prostitution is not one based on the shifting sands of public morality, but on the well-established and compelling state interest in regulating labor, commerce, and taxation. This section advances the hypothesis that the core problems associated with prostitution are best understood as issues of illicit labor. Typical prostitution arrangements, particularly those involving third-party managers, can be characterized as illegal enterprises that systematically violate fundamental labor standards, including worker classification, wage and hour laws, and tax obligations. By applying the modern legal lens of the gig economy and California's ABC test for worker classification, prostitution can be reframed as a problem of unregulated and exploitative labor, providing a more coherent and effective basis for state intervention.

  

### **4.1 The State's Compelling Interest: Regulating Labor Conditions, Not Private Morality**

  

In the wake of landmark privacy decisions such as *Lawrence v. Texas*, which affirmed the constitutional rights of adults to engage in private, consensual sexual conduct, justifications for criminal laws based solely on promoting a particular moral code have become increasingly tenuous. In contrast, the state's authority to regulate economic activity and labor conditions is firmly established and represents a compelling governmental interest. This includes the power to:

  - **Prevent Labor Exploitation:** The state has a profound interest in protecting workers from coercive, unsafe, and exploitative labor conditions.
  - **Ensure Proper Taxation:** The state has a compelling interest in the collection of income and payroll taxes to fund public services.
  - **Enforce Business and Licensing Standards:** The state regulates businesses to ensure public health, safety, and fair competition. This includes age verification requirements, professional licensing, and health code compliance.
  - **Provide a Social Safety Net:** The state requires employers to contribute to systems like workers' compensation and unemployment insurance to protect workers from injury and job loss.55

From this perspective, "naive prostitution contracts" and the informal arrangements of the sex trade are not merely immoral agreements; they are fundamentally schemes designed to circumvent this entire regulatory apparatus. They operate in an underground economy to evade taxes, deny workers legal protections, ignore licensing and health requirements, and avoid the legal and financial responsibilities of being an employer. Reframing the state's interest in these terms transforms the debate. It moves from an intractable conflict over private morality to a standard problem of regulatory enforcement, aligning with the legislative trend toward harm reduction and public safety identified earlier.

  

### **4.2 Application of the ABC Test to Prostitution Arrangements**

  

California law provides a powerful tool for analyzing labor relationships: the "ABC test," first established by the California Supreme Court in *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court* (2018) and later codified by Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5).57 This test presumes that a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can prove

*all three* of the following conditions:

  - **(A)** The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with the performance of the work.
  - **(B)** The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
  - **(C)** The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed.

This test has already been applied to legal sectors of the sex industry. For example, under AB 5, exotic dancers who were previously misclassified as independent contractors are often now considered employees of the clubs where they work, as the clubs typically fail prongs A and B.20 This precedent provides a direct legal basis for applying the same analytical framework to illicit prostitution arrangements.

### **4.3 Prostitution Contracts as a Scheme to Circumvent Labor, Tax, and Regulatory Law**

  

The results of the ABC test analysis reveal a crucial point: the structure of organized prostitution is inherently a system of illegal labor misclassification. When a pimp, agency, or online platform functions as an employer but treats the sex worker as an independent contractor (or, more often, as a non-person with no legal status at all), they are illegally circumventing a host of legal obligations. These violations include:

  - Failure to pay minimum wage and overtime.
  - Failure to provide workers' compensation insurance for on-the-job injuries.
  - Failure to pay the employer's share of payroll taxes (Social Security, Medicare).
  - Failure to contribute to state unemployment and disability insurance funds.55
  - Failure to adhere to workplace safety standards.

The informal "prostitution contract" is thus an agreement not only to perform a lewd act but also to participate in a system of illegal employment. The "indirection and denials" surrounding these arrangements are not just about avoiding the social stigma of prostitution; they are economically motivated tactics to evade the significant financial and legal responsibilities of being an employer. This perspective solidifies the argument that the state's most compelling and legitimate interest is in enforcing its economic, labor, and tax regimes against these illicit enterprises. Individuals who believe they have been subject to such labor violations can report them to the California Labor Commissioner's Office.64

  

### **4.4 Reinterpreting Pimping and Pandering as Illicit Employment and Human Trafficking**

  

Viewing prostitution through this labor framework provides a powerful and modern reinterpretation of the felony offenses of pimping and pandering.

  - **Pimping (PC § 266h):** This crime, which prohibits deriving support from the earnings of a prostitute, is no longer just a morals offense. It is the crime of illegally profiting from the labor of a misclassified, unprotected, and exploited employee. The pimp is, in effect, an illegal employer engaged in extreme wage theft and tax evasion.
  - **Pandering (PC § 266i):** This crime, which involves recruiting individuals into prostitution, is transformed into the crime of illicit and often coercive labor recruitment. When the pandering involves "promises, threats, violence, or by any device or scheme," it moves beyond simple illicit recruitment and aligns directly with the legal definition of labor trafficking.65

This reinterpretation provides a far more robust justification for the severe felony penalties attached to these crimes. They are not punished harshly simply because they are related to sex, but because they represent profound forms of labor exploitation, economic crime, and, in many cases, a form of modern slavery. This framework gives prosecutors a compelling legal theory that resonates with contemporary concerns about worker exploitation and human trafficking, offering a more effective and defensible basis for prosecution than antiquated arguments based on public morality.

  

## **Section 5: Synthesis and Recommendations**

  

The preceding analysis has deconstructed the legal concept of prostitution in California, moving beyond simplistic definitions to reveal a complex interplay of criminal, contract, constitutional, and labor law. By dissecting the statutory language, applying principles from landmark cases, and proposing a novel analytical framework, a more nuanced and coherent understanding emerges. This re-characterization demonstrates that prostitution is a narrowly defined transactional crime, distinct from the broader categories of "lewd acts" and "contracts for sexual services," and is most effectively regulated through the lens of illicit labor.

  

### **5.1 A Synthesized Definition: Distinguishing Prostitution from Lewd Acts and Licit Sexual Service Contracts**

  

Based on this comprehensive review, the key legal concepts can be synthesized into a clear, multi-layered definition that clarifies their distinct boundaries:

  - A **"Lewd Act,"** in the context of prostitution, is not a general term for any sexual behavior. It is a specific legal term of art referring to the physical touching of enumerated body parts (genitals, buttocks, female breast) of either the customer or the provider by the other, for the specific purpose of sexual arousal or gratification. It is a necessary but not sufficient component of the crime of prostitution.
  - A **Licit Contract for Sexual Services** is an agreement for compensated sexual activity that falls outside the narrow criminal definition of prostitution. Its legality is established if the service constitutes a form of constitutionally protected expression (as in *People v. Freeman*), is performed for a non-prurient purpose (e.g., therapeutic or educational), or does not involve a "lewd act" as defined by statute and interpreted by the courts. The principles of *Marvin v. Marvin* further affirm that lawful contracts for non-sexual consideration can coexist with a sexual relationship.
  - **Prostitution,** as defined by Penal Code § 647(b), is the specific, non-expressive, transactional crime of soliciting, agreeing to perform, or performing a "lewd act" in exchange for compensation. It is prosecuted as a form of disorderly conduct, focusing on the illicit commercial agreement rather than the morality of the act itself. It is a narrow legal category, distinct from the vast range of human sexual expression and contractual arrangements.

  

### **5.2 The Primacy of the Labor Framework in Modern Legal Analysis**

  

The most robust, constitutionally sound, and effective framework for understanding and regulating the harms associated with the commercial sex trade is the labor law paradigm. This approach correctly identifies the state's compelling interests not in policing private morality, but in preventing exploitation, ensuring fair labor standards, collecting taxes, and protecting public health and safety. It provides a clear and objective analytical tool—the ABC test—to diagnose the nature of the relationships within the sex trade, revealing that most organized forms of prostitution are predicated on the illegal misclassification of workers as non-employees. This framework aligns perfectly with modern legislative trends in California, which have moved decisively toward a harm-reduction model that prioritizes the safety and rights of individuals involved in sex work while targeting the exploitative structures that surround them.

  

### **5.3 Recommendations for a Coherent Legal and Policy Approach**

  

This analysis leads to a set of coherent recommendations for legal and policy efforts that are consistent with constitutional principles and focused on addressing the actual harms of the illicit sex trade:

1.  **Enforce Labor Laws Against Third-Party Exploiters:** Law enforcement and regulatory agencies, including the Labor Commissioner's Office, should prioritize investigations and prosecutions of pimps, exploitative agencies, and illicit online platforms as illegal employers. Civil and criminal actions should be pursued for wage theft, tax fraud, failure to provide workers' compensation, and other violations of the Labor Code, using the ABC test as the standard for determining employment status.
2.  **Utilize Tax and Business Regulations:** The Franchise Tax Board and other regulatory bodies should be utilized to target the financial infrastructure of organized prostitution. Pimping and pandering should be treated not only as sex crimes but as evidence of criminal tax evasion and the operation of an unlicensed, illegal business enterprise.
3.  **Continue the Legislative Trend of Decriminalizing the Worker:** The legislature should continue to build on the progress of SB 233 and SB 357. Policy should focus on decriminalizing the status and preparatory acts of individual sex workers while expanding access to diversion programs, healthcare, housing, and other social services. This approach recognizes that individual providers are often the most vulnerable parties and that criminalization is a barrier to their safety and well-being.
4.  **Clarify the Legal Status of Licit Sexual Services:** To prevent the chilling of constitutionally protected expression and other lawful activities, the judiciary and legislature should work to further clarify the legal boundaries established in *People v. Freeman*. Clear guidelines are needed to distinguish protected artistic, therapeutic, and educational sexual services from the narrow crime of prostitution, ensuring that artists, performers, and health practitioners are not improperly targeted by prostitution laws.
