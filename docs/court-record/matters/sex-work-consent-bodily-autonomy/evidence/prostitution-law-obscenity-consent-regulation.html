The Prostitution-Miller Test: A New Jurisprudence for Differentiating Licit Sex Work from Obscene Conduct

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Introduction: Reconciling Legality, Consent, and Commerce in Intimate Exchange

The legal and social discourse surrounding compensated sexual services is mired in a state of profound and perpetual ambiguity, trapped within a framework that fails to distinguish between coercive exploitation and consensual enterprise. This report introduces and defends a novel and powerful analytical model—the Prostitution-Miller Test—as a constitutionally sound and logically coherent framework for analyzing compensated sexual acts. This test provides a systematic method for distinguishing between the narrow, unprotected conduct that constitutes the crime of prostitution and the broader category of licit sex work, which can be a form of personal service or protected expression. The Prostitution-Miller Test achieves this by adapting the three-prong standard from the Supreme Court's obscenity jurisprudence in Miller v. California: (1) whether the act, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest; (2) whether it depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way; and (3) whether it lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.1 By applying these prongs not as a test for obscenity but as a categorical tool, a new jurisprudence emerges. An act fails to be criminal prostitution if its dominant motive is a substantial purpose beyond prurient interest, if it is not patently offensive due to the presence of ongoing affirmative consent, or if it constitutes a form of "serious work" that possesses artistic, scientific, or other value.

This inquiry is necessitated by two central and irreconcilable paradoxes in contemporary California law. The first is the paradox of consideration. The state's criminal law, specifically Penal Code § 647(b), defines prostitution as a bargained-for exchange of a sexual act for compensation, thereby implicitly acknowledging that a sexual act can function as legal "consideration".4 This creates a logical inconsistency, as the state's own family and contract law simultaneously recognize intimate services and reproductive acts as valuable, and in some cases, compensable consideration. Landmark cases like

Marvin v. Marvin and the comprehensive statutory framework governing paid gestational surrogacy demonstrate that the legal system is fully capable of valuing and enforcing complex agreements involving intimacy and economic exchange.5 The state, therefore, criminalizes a specific sexual bargain with one hand while validating analogous bargains with the other.

The second and more profound conflict is the paradox of consent. California law operates under two mutually exclusive consent regimes. The world of commerce is governed by the static, objective standard of contractual consent, where a "meeting of the minds" is assessed at the moment of formation and is designed to create predictable, binding obligations.16 In stark contrast, the intimate sphere is governed by the dynamic, subjective, and perpetually revocable standard of affirmative sexual consent, where ongoing, enthusiastic agreement is the paramount concern, and the right to withdraw that consent at any moment is absolute.16 This doctrinal schism creates an impossible legal bind for any agreement that contemplates both commercial terms and sexual performance, forcing a choice between the sanctity of the bargain and the non-negotiable right to bodily autonomy.

This report will navigate these paradoxes by systematically deconstructing the existing legal landscape and constructing a new, more coherent model. Part I will conduct a critical examination of the current legal frameworks, exposing the theoretical flaws in both California's prohibitory model and Nevada's highly regulated, surveillance-based system. It will demonstrate how these regimes create a fractured and contradictory legal reality for individuals. Part II will build the theoretical foundation of the Prostitution-Miller Test, demonstrating how each prong provides a distinct pathway to legality and gives rise to a specific set of attendant legal duties and obligations. Finally, Part III will move from theory to practice, proposing the "Laudable Contract" as a model for an agreement that is not only legally defensible under the Prostitution-Miller Test but is also ethically robust, grounded in principles of relationality and care, and designed to promote the dignity and flourishing of all parties.

Part I: The Fractured Legal Landscape of Commercial Sex

The legal treatment of commercial and quasi-commercial intimacy in the United States is characterized by deep internal contradictions. To construct a new, more coherent jurisprudence, it is first necessary to deconstruct the existing frameworks that have proven inadequate. This part of the report undertakes that deconstruction by examining two diametrically opposed models: California's regime of criminal prohibition, which creates a "legal void" around such transactions, and Nevada's system of administrative regulation, which replaces criminalization with a pervasive apparatus of state surveillance. A forensic analysis of these systems, along with the profound doctrinal conflict between contractual and sexual consent, reveals a legal landscape that is not merely flawed but is structurally incapable of providing a rational, just, or humane framework for governing these complex human arrangements.

Chapter 1: The Architecture of Prohibition: California's Legal Void

In California, the legal framework is meticulously designed to ensure that any agreement for the exchange of sexual services for compensation is legally nonexistent and unenforceable.4 The state's approach creates a legal vacuum in which no rights, obligations, or protections can exist for the parties involved, rendering the "prostitution contract" a legal nullity from its inception.4 This is achieved through a narrow statutory definition of the crime, a specific legal interpretation of the acts involved, and the application of fundamental contract law principles that refuse to recognize agreements to commit illegal acts.4

Forensic Analysis of Penal Code § 647(b)

The statutory heart of California's prohibitory model is Penal Code § 647, which defines various forms of "disorderly conduct".4 Within this statute, subdivision (b) specifically addresses prostitution, and a forensic analysis of its text reveals that it criminalizes not a single act but an entire transactional process. The law makes it a misdemeanor to solicit an act of prostitution, agree to engage in an act of prostitution, or actually engage in an act of prostitution.5 This statutory construction is critical because it means the crime is completed at the moment of contract formation; the verbal or non-verbal exchange that constitutes an offer and acceptance is, in itself, the prohibited conduct.4 The law is explicitly bilateral, applying with equal force to the person offering the sexual service and the customer who solicits it.4

The statutory definition of prostitution in California hinges on the concept of a "lewd act," which is defined as "any lewd act between persons for money or other consideration".4 This term is not left to subjective moral interpretation but has been given a precise legal definition through judicial precedent. A "lewd act" for the purposes of prostitution is the physical touching of the genitals, buttocks, or female breast of either the prostitute or the customer with some part of the other person's body.5 This definition is further qualified by two critical elements. First, the touching must be done for a specific purpose: the sexual arousal or gratification of either person. This intent requirement distinguishes a "lewd act" from incidental or accidental contact.4 Second, the act must be performed in exchange for "money or other consideration," which is the element that separates the crime of prostitution from other offenses involving lewd acts, such as "lewd conduct in public" under PC § 647(a).4

California case law has added another layer of specificity. In Wooten v. Superior Court, the court held that the definition of prostitution requires actual physical contact between the prostitute and the customer.4 This means that a performance, such as a striptease or a live sex show where the performers touch only each other but not the paying customer, does not constitute the underlying crime of prostitution, even if it is done for the customer's sexual gratification.4 This narrow, technical definition of a "lewd act" inadvertently creates a legal gray area for a wide range of compensated sexual services that do not meet this specific standard, such as online performances or non-contact fetish services. The prohibitory model, by focusing so narrowly on a specific physical act, is ill-equipped to address the diverse and evolving nature of the commercial sex industry. This reveals a fundamental flaw in its "one-size-fits-all" approach to criminalization. The state's prohibitory scheme is not a comprehensive ban on all forms of "sex for money" but a targeted ban on a specific transaction, leaving a vast, unregulated, and legally ambiguous market for other sexual services. This ambiguity undermines the state's purported goal of a wholesale rejection of commercial sex.

The Legacy of Marvin v. Marvin and the Doctrine of Meretricious Consideration

The criminalization of the transactional process under PC § 647(b) has profound consequences under contract law. The common law doctrine of legality, a foundational principle of jurisprudence, asserts that agreements that breach statutory laws or violate public policy are inherently unenforceable.4 A contract to commit a crime is the archetypal example of an agreement that is void

ab initio (from the beginning) as against public policy.4 Because California law criminalizes the very formation of the agreement, a "prostitution contract" is a legal nullity.4

However, the California Supreme Court's landmark decision in Marvin v. Marvin established a sophisticated legal framework for analyzing agreements between unmarried partners, creating a system of legal fictions to manage economic exchange in non-marital relationships.5 The court held that adults who voluntarily live together are as competent as any other individuals to contract with respect to their earnings and property rights.5 The core of the

Marvin doctrine is that a contract between nonmarital partners is unenforceable only to the extent that it explicitly rests upon the immoral and illicit consideration of meretricious sexual services.5 This principle introduced the critical concept of severability. By allowing enforcement of agreements based on "severable" non-sexual consideration (e.g., homemaking, companionship), the court implicitly valued the entire relationship while legally pretending the sexual component was not part of the bargain.5 In the lived reality of such a relationship, it is practically and conceptually impossible to neatly sever the "permissible" services of a companion and homemaker from the "impermissible" sexual component of a romantic partner. This judicial maneuver is the cornerstone of the "logic of obscurity," a state-sanctioned method of misrepresenting an agreement to achieve an equitable result.17

Legislative Evolution and the Shift to Harm Reduction

In recent years, the California Legislature has enacted significant reforms that signal a clear philosophical shift in the state's approach to prostitution, moving away from a purely punitive, morality-based enforcement model and toward a paradigm rooted in public health, harm reduction, and the protection of vulnerable individuals.5 This legislative trend provides strong support for the hypothesis that the state's compelling interest is not in punishing the act of prostitution itself, but in mitigating the dangerous conditions and violent crimes associated with its illicit status.5

Senate Bill 357, which took effect in 2022, repealed Penal Code § 653.22, the statute that criminalized loitering in a public place with the intent to commit prostitution.5 This former law was a powerful tool for law enforcement, allowing for arrests based on subjective assessments of a person's appearance and behavior, even without any evidence of solicitation or agreement.5 By repealing this statute, the legislature has narrowed the scope of criminalization to focus on the actual transactional conduct defined in PC § 647(b).5 Senate Bill 233, enacted in 2020, introduced two critical protections. First, it made the possession of condoms inadmissible as evidence in a prosecution for prostitution-related offenses, addressing a significant public health paradox.5 Second, it granted immunity from arrest and prosecution for prostitution to individuals who are victims or witnesses of serious felonies and report those crimes to law enforcement.5 Collectively, these reforms paint a clear picture of a state actively moving to protect people engaged in sex work from discriminatory enforcement, to encourage safe sex practices, and to empower them to report violent crime without fear of self-incrimination.5 This legislative trajectory is fundamentally inconsistent with a state interest primarily focused on punishing immorality and is perfectly consistent with an interest in mitigating the harms of a dangerous, unregulated, and underground industry.5

Chapter 2: The Architecture of Regulation: Nevada's Panoptic Gaze

In stark contrast to California's prohibitory model, Nevada has constructed a legal architecture that does not merely create an exception to prostitution laws but establishes an entirely different legal category: the regulated sexual service contract.4 This system operates by decriminalizing the act of exchanging sex for compensation under a strict set of conditions, thereby allowing for the formation of valid, albeit heavily regulated, commercial agreements.4 However, a critical analysis of Nevada's legal brothel system reveals it as a case study in modern governmental power, drawing on the theories of Michel Foucault.28 The system replaces punitive criminalization with a more pervasive and intimate form of administrative control, transforming the state's role from a punitive enforcer to a regulatory overseer.4

Mechanisms of Surveillance and Control

The transition from a prohibitionist to a regulatory model in Nevada is built upon a complex administrative apparatus designed to define, document, and control a specific class of worker.28 This bureaucratic framework functions not merely to license a business but to construct a new type of legal subject—the "registered sex worker"—whose existence is circumscribed by state-mandated criteria.28

At the heart of Nevada’s regulatory system is the mandatory registration and issuance of a "work card" by local law enforcement, typically the chief of police.28 This document is the culmination of an exhaustive data-gathering process that effectively creates a permanent, state-held archive on each individual worker.28 The application for registration requires a staggering amount of personal information, including the name given at birth and all subsequent names or aliases used, age, current address, a detailed physical description, a complete employment and working record for the preceding three years, and all residential addresses over the same period.28 This archival process is fortified by the collection of biometric data, as each application must be accompanied by a recent photograph and a full set of fingerprints.28 This meticulous documentation serves as a powerful Foucauldian technique of "individualization," rendering the worker a permanently legible and trackable subject for the state.28

The cornerstone of Nevada's regulatory model is a relentless and invasive regime of mandatory medical testing.28 This system, while publicly justified as a crucial public health initiative, functions as a primary site of biopolitical governance.28 State and local ordinances mandate that all registered prostitutes undergo testing at a relentless pace, which typically includes weekly tests for gonorrhea and

Chlamydia trachomatis and monthly blood tests for syphilis and the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV).27 The regulations are remarkably specific, dictating the precise methods of specimen collection with a level of granular control that underscores the depth of the state's intrusion.28 A critical legal instrument that enables this biopolitical system is the mandatory waiver of medical privacy. As a precondition for receiving a work card, applicants are required to sign a "waiver of release of medical information" and an "authorization to conduct an investigation".28 This act is not a choice but a requirement of entry into the legal profession, compelling the worker to formally surrender their right to medical confidentiality.28

The administrative and medical surveillance mechanisms that define Nevada's legal sex industry coalesce into a system that functions as a modern Panopticon.28 In this model, the roles of the chief of police and state health authorities are analogous to a central tower, as they are the designated recipients of the vast streams of information generated by the system.28 The knowledge that any deviation from the rules will be recorded and reported to this central authority induces what Foucault called "a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power".28 The major effect of the Panopticon is that it automates and disindividualizes power; the worker, caught in the power situation, begins to police themselves.28 The panoptic gaze of the state is extended and amplified through the deputization of brothel owners and managers as on-site enforcers, ensuring that the regulatory regime is implemented within the walls of their establishments.28

The State as Voyeur

The system of regulation in Nevada, often presented as a pragmatic alternative to prohibition, is not a system of liberation but of profound submission. The "contract" for legal sex work is fundamentally a contract to surrender privacy and bodily autonomy to the state's administrative and medical gaze. Legality is granted only in exchange for total transparency. To work legally in a Nevada brothel, a person must obtain a work card and submit to a rigorous testing schedule, which requires waiving medical privacy and authorizing a deep investigation into one's personal history.27 The state, therefore, makes the forfeiture of fundamental privacy rights a

precondition for legal employment. This reframes the entire system: it is not about the state "permitting" a transaction between two private parties; it is about the state inserting itself as the primary party to whom the worker must first submit. The worker's contract is not with the client; it is with the state, and the price of that contract is the surrender of autonomy. This institutionalized voyeurism, where the state's insatiable demand for knowledge of the worker's most private bodily details transcends neutral administration and becomes an end in itself, places the worker's body under the perpetual and exacting scrutiny of the state.28

Chapter 3: The Doctrinal Chasm of Consent

The foundation of any coherent legal model for sexual agreements rests upon a clear understanding of consent. However, California law employs two fundamentally distinct, and often conflicting, paradigms of consent. One is tailored to the world of commerce, prioritizing predictability and the objective manifestation of intent. The other is designed to protect the individual in moments of profound personal vulnerability, prioritizing subjective willingness and the absolute right to control one's own body. Understanding the deep chasm between these two legal constructs is the essential first step in building a framework that can bridge them.

Two Models of Consent

The cornerstone of an enforceable contract in California is "mutual assent" or a "meeting of the minds".16 Modern contract law has decisively shifted to an objective standard, focusing on outward expressions—words, writings, and conduct—and asking whether a reasonable person in the same circumstances would conclude that an agreement had been reached.16 This objective standard results in a static, point-in-time assessment of consent, evaluated at the moment of contract formation. Once mutual assent is objectively manifested, a binding contract is formed, and the consent, once given, is locked in.16

In stark contrast, California has established a subjective, dynamic, and rigorous standard for consent to sexual activity, often referred to as "Yes Means Yes".16 Codified in Penal Code § 261.6, this standard defines consent as "positive cooperation in act or attitude pursuant to an exercise of free will".16 Consent must be affirmative and active, conscious and voluntary, ongoing and specific, and, most critically, revocable at any time.16 A person who initially agrees to engage in sexual activity has the absolute right to change their mind and withdraw that consent at any point.16 Any sexual contact that occurs after consent has been withdrawn is non-consensual and may constitute sexual assault.16

The Consent Paradox

The philosophical underpinnings of these two legal standards are in direct opposition. The objective standard of contract law is a mechanism of economic efficiency and risk allocation, designed to protect the "benefit of the bargain".16 In contrast, the affirmative consent standard is a tool of personal protection, designed to safeguard the fundamental rights of bodily autonomy, dignity, and security.16 This creates a profound and irreconcilable conflict, a legal Catch-22 where it is impossible to simultaneously comply with both legal regimes in relationships involving economic exchange.17 The "logic of obscurity" required to avoid a prostitution charge under

Marvin is fundamentally incompatible with the "mandate of clarity" required for affirmative sexual consent.17

The state's conflicting consent standards create a form of systemic coercion. The affirmative consent standard requires an act to be free of coercion, force, or fear.16 However, the criminalization of prostitution, combined with its ambiguous application to relationships with financial components, creates a constant threat of investigation, arrest, and prosecution.5 This threat functions as a form of systemic coercion, where the state itself is the source of the fear that constrains the choices and communications of the individuals involved. Therefore, the state demands a standard of consent (clarity without fear) while its own laws create the very conditions (fear and the need for ambiguity) that make meeting that standard impossible. This is a profound legal contradiction.

Analogous Precedent for Heightened Consent

The doctrine of medical informed consent provides a powerful and direct analogy, demonstrating that for matters of significant bodily intrusion, California law has already abandoned the simple contractual model in favor of a heightened standard that prioritizes disclosure, knowing authorization, and an absolute right of revocation.16 California regulations explicitly state that a patient who consents to a treatment "has the right to revoke his or her consent for any reason at any time".16 The parallel to sexual consent is undeniable and compelling. If the law so fiercely protects a person's right to revoke consent to a potentially life-saving medical intervention, it must,

a fortiori, protect the right to revoke consent to a sexual act, regardless of any prior agreement.16

Part II: A Constructive Framework: The Prostitution-Miller Test in Theory and Practice

Having deconstructed the fractured and contradictory legal landscape, this report now turns to the construction of a new, coherent framework. This part builds the core of the proposed thesis, operationalizing the Miller test as a comprehensive analytical tool for differentiating licit sex work from the crime of prostitution. It will demonstrate how the California Supreme Court's precedent in People v. Freeman serves as the constitutional gateway for this analysis and will then systematically detail how each prong of the Prostitution-Miller Test functions to delineate the boundaries of legality and gives rise to a specific and corresponding set of legal obligations.

Chapter 4: The Constitutional Gateway: People v. Freeman and the Dominant Motive Test

The cornerstone of this First Amendment analysis is the California Supreme Court's decision in People v. Freeman (1988) 46 Cal.3d 419.5 In this landmark case, a film producer, Harold Freeman, was convicted of pandering for hiring and paying actors to engage in explicit sexual acts for a commercial adult film that was not alleged or found to be obscene.35 The California Supreme Court reversed the conviction, drawing a sharp constitutional line between artistic expression and prostitution.35 The court's reasoning hinged on the purpose of the financial transaction. It held that the payments made to the actors were not for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification, either for the producer or the actors themselves.36 Instead, the actors were paid fees for their services in creating a work of expression—a non-obscene film.36 The court concluded that applying the pandering statute to the hiring of actors for a non-obscene film would unlawfully impinge upon protected First Amendment rights.35

The Freeman decision does more than create an "exception" for pornography; it fundamentally redefines the mens rea (criminal intent) of prostitution. It shifts the legal inquiry from the act (sex for money) to the purpose of the transaction. Before Freeman, the simple exchange of a sexual act for money was sufficient to establish prostitution.5

Freeman adds a new element the prosecution must prove: that the payment was for the purpose of sexual gratification.35 This means that if the defendant can introduce reasonable doubt that the purpose was something else (e.g., art, therapy, procreation), the state cannot prove the requisite criminal intent. This transforms the legal landscape. The defense is no longer about denying the act occurred, but about re-characterizing its purpose. This aligns perfectly with the proposed thesis and provides a solid legal foundation for the Prostitution-Miller Test.

Connecting Freeman to the SLAPS Prong

The Freeman exception is a direct judicial analogue to the third prong of the Miller test ("lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" or SLAPS).5

Freeman essentially holds that if a compensated sexual act possesses "serious artistic value" (by being part of a non-obscene film), it is constitutionally protected expression and cannot be prosecuted as prostitution. This establishes the principle that "serious work" is a constitutionally recognized exemption from prostitution statutes. The creation of a non-obscene film is inherently an activity with "serious artistic value," thus removing it from the realm of unprotected conduct.5 This precedent provides a powerful legal basis for the argument that prostitution is a much narrower category of conduct than the broad class of all contracts for sexual services, and that non-obscenity, understood as the presence of "serious work," is a critical exemption.5

Chapter 5: A Tripartite Analysis of Compensated Sexual Acts

Building on the principles of Freeman and using the Miller test as an analytical framework, it is possible to construct a theoretical model for categorizing compensated sexual services. This model categorizes services based on which prong of the obscenity test they would fail, thereby distinguishing them from the conduct proscribed as prostitution.

Operationalizing the Prostitution-Miller Test

This model systematically applies each prong of the proposed test to delineate the boundaries of legality, integrating the specific claims from the initial query.

  • Prong 1: Prurient Interest vs. Substantial Purpose: This section analyzes how a dominant motive for a purpose other than "prurient interest" removes the act from the criminal sphere.5 The "prurient interest" is defined not as a healthy interest in sex, but as a shameful or morbid interest.39 A compensated sexual act undertaken for a substantial purpose other than appealing to a prurient interest would fall outside the rationale for both obscenity and prostitution laws. This could include sexual surrogacy, where a compensated partner works with a client under the supervision of a licensed therapist to address sexual dysfunction, with the purpose being therapeutic, not prurient.10 Another example is compensated gestational surrogacy, where the purpose is reproductive and has "serious scientific value".10

  • Prong 2: Patently Offensive Conduct, Affirmative Consent, and the Duty of Care: This section develops the novel connection between the "patently offensive" prong and the legal necessity of affirmative consent.16 It argues that in the context of a personal service, an act becomes "patently offensive" precisely when it violates the provider's autonomy. The failure to secure and maintain affirmative consent is, therefore, the failure of this prong. This is linked to a tort-based "duty of care" to prevent physical and emotional harm, where recklessness as to consent constitutes a breach of that duty.57 In California, Civil Code §1714(a) establishes a general duty of care that all persons owe to each other, holding individuals responsible for injuries caused by their actions, including negligence or recklessness.58 A breach of this duty of care is synonymous with negligence.58 In the context of a sexual encounter, this duty of care would require a person to act as a "reasonable person" would to prevent harm, which would include ensuring the presence of affirmative consent.

  • Prong 3: "Serious Work" and the Trigger for Regulation: This section elaborates on the link between the "SLAPS" value prong and the concept of "serious work".5 It argues that once an activity is established as having serious artistic, political, scientific, or other value, it transitions from a potentially illicit private act to a licit professional service. This transition triggers the application of the standard regulatory apparatus of a legitimate profession. The failure of this prong—meaning the act lacks serious value—is a necessary condition for it to be considered obscene conduct and, by analogy, prostitution. Conversely, the presence of serious value is what negates its criminality and subjects it to the routine obligations of legitimate work.

Chapter 6: The Attendant Obligations of a Licit Transaction

When a transaction for sexual services is deemed "serious work" and thus licit, it becomes subject to the same legal and regulatory obligations as any other professional service. This chapter details the specific duties that arise, focusing on taxation, worker classification, and other routine professional responsibilities.

Taxation and Worker Classification

An individual providing licit sexual services as "serious work" would generally be classified as an independent contractor or sole proprietor.63 This classification carries significant tax responsibilities. The service provider is considered self-employed and must report all income on their annual tax return (Form 1040 with a Schedule C) if they earn $400 or more.64 They are responsible for paying their own income tax and self-employment tax, which covers Social Security and Medicare taxes.63 These taxes must typically be paid in quarterly estimated payments to both the IRS and the California Franchise Tax Board (FTB) using forms like Form 540-ES.64

The hiring entity, or "service-recipient," also has obligations. If they pay an independent contractor $600 or more in a calendar year, they are required to report these payments to the IRS on Form 1099-NEC (Nonemployee Compensation) and to California's Employment Development Department (EDD) on a Report of Independent Contractor(s) (DE 542).63 This reporting is mandatory and helps state agencies track income for purposes such as child support enforcement.66

The ABC Test Applied

The most robust and constitutionally sound framework for analyzing the state's regulation of prostitution is not one based on public morality, but on the compelling state interest in regulating labor, commerce, and taxation.4 California law provides a powerful tool for analyzing labor relationships: the "ABC test," first established in

Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court and later codified by Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5).4 This test presumes that a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can prove all three of the following conditions: (A) The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity; (B) The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business; and (C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.18

When the ABC test is systematically applied to various models of sex work, it becomes clear that the legal classification of the worker is the lynchpin of a legitimate system. In most organized forms of prostitution, the relationship is one of employment, and the failure to treat the worker as such constitutes a direct violation of labor law.4 This reframes the felony offenses of pimping (PC § 266h) and pandering (PC § 266i) from morals offenses into crimes of severe labor exploitation.4 Pimping, the act of deriving support from the earnings of a prostitute, becomes the crime of illegally profiting from the labor of a misclassified, unprotected, and exploited employee—in effect, extreme wage theft and tax evasion.4 Pandering, the crime of recruiting individuals into prostitution, is transformed into the crime of illicit and often coercive labor recruitment, which, when it involves "promises, threats, violence, or by any device or scheme," aligns directly with the legal definition of labor trafficking.4

Regulatory Burdens

Beyond taxes and worker classification, engaging in licit sex work as a professional service entails other routine obligations. These include the necessity of using clear, written contracts to define the scope of work and payment terms, obtaining any required local business licenses, and implementing strict age verification protocols to ensure all parties have the legal capacity to contract.78 These are not extraordinary burdens but are the standard requirements for any individual operating as an independent contractor or sole proprietor in a personal services industry.78

Part III: The Laudable Contract: A Model for Legally and Ethically Sound Agreements

Having established a theoretical framework for distinguishing licit from illicit sexual services, the analysis now shifts from the abstract to the practical. This part of the report constructs a model for an agreement that is not merely legally defensible but also ethically robust and designed to promote the dignity and well-being of the parties involved. This "Laudable Contract" moves beyond the reactive logic of avoiding criminalization and instead builds a positive framework grounded in transparency, mutualism, and care.

Chapter 7: The Ethical Foundation: From Transactional Exchange to Relational Care

The creation of a laudable contract for personal, intimate services necessitates a profound theoretical shift away from the classical model of contract law, which is fundamentally ill-suited for this domain.23 Classical contract law, designed for discrete, one-off bargains between strangers, fails catastrophically when applied to contracts for ongoing, intimate, personal services.23 A new foundation is required—one that can account for the unique complexities of human intimacy, vulnerability, and interdependence. This foundation can be built by integrating the moral insights of Care Ethics with the legal doctrines of Relational Contract Theory.23

Care Ethics provides the moral substance for this new model. Rather than starting with the "autonomous-man-model," Care Ethics begins with a "relational ontology," conceiving of individuals not as isolated, self-sufficient atoms, but as beings who are fundamentally interconnected, vulnerable, and dependent.23 A moral contract, from this perspective, is one that obligates the parties not just to a set of transactional performances, but to a mutual practice of care, guided by principles of attentiveness, responsibility, competence, and responsiveness.23 While Care Ethics provides the moral "why," Relational Contract Theory provides the legal "how." This school of legal thought recognizes that many contracts are not discrete bargains but are better understood as frameworks for governing ongoing, long-term relationships.23 By explicitly framing a sexual services agreement as a relational contract, the parties can import the moral principles of Care Ethics directly into the legal structure of their agreement, transforming it from a static list of obligations into a dynamic charter for a relationship of mutual care.23

The Four-Quadrant Framework

By synthesizing the legal analysis of the licit/illicit axis with the ethical analysis of the moral/immoral axis, it becomes possible to create a comprehensive analytical tool: a four-quadrant framework.23 This matrix allows for a nuanced classification of any given sexual service agreement, moving beyond the simplistic and inaccurate collapse of all such agreements into a single, condemned category.23 It provides a map of the conceptual territory, identifying four distinct types of arrangements, each with its own legal and ethical character. Most importantly, the framework provides a clear strategic direction. It definitively identifies Quadrant 1, "The Laudable Contract," as the ideal state.23

The following table visualizes this framework, providing a clear taxonomy that deconstructs the monolithic category of "prostitution" and establishes the "Laudable Contract" as the clear objective for legal and ethical design.23

**Licit (Lawful Consideration)** Conduct is not prohibited by a narrow, intent-based reading of criminal statutes (e.g., PC § 647(b) per *Freeman*). **Illicit (Unlawful Consideration)** Conduct is prohibited by a broad reading of criminal statutes or fails to meet the requirements for a protected purpose.
**Moral (Ethically Sound)** Agreement respects autonomy, upholds dignity, and is structured relationally to prevent objectification and exploitation. **Quadrant 1: The Laudable Contract** - **Description:** A direct, transparent, and enforceable agreement for sexual services grounded in a protected purpose (e.g., art, procreation, study). - **Principles:** Relational, based on care, mutualism, and ethical design. Full affirmative consent is a non-negotiable condition precedent. - **Example:** A written contract between two adults for a long-term relationship involving sexual intimacy and mutual support, explicitly stating procreation or the creation of a stable family unit as a dominant motive. **Quadrant 2: The Principled but Unenforceable Pact** - **Description:** An agreement made between equals with full respect and care, but structured in a way that a court would likely deem a direct exchange for gratification. - **Principles:** Ethically sound in its interpersonal dynamics but legally naive. - **Example:** A verbal agreement for compensated companionship that includes sex, where the parties act with mutual respect but have no documentation of a dominant purpose beyond gratification, making it legally vulnerable.
**Immoral (Ethically Flawed)** Agreement is exploitative, objectifying, coercive, or fails to respect the full humanity and autonomy of one or both parties. **Quadrant 3: The Legally Permissible but Exploitative Arrangement** - **Description:** A contract that is technically legal under a permissive interpretation (*Freeman*) but is substantively unfair or coercive. - **Principles:** Transactional, power-imbalanced, and objectifying. - **Example:** A contract for an "artistic" performance that meets the *Freeman* test on its face but contains unconscionable clauses, pays far below a fair rate, and treats the performer as a disposable commodity. **Quadrant 4: The Meretricious and Unlawful Transaction** - **Description:** The archetypal prohibited act. The agreement is both illegal under the law and ethically indefensible. - **Principles:** Coercive, exploitative, and directly contravenes public policy and criminal statutes. - **Example:** Human trafficking, or any arrangement involving coercion, lack of meaningful consent, and a direct exchange of a lewd act for external compensation without any protected purpose.

Chapter 8: A Model Framework for the Laudable Contract

The culmination of this theoretical work is a practical, structural model of a laudable contract. This model integrates the legal requirements for licit consideration with the ethical principles of care, relationality, and mutualism. It is not a one-size-fits-all template but a flexible framework of essential components designed to be adapted to the specific needs of the parties while adhering to a core set of protective and empowering principles.23

Structural Components

A contract designed to fall within Quadrant 1 would contain the following key structural components 23:

  • Preamble and Statement of Purpose: This is the contract's foundational and most critical section. Legally, it will explicitly state the dominant, legally protected purpose of the agreement, thereby satisfying the "Dominant Motive Test" from People v. Freeman and establishing the sexual services as licit consideration. Ethically, it will define the agreement as a relational contract intended to foster a long-term relationship of mutual care, trust, and support, setting the interpretive frame for the entire document.23

  • Ethical Principles Clause: This is a dedicated clause that memorializes the ethical commitments of the parties. It would explicitly state that the parties agree to conduct their relationship in accordance with the core principles of Care Ethics: attentiveness, responsibility, competence, and responsiveness. Including these principles as express terms transforms them from abstract ideals into enforceable contractual obligations.23

  • Scope of Services and Mutual Obligations: This section will provide a clear description of the parties' roles and responsibilities, drafted with flexibility in mind, acknowledging that not all future needs can be foreseen. Care must be taken to define roles in a way that establishes an independent contractor relationship, avoiding the indicators of an employer-employee relationship that can create legal complications.23

  • The Consent Protocol (The Condition Precedent Solution): This clause is the technical and ethical linchpin that harmonizes contract law with sexual consent law. It operationalizes the bifurcated consent standard by framing ongoing, affirmative consent as a condition precedent to performance. The clause will state unequivocally that the performance of any sexual act contemplated by the agreement is subject to a Condition Precedent: the existence of ongoing, affirmative, enthusiastic, and freely given consent from all parties at the time of the act. It will further state, explicitly, that the withdrawal or revocation of consent at any point before or during a sexual act shall not constitute a breach of contract. Instead, the revocation of consent means the condition precedent for performance has failed. When a condition precedent fails, the legal duty to perform that specific act never arises. This structure elegantly resolves the "revocation-as-breach" paradox, protecting the absolute, non-waivable right to bodily autonomy while preserving the integrity of the contract's other commercial terms.16

  • Consideration and Mutual Benefit Structure: This clause will detail the financial and other forms of consideration, designed according to the principles of mutualism to create positive feedback loops and reinforce the value of the ongoing collaborative relationship.23

  • Communication and Dispute Resolution: Recognizing that conflict is a natural part of any long-term relationship, this section will establish a mandatory, multi-tiered process for addressing disagreements, designed to preserve the relationship rather than terminate it. It might require regular check-ins, structured good-faith conversations, and mandatory mediation before resorting to more adversarial options.23

Chapter 9: Case Studies in Permissible Purpose

This chapter will apply the report's entire framework (Prostitution-Miller Test and Laudable Contract model) to three concrete examples of "serious work" in California, demonstrating the practical application of the proposed legal and ethical principles.

Compensated Gestational Surrogacy

The most well-developed and legally sanctioned example of a compensated contract involving reproductive acts is gestational surrogacy.10 California's comprehensive and permissive legal framework, established in Family Code §§ 7960-7962, serves as a powerful precedent, demonstrating the state's ability to regulate and enforce complex agreements involving payment for the use of another person's body for a serious purpose, thereby distinguishing it from criminal activity.10 The legal framework for surrogacy in California provides a complete and successful blueprint for how the state can regulate lawful contracts involving bodily autonomy and compensation. The arrangement involves significant payment for the use of a person's body to achieve a specific and serious purpose—the creation of a family, which can be classified as having "serious scientific value" in the context of assisted reproductive technology.10 The existing legal model, with its requirements for independent counsel and detailed written contracts, has already solved the key challenges inherent in such agreements, demonstrating that the state is fully capable of drawing a bright line between a compensated act for a serious purpose and the crime of prostitution.48

Surrogate Partner Therapy

The intersection of sexual acts and therapeutic practices presents a legally complex and sensitive area.10 While California law strictly prohibits sexual contact within the context of licensed therapy to prevent exploitation, it also provides a separate framework for unlicensed healing arts.10 This creates a potential, albeit narrow, pathway for lawful agreements involving sexual acts for therapeutic purposes, provided they are structured with full transparency and outside the traditional, licensed medical paradigm.10 An individual acting as a "sexual surrogate" or providing other forms of hands-on sexual therapy, if structured correctly, might operate within the unlicensed practitioner framework. To do so, the practitioner would have to scrupulously avoid presenting themselves as a licensed medical or psychological professional and comply with all disclosure requirements.10 An agreement for such an act, framed under the explicit disclosures required by the Business and Professions Code, could be argued as a lawful contract for an unlicensed healing art, distinguishing it from both professional misconduct and the criminal act of prostitution by grounding its legality in the principles of informed consent, full disclosure, and a clearly articulated therapeutic (rather than purely gratificational) intent.10

Performance Art

The distinction between constitutionally protected artistic expression and a public order crime like indecent exposure provides another clear example of how intent and context define the legality of an act involving the human body.10 The same physical act—public nudity or a simulated sex act—can be either a criminal offense or a protected performance, with the decisive factor being the purpose behind the act.10 This principle directly parallels the distinction between prostitution and protected expression, offering a strong basis for legalizing compensated sexual acts performed as art.10 This legal logic directly mirrors the reasoning in

People v. Freeman. In Freeman, the court differentiated between payment for "sexual gratification" (which would be prostitution) and payment for "acting" in an expressive work. Similarly, in the context of public nudity, the law distinguishes between exposure for "lewd" gratification (indecent exposure) and exposure for an artistic or political message (protected expression). Intent is the dispositive legal factor in both scenarios.10 Therefore, a contract that frames a sexual act as part of an artistic performance would place the conduct squarely on the protected side of this legal line.

Conclusion: Toward a New Jurisprudence of Autonomy, Clarity, and Consent

The prevailing legal framework governing commercial sexual services is predicated on a categorical failure to distinguish between exploitation and enterprise. By grouping all exchanges of sex for compensation under the single, pejorative heading of "prostitution," the law employs the blunt instrument of criminalization to address a complex social and economic phenomenon, with demonstrably poor results. This report has constructed a multi-layered legal and constitutional argument for a fundamental paradigm shift toward a jurisprudence that recognizes the legitimacy of consensual "sexual service contracts" while retaining robust legal tools to combat the true harms of exploitation and coercion.

The analysis demonstrates that a legal framework presuming the legality of such arrangements is vastly superior to one presuming their illegality. A presumption of legality aligns with the bedrock principles of criminal justice, including the presumption of innocence and the state's burden of proof. It fosters legislative clarity, focuses law enforcement resources on actual harm such as coercion and trafficking, and protects the fundamental rights of personal autonomy and freedom of contract.10 The precedents set by

People v. Freeman and the Miller obscenity test provide a clear constitutional pathway for structuring lawful, enforceable contracts for sexual acts as a form of protected expression.10 Furthermore, California's successful and comprehensive regulation of paid surrogacy contracts offers a complete and functioning legislative and judicial blueprint for how the state can and does manage compensated agreements involving bodily autonomy for a "serious" purpose.10

Based on these findings, this report offers the following multi-layered recommendations to resolve the existing legal ambiguity in a manner that is consistent with constitutional principles, contractual freedom, and sound social policy.10

Legislative Recommendations

  • Amend Penal Code § 647(b) to Include a "Safe Harbor" Provision: The Legislature should amend PC § 647(b) to add a new subsection explicitly stating that the statute does not apply to sexual acts conducted pursuant to a written agreement in which the parties attest that the purpose of the exchange is for a lawful, non-prostitutive purpose. Such enumerated purposes could include, but not be limited to, artistic performance, therapeutic application, reproductive assistance, or as an element of a broader contract for companionship and domestic support.10

  • Create a Statutory Affirmative Defense: As an alternative or complementary measure, the Legislature should create a statutory affirmative defense to a charge under PC § 647(b). This defense would be established by the defendant producing a written contract, executed prior to the alleged offense, that details a lawful purpose for the exchange of value and the sexual acts.10

Judicial Recommendations

  • Adopt a Clarifying Jury Instruction: The California Judicial Council should develop and adopt a standard criminal jury instruction (CALCRIM) for PC § 647(b) prosecutions. This instruction should explicitly state that the prosecution bears the burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that the sole or dominant purpose of the compensation was a direct quid pro quo for a lewd act for the purpose of sexual gratification. The instruction should further clarify that the existence of other valid forms of consideration—such as companionship, emotional support, domestic services, or shared living expenses—can be sufficient to create reasonable doubt as to the element of specific criminal intent.10

  • Affirm the Applicability of Freeman Beyond Commercial Film: In relevant cases, appellate courts should explicitly affirm that the core principle of People v. Freeman—distinguishing payment for expressive performance from payment for sexual gratification—is not limited to the context of commercial filmmaking but applies to any situation where a sexual act is performed as part of a non-obscene expressive work.10

Policy Recommendations

  • Shift Law Enforcement Priorities: State and local law enforcement agencies should issue policy directives to shift resources away from undercover sting operations targeting consensual sexual activity between adults. Resources should be redirected toward investigating and combating non-consensual crimes, such as human trafficking, sexual assault, and the exploitation of minors, where tangible harm is evident.10

  • Promote Public Education on Contractual Rights: The State Bar of California and legal aid organizations should develop and disseminate public-facing educational materials informing citizens of their right to structure personal and intimate relationships through clear, consensual contracts. This would empower individuals to protect their interests and clarify their arrangements, reducing ambiguity and the potential for disputes or unwarranted criminal investigation.10
