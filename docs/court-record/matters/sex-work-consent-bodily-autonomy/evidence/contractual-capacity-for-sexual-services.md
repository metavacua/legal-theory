# **Beyond Illicidity: A Relational Theory of Moral and Licit Contracts for Sexual Services**

  
  

## **Introduction: The Limits of Ambiguity and the Need for a Constructive Framework**

  

The legal and social discourse surrounding compensated sexual services is mired in a state of perpetual ambiguity, largely trapped within a false dichotomy that presumes all such exchanges are inherently illicit. This presumption has given rise to a set of "conventional best practices"—such as decoupling finances from intimacy, framing support as a gift, or relying on severable non-sexual consideration—that are, at their core, reactive and defensive maneuvers. These strategies are predicated on an assumption of illegality; they are workarounds designed to obscure the nature of an agreement rather than to affirm its legitimacy. By operating from a posture of avoidance, they inadvertently reinforce the very legal and social prohibitions they seek to navigate, perpetuating a cycle of legal precarity and moral condemnation. This approach fails to provide a stable or dignified foundation for individuals seeking to structure their intimate and economic lives with clarity and security.

This report argues for a fundamental paradigm shift. It moves beyond the reactive logic of avoidance to construct a positive legal and ethical theory for direct, transparent, and enforceable contracts for sexual services. The purpose is not to find better ways to hide the bargain, but to articulate a framework under which the bargain itself can be rendered both lawful and moral. Such a framework is not only possible but is latent within existing California jurisprudence and well-established ethical theories. By systematically deconstructing the conflated categories of the moral and the legal, and by reconstructing a contractual model grounded in principles of relationality and mutualism, it is possible to define a category of "laudable" contracts that uphold the dignity, autonomy, and well-being of all parties.

The methodology employed herein is twofold. Part I is an act of deconstruction. It systematically untangles the legal and moral landscape by establishing two distinct analytical axes: the licit/illicit axis, which defines the boundaries of lawful consideration under California law, and the moral/immoral axis, which evaluates the ethical structure of an agreement. This analysis culminates in a four-quadrant framework that provides a clear taxonomy for classifying sexual service agreements, moving them beyond a monolithic and pejorative categorization. Part II is an act of construction. It builds upon this analytical foundation to propose a novel theory of the laudable contract. This model rejects the transactional premises of classical contract law, instead drawing upon the rich traditions of Care Ethics and Relational Contract Theory to propose a framework that is ethically robust, legally sophisticated, and practically achievable. This report, therefore, aims to transcend the current impasse by offering a coherent and principled path toward contractual enforceability, personal dignity, and radical autonomy.

## **Part I: Deconstructing the Legal and Moral Landscape: A Four-Quadrant Analysis**

  

The first step in constructing a viable theory for laudable sexual service contracts is to clear the conceptual ground. Historically, legal and moral judgments in this domain have been conflated, leading to a landscape where any compensated sexual act is presumptively both illegal and immoral. To move forward, these concepts must be rigorously separated and analyzed on their own terms. This part of the report is dedicated to that task. It will first establish the legal axis of licit versus illicit, demonstrating that the lawfulness of a contract is not an intrinsic quality of the sexual act but a contingent outcome of statutory interpretation. It will then establish the ethical axis of moral versus immoral, arguing that morality is determined not by the presence of compensation but by the relational structure of the agreement. Finally, it will synthesize these two axes into a comprehensive four-quadrant framework, providing the analytical clarity necessary for the constructive work that follows.

  

### **Chapter 1: The Licit/Illicit Axis - Defining the Boundaries of Lawful Consideration**

  

The enforceability of any contract hinges on the presence of lawful consideration.1 In the context of sexual services, the determination of what is "lawful" is not straightforward. It requires a nuanced understanding of the distinction between "licit," meaning permitted or not forbidden by law, and "illicit," meaning unlawful or forbidden.3 This chapter will demonstrate that for contracts involving sexual services, the lawfulness of the consideration is not an absolute, but is instead contingent upon the specific interpretation of the relevant criminal statutes.

  

#### **Defining the Terms: The Analogy of Licit and Illicit Substances**

  

A powerful analogy for separating the concepts of legality and morality can be found in the regulation of drugs. Licit substances, such as alcohol, caffeine, and prescription medications, are legal to produce, sell, and consume, often within certain regulatory frameworks.6 Their legality, however, does not preclude their potential for immoral or harmful use. A person can use alcohol in a way that is destructive to themselves and others, yet the substance itself remains licit. Conversely, illicit drugs like heroin or cocaine are classified as illegal because they are deemed to pose a significant threat to individuals and society.6

Applying this distinction to sexual services is illuminating. The prevailing view often treats all compensated sexual activity as if it were an illicit substance—categorically forbidden. However, a more precise analysis reveals a situation closer to that of a licit substance. The act of sex, like the consumption of alcohol, is a legal and constitutionally protected activity between consenting adults. The introduction of compensation does not automatically transform it into a categorically prohibited act. Instead, it places the conduct within a regulatory field where its legality becomes contingent on specific factors, much as the legality of driving is contingent on factors like speed and sobriety. The critical task, therefore, is to identify the specific conditions under which compensated sexual conduct crosses the line from licit to illicit under the law.

  

#### **The Contingency of "Lawful Consideration"**

  

In contract law, consideration is the "quid pro quo"—the something of value exchanged between parties that transforms a mere promise into an enforceable agreement.8 For a contract to be valid, the consideration must be "lawful".2 An agreement founded upon unlawful consideration—for example, a contract to commit a crime—is void and unenforceable as a matter of public policy.11

In California, the legal status of a contract for sexual services is held hostage by the interpretation of Penal Code § 647(b), the statute prohibiting prostitution. This statute criminalizes soliciting, agreeing to engage in, or engaging in a "lewd act" for "money or other consideration." The *Marvin v. Marvin* case, which governs non-marital partnership agreements, reflects this dependency. The court in *Marvin* held that contracts resting upon the "immoral and illicit consideration of meretricious sexual services" are unenforceable. The term "illicit" here is not an independent moral judgment by the court; it is a direct reference to conduct deemed unlawful by the criminal code.

This creates a critical chain of dependency: the civil enforceability of a contract for sexual services depends on whether the consideration (the sexual services) is lawful, and the lawfulness of that consideration is determined by whether the underlying exchange constitutes a violation of the criminal statute against prostitution. Therefore, to understand how to form a *licit* contract, one must first understand the precise boundaries of what constitutes an *illicit* act under PC § 647(b).

  

#### **Establishing Licit Consideration Through Legal Interpretation**

  

The analysis provided in the user's distillation reveals that the prohibition under PC § 647(b) is not absolute. Its scope is contested and subject to at least three distinct interpretations, two of which create clear pathways for sexual services to function as licit consideration.

First, the **Prohibitionist Interpretation (Argument II-A)** represents the conventional and most restrictive view. It holds that any agreement where external compensation is the quid pro quo for a "lewd act" (defined as physical touching of intimate parts for sexual gratification) constitutes criminal prostitution. Under this interpretation, sexual services provided for the purpose of gratification are, by definition, "meretricious" and therefore constitute illicit consideration, rendering any explicit contract for them void. This logic forces parties into the "workaround" strategies of severability and ambiguity.

Second, the **Intent-Based Interpretation (Argument II-B)**, derived from the California Supreme Court's ruling in *People v. Freeman*, provides a crucial exception. *Freeman* established that the exchange of money for a sexual act is not prostitution unless the payment is made for the *specific purpose of sexual gratification*. This creates what can be termed the "Dominant Motive Test." If the dominant motive or purpose of the exchange is something other than sexual gratification—such as artistic expression (e.g., performance art), scientific study, or education—then the requisite criminal intent (*mens rea*) is absent. Consequently, the act is not criminal, and the sexual service provided for that protected purpose constitutes *licit consideration*. The legality of the contract is thus determined not by the physical act itself, but by the legally recognized purpose for which the act is performed.

Third, the **Constitutional Interpretation (Argument II-C)** builds upon *Freeman* and reinforces its conclusion through the lens of the First Amendment. This view posits that PC § 647(b) must be interpreted narrowly to avoid infringing upon constitutionally protected rights of expression, privacy, and association. Under this framework, a compensated sexual act is only illicit if it constitutes either purely commercial conduct lacking any protected purpose (aligning with *Freeman*) or if it qualifies as "impermissible expression," such as legal obscenity under the *Miller* test. This interpretation suggests that compensated sexual acts undertaken for a serious, constitutionally protected purpose—which could plausibly include not only art and science but also procreation and the formation of a family unit—are not illegal. The sexual services rendered in such a context are therefore licit, and a contract explicitly memorializing such an arrangement would rest on lawful consideration.

The crucial takeaway from this analysis is that the legal status of a sexual contract is not fixed by the nature of the act itself, but by the *narrative, intent, and purpose* constructed around the act. This reframes the task of legal drafting. The goal is not to hide the sexual consideration but to situate it within a legally permissible framework, thereby affirmatively establishing it as "licit" and rendering the contract enforceable. This insight directly challenges the foundational assumption of the "decouple and obscure" logic that dominates conventional advice.

  

### **Chapter 2: The Moral/Immoral Axis - Engaging with the Ethics of Commodification and Autonomy**

  

A contract can be perfectly legal yet profoundly immoral. The second axis of analysis, therefore, moves beyond the question of what is permissible under the law to what is ethically defensible. This requires engaging with deep philosophical questions about the commodification of human intimacy and the nature of personal autonomy. This chapter will develop a framework for distinguishing "moral" contracts, which enhance human dignity and flourishing, from "immoral" ones, which are exploitative or objectifying.

  

#### **The Problem of Commodification**

  

Commodification is the process of transforming goods, services, ideas, or even people, which may exist outside the market, into commodities or objects of trade.12 When applied to intimate or personal services, this process raises significant ethical concerns, primarily articulated through two major philosophical traditions.

The **Marxist critique** focuses on the concept of alienation. In this view, an object or service has a "use-value"—its intrinsic worth and the human relationships and meanings associated with it—and an "exchange-value"—its price on the market.12 Commodification, particularly under capitalism, prioritizes exchange-value to such an extent that it erases use-value. Applied to sexuality, this critique suggests that a purely transactional exchange strips the act of its potential for human connection, intimacy, and mutual expression, alienating the participants from their own bodies and from each other.15 The service provider's labor is objectified, and the rich, relational context of sexuality is reduced to a sterile economic transaction.

The **Kantian critique** centers on the concept of human dignity. Immanuel Kant argued that rational beings possess an intrinsic worth, or dignity, that demands they always be treated as an "end in themselves" and never as a *mere means* to an end.13 Objects have a price and can be replaced; persons have dignity and are irreplaceable. From this perspective, an immoral sexual transaction is one in which a person is treated as a mere instrument for another's gratification—an object to be used. This is the essence of sexual objectification, a central concern in many feminist analyses of sex markets, which argue that such markets can harm women by reducing them to their sexual functions.16

  

#### **Counterarguments from Autonomy and Property**

  

Against these critiques stands a powerful counter-narrative rooted in the liberal tradition of individual liberty and self-ownership. Drawing from the philosophy of John Locke, this perspective views the body as a form of property over which the individual exercises sovereign control.13 If one owns one's body and labor, then one has the right to decide how to use them, including entering into voluntary contracts for their use.

This framework prioritizes the ethical principle of **autonomy**, defined as the right to self-determination and the capacity to make informed, uncoerced decisions about one's own life.19 From this viewpoint, to deny a competent adult the right to contract for sexual services is a form of paternalism that infringes upon their fundamental liberty. The ethical focus shifts from prohibiting the exchange to ensuring the conditions for true consent are met: that the decision is fully informed, voluntary, and free from coercion or exploitation.

  

#### **Defining the "Immoral" vs. "Moral" Contract**

  

These competing philosophical views do not lead to an impasse. Instead, they provide the criteria for distinguishing between moral and immoral agreements. The core ethical problem is not the exchange of a service for compensation, but the *manner and structure* of that exchange.

An **immoral contract** is one that embodies the negative aspects of commodification and violates personal autonomy. Its defining characteristics are:

  - **Exploitation:** The terms are grossly unfair, or one party leverages a significant power imbalance to take advantage of the other's vulnerability. This can manifest as unfair compensation, unsafe conditions, or coercive control.11
  - **Objectification:** The structure of the agreement treats the service provider as a mere instrument for gratification, disregarding their agency, dignity, and broader humanity. This is the Kantian harm of being treated as a mere means.13
  - **Alienation:** The exchange is structured as a purely transactional, depersonalized event that erases the human and relational element, fulfilling the Marxist critique.

Conversely, a **moral contract** is one that is intentionally designed to counteract these harms and is grounded in principles of respect and mutual flourishing. Its defining characteristics are:

  - **Respect for Autonomy:** The agreement is founded on the principle of self-determination. It requires full, informed consent from all parties and, crucially, recognizes that consent in the sexual context must be ongoing and revocable.19
  - **Upholding Dignity:** The contractual framework is structured to ensure that all parties are treated as whole human beings with inherent worth. It acknowledges their needs, boundaries, and well-being beyond the mere performance of the service.
  - **Fostering Connection (or Preventing Alienation):** The agreement prioritizes the relational "use-value" of the interaction. Even within a commercial context, it makes space for mutual respect, communication, and care, preventing the relationship from being entirely subsumed by its "exchange-value."

The contract document itself thus becomes a moral artifact. Its clauses are not neutral; they can either reinforce a harmful, transactional, and objectifying paradigm or they can actively establish a respectful, fair, and relational one. The morality of the agreement is not a pre-existing condition but is something that is constructed—or destroyed—through the act of contracting itself.

## **Part II: A Constructive Theory of the Laudable Contract**

  

Having deconstructed the legal and moral landscape and identified the "Laudable Contract" as the ideal, the task now shifts to construction. How can such a contract be designed, executed, and justified? This part of the report develops a comprehensive theory to answer that question. It argues that achieving a laudable contract requires a fundamental departure from the traditional, transactional model of contract law. It proposes a new foundation built on the synthesis of two powerful theoretical frameworks: Care Ethics, which provides the moral substance, and Relational Contract Theory, which provides the legal form. From this foundation, it derives a set of practical principles for ethical contract design and mutualism, culminating in a model framework for a contract that is legally defensible, ethically robust, and designed to support the flourishing of the individuals involved.

  

### **Chapter 4: The Ethical Foundation - From Transactional Exchange to Relational Care**

  

The creation of a laudable contract for personal, intimate services necessitates a profound theoretical shift. The classical model of contract law, which forms the bedrock of most legal thinking, is fundamentally ill-suited for this domain. A new foundation is required—one that can account for the unique complexities of human intimacy, vulnerability, and interdependence. This foundation can be built by integrating the moral insights of Care Ethics with the legal doctrines of Relational Contract Theory.

  

#### **The Inadequacy of the Classical Model**

  

Classical contract law, as it developed in the 19th century, was implicitly designed for a specific paradigm: discrete, one-off bargains between strangers transacting in a perfect market.22 It views contracting parties as rational, self-interested, and autonomous actors. Its principles are objective, standardized, and static, focusing on enforcing the explicit terms of a bargain as it was struck at a single point in time.

This model fails catastrophically when applied to contracts for ongoing, intimate, personal services. Such relationships are not discrete transactions; they are evolving processes. The parties are not strangers but are deeply interconnected. They are not always perfectly rational or invulnerable; the context is often one of profound vulnerability, emotional complexity, and asymmetrical dependency. To apply the rigid, transactional logic of classical contract law to such a relationship is to use a tool that is not merely inadequate but actively harmful, as it ignores the very elements—trust, communication, care, flexibility—that are essential for the relationship's success and the parties' well-being.

  

#### **Adopting a Relational Ontology with Care Ethics**

  

The necessary alternative begins with adopting a different understanding of the human person, one offered by the moral theory of Care Ethics. Rather than starting with the "autonomous-man-model," Care Ethics begins with a "relational ontology".23 It conceives of individuals not as isolated, self-sufficient atoms, but as beings who are fundamentally "mutually interconnected, vulnerable and dependent".23 This perspective, which draws its initial insights from the relationship between a caregiver and a cared-for, provides a far more accurate and useful model for understanding intimate relationships than the classical legal model of two equal and detached bargainers.25

Care Ethics is not merely a descriptive theory; it is a normative one that provides a set of guiding principles for moral conduct within these relationships. As articulated by theorist Joan Tronto, the practice of care involves four key ethical elements 24:

1.  **Attentiveness:** Acknowledging and being attuned to the needs of others.
2.  **Responsibility:** Taking action to meet those identified needs.
3.  **Competence:** Possessing the skills to provide care effectively.
4.  **Responsiveness:** Being receptive to the feedback of the care-receiver to ensure their needs are actually being met.

A moral contract, from this perspective, is one that is built upon these principles. It is an agreement that obligates the parties not just to a set of transactional performances, but to a mutual practice of care. It seeks to maintain and strengthen the relationship itself by promoting the well-being of both parties.25

  

#### **Relational Contract Theory as the Legal Vehicle**

  

While Care Ethics provides the moral "why," Relational Contract Theory provides the legal "how." This school of legal thought, developed in response to the shortcomings of the classical model, recognizes that many contracts are not discrete bargains but are better understood as frameworks for governing ongoing, long-term relationships.26 These contracts are often incomplete by design, as the parties cannot possibly foresee all future contingencies. Their success depends less on the explicit written terms and more on the implicit norms of trust, cooperation, flexibility, and good faith that develop between the parties.28

Relational Contract Theory is the perfect legal vehicle for operationalizing the values of Care Ethics. It allows a court to look beyond the four corners of the document and recognize the nature of the underlying relationship. In cases identified as "relational," courts have shown a greater willingness to imply duties of good faith and fair dealing, not as a generic default rule, but as a necessary component of the parties' bargain.28 These contracts "require a high degree of communication, cooperation and predictable performance based on mutual trust and confidence and involve expectations of loyalty".28

By explicitly framing a sexual services agreement as a relational contract, the parties can import the moral principles of Care Ethics directly into the legal structure of their agreement. The contract ceases to be a static list of obligations and becomes a dynamic charter for a relationship of mutual care. This synthesis is transformative: Care Ethics provides the moral justification for why these agreements demand a different approach, while Relational Contract Theory provides the legal doctrine to implement that approach in a way that courts can recognize and enforce.

  

### **Chapter 5: Principles of Ethical Contract Design and Mutualism**

  

Grounding a contract in the theories of care and relationality is the necessary first step. The next is to translate those abstract theories into a concrete set of actionable principles for the drafting and negotiation process. A laudable contract must be ethically designed from its inception, and its internal logic must be structured for mutualism, creating a self-sustaining and cooperative relationship.

  

#### **The Contract as an Ethical Artifact**

  

The concept of "Ethical Design," often applied to technology and product development, is powerfully applicable to the creation of legal agreements.31 This approach views the contract not as a neutral technical document but as an artifact—a "user interface" for the relationship—that should be intentionally designed to promote ethical outcomes. It requires a conscious focus on core principles throughout the design process.31

Key principles of ethical contract design include:

  - **Transparency and Disclosure:** The contract must be written in clear, understandable language, avoiding legal jargon or "dark patterns"—manipulative clauses designed to confuse or mislead.31 All terms, risks, benefits, and expectations must be fully and honestly disclosed, ensuring genuine informed consent.34
  - **Fairness and Equity:** The substance of the agreement must be fair and equitable. This involves not only fair compensation but also a just allocation of risks, responsibilities, and rewards, explicitly avoiding exploitative terms that take advantage of power imbalances.34
  - **Collaborative Creation:** An ethically designed contract is not imposed by one party upon the other. It is co-created through a process of "value negotiation," where all stakeholders are involved in defining the goals and ethical boundaries of the project.33 This fosters buy-in and mutual respect from the outset.
  - **Prioritizing Well-being:** The ultimate goal of the design is to protect and promote the holistic well-being—physical, emotional, and financial—of all parties involved. The contract should be a tool for safety and security, not a source of stress or precarity.19

  

#### **Structuring for Mutualism and Self-Enforcement**

  

Beyond its ethical design, the internal mechanics of a laudable contract should be structured to foster mutualism. A mutualistic relationship is one in which the actions that benefit one party also benefit the other, creating a system of aligned interests.38 The goal is to design a contract that is largely self-enforcing because cooperation is the most rational and beneficial strategy for everyone involved.36

This can be achieved by applying the concept of **Partner Fidelity Feedback (PFF)**, a theory drawn from evolutionary biology and economics to explain the stability of cooperative systems.40 PFF occurs when the benefits a donor provides to a recipient automatically feed back to the donor. For example, a bee pollinating a flower receives nectar; the flower, in turn, gets to reproduce. There is no need for external enforcement or punishment, because the feedback loop is inherent to the interaction.

In a contractual context, this means structuring the consideration and obligations to create a positive feedback loop. Instead of a simple fee-for-service model, the agreement might include:

  - **Shared Goals and Rewards:** Tying compensation or benefits to the achievement of mutual objectives that require collaboration.
  - **Interdependent Performance:** Structuring obligations so that one party's successful performance enables or enhances the other's ability to perform and receive benefits.
  - **Gain/Pain Sharing:** Implementing clauses where both parties share in the upside of unexpected successes and the downside of unforeseen challenges, fostering a "we're in this together" mentality.26

By building such a system, the contract transforms from a static document focused on remedies for breach into a dynamic system for managing a relationship. Its primary function becomes the active cultivation of trust and cooperation. Legal enforcement remains a crucial backstop, but it is a secondary mechanism, not the primary driver of behavior. A truly laudable contract is not one that is merely effective in court after a breach, but one that is so well-designed that it prevents the breach from occurring in the first place by making the continuation of the healthy relationship more valuable to both parties than any potential gain from defection.

The culmination of this theoretical work is a practical, structural model of a laudable contract. This model integrates the legal requirements for licit consideration with the ethical principles of care, relationality, and mutualism. It is not a one-size-fits-all template but a flexible framework of essential components designed to be adapted to the specific needs of the parties while adhering to a core set of protective and empowering principles.

  - A commitment to regular, scheduled check-ins for open communication.
  - A requirement for a structured, good-faith conversation as a first step in any dispute.
  - Progression to formal mediation with a neutral third party before any party can resort to more adversarial options like arbitration or litigation.35

By adopting this comprehensive and ethically informed framework, parties can create agreements for sexual services that are not merely legally defensible under permissive interpretations of California law, but that are also morally laudable. Such a contract becomes a tool not for exploitation or objectification, but for the clear, safe, and respectful structuring of a relationship that promotes the autonomy, dignity, and flourishing of all involved.

  

## **Conclusion: The Path to Enforceability, Dignity, and Autonomy**

  

This report has charted a course from the deconstruction of a flawed and ambiguous legal landscape to the construction of a coherent and robust theory of the laudable contract for sexual services. The journey began by untangling the conflated concepts of legality and morality, revealing that the lawfulness of such an agreement under California law is not absolute but contingent on intent and constitutionally protected purpose, while its morality is determined by the relational structure of the exchange. This analysis produced a four-quadrant framework that provides a clear taxonomy for these agreements, identifying the "Laudable Contract"—one that is both licit and moral—as the ultimate objective.

The constructive part of this work proposed a fundamental paradigm shift, moving away from the inadequate transactional model of classical contract law. In its place, it erected a new foundation based on the synthesis of Care Ethics, which provides the moral imperative to center vulnerability and mutual well-being, and Relational Contract Theory, which provides the legal vehicle for recognizing and enforcing agreements built on trust and cooperation. From this foundation, a set of practical principles for ethical contract design and mutualism were derived, culminating in a model framework for a contract that is transparent, fair, collaborative, and self-reinforcing. This model, with its critical "Consent as Condition Precedent" protocol, demonstrates how to harmonize the rigid demands of contract law with the fluid, absolute right to bodily autonomy.

With this new theory in hand, the "conventional best practices" can be seen for what they are: obsolete artifacts of a prohibitionist mindset. The advice to decouple sex and finances, to rely on ambiguity, or to frame support as a gift is a strategy of evasion born from the assumption of illegality. The relational model proposed here offers a superior path, one grounded in transparency, ethical commitment, and legal precision. It does not seek to hide the bargain but to structure it in a way that is both legally sound and morally defensible. It replaces the logic of avoidance with a logic of affirmation.

The broader implications of adopting such a framework are profound. For individuals, it offers a path to structuring their intimate and economic lives with clarity, security, and dignity, replacing legal precarity with contractual certainty. For the legal system, it provides a more sophisticated and humane lens through which to analyze complex personal service agreements, moving beyond blunt prohibitions to a nuanced assessment of intent and relational dynamics. For society, it has the potential to foster a more honest and mature public discourse on sexuality, labor, and commerce—one that recognizes the capacity of consenting adults to forge agreements that are not exploitative or meretricious, but are instead expressions of mutual respect and autonomous choice. The future of contract law in these deeply personal domains lies not in attempting to force complex human relationships into the rigid containers of transactional forms, but in developing flexible, ethical, and relational frameworks designed to support human flourishing.
