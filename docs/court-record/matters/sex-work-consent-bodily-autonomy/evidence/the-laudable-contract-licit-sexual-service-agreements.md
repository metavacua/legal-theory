# **The Laudable Contract: A New Jurisprudence for Licit Sexual Service Agreements**

  
  

## **Preamble: Reconciling Legality, Consent, and Commerce in Intimate Exchange**

  

The legal and social discourse surrounding compensated sexual services is mired in a state of profound ambiguity, trapped within a framework that largely fails to distinguish between coercive exploitation and consensual enterprise.1 The monolithic term "prostitution" is often used to encompass a vast spectrum of activities, from human trafficking to autonomous commercial transactions between adults.2 This report constructs a new, constitutionally sound, and logically coherent framework for analyzing compensated sexual acts, one that moves beyond the presumption of illegality to a presumption of innocence and contractual freedom.1

This new jurisprudence is necessitated by two central paradoxes in contemporary California law. The first is the **paradox of consideration**. The state's criminal law, specifically Penal Code § 647(b), defines prostitution as a bargained-for exchange, thereby implicitly acknowledging that a sexual act can function as legal "consideration".1 This stands in direct conflict with the state's own family and contract law, where landmark cases like

*Marvin v. Marvin* and the comprehensive statutory framework governing paid gestational surrogacy demonstrate that the legal system is fully capable of valuing and enforcing complex agreements involving intimacy and economic exchange.1 The state, therefore, criminalizes a specific sexual bargain with one hand while validating analogous bargains with the other.

The second and more profound conflict is the **paradox of consent**. California law operates under two mutually exclusive consent regimes. The world of commerce is governed by the static, objective standard of contractual consent, designed to create predictable, binding obligations.1 In stark contrast, the intimate sphere is governed by the dynamic, subjective, and perpetually revocable standard of affirmative sexual consent, where the right to withdraw consent at any moment is absolute. This doctrinal schism creates an impossible legal bind for any agreement that contemplates both commercial terms and sexual performance.1

This report will navigate these paradoxes by deconstructing the existing legal landscape and constructing a new, more coherent model. It introduces the **Prostitution-Miller Test** as a systematic method for distinguishing between the narrow, unprotected conduct that constitutes the crime of prostitution and the broader category of licit sex work. It then proposes the **"Laudable Contract"** as a practical model for an agreement that is not only legally defensible but is also ethically robust, grounded in principles of relationality and care, and designed to promote the dignity and flourishing of all parties.1

  

## **Part I: Deconstructing the Illicit—The Legal Architecture of Prohibition in California**

  

In California, the legal framework is designed to ensure that any agreement for the exchange of sexual services for compensation is legally nonexistent, creating a legal vacuum in which no rights or protections can exist for the parties involved.1 This is achieved through a narrow statutory definition of the crime and the application of fundamental contract law principles that refuse to recognize agreements to commit illegal acts.1

  

### **1.1 Forensic Analysis of Penal Code § 647(b)**

  

The statutory heart of California's prohibitory model is Penal Code § 647(b), which criminalizes not a single act but an entire transactional process: soliciting, agreeing to engage in, or engaging in an act of prostitution. The crime is completed at the moment of contract formation, provided there is an overt act in furtherance of the agreement. The statute's definition hinges on the concept of a "lewd act," which is a precise legal term of art: the physical touching of the genitals, buttocks, or female breast of either party by the other, for the specific purpose of sexual arousal or gratification, in exchange for compensation.5

Crucially, California case law in *Wooten v. Superior Court* held that this definition requires actual physical contact *between* the provider and the customer.1 A performance where performers touch only each other, even for a paying customer's gratification, does not meet the statutory definition of the crime.1 This narrow, technical definition inadvertently creates a legal gray area for a wide range of compensated sexual services that do not meet this specific standard.1

  

### **1.2 The Legacy of** ***Marvin v. Marvin*** **and Meretricious Consideration**

  

The criminalization of the transactional process under PC § 647(b) renders a "prostitution contract" void *ab initio* (from the beginning) as a matter of public policy.1 However, the California Supreme Court's landmark decision in

*Marvin v. Marvin* established a more sophisticated framework for analyzing agreements between unmarried partners.10 The court held that a contract between nonmarital partners is unenforceable

**only to the extent that it explicitly rests upon the immoral and illicit consideration of meretricious sexual services**. The court clarified that an agreement is unlawful if it is "in essence, an agreement for prostitution".

This introduced the critical doctrine of **severability**. If an agreement is based on lawful, non-sexual consideration (e.g., homemaking, companionship, business partnership), it can be enforced even if the parties also have a sexual relationship.11 Subsequent case law has clarified this distinction:

  - In ***Jones v. Daly***, a "cohabitors agreement" was found unenforceable because the complaint alleged that the plaintiff's services as a "lover" were an express and inseparable part of the consideration.
  - In ***Whorton v. Dillingham***, an agreement was found enforceable because the plaintiff alleged a wide range of compensable services—chauffeur, bodyguard, social secretary, and business partner—that were substantially independent of the sexual relationship. The agreement also contained an explicit severability clause.

These cases demonstrate that California courts will not enforce agreements where sexual services are the predominant and inseparable consideration, but will enforce agreements where lawful services provide independent consideration.

  

### **1.3 Legislative Evolution and the Shift to Harm Reduction**

  

Recent legislative reforms signal a clear shift in California's approach, moving away from a purely punitive model toward one rooted in public health and harm reduction.1

  - **Senate Bill 357 (2022)** repealed the statute criminalizing loitering with the intent to commit prostitution, a law criticized for enabling discriminatory enforcement.1
  - **Senate Bill 233 (2020)** made the possession of condoms inadmissible as evidence of prostitution and granted immunity from arrest to individuals who are victims or witnesses of serious felonies and report those crimes.

These reforms indicate that the state's compelling interest is evolving from punishing immorality to mitigating the harms of a dangerous, unregulated industry, aligning with a labor and safety framework.1

  

## **Part II: A New Jurisprudence—The Prostitution-Miller Test**

  

To construct a coherent legal framework, this report proposes the **Prostitution-Miller Test**, a novel analytical tool that adapts the three-prong standard from the Supreme Court's obscenity jurisprudence in *Miller v. California*. An act fails to be criminal prostitution if it satisfies any one of the following conditions, thereby establishing its legality.

  

### **2.1 Prong 3: The "Serious Work" Standard (Lacks Serious Value)**

  

The constitutional gateway for this analysis is the California Supreme Court's decision in ***People v. Freeman*** (1988).13 In that landmark case, the court reversed the pandering conviction of a producer who hired and paid actors to perform explicit sexual acts for a non-obscene film.13 The court's reasoning was that the payments were "acting fees" for creating a work of expression, not compensation paid for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification.14

This decision establishes a powerful legal principle: a compensated sexual act that possesses "serious artistic value" (by being part of a non-obscene film) is constitutionally protected expression and cannot be prosecuted as prostitution.13 This "serious work" standard is the key to legality.

  

### **2.2 Prong 1: The Substantial Purpose Test (Prurient Interest)**

  

This prong expands the "serious work" concept to other non-prurient purposes. A compensated sexual act undertaken for a substantial purpose other than appealing to a "shameful or morbid interest in sex" falls outside the rationale of prostitution laws.1 California law provides powerful, real-world examples of such licit contracts:

  - **Compensated Gestational Surrogacy:** California has a comprehensive legal framework (Family Code §§ 7960-7962) that regulates and enforces complex agreements involving payment for the use of a person's body for the serious purpose of procreation.1 This demonstrates the state's ability to distinguish a lawful contract for bodily services from a criminal act.1
  - **Surrogate Partner Therapy:** This practice exists in a legal gray area but presents a potentially lawful agreement for sexual acts with a therapeutic purpose. Structured correctly under the framework for unlicensed healing arts and with full disclosure, such an agreement's dominant motive is therapeutic, not gratificational.1
  - **Parentage by Agreement:** California's Uniform Parentage Act allows for the creation of complex parenting agreements when a child is conceived through sexual intercourse, including recognizing three-parent families.3 These agreements, which manage the legal outcomes of a sexual act, are another example of enforceable contracts in the intimate sphere.3

  

### **2.3 Prong 2: The Affirmative Consent Standard (Patently Offensive)**

  

This prong connects the concept of "patently offensive" conduct directly to the principles of affirmative consent.1 In the context of a personal service, an act becomes "patently offensive" precisely when it violates the provider's autonomy. Therefore, the failure to secure and maintain ongoing, enthusiastic, and freely given affirmative consent is what makes the conduct offensive and unlawful. This creates a

**duty of care** under California Civil Code §1714(a), which holds individuals responsible for injuries caused by their actions.1 A reckless disregard for consent constitutes a breach of this duty, creating potential tort liability in addition to any criminal liability.1

  

## **Part III: The Laudable Contract—A Model for Licit and Ethical Agreements**

  

Building on this legal foundation, the "Laudable Contract" is a practical model for an agreement that is both legally defensible and ethically robust.

  

### **3.1 Ethical Foundation: Relational Contracts and Care Ethics**

  

Classical contract law, designed for discrete bargains between strangers, is ill-suited for ongoing, intimate services.1 A new foundation is required, built by integrating the legal doctrines of

**Relational Contract Theory** with the moral insights of **Care Ethics**.1

  - **Relational Contract Theory** views contracts not as static transactions but as frameworks for governing ongoing relationships, emphasizing flexibility and mutual understanding.24
  - **Care Ethics** conceives of individuals as fundamentally interconnected and vulnerable, obligating parties not just to a set of performances but to a mutual practice of care, guided by principles of attentiveness, responsibility, and responsiveness.27

Together, these theories transform the agreement from a list of obligations into a dynamic charter for a relationship of mutual care.1

  

### **3.2 Structural Components of the Laudable Contract**

  

A contract designed to be licit and ethical would contain the following key components:

  - **Preamble and Statement of Purpose:** This foundational section explicitly states the dominant, legally protected purpose of the agreement (e.g., artistic, therapeutic, procreative), thereby satisfying the "Dominant Motive Test" from *People v. Freeman* and establishing the services as licit consideration.1
  - **The Consent Protocol (Condition Precedent Solution):** This is the technical linchpin that harmonizes contract law with sexual consent law. The clause frames ongoing, affirmative consent as a **condition precedent** to performance. It states unequivocally that the performance of any sexual act is subject to the existence of ongoing, affirmative, and freely given consent. The withdrawal of consent means the condition precedent for performance has failed; therefore, the legal duty to perform that specific act never arises, and its non-performance does not constitute a breach of contract.1 This elegantly resolves the paradox of revocability.
  - **Asymmetrical Enforceability:** The contract is asymmetrically enforceable, analogous to contracts with minors under California law. The party performing the sexual service (the "Performer") retains an absolute right to revoke consent and terminate performance without penalty. The Performer *can*, however, enforce the other party's (the "Patron's") obligations (e.g., payment for services rendered before revocation). The Patron cannot enforce performance against the Performer. This legal structure protects the Performer's absolute right to bodily autonomy while ensuring they have legal recourse for the Patron's breach of their obligations.
  - **Reciprocal Roles and the Living Contract:** The agreement should be a two-tiered "living contract". A static "meta-agreement" establishes the foundational principles (asymmetrical revocability, dispute resolution), while a dynamic "operational agreement" details specific services and is designed for ongoing amendment, adapting to the evolving relationship and acknowledging that the roles of Patron and Performer may be fluid and reciprocal.
  - **Dispute Resolution via Restorative Justice:** The contract should mandate a bespoke dispute resolution system based on the principles of **restorative justice**. Instead of adversarial litigation, this model uses facilitated dialogue to repair harm, address the needs of all parties, and either restore the relationship or provide for an amicable dissolution.

  

## **Part IV: The Labor Paradigm—Reframing Exploitation and Regulation**

  

The state's most robust and constitutionally sound interest in this area is not the policing of morality but the regulation of labor and the prevention of exploitation.4 California's

**ABC test** for worker classification provides a powerful lens for this analysis.1 When applied to various models of sex work, it becomes clear that in most organized forms (pimp-controlled, agency-based, platform-based), the worker is a misclassified employee, and the operation is a system of illegal labor exploitation.1 This reframes the felonies of pimping and pandering from morals offenses into crimes of severe labor exploitation, wage theft, and human trafficking.1

This labor-focused approach contrasts sharply with Nevada's regulatory model. While Nevada has legalized the transaction, it has done so by creating a system of pervasive state surveillance.1 Analyzed through a Foucaultian lens, Nevada's regime of mandatory registration, invasive medical testing, and waived privacy rights constitutes a "panoptic gaze" that exercises disciplinary and biopolitical power over the worker's body.30 Legality is granted only in exchange for the total surrender of autonomy to the state's administrative and medical gaze, a system that functions as a form of institutionalized voyeurism.30 While an alternative to prohibition, this model is not ideal, as it replaces criminalization with a different form of state control rather than empowering worker autonomy.30

  

## **Conclusion and Recommendations**

  

The prevailing legal framework governing commercial sexual services, predicated on a categorical failure to distinguish exploitation from enterprise, is constitutionally and ethically inadequate. This report has constructed a multi-layered argument for a paradigm shift toward a jurisprudence that recognizes the legitimacy of consensual "sexual service contracts" under specific, narrow conditions.

A licit, ethical, and enforceable sexual service contract is legally plausible in California. The key is to structure the agreement as a form of "serious work" with a non-prurient purpose, thereby satisfying the constitutional requirements established in *People v. Freeman* and falling outside the narrow statutory definition of criminal prostitution. The "Laudable Contract" model—built on relational ethics, a condition precedent for consent, and the principle of asymmetrical enforceability—provides a practical and legally sophisticated blueprint.

Based on these findings, the following recommendations are proposed:

**Legislative Recommendations**

  - **Amend Penal Code § 647(b) to Include a "Safe Harbor" Provision:** The Legislature should amend the statute to explicitly state that it does not apply to sexual acts conducted pursuant to a written agreement in which the parties attest that the dominant purpose is lawful and non-prostitutive (e.g., artistic, therapeutic, reproductive).1
  - **Create a Statutory Affirmative Defense:** The Legislature should create an affirmative defense to a charge under PC § 647(b), established by the defendant producing a written contract detailing a lawful purpose for the exchange.1

**Judicial Recommendations**

  - **Adopt a Clarifying Jury Instruction:** The California Judicial Council should adopt a standard jury instruction for PC § 647(b) prosecutions that clarifies the prosecution's burden to prove the dominant purpose of the compensation was a direct quid pro quo for a lewd act for sexual gratification, and that the existence of other valid consideration can create reasonable doubt.1
  - **Affirm the Broad Applicability of** ***Freeman*****:** Appellate courts should affirm that the core principle of *People v. Freeman* is not limited to commercial filmmaking but applies to any situation where a sexual act is performed as part of a non-obscene expressive or "serious" work.1

**Policy Recommendations**

  - **Shift Law Enforcement Priorities:** State and local law enforcement should redirect resources away from consensual sexual activity between adults and toward investigating and combating non-consensual crimes, such as human trafficking and labor exploitation.1
  - **Promote Public Education on Contractual Rights:** The State Bar and legal aid organizations should develop materials to educate the public on their right to structure personal and intimate relationships through clear, consensual contracts, empowering individuals to reduce ambiguity and the potential for unwarranted criminal investigation.1
