The Nexus of Consent and Consideration: A Legal Framework for Navigating Modern Relationships in California

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Part I: The Tripartite Legal Foundation: Prostitution, Consent, and Contract

The legal landscape governing intimate relationships between consenting adults in California is defined by the complex interplay of three distinct yet overlapping domains of law: criminal statutes prohibiting commercial sex, criminal statutes mandating affirmative consent to prevent sexual assault, and civil laws regulating contracts and the exchange of value. Understanding the precise boundaries and requirements of each is essential to navigating the social and legal protocols of modern dating, mate selection, and long-term partnerships. This report will first establish this tripartite legal foundation before applying its principles to the nuanced scenarios of contemporary relational conduct.

The Commercial Boundary - Prostitution and the Centrality of Intent

At the core of the legal distinction between a personal relationship and a commercial transaction is California Penal Code § 647(b). This statute criminalizes not only the act of prostitution itself but also the preliminary steps of soliciting or agreeing to such an act, reflecting a legislative intent to intervene before any sexual contact occurs.1 The law is structured to apply equally to all parties involved in the transaction—both the individual offering a sexual act for compensation and the individual soliciting it.2

Statutory Definition and Key Elements

Penal Code § 647(b) defines the misdemeanor offense of disorderly conduct to include any person who "solicits, or who agrees to engage in, or who engages in, an act of prostitution".4 The statute further clarifies that "prostitution includes any lewd act between persons for money or other consideration".4 A "lewd act" is judicially defined as the touching of the genitals, buttocks, or female breast for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification.2

To secure a conviction under this statute, a prosecutor must prove several key elements beyond a reasonable doubt for each of the three prohibited behaviors:

  1. Engaging in Prostitution: The prosecution must prove that the defendant willfully engaged in sexual intercourse or a lewd act with another person in exchange for money or other compensation.5 The term "willfully" signifies that the act was done purposefully, though it does not require a specific intent to violate the law.5

  2. Soliciting Prostitution: The elements for solicitation are that the defendant (1) requested that another person engage in an act of prostitution, (2) possessed the specific intent to actually engage in an act of prostitution with that person, and (3) the other person received the communication containing the request.2 This element of specific intent is a crucial subjective standard. Actions that are ambiguous, such as waving at a car, standing on a corner in a particular style of dress, or being in an area known for prostitution, are insufficient on their own to prove the requisite intent.5

  3. Agreeing to Engage in Prostitution: For this offense, the prosecution must prove that the defendant (1) agreed to engage in an act of prostitution, (2) intended to do so, and (3) performed some "overt act" in furtherance of the commission of the act.5 This "overt act" must be more than the mere verbal agreement itself. The law requires a subsequent action that substantiates the intent, such as withdrawing money from an ATM for the purpose of payment, driving to an agreed-upon location for the encounter, or handing over the compensation.2

The statute's definition of compensation is notably broad, encompassing not just money but "anything of value" or "other consideration".2 This breadth is a primary source of the legal tension in relationships where resources are exchanged. The fact that the sexual act does not need to be completed for a crime to occur is a cornerstone of the statute's enforcement, particularly in the context of undercover sting operations where an agreement is the sole objective.1

Judicial Interpretation: The Purpose of the Payment

California courts have provided critical interpretations that shape the statute's application. The landmark case of People v. Freeman (1988) established that for a sex act to constitute prostitution, the payment must be made for the specific purpose of sexual arousal or gratification. In Freeman, a film producer who paid actors "acting fees" to perform sex acts in a non-obscene film was charged with pandering (procuring for the purpose of prostitution). The California Supreme Court reversed his conviction, holding that there was no evidence the defendant paid the fees for his own or the actors' sexual gratification. The court found that Freeman lacked the requisite mens rea (criminal intent) for the crime because the purpose of the payment was to compensate actors for their performance in a film, not to purchase sexual arousal.

This ruling is pivotal: it demonstrates that an exchange of money for a sex act is not, per se, illegal prostitution. The legality hinges on the provable purpose and dominant intent behind the transaction. If the primary purpose is something other than sexual gratification—such as artistic expression—the act may fall outside the narrow definition of prostitution. This principle creates a potential legal framework for other mixed-motive arrangements where sex and compensation are exchanged but the dominant purpose is not sexual gratification.

The Affirmative Mandate - Sexual Consent and Its Vitiating Factors

Distinct from the prohibition on commercial sex is the legal framework governing sexual consent, which aims to protect bodily autonomy and prevent sexual violence. The central tenet of California law is that any sexual contact without valid consent is a criminal act. This principle is primarily codified in statutes defining sexual battery and is reinforced by the state's pioneering affirmative consent standard and specific rules regarding factors that invalidate consent, such as fraud.

Defining Sexual Assault and Battery

Under California Penal Code § 243.4, sexual battery is defined as the touching of an "intimate part of another person" against that person's will and for the purpose of sexual arousal, sexual gratification, or sexual abuse.10 An "intimate part" includes the sexual organs, groin, anus, buttocks, or a female's breast.11 The statute is broad in scope; "touching" can occur through the victim's clothing, and no physical injury or penetration is required for the act to be a crime.13 The offense can range from a misdemeanor for unwanted groping to a felony if the act involves unlawful restraint or is committed against a medically incapacitated person.11 The defining element of the offense is not the nature of the touch itself, but the absence of valid consent.

The Affirmative Consent Standard ("Yes Means Yes")

California law has moved beyond the traditional "no means no" model of consent to a more stringent "affirmative consent" standard, colloquially known as "Yes Means Yes." While this standard was formally codified in Education Code § 67386 through Senate Bill 967, applying directly to postsecondary educational institutions, its principles have profoundly influenced the broader legal and social understanding of consent across the state.15

Affirmative consent is defined as an "affirmative, conscious, and voluntary agreement to engage in sexual activity".15 This standard is built on several core principles:

  • Active Agreement: Consent must be expressed through clear words or actions that indicate a willingness to participate. The absence of protest or resistance does not constitute consent, nor does silence.15 Ambiguous or unclear actions do not qualify as affirmative consent.19

  • Ongoing and Revocable: Consent is not a one-time agreement but a continuous process. It must be present throughout a sexual encounter and can be revoked at any point.15 Once consent is withdrawn, the sexual activity must cease immediately.

  • Specific: Consent to one form of sexual activity does not imply consent to other forms. Similarly, a past dating relationship or prior sexual history between individuals is never, by itself, an indicator of consent for a current encounter.15

  • Responsibility of the Initiator: The law places the responsibility on each person involved, particularly the initiator of a sexual act, to ensure they have the affirmative consent of the other participants.15

Conditions Invalidating Consent: Fraud, Duress, and Incapacitation

Valid consent cannot be procured through force, fear, fraud, or deceit.50 California law specifies several conditions under which apparent consent is legally invalid:

  • Incapacitation: A person cannot give valid consent if they are asleep, unconscious, or incapacitated due to the influence of drugs, alcohol, or medication to the extent that they cannot understand the fact, nature, or extent of the sexual activity.15

  • Fraud: Under Penal Code § 261, consent is vitiated by fraud in specific circumstances. The law distinguishes between "fraud in the factum" (a misunderstanding of the act itself) and "fraud in the inducement" (a misunderstanding of the surrounding circumstances).

  • Fraud in the Factum: Consent is invalid if the victim is "unconscious of the nature of the act" due to the perpetrator's fraudulent representation. This includes situations where the perpetrator fraudulently claims the act serves a professional purpose (e.g., a fake medical exam) or where the victim is deceived into believing the perpetrator is someone else, such as their spouse.58 This type of fraud negates consent because the victim did not agree to the act that occurred.

  • Fraud in the Inducement: This occurs when a person is tricked into consenting to a sexual act by lies about surrounding circumstances (e.g., promises of love, wealth, or marriage). Historically, California courts have held that fraud in the inducement does not vitiate consent for the purposes of a rape charge, on the theory that the person still consented to the sexual act itself, even if under false pretenses.59 However, Penal Code § 266c makes it a crime to induce sexual intercourse through a "false or fraudulent representation or pretense that is made with the intent to create fear". Furthermore, Penal Code § 266 criminalizes procuring a person for "illicit carnal connection" by fraudulent means. While the general rule that fraud in the inducement does not constitute rape remains, these related statutes criminalize certain forms of sexual deception.

The legal requirement for active, informed, and fraud-free consent creates a direct legal burden on individuals to obtain clear and genuine permission for sexual activity.

The Architecture of Agreement - Contract Law, Consideration, and Gifts

The third legal pillar is the civil law of contracts, which governs enforceable agreements and the exchange of value. This framework is essential for understanding how financial support and material exchanges within a relationship are legally characterized.

Elements of a Valid Contract

For a contract to be legally binding in California, it must contain four essential elements: (1) parties capable of contracting, (2) their mutual consent, (3) a lawful object, and (4) sufficient cause or "consideration".23 Consent to a contract can also be vitiated by fraud. "Fraud in the inducement" in a contractual context occurs when one party is misled by false representations into entering an agreement, making the contract voidable by the defrauded party.60

Defining "Consideration"

Consideration is the foundational element that distinguishes a binding promise from an unenforceable one. California Civil Code § 1605 defines "good consideration" as:

"Any benefit conferred, or agreed to be conferred, upon the promisor, by any other person, to which the promisor is not lawfully entitled, or any prejudice suffered, or agreed to be suffered, by such person, other than such as he is at the time of consent lawfully bound to suffer, as an inducement to the promisor..." 26

In essence, consideration is the bargained-for exchange of value—the "this for that" element of a contract. Each party must give something of value (a promise, an act, or a forbearance) to the other.24

Implied-in-Fact Contracts

A contract does not need to be written or even spoken to be enforceable. California Civil Code § 1621 recognizes implied-in-fact contracts, where the "existence and terms of which are manifested by conduct".62 Such an agreement arises from the actions and circumstances of the parties, which suggest a mutual intent to be bound.63 In a relational context, an implied contract could be formed if one partner consistently provides financial support while the other provides domestic or other services, creating a pattern of conduct that implies an agreement to share resources.64 The existence of such contracts is a key component of the Marvin doctrine discussed in Part II.

The Legal Concept of a "Gift"

In stark contrast to a contract is a gift. Under California Civil Code § 1146, a gift is defined as "a transfer of personal property, made voluntarily, and without consideration".30 The three necessary elements for a valid gift are (1) donative intent on the part of the giver, (2) delivery of the property (actual or symbolic), and (3) acceptance by the recipient.30 The crucial distinction is the absence of consideration. A gift is a unilateral transfer; it is not given in exchange for a promise or performance. Once a gift is completed, it is generally irrevocable.31

Part II: The Spectrum of Relational Agreements and Financial Support

Building upon the foundational legal principles, this section examines their application in structuring relationships where intimacy and finances are intertwined. California law has developed specific doctrines to address the financial realities of non-marital partnerships, creating legal mechanisms that recognize their validity while carefully delineating them from prohibited commercial sex transactions.

Cohabitation, Contracts, and Palimony - The Legacy of Marvin v. Marvin

For decades, California courts struggled with the legal status of unmarried cohabiting couples, often leaving parties without recourse upon separation. This changed dramatically with the landmark 1976 California Supreme Court decision in Marvin v. Marvin.33

The Marvin Holding and the Rise of "Palimony"

In Marvin, the court held that express and implied contracts between unmarried partners concerning their earnings and property rights are enforceable.35 The court reasoned that adults who voluntarily live together are as competent as any other persons to contract respecting their property.36 This ruling established the legal foundation for what is now known as "palimony"—financial support paid by one partner to another after a non-marital relationship ends.37 Unlike spousal support (alimony), which is governed by the Family Code, palimony claims are rooted in contract law and are adjudicated in civil court.38

The Marvin decision recognized that courts can protect the reasonable expectations of the parties by enforcing their agreements, whether written, oral, or implied from their conduct.35 This allows for equitable remedies to prevent one party from being unjustly enriched at the other's expense upon the dissolution of a long-term, marriage-like relationship.43

The "Meretricious Consideration" Exception and Severability

The most critical aspect of the Marvin holding is its limitation. The court was careful to distinguish enforceable relationship agreements from illegal contracts for prostitution. It held that contracts between nonmarital partners are unenforceable only to the extent that they explicitly and inseparably rest upon the "immoral and illicit consideration of meretricious sexual services".

This "meretricious consideration" doctrine is the firewall that separates a valid cohabitation agreement from a void contract for prostitution. The key insight from Marvin and subsequent cases like Whorton v. Dillingham is the principle of severability. If an agreement is based on lawful consideration that is severable from the sexual aspects of the relationship—such as companionship, homemaking, or providing services as a "chauffeur, bodyguard, secretary, and partner and counselor in real estate investments"—it can be enforced. The court in Whorton distinguished these monetizable services from those "naturally flowing from sexual cohabitation," which it deemed inseparable. This judicial framework implies that sexual services can be a part of a relationship agreement, provided they are not the sole and inseparable basis of the contract. If other, lawful forms of consideration exist and can be separated from the sexual component, the agreement can be upheld.

The "Dominant Motive" Test: A Framework for Legitimate Sex-for-Value Exchanges

The California Supreme Court's decision in People v. Freeman established that the purpose of a payment for sex is the determinative factor in a prostitution case. This opens the door to a more nuanced analysis of relationships that involve an exchange of sex for significant value. While not explicitly adopted by California courts in this context, the "dominant motive" test, derived from federal jurisprudence on the Mann Act, provides a powerful analytical framework for distinguishing lawful from unlawful conduct in such mixed-motive scenarios.65

The test asks whether the illicit purpose (prostitution) was "one of the efficient and compelling purposes" of the arrangement, or whether it was merely "incidental" to a legitimate primary purpose.66 Applying this framework, an arrangement where tuition is paid in exchange for a "sexual companionship relationship" could be deemed legal if the dominant motive is educational advancement, with the sexual component being incidental to a broader relationship of companionship and support.67

Factors a court might consider in determining the dominant motive include:

  • The Nature of the Agreement: Does the agreement focus on a legitimate, non-sexual goal? An agreement to fund an education, support an artistic career, or provide for living expenses within a genuine companionship relationship points away from a dominant motive of purchasing sexual gratification.44

  • Severable Consideration: As in Whorton, does the relationship involve services with independent monetary value, such as business counseling, secretarial work, or property management, that can be severed from the sexual component?

  • Breadth of the Relationship: Does the relationship involve activities beyond sexual encounters, such as public dates, social events, mentorship, or shared hobbies? A broader relationship supports the argument that companionship is a genuine and dominant motive.44

The Freeman decision and the dominant motive framework suggest that a well-structured agreement for personal services that includes a sexual component is not a priori illegal. If the arrangement is structured as a form of employment or an independent contractor relationship for legitimate personal services (e.g., companionship, personal assistance), and it complies with other legal requirements, it may be defensible against a prostitution charge, provided the dominant motive is not the purchase of sexual gratification.

Part III: Navigating Social Protocols and Legal Risk

This section applies the established legal principles to the practical scenarios of modern dating and relationship formation. It focuses on the acute tension between the legal necessity for explicit communication to ensure affirmative consent and the risk that such communication, especially when financial elements are present, could be interpreted as criminal solicitation.

From Ambiguous Invitation to Affirmative Consent - A Legal Dissection of Modern Dating Scenarios

Modern dating is replete with social scripts and euphemisms that carry implicit meanings. However, these social conventions often fail to meet the rigorous legal standards for consent required in California. The social ambiguity favored in intimate negotiations is, in part, a direct consequence of the legal risks associated with explicit communication about sex and finances.

The Ambiguous Invitation and Its Legal Insufficiency

Common invitations like "getting coffee" or the more suggestive "Netflix and chill" are, in a legal sense, merely social overtures.46 They do not, in themselves, constitute affirmative consent for any sexual activity. While "Netflix and chill" has become a widely understood euphemism for a casual sexual encounter, relying on this shared cultural understanding as a substitute for explicit consent is legally perilous.47 California's affirmative consent standard requires a "conscious and voluntary agreement," and explicitly states that silence, passivity, or a lack of resistance does not equal consent.15 Therefore, acting on the implied meaning of an invitation without obtaining clear, explicit consent at the time of the activity would legally constitute sexual battery.

The Path of Escalation and the Mandate for Communication

Under the "Yes Means Yes" standard, a lawful sexual encounter can only proceed from an initial invitation through a series of clear, affirmative, and ongoing expressions of consent.15 This "path of escalation" requires active communication. Best practices for ensuring consent is freely given, reversible, informed, enthusiastic, and specific (F.R.I.E.S.) include 49:

  • Verbal Check-ins: Asking direct questions like, "Is this okay?" or "Do you want to continue?" at each stage of increasing intimacy.22

  • Respecting Non-verbal Cues: Paying close attention to a partner's body language. Signs of hesitation, disengagement, or pulling away should prompt an immediate pause and verbal check-in.49

  • Understanding Specificity: Recognizing that consent to one act (e.g., kissing) does not imply consent to another (e.g., intercourse).49

This legal requirement creates a social paradox. The law mandates clear communication for safety and legality, but the fear of such communication being misconstrued as criminal solicitation—especially if any financial support is part of the relationship—drives individuals toward the very ambiguity the law disfavors.

Application to One-Night Stands

These principles apply with even greater force to encounters between strangers. An initial verbal agreement to have sex later is not a binding waiver of consent; affirmative consent must still be ongoing throughout the actual encounter and can be revoked at any moment.8 Furthermore, if such an agreement is followed by an overt act in furtherance of the agreement, such as one party paying for accommodations, it could potentially form the basis for a prostitution charge if the requisite intent for a commercial exchange of sex for gratification is present.8

The Contractual First Date - An Analysis of Express Commercial Sexual Arrangements

We now analyze the hypothetical of a prospective partner arriving at a first date with a written contract that outright states the commercial nature of a proposed sexual arrangement, proceeding from the presumption that the parties are making a good-faith effort to form a well-formed, legal contract.

Civil Law Analysis: Enforceability and the "Meretricious Consideration" Doctrine

Such a contract, by explicitly stating its commercial nature, directly confronts the "meretricious consideration" doctrine from Marvin v. Marvin.

  • Unenforceability: A contract that explicitly and inseparably links financial payments to the performance of sexual acts would be deemed void as against public policy. The court in Marvin was clear that "one cannot lawfully contract to pay for the performance of sexual services since such an agreement is in essence a bargain for prostitution". By being explicit about its commercial nature, the contract would likely fail the severability test established in cases like Whorton, as the sexual services would be the direct and inseparable consideration for the payment.

  • Potential for Enforceability: To be enforceable, the contract would need to be structured around a non-sexual dominant purpose, with severable consideration. For example, a contract for "personal companionship, business mentorship, and social escort services, which may include an intimate relationship," where compensation is tied to the non-sexual services, might be enforceable. The explicit commercial framing must be for the licit services, not the sexual acts themselves.

Criminal Law Analysis: The Freeman Test and Dominant Motive

From a criminal law perspective, such a contract would serve as the primary evidence of the parties' intent. Its legality would hinge on the purpose of the payment, as articulated in People v. Freeman.

  • High Risk of Illegality: A contract that frames the payment as a direct quid pro quo for sexual acts for the purpose of gratification would likely be viewed as conclusive evidence of solicitation under PC § 647(b). It would memorialize the exact elements of the crime.

  • Path to Legality: To be presumptively legal, the contract must explicitly state a dominant purpose other than sexual gratification. For instance, a contract stating, "Party A agrees to pay Party B's university tuition in consideration for Party B providing companionship and a committed intimate relationship, with the dominant purpose of supporting Party B's educational and personal development," attempts to frame the arrangement under the Freeman logic. It argues the payment is for education, not sexual gratification, even though sex is an expected part of the relationship. While novel and untested, this approach directly engages with the legal standard set by the California Supreme Court. It shifts the argument from whether there is an exchange of sex for value to what the purpose of that exchange is.

Ultimately, presenting an explicit commercial contract for a sexual relationship is legally perilous. While it attempts to create clarity, it does so by documenting a transaction that sits at the very edge of criminal law. Its success would depend entirely on its ability to convincingly frame the arrangement's dominant motive as something other than the purchase of sexual gratification.

Part IV: Procedural Safeguards, Synthesis, and Best Practices

While the substantive law creates a precarious legal environment, the American legal system provides crucial procedural safeguards for any individual accused of a crime. This final section details those protections and synthesizes the report's analysis into a set of actionable best practices for good faith engagement in modern relationships.

The Presumption of Innocence and Due Process in Practice

The presumption of innocence is a bedrock principle of the U.S. and California legal systems. It dictates that an individual is considered innocent until proven guilty, and the government bears the entire burden of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.52 This principle is protected by a suite of due process rights guaranteed by the Constitution.

Constitutional Protections and the Burden of Proof

From investigation through appeal, an individual accused of any crime, including prostitution, is afforded significant due process rights. These include the right to be free from unreasonable searches and seizures, the right against self-incrimination, the right to counsel, and the right to a fair and public trial by an impartial jury.55

In the context of a prostitution charge, the prosecution must prove every element of the offense—including the crucial element of specific intent—beyond a reasonable doubt.52 Because intent is an internal state of mind, proving it is often the most challenging aspect of the prosecution's case and the most fertile ground for a defense.

Common Defenses in Prostitution Cases

The procedural safeguards of the legal system allow for the assertion of several common defenses that directly address the ambiguities discussed throughout this report:

  • Insufficient Evidence / Lack of Intent: This is the most common defense. The defense argues that the prosecution has failed to provide sufficient evidence to prove the specific intent to exchange sex for compensation for the purpose of sexual gratification. The defense would seek to re-contextualize the interaction as social, romantic, or having a different dominant motive, such as companionship or artistic expression.

  • Entrapment: This defense is often raised in cases arising from undercover police stings. Entrapment occurs when law enforcement uses overbearing conduct to induce or coerce a person into committing a crime they were not otherwise predisposed to commit.5

  • False Accusation: A defendant can always argue that the accusation is false, stemming from motives such as jealousy, revenge, or a misunderstanding.5

Due process and the high burden of proof are the most critical safeguards for individuals navigating these legal gray areas. The subjective nature of intent in prostitution cases means that the prosecution's ability to prove it beyond a reasonable doubt is the primary firewall protecting non-commercial relationships from wrongful prosecution.

Synthesis and Recommendations for Good Faith Engagement

The central dilemma explored in this report is the conflict between the legal mandate for explicit communication to ensure affirmative consent and the criminalization of explicit offers to exchange compensation for sex. A sophisticated understanding of the case law surrounding consent, contract, and criminal law allows for the development of communication strategies that can mitigate risk while fostering healthy, respectful intimate relationships.

Based on the comprehensive legal analysis, the following are recommended as "best practices" for good faith engagement and communication:

  1. Prioritize Affirmative and Enthusiastic Consent: All sexual activity must be governed by the "Yes Means Yes" standard. Communication should be clear, direct, ongoing, and respectful. Use verbal check-ins, pay attention to non-verbal cues, and never make assumptions based on past behavior, relationship status, or ambiguous invitations. The responsibility to obtain consent rests with the initiator of the activity.

  2. Structure Agreements Around a Lawful Dominant Motive: When a relationship involves significant material support and intimacy, any agreement (express or implied) should be structured around a lawful primary purpose other than sexual gratification, in line with the principles of People v. Freeman. This could be education, mentorship, companionship, or other legitimate personal services. The sexual component of the relationship should be legally incidental to this primary purpose.

  3. Ensure Consideration is Severable and Lawful: In any express or implied contract, ensure that the consideration for material support is lawful and, where possible, severable from the sexual aspect of the relationship, as guided by Marvin v. Marvin and Whorton v. Dillingham. Frame compensation in terms of demonstrable, monetizable services (e.g., personal assistance, business counseling) rather than as a direct payment for sex.

  4. Use Explicit, Well-Drafted Written Contracts: For long-term relationships with significant financial entanglement, a written cohabitation agreement is the most prudent measure. Such an agreement should be drafted to be enforceable under contract law, clearly defining the lawful consideration and the dominant purpose of the arrangement to avoid being classified as a contract for "meretricious sexual services."

  5. Ensure All Consent is Free from Fraud and Duress: Both contractual and sexual consent must be freely and voluntarily given. Any agreement procured through fraudulent inducement—such as misrepresenting one's intentions, identity, or financial status—is voidable. Similarly, sexual consent obtained through fraud, coercion, or duress is legally invalid and can lead to criminal charges.

In conclusion, the legal framework in California, while complex, does not impose a blanket prohibition on relationships involving sex and financial exchange. Instead, it requires a careful analysis of intent, purpose, and the specific nature of the agreement. The ultimate "best practice" is to structure such relationships with transparency and a clear, lawful dominant purpose, ensuring that any material support flows from that purpose, rather than as a bargained-for price for sexual gratification. This aligns the conduct of the parties with the legal principles that distinguish a protected, enforceable personal services contract from a criminal commercial transaction.
