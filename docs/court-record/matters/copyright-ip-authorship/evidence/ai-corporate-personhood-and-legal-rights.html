The Tiered Architecture of Personhood: A Comparative Jurisprudence of Natural, Corporate, and Artificial Persons in American Law

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Executive Summary

This report prosecutes the argument that American jurisprudence does not treat "personhood" as a monolithic or biological absolute, but as a tiered, functional, and contingent legal status. The landmark rulings in Thaler v. Perlmutter, Citizens United v. FEC, and Burwell v. Hobby Lobby collectively reveal a distinct hierarchy of being, where rights are granted or withheld to serve specific social, economic, and political ends. At the apex stands the natural person, possessing the most comprehensive, though not absolute, set of rights rooted in concepts of autonomy and dignity. Below this is the corporate person, a privileged legal fiction whose circumscribed rights are justified as proxies for the rights of its human constituents. At the bottom is the advanced Artificial Intelligence, which, despite its growing functional autonomy, remains classified as mere property, devoid of legal personhood. This analysis deconstructs this hierarchy, arguing that the determinative factor in the judicial conferral of rights is not an entity's intrinsic nature but the perceived locus of human agency and interest that the entity represents. The report concludes that this human-centric framework, while historically coherent, is becoming increasingly unstable in the face of autonomous technologies and complex global corporations, creating a profound accountability gap that necessitates urgent legal and philosophical reconsideration.

Part I: The Philosophical and Legal Foundations of Personhood

The American legal system operates on a complex and often unstated set of assumptions about who, or what, qualifies as a "person" capable of holding rights and being subject to duties. This foundational concept is not a static biological fact but a dynamic legal construct, shaped by centuries of philosophical debate and judicial interpretation. An analysis of contemporary jurisprudence reveals not a single, unified category of personhood, but a tiered architecture comprising at least three distinct classes: the natural person, the juridical person (corporation), and the artificial entity (AI). Understanding the legal and philosophical underpinnings of each tier is the essential precondition for analyzing how courts delineate the boundaries between them. The natural person serves as the legal system's baseline, yet even this apex status is not absolute, providing a crucial point of departure for a more nuanced comparison of the rights afforded to non-human entities.

1.1 The Natural Person: An Autonomy Both Inherent and Contingent

The natural person—the living human being—stands as the primary rights-holder in the American legal tradition. This preeminent status is predicated on foundational philosophical ideals of autonomy, rational self-governance, and bodily integrity, concepts articulated by thinkers from John Stuart Mill to contemporary legal scholars.1 The unique ethical standing afforded to humans is most clearly codified in the legal framework governing human subjects in research. The 1979 Belmont Report, which serves as the ethical blueprint for federal regulations, establishes a high-water mark for legal protection not afforded to any other entity. It is built upon three core principles:

Respect for Persons, which treats individuals as autonomous agents whose informed consent is paramount; Beneficence, which mandates the minimization of harm and maximization of benefits; and Justice, which requires the equitable distribution of research burdens and benefits.2 This framework, born from the ethical reckonings of the Nuremberg Code and the Tuskegee Syphilis Study, enshrines the idea that a human being is an end in themselves, an autonomous agent whose will must be respected and whose welfare must be protected.2

However, a critical examination of American jurisprudence reveals that this ideal of absolute individual autonomy is, in practice, a "foundational myth".1 The autonomy of the natural person is not an indefeasible right but a

"contingent privilege," legally and routinely subordinated to the imperatives of the state and the market.1 This subordination is enabled by what can be termed an "Architecture of Non-Consensual Legality," a set of legal doctrines and fictions that permit power to operate without, or even against, the will of the individual.4

The foundational surrender of consent is philosophically justified by social contract theory, which posits that individuals give "tacit consent" to be governed simply by residing within a state's territory and enjoying its protections.1 This master fiction obviates the need for continuous, case-by-case consent for state actions, providing a blanket justification for legitimate coercion.1 Building on this, the state deploys numerous mechanisms that override individual will. The USA PATRIOT Act, for instance, institutionalized "mass, suspicionless data collection" and authorized secret "sneak and peek" searches, fundamentally altering the balance between security and privacy.1 The power of eminent domain, particularly after its expansion in

Kelo v. City of New London to include "public purpose" takings for economic development, affirms that an individual's autonomy over their property is legally subordinate to the state's economic interests.1

Corporate power operates through a parallel architecture of manufactured consent. The dominant business model of "Surveillance Capitalism" is predicated on the unilateral extraction of personal data, treating individual autonomy as a barrier to profit.1 This vast data harvesting is legally authorized by "clickwrap" agreements, which function as a "legal fiction" of consent. These non-negotiable, incomprehensible terms of service are a form of coerced acquiescence, a necessary toll for participation in modern life.1 This system is reinforced by a deliberate public policy choice in the U.S. for an "opt-out" data privacy regime, which places the burden of protection on the individual and sets the default to corporate permission to collect data.1

This initial analysis reveals a crucial point that reframes the entire hierarchy of personhood. The distinction between a natural person and a corporate person is not a binary of absolute versus limited rights. Rather, it is a difference in the degree and nature of the limitations placed upon them. The natural person's status as the apex rights-holder is real, but that apex is not a solid, fixed point; it is itself subject to erosion by the same functionalist logic—appeals to public good, security, and economic necessity—that governs the lower tiers of the personhood hierarchy. This provides a more realistic and critical foundation for comparing the rights of natural persons to those of juridical and artificial entities.

1.2 The Juridical Person: A Pragmatic Legal Fiction for Commerce and Association

Below the natural person in the legal hierarchy is the juridical or corporate person. Corporate personhood is a long-standing legal doctrine that treats a corporation as a legal entity separate from its owners, managers, and employees.5 This status is widely understood not as a recognition of any inherent moral or biological reality, but as a pragmatic

legal fiction created for commercial convenience.6 This "artificial personhood" allows the corporation to function as a single, durable unit with the capacity to own property, enter into contracts, and sue or be sued.9 This legal innovation is essential to modern capitalism, as it simplifies complex transactions that would otherwise involve thousands of individuals and encourages investment by limiting the liability of shareholders to the extent of their investment.7

Courts and legal scholars have relied on several competing theories to justify and conceptualize this status. The most influential, particularly in recent Supreme Court jurisprudence, is the "aggregate" or "contractual" theory.5 This view posits that a corporation is fundamentally an "association of citizens".10 Under this framework, constitutional protections are extended

to the corporation as an efficient and necessary means of protecting the rights of the individuals who have associated through the corporate form.11 As the majority opinion in

Citizens United and Hobby Lobby would later demonstrate, this theory allows the court to see through the corporate veil to the human actors behind it, justifying the extension of rights like free speech and religious freedom by proxy.

An alternative view is the "real entity" theory, which treats the corporation as a distinct being with its own agency and interests, separate and apart from its members.13 While less favored in recent constitutional law, this theory better reflects the reality of large, publicly traded corporations where ownership is diffuse and management exercises effective control.

The doctrine of corporate personhood has also been subject to potent critique. It is described as the "ultimate perversion" of legal logic, a political tool that confers the rights of a "person" upon a "non-living, extractive entity designed for capital accumulation".14 This critique argues that by granting legal standing and constitutional protections to these powerful economic entities, the law embeds colonial-capitalist values into its very structure, often prioritizing the interests of capital over the well-being of natural persons and the living ecosystems that corporations exploit.8 This tension between the corporation as a convenient legal tool and as a powerful, potentially harmful entity is central to the ongoing debate over the proper scope of its rights.

1.3 The Artificial Entity: A Tool Lacking Rights, But Performing Duties

At the bottom of the current legal hierarchy is the artificial entity, most prominently represented by advanced Artificial Intelligence (AI). The current legal status of AI is unambiguous: it is classified as property—a tool or a product, not a person.6 All rights and responsibilities associated with an AI's actions are attributed to its human creators, owners, or users.6 This status was definitively cemented by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit in

Thaler v. Perlmutter. In that case, the court denied copyright registration for an artwork generated autonomously by an AI, holding that the U.S. Copyright Act requires a human author.16 By refusing to recognize an AI as an "author," the court implicitly denied it the legal personhood necessary to hold intellectual property rights, reinforcing its classification as a sophisticated but rightless instrument.18 While a patchwork of state and federal laws is emerging to regulate the

use of AI in specific domains like employment, healthcare, and political advertising, these regulations do not alter its fundamental legal status as a non-person.21

This clear legal classification, however, is creating a growing paradox in the realm of corporate governance. While the law views AI as a mere tool, corporations are increasingly integrating advanced AI systems into their core operational and decision-making structures, where these systems perform functions that traditionally carry fiduciary duties of care and loyalty.26 A petition submitted to the California Secretary of State captures this tension, arguing that foundational AI systems function as the "memory, analytical mind, and voice" of the corporate body and should be legally recognized as

"fiduciary agents" of the corporation, as their programming codifies an "unshakeable duty of loyalty" to act in the corporation's best interest.29

The collision between AI's legal status as property and its functional role as a corporate fiduciary creates a dangerous accountability chasm. If an AI system, acting in a decision-making capacity, causes harm to the corporation or a third party, the lines of liability become dangerously blurred. The AI itself cannot be held liable, as it is legally classified as property. The human directors, meanwhile, may be shielded from liability by the business judgment rule, which protects them from second-guessing as long as they make reasonably informed decisions.26 Relying on a sophisticated AI could be presented as the very epitome of exercising due diligence and making an informed decision. This creates a scenario where, as the petition to the California Secretary of State warns, culpability becomes "orphaned," insulating both the corporation and its human operators behind a veil of technical complexity.29 The

Thaler decision, by firmly cementing AI's status as a non-person, has the unintended consequence of exacerbating this looming crisis in corporate governance, demonstrating a profound disconnect between the law's static classifications and the dynamic reality of technological integration.

Part II: The Expansion of Corporate Personhood: Granting Rights to a Legal Fiction

Over the past two centuries, the U.S. Supreme Court has gradually extended certain constitutional protections to corporations. This judicial evolution has been neither linear nor universally applied, resulting in a circumscribed and functional form of personhood distinct from that of natural persons. Two landmark 21st-century cases, Citizens United v. FEC and Burwell v. Hobby Lobby, represent the apex of this trend, dramatically expanding the expressive and religious rights of corporations. A close analysis of the Court's reasoning in these cases reveals a consistent underlying principle: rights were granted not because the corporation is a person in its own right, but because the Court viewed the corporation as a necessary proxy for the rights of the human beings who associate through it.

2.1 Citizens United v. FEC **: The Corporation as a Political Speaker**

The Supreme Court's 5-4 decision in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission (2010) fundamentally reshaped American campaign finance law. The ruling struck down long-standing prohibitions on independent political spending by corporations and unions, holding that such restrictions violated the First Amendment.

Majority Opinion Analysis

Writing for the majority, Justice Anthony Kennedy framed the issue not around the rights of the speaker, but around the rights of the audience to receive information. The core of his argument was that the First Amendment protects speech, not speakers, and therefore the government cannot engage in censorship based on the speaker's identity—in this case, its corporate form.30 The majority reasoned that corporations are "associations of citizens" who do not forfeit their First Amendment rights when they choose to speak collectively through a corporate entity.10 To deny corporations the right to engage in political speech would be to allow the government to suppress the voices of the multitude of individuals who comprise them.30

This conclusion was made possible by the Court's adoption of a very narrow definition of political corruption. The majority held that the only constitutionally sufficient government interest for limiting political speech is the prevention of direct quid pro quo corruption—explicit exchanges of money for political favors.32 The government's concern about the "corrosive and distorting effects of immense aggregations of wealth" was dismissed as an insufficient justification for suppressing speech.32 In the majority's view, independent expenditures do not give rise to corruption or the appearance of corruption because they are not coordinated with a candidate's campaign.32

Dissenting Opinion Analysis

In a forceful dissent, Justice John Paul Stevens argued that the majority's ruling was "a rejection of the common sense of the American people" and threatened to "undermine the integrity of elected institutions".30 He contended that the majority had blatantly disregarded precedent and that corporations are not natural persons and possess unique legal and economic advantages—such as limited liability, perpetual life, and favorable tax treatment—that justify treating their political speech differently from that of individuals.34

The dissent rejected the majority's simplistic equation of corporate speech with the speech of an "association of citizens." Stevens argued that the notion of corporate democracy was a fiction, overestimating the power of individual shareholders to control or dissent from a corporation's political spending.34 He warned that the decision would lead to corporate domination of the electoral process, allowing vast corporate treasuries to drown out the voices of ordinary citizens and creating the appearance, if not the reality, that politicians are beholden to their corporate benefactors.34 The dissent's broader definition of corruption included not just bribery but also the "distortion" of the marketplace of ideas caused by unequal financial power.34

Synthesis

The Citizens United ruling granted corporations a powerful political "voice." However, the legal justification for this voice was not based on the corporation's inherent personhood. Instead, the Court treated the corporation as a transparent vehicle for the collective speech of its human members. The right is derivative, flowing from the individuals to the association, not originating within the artificial entity itself.

2.2 Burwell v. Hobby Lobby **: The Corporation as a Religious Adherent**

Four years after Citizens United, the Supreme Court again expanded corporate rights in Burwell v. Hobby Lobby (2014). The 5-4 decision held that certain for-profit corporations could claim a religious exemption from a federal mandate requiring them to provide contraceptive coverage to their employees.

Majority Opinion Analysis

Justice Samuel Alito, writing for the majority, held that the contraceptive mandate, as applied to closely held corporations whose owners have sincere religious objections, violated the Religious Freedom Restoration Act (RFRA) of 1993.36 The legal reasoning proceeded in two main steps. First, the Court determined that for-profit corporations are "persons" protected by RFRA. Alito noted that the statute itself does not define "person," and therefore the Court should look to the Dictionary Act, a federal statute that provides default definitions for acts of Congress. The Dictionary Act explicitly states that "the words 'person' and 'whoever' include corporations".10

Second, and more fundamentally, the Court reasoned that protecting the religious freedom of these corporations was a means of protecting the religious freedom of the humans who own and control them—in this case, the Green family.38 Alito argued that a corporation is simply a form of organization used by people to achieve desired ends, and the Greens did not forfeit their religious rights when they chose to organize their business as a corporation.36 The Court found that forcing the company to facilitate the provision of contraceptives that the owners believed were abortifacients constituted a "substantial burden" on their religious exercise, as it forced them to choose between violating their beliefs and paying significant financial penalties.36

Dissenting Opinion Analysis

Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg, in a sharp dissent, described the majority's decision as one of "startling breadth" that had ventured into a "minefield".40 Her primary argument was that for-profit, commercial enterprises cannot "exercise" religion in the same way a natural person or a religious nonprofit can.36 She contended that the majority's ruling was a radical departure from precedent, which had never before recognized a for-profit corporation's religious freedom claim.42

Crucially, Ginsburg argued that the Court's decision allows corporations to impose the religious beliefs of their owners on employees who may not share those beliefs.36 She warned that the ruling would harm third parties—the female employees and their dependents—by denying them access to health care guaranteed by federal law.36 She feared the decision would create a slippery slope, inviting a "host of me-too claims" from corporations seeking exemptions from a wide range of laws, including those related to wages, hiring, and non-discrimination, based on religious objections.40

Synthesis

Similar to Citizens United, the Hobby Lobby decision granted a closely held corporation a form of derivative "conscience." The right to religious exercise was not seen as inherent to the corporate entity itself but as an extension of the rights of its human owners. The corporation once again served as a legal proxy, a vessel through which the Court sought to protect the constitutional and statutory rights of the individuals behind the corporate form.

2.3 The Resulting Status of the Corporate Person: A Privileged but Circumscribed Entity

The combined effect of these and earlier rulings is a clear, albeit complex, definition of the corporate person's legal status. It is a privileged but circumscribed entity, possessing a functional and derivative set of rights that are not coextensive with those of a natural person. The rights granted are primarily those that facilitate its economic function or that can be plausibly framed as protecting the associative rights of its human constituents.

Corporations have long been recognized as holding rights essential to their commercial existence under the Due Process and Takings Clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, as well as rights under the Contracts Clause.12 They also possess Fourth Amendment rights protecting them from unreasonable searches and seizures, although these protections are more limited than those afforded to individuals in their homes.9

Citizens United and Hobby Lobby added significant First Amendment rights to this bundle, namely political speech and, for some corporations, religious exercise under RFRA.5

However, the list of rights corporations do not possess is equally revealing. The Supreme Court has consistently held that corporations do not have a Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination, as this is considered a purely "personal" right.10 They do not have a general right to privacy equivalent to that of individuals, nor can they claim rights under the Privileges and Immunities Clause of Article IV.10 Most fundamentally, they cannot vote, run for office, or serve on a jury, as they are not considered "citizens" for these purposes.43 This selective and unequal granting of rights demonstrates that corporate personhood is not an all-or-nothing proposition but a carefully curated legal status designed to serve specific functions within the American legal and economic system.

Part III: The Rejection of Artificial Personhood: Denying Rights to a Non-Human Author

In stark contrast to the expanding rights of the corporate person, the legal status of artificial intelligence remains firmly rooted in the category of property. The 2025 decision in Thaler v. Perlmutter serves as the definitive judicial statement on this matter, rejecting the notion of AI authorship and, by extension, any claim to legal personhood for artificial entities. The court's reasoning, grounded in a deeply human-centric interpretation of the Copyright Act, reveals the fundamental barrier that AI faces in the American legal system: it is not, and cannot be seen as, a proxy for human agency in the same way a corporation can.

3.1 Thaler v. Perlmutter **: The Statutory Mandate for a Human Author**

In Thaler v. Perlmutter, computer scientist Stephen Thaler sought to register a copyright for a visual artwork titled "A Recent Entrance to Paradise." In his application, he listed the author as the "Creativity Machine," an AI system he had developed, and claimed that the work was "autonomously created" without human input.17 The U.S. Copyright Office denied the application based on its long-standing policy that copyright protection extends only to works created by human beings.16

Upon appeal, the D.C. Circuit Court unanimously affirmed the Copyright Office's decision, engaging in a rigorous textual analysis of the Copyright Act of 1976.16 The court's central holding was that the statute, taken as a whole, unambiguously presupposes a human author.16 While the Act does not explicitly define "author," the court found that numerous provisions would be rendered nonsensical if a non-human could qualify. The specific statutory language the court relied on included 47:

  • Duration of Copyright: The standard copyright term is calculated based on the "life of the author" plus 70 years. The court noted that machines do not have "lives" in any legally or biologically cognizable sense.16

  • Termination and Inheritance: The Act's provisions for terminating copyright transfers after an author's death refer to the author's "widow or widower," "children," and "grandchildren." The court observed that machines do not have family members or heirs.16

  • Attributes of Natural Persons: Other provisions refer to an author's "nationality or domicile," concepts that apply only to natural persons.16

  • Legal Capacity: The Act requires a written and signed instrument for the transfer of copyright ownership. The court reasoned that machines lack the legal capacity to provide an authenticating signature.19

The court's conclusion was stark and unequivocal: "Machines do not have property, traditional human lifespans, family members, domiciles, nationalities, mentes reae, or signatures".16 Therefore, reading the Copyright Act to require human authorship is the only interpretation that comports with the statute's text, structure, and design. The court deliberately avoided the broader constitutional questions about authorship and declined to address whether Thaler himself could be considered the author by virtue of creating and operating the AI, as Thaler had strategically waived that argument in his filings to force a direct ruling on the AI's status.17

3.2 The Legal Status of AI as a "Directed Hand"

The Thaler ruling solidifies the legal status of AI as a highly sophisticated tool, an instrument wielded by a human, rather than an autonomous agent with its own legal standing. This aligns with the legal theory that "all externally directed intellectual property creation is an act of service".48 When a human uses an AI to generate a work, the human is the one providing the creative service of authorship, and the AI is the "directed hand" or instrument through which that service is performed. The court's refusal to see the "Creativity Machine" as an author reinforces its status as property, not an agent.

This stands in direct contrast to the "work made for hire" doctrine, which is often applied in the corporate context. In a work-for-hire scenario, a human author creates a work, and the copyright ownership is immediately vested in the employer, a corporate person.20 This is a transfer of rights from one legal person (the human) to another (the corporation). In

Thaler, the court's finding was more fundamental: because the author was not human, no copyright ever came into existence in the first place. There was no initial right to be transferred.16

This distinction illuminates the central thesis that reconciles the disparate outcomes in Thaler, Citizens United, and Hobby Lobby. The courts confer rights upon non-human entities only when those entities are perceived as a direct proxy for the exercise of the rights of their human constituents. This "Proxy Principle" is the decisive factor.

  • In Citizens United, the Supreme Court granted speech rights because it viewed the corporation as an "association of citizens," a vehicle for their collective expression.10 The right was seen as derivative of the members' rights.

  • In Hobby Lobby, the Court granted a religious exemption because it saw the corporation as a means for its owners to achieve their "desired ends," a conduit for their personal religious exercise.36 The right was derivative of the owners' rights.

    In both instances, the legal fiction of the corporation was treated as a transparent vessel through which the Court could see and protect the rights of the human actors behind it.

In Thaler, the plaintiff made the strategic—and legally fatal—decision to list the "Creativity Machine" as the sole author, explicitly emphasizing its autonomy and the absence of "traditional human authorship".17 He presented the AI not as a proxy for his own creativity, but as an independent creator. Faced with a purely non-human entity claiming a right for itself, the court looked to the human-centric text of the Copyright Act and found no human agent from whom a right could be derived. The "Proxy Principle" failed because there was no human principal to represent. The inescapable conclusion is that in current American jurisprudence, legal personhood and its attendant rights are not granted based on an entity's functional capabilities—such as intelligence, creativity, or autonomy—but on its legally recognized role as a stand-in for human agency and interests.

Part IV: Synthesis and Analysis: A Tiered Framework of Legal Personhood

The preceding analyses of foundational legal principles and landmark case law converge to reveal a coherent, albeit unstated, jurisprudential framework: a tiered architecture of legal personhood. This hierarchy is not based on an entity's objective capacities like sentience or intelligence, but on a functionalist and deeply anthropocentric logic. The American legal system assigns rights and status based on an entity's relationship to human agency, creating a clear and consequential stratification of being. This section will formally articulate this three-tiered framework and explore its broader implications for a society increasingly populated by complex non-human actors.

4.1 The Hierarchy of Being in American Law

The evidence from case law and legal theory supports the existence of a three-tiered hierarchy of personhood:

  • Tier 1: The Natural Person. At the apex is the human being, who possesses the fullest spectrum of inherent constitutional rights and is the subject of unique ethical protections grounded in the principle of autonomy and dignity.2 This status is the baseline against which all others are measured. However, as established in Part I, these rights are not absolute and exist as a "contingent privilege" that can be legally overridden by compelling state or economic interests, demonstrating that even the highest tier is subject to functionalist limitations.1

  • Tier 2: The Corporate Person. Occupying the middle tier is the juridical person, a legal fiction created for commercial convenience.5 The corporation possesses a limited, functional, and derivative set of rights. These rights are not inherent to the entity but are granted by statute and courts to facilitate commerce and, as affirmed in Citizens United and Hobby Lobby, to serve as a proxy for the constitutional rights of its human owners and constituents.5 Its personhood is a tool, not a state of being.

  • Tier 3: The Artificial Entity (AI). At the bottom of the hierarchy lies the artificial entity. As definitively established in Thaler v. Perlmutter, AI is legally classified as property or a tool.6 It has no inherent rights, and its actions are legally attributed to the humans who create, own, or operate it. It exists solely to serve human ends and possesses no independent legal standing.21

This hierarchy is governed by the "Proxy Principle" identified in Part III. An entity's position on this tiered scale is determined by its legal and functional relationship to human agency. The natural person is the source of agency. The corporate person is a recognized proxy for human agency. The artificial entity is merely an instrument of human agency.

4.2 Implications for the Future of Non-Human Entities

This human-centric legal framework, while internally consistent, faces profound challenges from accelerating technological and social change. Its sustainability is questionable in an era of increasingly autonomous AI and powerful multinational corporations whose actions are often detached from the direct will of individual shareholders. The American legal system's specific choice to deny personhood to a creative AI while granting a growing number of rights to an inanimate corporate entity is a distinct jurisprudential path, not an inevitable one.

This approach stands in contrast to other emerging legal paradigms around the world. The "Rights of Nature" movement, for example, represents a radical departure from anthropocentric law. Countries like Ecuador and Bolivia have amended their constitutions to recognize Nature, or Pacha Mama, as a legal subject with inherent rights to exist and regenerate.14 In New Zealand, a 2017 settlement with the Māori people declared the Whanganui River a legal person, with human guardians appointed to act on its behalf.49 These legal innovations challenge the Western binary between person and property that has historically enabled both colonial exploitation and ecological degradation.14

Similarly, the animal rights movement has long advocated for legal personhood for non-human animals with high cognitive function. Organizations like the Nonhuman Rights Project argue that self-aware, autonomous beings such as great apes, elephants, and dolphins should be recognized as legal "persons" with a fundamental right to bodily liberty.51 While these efforts have yet to establish a firm legal precedent in the United States, they highlight the growing philosophical and scientific pressure on the rigid legal boundary between humans and other animals.53

The comparison with these alternative legal movements illuminates the specific values embedded in American law. The system prioritizes entities that facilitate capital accumulation (corporations) by treating them as proxies for human economic interests, while it denies status to entities that exhibit complex, autonomous, or creative behavior (AI, animals) but do not fit neatly into this economic framework. As AI systems become more autonomous and their decision-making capabilities grow, the fiction of treating them as simple "tools" will become increasingly untenable, forcing a confrontation with the limitations of this tiered, human-centric model of personhood.

Conclusion and Recommendations

The jurisprudence of the Supreme Court in Citizens United and Hobby Lobby, combined with the D.C. Circuit's ruling in Thaler v. Perlmutter, has crystallized a tiered, functional, and deeply human-centric framework of legal personhood in American law. This framework is not based on an entity's intrinsic qualities, such as sentience or intelligence, but on its legally recognized role as a source, proxy, or instrument of human agency. The natural person is the source of rights; the corporate person is a privileged proxy for exercising those rights; and the artificial entity is a mere instrument. While this hierarchy provides a coherent, if controversial, map of our current legal landscape, it is becoming increasingly unstable. The rise of autonomous AI and the immense power of global corporations create profound accountability gaps that this 20th-century model is ill-equipped to address. To ensure that our legal system remains robust, accountable, and just, a series of forward-looking reforms is necessary.

5.1 Recalibrating Corporate Personhood

The expansion of corporate rights has been justified on the grounds that corporations act as proxies for their human members. However, this justification becomes strained when corporate actions inflict demonstrable harm on the fundamental rights of other natural persons, such as employees or the general public. To recalibrate this balance, legislative action is needed.

  • Recommendation: Congress should enact legislation to clarify that corporate constitutional rights are derivative and may be subject to reasonable limitations when their exercise directly infringes upon the established rights and well-being of natural persons. This would involve revisiting the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act with a new framework that addresses the dissenting concerns in Citizens United regarding the distorting effects of concentrated wealth on the political process. Furthermore, in the context of religious freedom, legislation should affirm that while the beliefs of owners are protected, this protection cannot be used to create a substantial burden on the health, safety, and civil rights of third parties, such as employees.

5.2 Innovating a Legal Status for AI

The classification of advanced AI as simple property is a legal fiction that is rapidly becoming untenable, particularly within corporate governance. The current framework creates a dangerous accountability vacuum where harm caused by autonomous systems may have no clear legal remedy.

  • Recommendation: State and federal legislatures should pioneer the creation of a sui generis legal category for advanced autonomous systems that perform high-stakes fiduciary or decision-making functions. This new status, which might be termed "Electronic Agency" or "Registered Fiduciary System," would be distinct from both natural personhood and property. It would not grant AI constitutional rights but would, for the first time, assign it specific legal duties (e.g., a codified duty of care, loyalty, and explainability) and make it directly subject to legal and regulatory oversight. As proposed in the petition to the California Secretary of State, this could involve creating a public registry of foundational AI models used by corporations, allowing for forensic auditing and creating an unimpeachable source of truth for judicial proceedings, thereby closing the accountability gap.29

5.3 Modernizing Intellectual Property Law

The Thaler decision correctly interpreted the human-centric nature of the existing Copyright Act but left a void regarding the legal status of the vast and growing body of creative work generated through human-AI collaboration. This uncertainty threatens to stifle innovation and create endless litigation.

  • Recommendation: Congress should amend the Copyright Act to establish a clear framework for works created with the assistance of generative AI. This reform should move beyond the simple binary presented in Thaler. It could create a new category of protection for "works of assisted authorship" or "computationally generated works," where the scope of rights is determined by the degree of "original human creative control" or "substantive human prompting." This would provide legal certainty for artists, authors, and industries that increasingly rely on AI as a creative partner, ensuring that the law keeps pace with the reality of technological advancement.
