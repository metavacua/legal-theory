The Prompt as Expression: A Critical Analysis of the U.S. Copyright Office's Stance on AI Authorship

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

The U.S. Copyright Office (USCO), in its recent guidance, has drawn a firm line in the sand: works generated by artificial intelligence are not copyrightable if the human contribution is limited to providing a text prompt.1 The rationale rests on a cornerstone of copyright law—the idea-expression dichotomy—and a specific interpretation of creative control. The USCO argues that a prompt is merely an unprotectable "idea," and because the AI model's process is unpredictable and not fully controlled by the user, the human prompter cannot be considered the "author" of the final expressive output.3

This position, while attempting to preserve the "bedrock" requirement of human authorship, is founded on a flawed analogy, creates a significant logical paradox when contrasted with the debate around AI training data, and carries the profound, unintended consequence of disenfranchising creators who rely on assistive technologies for expression.

1. The Flawed Analogy: Creative Control in Prompts vs. Photography

The USCO's central argument is that a user lacks sufficient control over an AI's output to be considered its author.4 They analogize a prompter to a client who gives "general directions" to a human artist, rather than being the artist themselves.3 To illustrate this, the Office points to the "gaps" between a prompt and the final image, where the AI fills in unspecified details, demonstrating a lack of total human control.4

This reasoning, however, ignores over a century of copyright precedent established with another transformative technology: the camera. In the landmark 1884 Supreme Court case Burrow-Giles Lithographic Co. v. Sarony, the court affirmed that a photograph was not a mere mechanical reproduction but a work of authorship.3 The photographer's copyright was secured by their creative choices: posing the subject, selecting and arranging the costume, and arranging the lighting and background.3

A skilled prompt engineer engages in a strikingly similar process of creative selection and arrangement. They are not merely stating an idea ("a cat") but are making a series of expressive choices about composition, style, lighting, and subject matter that are directly analogous to the photographer's art. The iterative process of refining prompts, which the USCO dismisses as "re-rolling the dice" and an unprotectable "sweat of the brow" argument, is more accurately compared to an artist making numerous sketches to arrive at a final composition.1

Furthermore, the USCO's focus on the "unpredictability" of AI output as a disqualifier for authorship is a weak standard.4 Many accepted art forms, from Jackson Pollock's drip paintings to forms of improvisational performance, embrace elements of randomness and chaos. A wildlife photographer cannot "control" an animal that steps into the frame at the perfect moment, yet their authorship of the resulting photograph is not in question.4 The law has consistently recognized that the author is the one who, through their intellectual labor and creative choices, causes the work to be fixed in a tangible medium. By this standard, a detailed and refined prompt is not just an idea; it is the primary creative act that brings the work into being.

2. The Training Data Paradox

The USCO's position on prompts becomes even more tenuous when viewed alongside the parallel legal debate over the use of copyrighted materials in AI training datasets. Copyright holders argue that the scraping and copying of their works to train models is mass infringement, while AI developers counter that this is a "transformative" fair use because the models are learning statistical patterns, not the expressive content itself.5

The USCO's emerging guidance on this issue creates a logical contradiction. In its May 2025 report, the Office suggests that training AI models is not always fair use, particularly when the AI's output could harm the market for or substitute the original works it was trained on.7 This implies that the ingestion and processing of training data

does implicate the expressive, copyrightable elements of the source material.

Herein lies the paradox: The USCO's position requires believing two contradictory things simultaneously.

  • On Input (Training): An AI model's ingestion of a copyrighted book is considered a reproduction of its creative expression, which may constitute infringement.

  • On Output (Prompts): A human's detailed instructions to that same model are considered a mere idea, and the resulting output is not a product of human expression because the AI made the final choices.

This is an inconsistent and unsustainable legal posture. The generative AI model is a complex process that transforms expressive input (training data) into expressive output (the generated work). The USCO cannot logically argue that the front end of this process involves copyrightable expression while the back end, initiated by a human's specific and creative instructions, does not. This inconsistency appears to be an attempt to penalize AI developers for their training methods while simultaneously denying copyright protection, and thus economic incentive, to the human creators who use these tools.9

3. The Unintended Consequence: Disenfranchising Disabled Creators

Perhaps the most damaging flaw in the USCO's guidance is its potential impact on creators with disabilities. The rigid definition of "human authorship" as a function of direct, physical control over a medium fails to account for the essential role of assistive technology (AT) in modern expression.11

Consider the case of Stephen Hawking. His books and speeches were authored through a computer-based communication system sponsored by Intel, which used predictive algorithms to translate his minimal physical inputs—a twitch of his cheek—into words, which were then vocalized by a speech synthesizer.12 No one would seriously argue that Intel or the software were the authors of his work. Hawking was the author because the creative and intellectual labor was his; the technology was the tool that translated his mental conception into a fixed expression. Indeed, Hawking himself was known to joke that his iconic synthesized voice was copyrighted.14

The USCO's guidance makes a distinction between AI as an "assistive tool" (permissible) and AI as a "stand-in for human creativity" (not permissible).15 This is a distinction without a meaningful difference for a creator whose disability requires them to use an advanced system to express their ideas. For a person with limited mobility, the act of crafting a detailed, iterative series of prompts is their method of exercising creative control. It is their paintbrush, their camera, their chisel.

By denying copyrightability based on a lack of direct control over the AI's "black box," the USCO risks creating a legal framework that denies authorship to those who rely on the most advanced assistive technologies. This narrow interpretation of creativity could silence the voices of disabled creators, a result that runs contrary to the very purpose of copyright: to promote the progress of science and the useful arts by incentivizing all creators, not just those who can hold a pen.9 The focus should not be on the mechanics of the tool, but on the locus of the creative intent.

The USCO's current position is an overly restrictive and logically inconsistent reaction to a new technology. It relies on a poor analogy that ignores the history of copyright for photography, creates a legal paradox regarding training data, and threatens to exclude disabled creators from copyright protection. A more durable and equitable framework would recognize that a sufficiently detailed and creative prompt is not merely an idea, but a legitimate form of literary and artistic expression worthy of protection.
