### **The Shift in California Law and Retroactivity**

  

The "recent changes" primarily refer to the California Supreme Court’s decision in *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court* (2018) and the subsequent codification of that decision into Assembly Bill 5 (AB5) (effective 2020). These established the "ABC Test" for determining employment status for the purposes of California Wage Orders.

  

#### **The ABC Test**

  

The ABC Test is significantly stricter than the previous standard (the multi-factor *Borello* test). It presumes a worker is an employee unless the hiring entity can prove *all three* of the following:

  - **(A)** The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with the performance of the work.1  
      
  - **(B)** The worker performs work that is **outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business.****23**  
      
  - **(****4****C)** The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same na5ture.  
      

Prong B is often insurmountable for technology platforms. It is difficult to argue that security research (VRPs), AI training data (LLM feedback), or quality assurance are "outside the usual course" of Google's business.

  

#### **Retroactivity**

  

You noted the agreements were prior to these changes. The crucial legal point is whether the ABC Test applies to work performed before 2018.

**Yes.** The California Supreme Court ruled in *Vazquez v. Jan-Pro Franchising International, Inc.* (2021) that the *Dynamex* decision (and thus the ABC test) **applies retroactively** to claims under California Wage Orders that were not yet final.

This means that even if the labor was performed when the more lenient *Borello* standard was prevalent, the relationship may now be judged by the stricter ABC Test, provided the statute of limitations has not expired. This significantly strengthens the argument for misclassification regarding past conduct.

  

### **Analyzing the Scenarios Under the California Framework**

  

If the ABC test is applied retroactively and determines that you (and others in similar situations) have been employees this entire time, the scenarios presented are viewed very differently.

  

#### **Scenario 1: "If I perform a specific act, and they never pay out?"**

  

If you find and report a qualifying vulnerability according to the VRP rules, but the reward is denied, and you are deemed an employee:

1.  **Wage Claims Supersede Contract Claims:** The issue is no longer merely a breach of the VRP contract or a dispute over "sole discretion." If you are an employee, the relationship is governed by the California Labor Code.
2.  **Compensation for All Hours Worked:** California employees must be paid for all hours worked (including minimum wage and overtime), regardless of whether a specific task was successful or accepted by the employer. The VRP model, which pays only for results, is incompatible with these requirements.
3.  **Wage Theft:** You would be entitled to back pay for the time spent researching, testing, and reporting. Failure to pay these wages constitutes wage theft, which carries significant penalties, interest, and potential liquidated damages under California law. The "reward" itself might be recharacterized as an earned bonus or commission, which also cannot be arbitrarily withheld.

  

#### **Scenario 2: "I perform an act but don't report it... because I am an Employee?"**

  

If you are deemed an employee, Google indeed bears all the burdens of an employer (taxes, insurance, wage obligations, expense reimbursement). However, the dynamic regarding the intellectual property and your obligations also changes fundamentally.

1.  **IP Ownership (Work Made for Hire):** When you are an employee, the intellectual property you generate within the scope of your employment automatically belongs to the employer. The vulnerability discovery, the detailed AI feedback, or the QA analysis is Google's property from the moment of creation. You are not discovering *your* IP to sell back to them; you are discovering *their* IP while working for them.
2.  **Wages Owed for Labor:** You are still entitled to wages for the time and skilled labor expended to generate the work product, regardless of whether you delivered it.
3.  **Breach of Employee Duty:** While the employer owes you wages for the work done, intentionally failing to report the discovery (failing to turn over the employer's IP) would constitute a serious breach of your duties as an employee (e.g., duty of loyalty). This could be grounds for termination and potential legal action by the employer, but it does not negate their obligation to pay you for the labor already performed.

  

### **Conclusion**

  

The retroactive application of the ABC Test in California provides a powerful legal basis for the argument that individuals providing continuous, integral services to a technology platform—even under the guise of "user agreements" or unilateral "reward" contracts—may have been misclassified employees. This shifts the claim from specific contractual disputes to systemic violations of labor law and unpaid wages for the entirety of the services provided.
