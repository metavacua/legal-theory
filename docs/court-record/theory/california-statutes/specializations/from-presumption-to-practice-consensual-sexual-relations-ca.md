# **From Presumption to Practice: A Legal Analysis of Consensual Sexual Relations and Economic Exchange in California**

  
  

## **Introduction**

  

California law presents a profound legal and logical ambiguity concerning sexual acts that involve material support or economic benefit. At the heart of this ambiguity lies Penal Code § 647(b), the statute criminalizing prostitution. By defining the crime as a specific type of bargained-for exchange—a sexual or lewd act for "compensation, money, or anything of value"—the law implicitly acknowledges a foundational principle of contract law: that a sexual act can function as legal "consideration".1 This acknowledgment creates a paradox. If a sexual act can be the object of an

*illegal* contract, it must logically be capable of being the object of a *legal* one. The statute, therefore, does not prohibit all economic exchanges involving sex but only a narrowly defined subset. This forces a critical legal question: what distinguishes a lawful, consensual sexual relationship involving economic exchange from the crime of prostitution? The answer, as this report will argue, lies in the formation of a lawful contract, whether express or implied, and the purpose for which the exchange is made.

This report establishes that to avoid criminality under the current statutory scheme, the formation of a lawful contract for sexual acts is a logical necessity. The state's own methods of prosecuting prostitution, which often rely on proving an implied criminal agreement through conduct and circumstance, affirm that such agreements can exist and have legal significance. It follows that lawful agreements, similarly manifested, must also be legally cognizable.

To explore the full ramifications of this legal landscape, this report will employ a rigorous comparative analysis of two opposing legal presumptions. The first framework presumes that any solicitation of a sex act involving an economic exchange is an act of prostitution unless the defendant can prove otherwise. The second, and opposing, framework presumes that any such solicitation between consenting adults is lawful unless the state can prove, beyond a reasonable doubt, that it meets the specific criminal definition of prostitution. By comparing and contrasting these two frameworks, this report will dissect their cascading consequences on court decisions, legislative policy, community standards, and the fundamental organization of personal relationships in California.

The analysis will proceed in five parts. Part I will deconstruct the statutory elements of prostitution and establish the logical necessity of the implied lawful contract for sexual acts. Part II will examine the robust protections afforded by the First Amendment, arguing that the precedent set in *People v. Freeman* and the constitutional obscenity standard from *Miller v. California* provide a framework for structuring enforceable, non-criminal contracts for sexual acts as a form of protected expression. Part III will form the analytical core of the report, presenting a detailed comparative analysis of the two opposing legal presumptions and their systemic impacts. Part IV will explore concrete classifications of "serious works"—sexual acts performed for lawful purposes such as reproductive rights, therapeutic practices, and artistic expression—that fall outside the ambit of prostitution. Finally, Part V will analyze the legal treatment of marriage as a hybrid of status and contract to demonstrate that California law already recognizes and quantifies the economic value within intimate, sexual relationships, exposing the logical inconsistency of criminalizing similar exchanges in non-marital contexts. The report will conclude with a synthesis of these findings and offer legislative, judicial, and policy recommendations aimed at resolving the current ambiguity in favor of personal autonomy, contractual freedom, and a more rational legal order.

  

## **Part I: The Statutory and Contractual Foundation of Sexual Exchange**

  

This part establishes the foundational legal principles from California's Penal and Civil Codes that govern the analysis. It deconstructs the crime of prostitution to its essential elements, revealing that the law primarily targets the formation of a specific type of forbidden contract. Building on this contractual understanding, it then posits that the existence of an implied lawful contract for sexual acts is not merely a theoretical possibility but a logical necessity for distinguishing consensual, non-criminal exchanges from the specific conduct proscribed by statute.

  

### **1.1 Deconstructing Penal Code § 647(b): The Elements of Prostitution**

  

California Penal Code § 647(b) serves as the primary statutory instrument regulating sexual commerce. A meticulous examination of its text and judicial interpretation reveals that the law is not a blanket prohibition on sex involving economic benefit, but a precise criminalization of a specific contractual arrangement. The statute makes it a misdemeanor offense to commit any one of three distinct acts: to solicit an act of prostitution, to agree to engage in an act of prostitution, or to actually engage in an act of prostitution.3 This tripartite structure is critical, as it allows for criminal liability to attach well before any sexual act occurs. The legal focus is often on the communication and formation of an agreement, rather than on the physical act itself.

The core of the offense is the bargained-for exchange. Prostitution is defined as the performance of "sexual intercourse or a lewd act" in exchange for "money or other compensation" or, more broadly, "anything of value".1 The phrase "anything of value" is intentionally broad, encompassing not just direct monetary payment but also goods, services, or any other form of material support or economic benefit, which lies at the heart of the central query of this report. For example, proposing to provide drugs in exchange for a sexual act falls within the statute's purview.5

The definition of a "lewd act" further narrows the statute's scope. It is not any physical touch, but specifically the "touching of the genitals, buttocks, or female breast of either the prostitute or customer with some part of the other person’s body for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification of either person".2 This element of specific intent—the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification—is a crucial distinguishing factor. An exchange involving physical contact for a different purpose, such as a medical examination or an artistic performance, would not meet this definition.

Perhaps the most significant element from a contractual perspective is the treatment of an "agreement" to engage in prostitution. California law requires more than just verbal assent for such an agreement to be criminal. An individual agrees to engage in prostitution when, with specific intent, they manifest acceptance of an offer; however, no such agreement constitutes a violation "unless some act, in addition to the agreement, is done within this state in furtherance of the commission of an act of prostitution".3 This requirement of an "overt act" reinforces the contractual nature of the crime. This act demonstrates a concrete step toward performance of the illegal contract. Examples cited in legal analyses include withdrawing money from an ATM to make the agreed-upon payment, driving to the designated location for the encounter, or instructing the other party to undress.2

The legal framework of PC § 647(b) is thus not primarily concerned with the morality of a sexual act itself, but with the formation and execution of a specific type of commercial contract deemed illicit by the state. The elements of the crime—solicitation (offer), agreement (acceptance), specific intent, compensation (consideration), and an overt act in furtherance—are direct parallels to the elements of contract formation. The state, in a prostitution case, effectively prosecutes the parties for entering into and taking steps to perform a forbidden contract. This focus on the *agreement* is the lynchpin of this report's analysis, as it logically opens the door to an examination of what constitutes a *lawful* agreement involving sexual acts.

  

### **1.2 The Implied Contract for Lawful Sexual Acts: A Logical Necessity**

  

The contractual nature of the crime of prostitution creates a logical imperative for the existence of lawful contracts for sexual acts. If a sexual act can serve as the consideration for an illegal contract, it must also be capable of serving as the consideration for a legal one. To argue otherwise would be to suggest that a sexual act is an object that can only ever have illegal contractual significance, a position contrary to the presumptive legality of consensual sexual activity between adults. This section develops the argument that an implied contract for lawful sexual acts is a necessary legal construct to distinguish permissible exchanges from those criminalized by PC § 647(b).

Under California Civil Code § 1621, an implied contract is defined as one "the existence and terms of which are manifested by conduct".8 Unlike an express contract, where terms are stated in words, an implied-in-fact contract is inferred from the actions, circumstances, and relationship of the parties.8 To be enforceable, an implied contract must still contain the essential elements of any contract: an unambiguous offer and acceptance, mutual intent to be bound, and consideration.11 Consideration, as defined by California Civil Code § 1605, is any benefit conferred upon the promisor or any prejudice suffered by the promisee as an inducement for the promise.13 It is the bargained-for exchange of value that makes an agreement legally binding.14

The state's method of prosecuting prostitution cases provides a powerful, albeit unintentional, validation of this contractual framework. Prosecutors frequently build cases based on circumstantial evidence, using the conduct of the parties to prove the existence of an implied criminal agreement. An undercover officer's testimony about gestures, coded language, the location of the encounter, and discussions of money are all used to demonstrate that an illicit bargain was struck, even without a formal written or oral agreement.15 This prosecutorial strategy is, in effect, an argument that an

*implied criminal contract* was formed, with the parties' conduct manifesting its illegal terms.

This legal practice has a profound logical consequence. If the state can successfully argue that a criminal contract for sex can be implied from conduct, it cannot simultaneously deny that a lawful contract for sex can also be implied from conduct. The state's own legal arguments affirm the underlying principle that a contract for a sexual act can be inferred from the circumstances of an interaction. Given that consensual sex between adults is presumptively legal, any such interaction that involves a bargained-for exchange must, to avoid criminality, be construed as a lawful contract.

Therefore, the implied lawful contract is not just a theoretical construct; it is a logical necessity. It serves as the essential legal mechanism for distinguishing a lawful exchange of value between consenting adults from the specific, illegal contract of prostitution. For example, in a relationship where one partner provides financial support and the other provides companionship, domestic services, and sexual intimacy, their ongoing conduct manifests an implied agreement. To avoid this being captured by the broad language of PC § 647(b) ("anything of value"), the law must recognize this as an implied, lawful contract where the consideration is a complex bundle of benefits, not merely a quid pro quo for a lewd act. The state cannot logically prosecute implied criminal contracts based on conduct while refusing to recognize the existence of implied lawful ones based on different conduct and intent.

  

## **Part II: The First Amendment Shield: Sexual Acts as Protected Expression**

  

While contract law provides the structure for understanding sexual exchanges, the First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution offers a powerful shield for their legality. By classifying sexual acts as a form of non-obscene expressive conduct, the First Amendment provides a robust framework for structuring lawful, enforceable contracts that fall outside the purview of prostitution laws. This part analyzes the foundational California Supreme Court precedent in *People v. Freeman*, applies the federal obscenity test from *Miller v. California* to private conduct, and examines the practicalities of enforcing such contracts and the remedies available for their breach.

  

### **2.1 Beyond the Silver Screen: The Enduring Precedent of** ***People v. Freeman***

  

The California Supreme Court's decision in *People v. Freeman*, 46 Cal. 3d 419 (1988), is a landmark ruling that fundamentally altered the legal analysis of commercialized sexual conduct in the state. Though the case involved the production of an adult film, its reasoning extends far beyond that specific context, establishing a crucial principle: the purpose of a payment determines whether an exchange constitutes prostitution or protected expression. The case should not be viewed as a narrow exception for the pornography industry, but as a foundational statement on the constitutional limits of prostitution and pandering laws when they intersect with expressive activity.

In *Freeman*, the defendant, a producer of adult films, was convicted of pandering under Penal Code § 266i for hiring and paying actors to engage in sexually explicit acts, including intercourse and oral copulation, for a non-obscene film.17 The prosecution argued that because the actors performed sexual acts for money, they were engaging in prostitution, and the defendant was therefore guilty of procuring them for that purpose.17

The California Supreme Court unanimously reversed the conviction. The court's central reasoning hinged on a critical distinction regarding the *mens rea*, or criminal intent, associated with the payment. It held that to constitute prostitution, "the money or other consideration must be paid for the purpose of sexual arousal or gratification".18 In Freeman's case, the court found no evidence that the payments were for his own or the actors' sexual gratification. Instead, they were "acting fees" paid for the actors' performance in a film—an expressive work.18 The court concluded that Freeman "did not engage in either the requisite conduct nor did he have the requisite mens rea or purpose to establish procurement for purposes of prostitution".19

Crucially, the court did not stop at this statutory interpretation. It explicitly grounded its decision in constitutional principles, stating that to construe the pandering statute to apply to the hiring of actors for a non-obscene motion picture would "unlawfully impinge upon protected First Amendment rights".17 This linkage is paramount. It establishes that when a sexual act is part of a non-obscene expressive work, the entire commercial apparatus surrounding it—hiring, payment, production—is shielded by the First Amendment. The precedent, therefore, decriminalizes the

*commercialization* of lawful sexual expression. The key determinant is not the presence of sex and money, but the purpose of the exchange. If the purpose is expressive, and the expression is not obscene, the conduct is constitutionally protected. This principle is directly applicable to any context, commercial or private, where a sexual act is performed for an expressive purpose in exchange for compensation.

  

### **2.2 Applying the** ***Miller*** **Test to Private Conduct**

  

The precedent set in *Freeman* hinges on the determination that the expressive work is "non-obscene." The legal standard for obscenity was established by the U.S. Supreme Court in *Miller v. California*, 413 U.S. 15 (1973). This test, known as the *Miller* test, provides a clear, three-pronged framework that can be proactively used to structure a private sexual exchange as constitutionally protected speech, thereby placing it outside the reach of both obscenity and prostitution laws.21

For a work or conduct to be deemed obscene, and thus lose its First Amendment protection, it must meet *all three* of the following criteria:

1.  The average person, applying contemporary community standards, would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the "prurient interest" (i.e., a shameful or morbid interest in sex).23
2.  The work depicts or describes, in a "patently offensive way," sexual conduct specifically defined by applicable state law.23
3.  The work, taken as a whole, "lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" (often referred to as the SLAPS test).22

Because the test is conjunctive, if the conduct or work in question fails even one of these three prongs, it is not legally obscene and is protected by the First Amendment.25 This provides a clear roadmap for structuring a lawful contract for sexual acts.

A private agreement for sexual acts could be designed to fail the *Miller* test in multiple ways. First, if the acts are undertaken for a purpose other than appealing to a prurient interest—for example, for therapeutic exploration, relational development, performance art, or political protest—the conduct would fail the first prong. The focus is not on base arousal but on a different, legitimate goal.

Second, and most powerfully, the agreement can be structured to ensure it does not fail the third "SLAPS" prong. A contract that explicitly defines the purpose of the sexual exchange as having artistic, political, scientific (e.g., reproductive), or even serious relational or therapeutic value would provide strong evidence against a finding of obscenity. The contract itself becomes a critical part of the "work, taken as a whole." The very existence of a detailed, negotiated instrument that articulates the serious intentions of the parties is powerful evidence that the participants themselves ascribe serious value to the activity. A court would have to find that this contractual framework was a mere sham to disregard it. Therefore, a well-drafted contract can function as a legal shield, creating its own evidence that the conduct possesses "serious value" and is thus constitutionally protected expression.

  

### **2.3 Enforceability and Remedies for Breach**

  

Once a contract for sexual performance is established as lawful and constitutionally protected, the practical question of its enforceability arises. If one party breaches the agreement—for instance, by failing to perform the promised expressive act or by failing to provide the agreed-upon compensation—the legal system must provide a remedy. The framework for remedies in California contract law is well-established and, critically, avoids the most problematic aspects of enforcing such a personal agreement.

A primary ethical and legal concern with "sex contracts" is the specter of compelled sexual activity or indentured servitude. However, California law has a pre-existing and unequivocal solution to this problem. California Civil Code § 3390(a) explicitly states that an "obligation to render personal service" cannot be specifically enforced.27 This means that a court cannot issue an order compelling a person to perform a personal service, whether that service is singing an aria, painting a portrait, or engaging in a sexual act. This long-standing principle of contract law acts as a crucial safeguard. It ensures that no individual can be judicially forced to have sex against their will, even if they have contractually agreed to do so.

The unavailability of specific performance does not, however, render the contract unenforceable. The standard remedy for a breach of contract is an award of monetary damages.29 The goal of damages is to compensate the non-breaching party for the "benefit of the bargain"—that is, to put them in the economic position they would have been in had the contract been fully performed.31

In the context of a breach of a lawful contract for sexual performance, the legal analysis is straightforward. If the breach is the non-performance of the sexual act, then no sex act has occurred, and therefore no crime of prostitution has been committed. A lawsuit for damages is not an action to enforce prostitution; it is an action to remedy the financial or other harm caused by the failure to perform a promised, lawful act. The legal question then becomes one of valuation. A court would need to determine the monetary damages resulting from the breach. This valuation could be established in several ways:

  - **Liquidated Damages:** The contract itself could contain a liquidated damages clause, as permitted by California Civil Code § 1671, specifying a pre-agreed amount of damages in the event of a breach. Such a clause is valid so long as the amount was a reasonable estimate of potential damages at the time the contract was formed.33
  - **Value of Consideration:** The damages could be based on the value of the consideration that was exchanged or promised.
  - **Expert Testimony:** Damages could be established through expert testimony regarding the value of similar expressive or therapeutic performances, analogous to how damages are calculated for a cancelled artistic performance or a breach of a contract for unique personal services.32

The inability to compel performance is therefore a feature, not a bug, of this legal framework. It cleanly separates the enforcement of the agreement from any physical compulsion, transforming the dispute from one about bodies and autonomy into one about economics—a domain that courts are well-equipped to adjudicate. This makes the concept of an enforceable contract for sexual acts far more legally manageable and ethically palatable than it might initially appear.

  

## **Part III: A Tale of Two Presumptions: Comparative Analysis of Legal Frameworks**

  

The legal status of sexual relations involving economic exchange in California hinges on a pivotal, yet unstated, legal presumption. Does the law presume such arrangements are criminal until proven otherwise, or does it presume they are lawful until the state proves criminality? The choice between these two frameworks is not a minor legal technicality; it is a foundational decision with cascading effects on the burden of proof in court, the direction of legislative policy, the focus of law enforcement, and the social norms governing personal relationships. This part systematically compares these two opposing legal frameworks—a presumption of illegality versus a presumption of legality—to illuminate their profound and divergent consequences.

  

### **3.1 Framework A: The Presumption of Illegality (Solicitation as Presumptively Prostitution)**

  

This framework operates on the assumption that any solicitation for a sexual act that involves the exchange of "anything of value" is presumptively a violation of Penal Code § 647(b). Under this model, the burden falls upon the individuals involved to demonstrate that their conduct falls within a legally recognized exception. This presumption is strengthened considerably by examining the special case where the *only* consideration exchanged between competent, consenting adults is the mutual performance of sexual acts. Foundational contract law defines "consideration" as something of value exchanged between parties.12 If a sexual act itself is deemed to have economic value—a necessary premise for it to be the object of the crime of prostitution—then a mutual promise to exchange sexual services is a contract where each promise serves as consideration for the other.76 Consequently, under a presumption of illegality, the broad language of Penal Code § 647(b) would apply to nearly every communicated attempt to have a sexual relationship. An offer to engage in consensual sex would be legally indistinguishable from a solicitation for prostitution, as it is a request to exchange one "thing of value" (one person's sexual act) for another. This interpretation would render every sexual proposition between any two people presumptively criminal, regardless of their legal competence or the consensual nature of the interaction, until proven otherwise.

  

#### **Judicial Consequences**

  

The most immediate and dramatic consequence of this framework would be a fundamental shift in the burden of proof in criminal proceedings. In the American justice system, the prosecution bears the burden of proving every element of a crime beyond a reasonable doubt, and the defendant is presumed innocent.36 A presumption of illegality would invert this principle. The prosecution's initial burden would be significantly lightened; it would only need to establish that there was a solicitation for a sexual act connected to some form of compensation or benefit.

Once this prima facie case is made, the burden would shift to the defendant to prove that their conduct was lawful.38 This would force the defendant to raise and prove an affirmative defense.40 For example, the defendant would have to affirmatively prove that the exchange was for a legitimate purpose, such as artistic expression, reproductive assistance, or that it was a non-transactional component of a broader supportive relationship. This places the accused in the untenable position of having to prove their innocence, contradicting the core tenets of criminal law. Defenses like "mistake of fact" (e.g., a genuine belief that the arrangement was lawful) or the novel defense that the act constituted a "serious work" would have to be established by the defendant by a preponderance of the evidence, rather than the state having to disprove them beyond a reasonable doubt.41

  

#### **Legislative and Policy Consequences**

  

A presumption of illegality would drive a specific legislative approach: the creation of a broad prohibition with narrowly tailored, specific statutory exceptions. The law would function as a "guilty until proven exempt" scheme. Instead of a legal system that protects a broad sphere of private consensual activity, the legislature would be tasked with identifying and explicitly legalizing certain acceptable forms of exchange. We see this already with surrogacy statutes, but this approach would need to be extended to every conceivable lawful arrangement. Any relationship structure not fitting neatly into a pre-approved legislative box would remain under a cloud of potential criminality.

This framework would also shape law enforcement policy. It would empower and encourage police to investigate a wide array of personal relationships where financial support is a component. Undercover sting operations could target not only street-level sex work but also online platforms for "sugar dating" or other compensated companionship arrangements. This would lead to a significant expansion of policing into the private lives of consenting adults, raising serious privacy concerns and risking the misapplication of criminal resources away from cases of actual harm, such as trafficking and coercion.6

  

#### **Social and Community Consequences**

  

The social impact of presuming illegality would be profound and largely negative. It would cast a chilling effect over any relationship that deviates from a traditional, purely romantic model where finances are either completely separate or merged only through marriage. Relationships involving significant age or wealth disparities, where one partner provides substantial financial support, would become legally perilous. Cohabitation arrangements where one partner supports the other in exchange for domestic management and intimacy could be viewed with suspicion.

This legal presumption would reinforce and amplify social stigma against individuals in transactional relationships. It could easily be weaponized in personal disputes. For example, in a contentious breakup or divorce, one party could threaten to report the other for prostitution to gain leverage in civil proceedings.43 This mirrors the documented negative social impacts of broad criminalization of sex work, which increases vulnerability and marginalizes individuals rather than protecting them.44 The community standard would shift toward suspicion, forcing many to conceal the true nature of their consensual arrangements for fear of legal reprisal.

  

### **3.2 Framework B: The Presumption of Legality (Solicitation as Presumptively Lawful)**

  

This alternative framework aligns with traditional principles of criminal law and personal liberty. It presumes that any solicitation for a sexual act between consenting adults is lawful private conduct. The burden remains squarely on the state to prove, beyond a reasonable doubt, that a specific act of solicitation meets all the statutory elements of criminal prostitution.

  

#### **Judicial Consequences**

  

This framework maintains the integrity of the criminal justice process. The prosecution would carry the full burden of proof on every element of the offense as defined in PC § 647(b).15 This includes proving not just an exchange, but the specific criminal intent (

*mens rea*) that the compensation was a direct quid pro quo for a lewd act intended for sexual gratification, as articulated in *People v. Freeman*.18

Under this presumption, the defendant's primary legal strategy would not be to prove an affirmative defense, but to create reasonable doubt regarding the prosecution's case.43 Evidence of a different, lawful purpose for the exchange—such as companionship, artistic collaboration, or shared living expenses—would not be part of an affirmative defense but would be introduced to directly rebut the prosecution's claim of criminal intent. The existence of a lawful contract, express or implied, would serve as powerful evidence to negate the element of criminal intent, making it significantly more difficult for the state to secure a conviction based on ambiguous circumstances.

  

#### **Legislative and Policy Consequences**

  

A presumption of legality would encourage a more rational and focused legislative approach. Instead of creating a patchwork of exceptions, legislative efforts could focus on clarifying and, if necessary, narrowing the definition of prostitution to more effectively distinguish it from the vast array of other interpersonal economic exchanges. This approach would be consistent with public health and human rights-oriented policies that advocate for the decriminalization of consensual sexual activity.45

Law enforcement policy would consequently shift. With a higher burden of proof and a legal presumption in favor of liberty, police resources would be more efficiently directed away from monitoring consensual behavior and toward investigating and prosecuting genuine crimes of exploitation, human trafficking, and violence.47 This aligns with the goals of decriminalization movements, which argue that removing the criminal stigma from consensual acts empowers individuals to report abuse and violence, leading to better safety and health outcomes for everyone.

  

#### **Social and Community Consequences**

  

Socially, a presumption of legality would affirm and protect personal autonomy and the right of individuals to form diverse types of relationships without fear of state interference. It would recognize that consenting adults are capable of structuring their intimate lives and financial arrangements in ways that suit them, and that such arrangements are not inherently criminal.

This framework would reduce the stigma associated with transactional relationships and create an environment where individuals could be more open about their arrangements. Crucially, it would allow parties to these relationships to seek legal protection when needed. For instance, a person who has been defrauded or has had a contract breached by a partner could seek recourse in civil court without the fear that doing so would expose them to criminal prosecution. This fosters a community standard based on consent, transparency, and accountability rather than one based on fear, secrecy, and suspicion.

## **Part IV: Classifying "Serious Works": Lawful Purposes for Contractual Sexual Acts**

  

The distinction between a criminal act of prostitution and a lawful exchange involving sex often hinges on the purpose of the act. Building on the First Amendment framework established in Part II, this section provides concrete examples of sexual acts that, when contextualized by their purpose, fall outside the scope of prostitution and may qualify for legal protection under the "serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" prong of the *Miller* test. These examples demonstrate that California law already recognizes the legality of compensated arrangements involving bodily autonomy when a "serious" purpose is present.

  

### **4.1 Reproductive Rights as "Serious Scientific Value": The Case of Surrogacy**

  

The most well-developed and legally sanctioned example of a compensated contract involving reproductive acts is gestational surrogacy. California's comprehensive and permissive legal framework for surrogacy serves as a powerful precedent, demonstrating the state's ability to regulate and enforce complex agreements involving payment for the use of another person's body for a serious purpose, thereby distinguishing it from criminal activity.

California law, through both statute and landmark case law, explicitly permits and enforces paid gestational surrogacy contracts.50 These are not simple agreements; they are highly detailed legal instruments that meticulously govern the entire process. The contracts cover the surrogate's base compensation, reimbursement for medical expenses, lost wages, and other costs, as well as crucial terms regarding medical procedures, decision-making during pregnancy, and the establishment of parental rights.51 This level of detail underscores the contractual nature of the arrangement.

Critically, the California Family Code mandates that both the intended parents and the surrogate must have separate, independent legal counsel throughout the negotiation and execution of the agreement.50 This requirement is a key safeguard, ensuring that the contract is entered into voluntarily, with full information, and without coercion or exploitation. It is a formal recognition of the gravity of the agreement and a mechanism to balance the power dynamics between the parties.

Furthermore, the state's judicial system actively participates in enforcing these contracts. Courts in California routinely issue "pre-birth orders," which are judgments that legally establish the intended parents as the child's parents before the birth even occurs.50 This legal procedure confirms the state's unequivocal recognition and enforcement of the parties' original intent as expressed in the contract, making clear that the surrogate has no parental rights or responsibilities.52

The legal framework for surrogacy in California provides a complete and successful blueprint for how the state can regulate lawful contracts involving bodily autonomy and compensation. The arrangement involves significant payment for the use of a person's body to achieve a specific and serious purpose—the creation of a family, which can be classified as having "serious scientific value" in the context of assisted reproductive technology. The existing legal model has already solved the key challenges inherent in such agreements: it ensures informed consent through independent counsel, provides clarity and predictability through detailed written contracts, and guarantees legal certainty through judicial enforcement and pre-birth orders. This demonstrates that the state is fully capable of drawing a bright line between a compensated act for a serious purpose and the crime of prostitution. This framework can therefore serve as a direct legislative and judicial model for recognizing other contracts for sexual acts that have a different, but equally "serious," artistic, therapeutic, or relational purpose.

  

### **4.2 Therapeutic and Healing Practices: Navigating the Legal Boundaries**

  

The intersection of sexual acts and therapeutic practices presents a legally complex and sensitive area. While California law strictly prohibits sexual contact within the context of licensed therapy to prevent exploitation, it also provides a separate framework for unlicensed healing arts. This creates a potential, albeit narrow, pathway for lawful agreements involving sexual acts for therapeutic purposes, provided they are structured with full transparency and outside the traditional, licensed medical paradigm.

The state has a compelling interest in protecting patients from abuse by licensed professionals. California law creates a clear, bright-line rule: sexual contact of any kind between a licensed therapist (such as a psychologist, psychiatrist, or clinical social worker) and a current client is illegal and unethical.53 This prohibition extends for two years after the termination of therapy, recognizing the enduring power imbalance and potential for undue influence that persists even after the formal therapeutic relationship has ended.53 This rule is absolute and is designed to protect vulnerable individuals within a relationship of trust and authority.

However, California law also acknowledges the existence of a wide range of alternative and complementary healing modalities that fall outside the scope of state-licensed medicine. The Business and Professions Code creates a legal framework for *unlicensed* practitioners, such as naturopaths or homeopaths, to operate lawfully.54 The key requirement under sections 2053.5 and 2053.6 is disclosure. These practitioners must inform each client in writing, before treatment begins, that they are "not licensed by the state as a healing arts practitioner" and must have the client sign an acknowledgment of this disclosure.54 They are also prohibited from performing certain medical acts, such as prescribing drugs or performing surgery, and may not diagnose physical or mental conditions.54

This dual system—a strict prohibition for licensed professionals and a disclosure-based system for unlicensed ones—suggests a potential legal space for consensual, compensated sexual acts intended for a therapeutic purpose. An individual acting as a "sexual surrogate" or providing other forms of hands-on sexual therapy, if structured correctly, might operate within the unlicensed practitioner framework. To do so, the practitioner would have to scrupulously avoid presenting themselves as a licensed medical or psychological professional and comply with all disclosure requirements. An agreement for such an act, framed under the explicit disclosures required by the Business and Professions Code, could be argued as a lawful contract for an unlicensed healing art. This would distinguish it from the professional misconduct of a licensed therapist, on one hand, and from the criminal act of prostitution, on the other, by grounding its legality in the principles of informed consent, full disclosure, and a clearly articulated therapeutic (rather than purely gratificational) intent.

  

### **4.3 Artistic and Expressive Performance: Differentiating Art from Indecency**

  

The distinction between constitutionally protected artistic expression and a public order crime like indecent exposure provides another clear example of how intent and context define the legality of an act involving the human body. The same physical act—public nudity or a simulated sex act—can be either a criminal offense or a protected performance, with the decisive factor being the purpose behind the act. This principle directly parallels the distinction between prostitution and protected expression, offering a strong basis for legalizing compensated sexual acts performed as art.

California Penal Code § 314 criminalizes indecent exposure, which involves three key elements: (1) the defendant willfully exposed their genitals; (2) in a public place or where others were present who might be offended; and (3) the exposure was done with a *lewd intent*, for the purpose of sexual gratification or to offend others.55 The requirement of lewd intent is the critical element that separates criminal conduct from other forms of nudity.

The law itself implicitly recognizes an exception for artistic expression. For example, a nude model posing for a university art class is not committing indecent exposure because there is no lewd intent; the purpose is educational and artistic.55 This principle is rooted in the First Amendment, which protects expressive conduct—behavior designed to convey a particular message that is likely to be understood by its audience.59 A performance artist using nudity or simulated sexual acts to make a political or social statement is engaging in a form of speech, and this conduct is constitutionally protected as long as it is not obscene under the

*Miller* test.61

This legal logic directly mirrors the reasoning in *People v. Freeman*. In *Freeman*, the court differentiated between payment for "sexual gratification" (which would be prostitution) and payment for "acting" in an expressive work. Similarly, in the context of public nudity, the law distinguishes between exposure for "lewd" gratification (indecent exposure) and exposure for an artistic or political message (protected expression). Intent is the dispositive legal factor in both scenarios.

Therefore, a contract that frames a sexual act as part of an artistic performance would place the conduct squarely on the protected side of this legal line. If two or more individuals agree to perform a sexual act as a piece of performance art, whether for a live audience or for documentation, and the agreement specifies that any compensation is for the artistic labor and performance, the arrangement falls under the protection of the First Amendment. The contract itself would serve as powerful evidence of the parties' artistic intent, rebutting any presumption of lewd intent or a prostitutive purpose. This allows for a clear legal distinction between commercial sex work as art and the crime of prostitution.

  

## **Part V: The Marriage Analogy: Reconciling Contract, Status, and Economic Exchange**

  

The institution of marriage, as legally defined and regulated in California, provides a powerful and instructive analogy for the broader arguments of this report. The law governing marriage and its dissolution demonstrates a sophisticated, if sometimes contradictory, acceptance of the commingling of personal intimacy, sexual relations, and significant economic exchange. By examining how the law treats marriage as both a special legal "status" and a "civil contract," it becomes clear that the state is fully capable of recognizing and quantifying the economic components of an intimate relationship. This existing legal framework exposes the logical inconsistency of imposing a blanket criminal presumption on similar economic exchanges that occur outside the formal institution of marriage.

  

### **5.1 Marriage as a Domestic Services Contract**

  

Despite its perception as a romantic and sacred institution, marriage under California law has undeniable contractual and economic dimensions that are explicitly recognized by both statute and case law. The argument that marriage functions as a contract for services with clear economic value is supported by multiple pillars of California family law.

First, the California Family Code itself begins by defining marriage as a "personal relation arising out of a civil contract".62 While it clarifies that consent alone is not sufficient and state sanction (a license and solemnization) is required, the foundational concept is contractual.65 This contractual framing sets the stage for the economic analysis that follows, particularly upon the dissolution of the marriage.

Second, and most explicitly, California Family Code § 4320 provides a list of factors courts must consider when ordering spousal support (alimony). Among these is a direct statutory valuation of domestic labor. Section 4320(a)(2) requires the court to consider "\[t\]he extent to which the supported party's present or future earning capacity is impaired by periods of unemployment that were incurred during the marriage to permit the supported party to devote time to domestic duties".67 This provision is a clear legislative acknowledgment that domestic labor is not a gratuitous act of love but a form of work with quantifiable economic consequences. It recognizes that performing this labor often comes at the cost of career advancement and earning potential, and it provides a legal mechanism for compensating the spouse who undertook those duties.

Third, the landmark California Supreme Court case *Marvin v. Marvin*, 18 Cal. 3d 660 (1976), extended this principle to unmarried partners. The court held that an express or implied contract between nonmarital partners to share property is enforceable, and that homemaking services can constitute valid legal consideration for such a contract.70 The

*Marvin* decision was revolutionary because it explicitly rejected the argument that such services are inseparable from the sexual relationship and are therefore merely "gratuitous acts of affection." It legally recognized that domestic labor has economic value and can form the basis of a binding contract, even outside of marriage. Together, these statutory and judicial principles demonstrate that California law does not shy away from viewing the domestic and supportive aspects of an intimate relationship through an economic and contractual lens.

  

### **5.2 Marriage as a Unique Legal Status**

  

The counterargument to the contractual view is that marriage is not merely a private agreement but a unique legal status conferred and regulated by the state. This perspective emphasizes that once individuals enter into the marital state, they become subject to a host of rights and obligations that are imposed by law, not negotiated by the parties.

California Family Code § 300, while calling marriage a "civil contract," immediately qualifies this by stating that "consent alone does not constitute marriage." It requires the "issuance of a license and solemnization" by the state.62 This state involvement transforms the relationship from a purely private agreement into a public, state-sanctioned status. The state acts as a third party to every marriage, defining its terms and the conditions for its creation and dissolution.72

Many of the core features of marriage are status-based duties, not bargained-for contractual terms. For example, the mutual duty of support between spouses and the entire framework of community property are imposed by the Family Code as a consequence of being married.74 These are not optional terms that couples can negotiate away without a formal prenuptial or postnuptial agreement. The remedies for the termination of the relationship are also governed by the specific statutory scheme of the Family Code (dissolution of marriage), not by a standard civil action for breach of contract.

Furthermore, there is a historical legal presumption that domestic services performed between spouses are gratuitous, rendered out of "love and affection" rather than as part of a bargain.72 While this presumption has been significantly eroded by modern statutes like Family Code § 4320 and cases like

*Marvin*, its historical residue supports the idea that marriage is fundamentally different from a commercial service contract. The law treats marriage as an "institution" or a "relation" that affects society as a whole, placing it in a separate category from ordinary private contracts.72

  

### **5.3 Synthesis: The Hybrid Nature of Marriage as Precedent**

  

The legal reality of marriage in California is not one of either pure contract or pure status, but a complex hybrid of both. This very duality, and the legal tension it creates, is what makes marriage such a powerful precedent for the issues at hand. The legal treatment of marriage reveals a fundamental inconsistency in how the state approaches economic exchange within intimate relationships.

California law effectively treats marriage as a special status that parties enter into via a civil contract. During the marriage, the "status" aspect is often dominant, with duties and obligations imposed by law. However, upon the dissolution of that status, the law reverts to a deeply contractual and economic analysis. The courts are statutorily mandated to place a value on years of domestic labor, to calculate the economic harm of impaired earning capacity, and to divide the accumulated assets of the marital "partnership".67 In essence, the law retroactively quantifies the economic components of an intimate, sexual relationship.

This legal framework proves that the state is not only capable of recognizing and valuing the economic aspects of such relationships, but that it has created a sophisticated system for doing so. It demonstrates that the law can, and does, distinguish between the overall purpose of a relationship and its discrete economic components.

It is, therefore, logically and legally inconsistent for the state to fully embrace this economic reality within the state-sanctioned institution of marriage while simultaneously applying a presumption of criminality to similar economic exchanges in non-marital relationships. If the law can parse the economic value of domestic duties from the intimacy of a marriage, it can surely do the same for a non-marital relationship where one partner provides financial support in exchange for companionship and intimacy. The legal framework for marriage proves that the law is not incapable of handling this complexity. The differential treatment is not a matter of legal capacity but of policy choice—a choice that, as this report argues, currently rests on a flawed and inconsistent foundation.

  

## **Conclusion and Recommendations**

  

The legal framework governing sexual relations and economic exchange in California is built upon a central paradox. Penal Code § 647(b), by criminalizing a specific type of contract for a sexual act, implicitly validates the concept that sexual acts can be the subject of a contract. This reality necessitates a clear legal distinction between the forbidden contract of prostitution and the universe of lawful, consensual arrangements involving sex and material support. This report has argued that this distinction is best achieved through the application of established principles of contract law and the constitutional protections of the First Amendment, grounded in a foundational presumption of legality for private, consensual conduct.

The analysis demonstrates that a legal framework presuming the legality of such arrangements (Framework B) is vastly superior to one presuming their illegality (Framework A). A presumption of legality aligns with the bedrock principles of criminal justice, including the presumption of innocence and the state's burden of proof. It fosters legislative clarity, focuses law enforcement resources on actual harm such as coercion and trafficking, and protects the fundamental rights of personal autonomy and freedom of contract. Conversely, a presumption of illegality inverts the burden of proof, encourages intrusive policing of private lives, and casts a chilling effect on a wide range of non-traditional relationships, reinforcing stigma and creating opportunities for legal abuse.

The precedents set by *People v. Freeman* and the *Miller* obscenity test provide a clear constitutional pathway for structuring lawful, enforceable contracts for sexual acts as a form of protected expression. Furthermore, California's successful and comprehensive regulation of paid surrogacy contracts offers a complete and functioning legislative and judicial blueprint for how the state can and does manage compensated agreements involving bodily autonomy for a "serious" purpose. Finally, the hybrid contract-status nature of marriage reveals the logical inconsistency of the state's current approach, as it already possesses a sophisticated legal apparatus for valuing the economic components of intimate, sexual relationships.

Based on these findings, this report offers the following multi-layered recommendations to resolve the existing legal ambiguity in a manner that is consistent with constitutional principles, contractual freedom, and sound social policy.

  

### **Legislative Recommendations**

  

1.  **Amend Penal Code § 647(b) to Include a "Safe Harbor" Provision:** The Legislature should amend PC § 647(b) to add a new subsection explicitly stating that the statute does not apply to sexual acts conducted pursuant to a written agreement in which the parties attest that the purpose of the exchange is for a lawful, non-prostitutive purpose. Such enumerated purposes could include, but not be limited to, artistic performance, therapeutic application, reproductive assistance, or as an element of a broader contract for companionship and domestic support.
2.  **Create a Statutory Affirmative Defense:** As an alternative or complementary measure, the Legislature should create a statutory affirmative defense to a charge under PC § 647(b). This defense would be established by the defendant producing a written contract, executed prior to the alleged offense, that details a lawful purpose for the exchange of value and the sexual acts. This would provide a clear, evidentiary path for defendants to demonstrate non-criminal intent.

  

### **Judicial Recommendations**

  

1.  **Adopt a Clarifying Jury Instruction:** The California Judicial Council should develop and adopt a standard criminal jury instruction (CALCRIM) for PC § 647(b) prosecutions. This instruction should explicitly state that the prosecution bears the burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that the *sole or dominant purpose* of the compensation was a direct quid pro quo for a lewd act for the purpose of sexual gratification. The instruction should further clarify that the existence of other valid forms of consideration—such as companionship, emotional support, domestic services, or shared living expenses—can be sufficient to create reasonable doubt as to the element of specific criminal intent.
2.  **Affirm the Applicability of** ***Freeman*** **Beyond Commercial Film:** In relevant cases, appellate courts should explicitly affirm that the core principle of *People v. Freeman*—distinguishing payment for expressive performance from payment for sexual gratification—is not limited to the context of commercial filmmaking but applies to any situation where a sexual act is performed as part of a non-obscene expressive work.

  

### **Policy Recommendations**

  

1.  **Shift Law Enforcement Priorities:** State and local law enforcement agencies should issue policy directives to shift resources away from undercover sting operations targeting consensual sexual activity between adults. Resources should be redirected toward investigating and combating non-consensual crimes, such as human trafficking, sexual assault, and the exploitation of minors, where tangible harm is evident.
2.  **Promote Public Education on Contractual Rights:** The State Bar of California and legal aid organizations should develop and disseminate public-facing educational materials informing citizens of their right to structure personal and intimate relationships through clear, consensual contracts. This would empower individuals to protect their interests and clarify their arrangements, reducing ambiguity and the potential for disputes or unwarranted criminal investigation. Such a campaign would foster a social standard based on transparency, consent, and personal responsibility.
