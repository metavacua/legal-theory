Patronage in California Law

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

The term "patronage" appears throughout the California Codes. Its legal meaning and implications diverge into two primary categories. The first category treats patronage as a neutral or positive concept representing a business's customer base, the economic activity of members in a cooperative, or the ridership of a public transit system. The second category treats patronage as a prohibited and illegal kickback for referrals, particularly in healthcare, workers' compensation, and disability insurance claims.

Part I: Patronage as Economic Activity, Support, and Ridership

In this context, "patronage" refers to the legitimate support a business or organization receives from its customers, members, or the public.

1. General Business "Good Will" and Commerce

The law provides a foundational definition of patronage as a core component of a business's value ("good will") and uses the term more generally to refer to customers and business operations.

  • Foundational Definition (BPC §14100): This section provides the simplest definition: "The 'good will' of a business is the expectation of continued public patronage."

  • Eminent Domain Context (CCP §1263.510): This section defines goodwill as "the benefits that accrue to a business as a result of its location, reputation for dependability, skill or quality, and any other circumstances resulting in probable retention of old or acquisition of new patronage."

  • Displacement Context (GOV §7262): When a public project displaces a business, the business may be eligible for a fixed relocation payment if it "cannot be relocated without substantial loss of patronage."

  • General Commerce (BPC §17533, §25503.42): The term is used as a synonym for "customers" or "business." It is unlawful to misrepresent circulation to secure "advertising or other patronage," and rules for alcohol advertising at large venues refer to a venue's "patronage capacity."

2. Public Transit Ridership and Competition

In the Public Utilities Code and Streets and Highways Code, "patronage" is consistently used to mean the ridership of a public transit system. The law uses this concept to establish rules of competition and protect the revenue of existing transit services.

  • Protection from Competition (PUC §30637, §99280, §99281, §103300): The law prohibits a transit district or municipal operator from establishing new services that will "divert, lessen, or compete for the patronage or revenues" of an existing system without providing notice and obtaining consent.

  • Competition with Taxis (PUC §103301.5, §103301.6): Specific rules apply when a district's demand-activated service competes for the "patronage or revenues" of an existing taxi service. If the new service causes a loss of patronage of 10% or more, the district must cease operations until it has purchased the existing taxi service.

  • Notice Requirement (PUC §40222, §98301, §100055, §101208.1): Before a transit district can establish a new service that might compete for the patronage of an existing system, it must provide written notice to the current operator.

  • Planning and Funding (PUC §99221, §99275.5, §102205, §185033; SHC §2177): The Legislature's policy is to fund new transit services that "do not compete with, or divert patronage from, an existing operating bus system." When applying for funding, creating budgets, or developing business plans (including for high-speed rail), transit agencies must prepare estimates and forecasts of projected patronage and may be directed to allocate funds to centers that give the "greatest increase in patronage."

  • Peak Hours (PUC §99260.2): "Peak hours" for transit services are defined as the periods with a "greater-than-average level of patronage."

3. The Legal Cooperative Model

Patronage is the foundational and legal principle for cooperative corporations, where it represents the economic participation of its members. This model is detailed across the Corporations Code and the Food and Agricultural Code.

  • Primary Purpose (CORP §12201): A cooperative must conduct its business for the "mutual benefit of its members as patrons," and earnings are distributed "based upon their patronage."

  • Defining "Patron" and "Patronage" (CORP §12243, §12253.5):

    • Patrons are the members who use the co-op's services or sell products through it.

    • Patronage is the measure of that activity (e.g., value of purchases).

    • In a Worker Cooperative, the patrons are the worker-members, and their patronage is uniquely defined as "labor contributed to or other work performed for the corporation," which can be measured by wages, hours, or jobs created.

  • Defining "Patronage Distribution" (CORP §12244): This is any transfer of earnings (cash, stock, etc.) to a patron, calculated based on their specific patronage. For worker cooperatives, these distributions are apportioned based on each member's patronage relative to the total patronage of all members (CORP §12201.5) and can only be made to the worker-member class (CORP §12420).

  • Agricultural Cooperatives (FAC §61331, §61332, §61871, §61872): A co-op's legal status as a "producer" or "handler" depends on its transactions and whether it "accounts on a patronage basis" to its members.

  • "Accounting on a Patronage Basis" (FAC §61333, §61873): In the dairy industry, this specifically means accounting to each producer for their share of net proceeds based on the quantity and quality of the milk or cream they provided.

  • Corporate Governance (CORP §12314, §12331, §12454): The concept of patronage is embedded in co-op governance. Bylaws must define the manner of "patronage distributions," and voting power can be based on a member's patronage.

  • Financial and Dissolution Rules:

    • Insolvency Limitation (CORP §12453): A cooperative cannot make a patronage distribution if it would become unable to meet its liabilities. It is also prohibited from making distributions to members from earnings generated by nonmember patronage.

    • Non-Patronage Income (CORP §12454.5): A worker cooperative may establish an "indivisible reserves account" funded only by non-patronage-sourced income.

    • Proprietary Interest (CORP §12446; FAC §54041; BPC §26222.6): A member's ownership stake in a cooperative includes all accrued and unpaid "patronage distributions."

    • Dissolution (CORP §12656.5): Upon dissolution, a worker cooperative's capital is distributed to members based on patronage, capital contributions, or both.

  • Liability for False Reports (CORP §12575): Officers are liable for damages from publishing a financial report that is false regarding "patronage distributions."

4. Patronage as a Basis for Legal Exemptions

The cooperative business model, which is defined by its return of benefits on the basis of patronage, is the specific reason these entities are exempted from other major regulatory frameworks.

  • Franchise Law Exemption (BPC §20001; CORP §31005): The legal definition of a "franchise" explicitly does not include certain nonprofit cooperatives where a key criterion is that "substantially equal benefits pass to the members on the basis of patronage of the organization."

  • Securities Law Exemption (CORP §25100(r)): Memberships issued by a cooperative are generally exempt from certain securities laws. Crucially, the standard $1,000 investment limit for this exemption does not apply to shares or credits issued as part of a patronage distribution, legally distinguishing it from a typical capital investment.

5. Tax Treatment of Patronage Allocations

The Revenue and Taxation Code provides specific rules for how patronage dividends and allocations from cooperatives are treated for tax purposes.

  • Taxpayer Election for Non-Cash Allocations (RTC §17086, §24273.5): A taxpayer receiving a non-cash patronage allocation from a farmers' cooperative can elect to either include its face value in their gross income in the year it is received or defer including it until the year it is redeemed.

  • Cooperative Income Deductions (RTC §24404): For tax purposes, a farmers' cooperative's deductible income includes all amounts allocated to members during the year, "whether or not derived from patronage."

  • Patronage Refund Deductions (RTC §24406, §24406.5): Cooperative associations (including gas producers' co-ops) may deduct "patronage refunds" paid to patrons, provided the refunds are made based on a pre-existing obligation and are allocated ratably according to patronage.

  • Reporting Requirements (RTC §18640): Any corporation that allocates "patronage dividends, rebates, or refunds" must file a return with the Franchise Tax Board, reporting the name, address, and amount for each patron who received $100 or more.

6. Sponsorship and Governmental Contexts

Finally, the term is used in a figurative or general sense in the context of institutional sponsorship and government affairs.

  • Sponsorship (EDC §94400, §94403): This refers to colleges that are "under the patronage" of a benevolent, religious, or fraternal organization, using the term to mean sponsorship or oversight, which may have First Amendment implications.

  • Political Context (GOV §9901): The law describes the concentration of power in legislative leadership as having created a "system of patronage and punishment," using the term in its classic political science sense to mean the distribution of favors for political support.

  • Grant Eligibility (GOV §12100.101): To be eligible for certain state-run small agricultural business relief grants, a business must not "restrict patronage for any reason other than capacity," ensuring it is open to the public.

Part II: Patronage as Prohibited Inducement or Kickback

In this context, "patronage" in the form of a "patronage dividend" or other consideration is treated as an illegal payment to induce referrals. These laws are designed to prevent conflicts of interest and fraud.

7. Workers' Compensation System

The Labor Code contains extensive and strict prohibitions against using patronage as kickbacks for referrals within the workers' compensation system.

  • General Prohibition (LAB §3215): It is a crime to offer or accept any "patronage... or other consideration" as an inducement for referring clients for workers' compensation services.

  • Prohibition on Referrals (LAB §139.3, §139.32, §3820): These sections make it unlawful for physicians or other "interested parties" (including employers, insurers, and attorneys) to offer or accept any "patronage dividend" as compensation for referrals.

  • Felony for Adjuster Kickbacks (LAB §3219): It is a felony to offer or accept any "patronage... or other consideration" to a claims adjuster as a reward for the referral or settlement of a claim.

  • Required Disclosure (LAB §4906): Attorneys in workers' compensation cases must file a statement under penalty of perjury declaring they have not accepted any "patronage dividend" for referrals.

8. General Healthcare Referrals

Similar rules apply to the broader healthcare field to prevent conflicts of interest.

  • BPC §650 - Unlawful Referrals: It is unlawful for any licensed healthcare professional to offer or accept a "patronage dividend" or other consideration as a reward or "inducement for referring patients, clients, or customers."

  • BPC §652 - Penalties for Violation: A violation constitutes unprofessional conduct and can lead to license suspension/revocation and misdemeanor charges.

9. Disability Insurance Claims

The prohibition on referral kickbacks extends to the state's disability insurance program.

  • UIC §2116 - Unlawful Inducement: It is unlawful to knowingly solicit, receive, or accept any "patronage... or other consideration" as compensation or inducement for soliciting a claimant to apply for disability insurance benefits.
