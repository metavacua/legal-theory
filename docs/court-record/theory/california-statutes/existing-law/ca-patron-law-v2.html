Principles of Patron Law in California

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

A compilation of selected California codes governing the rights, distributions, and conduct of patrons.

Part 1: Patronage in Cooperative Corporations

This part defines the financial and governance relationship between a cooperative corporation and its patrons.

Foundational Principles & Definitions

  • Section 12201 (Corporations Code). Primary Purpose: A cooperative corporation must be organized and conduct its business primarily for the mutual benefit of its members as patrons. Its earnings, savings, or benefits should be used for the general welfare of the members or be equitably distributed to them based on their patronage.

  • Section 14002 (Financial Code). Credit Unions as Cooperatives: A credit union is a type of cooperative organized to promote thrift and provide credit. It conducts its business for the mutual benefit of its members, with earnings and benefits being distributed to its members as patrons.

  • Section 12243 (Corporations Code). Definition of "Patron" and "Patronage":

  • In a cooperative that provides goods or services, the "patrons" are those who purchase or use them.

  • In a cooperative that markets its members' products, the "patrons" are those members whose products are handled by the corporation.

  • In a worker cooperative, the "patrons" are its worker-members.

  • "Patronage" is the volume or value of business a patron does with the cooperative.

  • Section 12244 (Corporations Code). Definition of "Patronage Distribution": A "patronage distribution" is any transfer made to a patron, where the amount is calculated based on the patron's level of business (patronage) with the corporation.

Financial Rights and Tax Treatment for Patrons

  • Sections 6011.1 & 6012.1 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Sales Tax and Membership Fees: For consumer cooperatives, the "sales price" and "gross receipts" subject to sales tax do not include the value of initial or periodic membership fees or labor performed in lieu of fees. This ensures that only the cost of the property sold is taxed, not the value of participation itself.

  • Section 6359 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Sales Tax on Food Products: The general sales tax exemption for food products does not apply when food is sold for immediate consumption near parking facilities provided for the use of patrons.

  • Sections 24406 & 24406.5 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Tax Deductions for Patronage Refunds: Cooperative corporations can deduct patronage refunds from their income for tax purposes if the refunds are made based on a pre-existing obligation, are derived from business done with the patrons, and the patrons are notified in a timely manner.

  • Section 24406.6 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Calculation of Net Earnings: When calculating earnings for patronage distributions, a cooperative cannot reduce its earnings by the amount paid out as dividends on capital stock if the governing documents state that such dividends are in addition to amounts otherwise payable to patrons.

  • Section 18640 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Patron's Right to Information: A cooperative that allocates $100 or more in patronage dividends to a patron during a calendar year must file a return with the state and must also furnish a written statement to that patron showing the amount allocated to them and the cooperative's contact information.

  • Section 1516 (Code of Civil Procedure). Unclaimed Property Rights: Any dividend, profit, distribution, or other sum owed by a business association (including a cooperative) to a participating patron that goes unclaimed for three years escheats to the state. The business must make a reasonable effort to notify the patron before their interest is transferred to the state.

Governance and the Patron Relationship

  • Sections 12373 & 12592 (Corporations Code). Patron Relationship and Conflicts of Interest: The law clarifies that a "member-patron relationship on terms available to all members" does not, by itself, constitute a material financial interest or a conflict of interest that would void a corporate transaction or require special annual reporting.

Part 2: Rights and Conduct of Patrons in Public and Commercial Venues

This part outlines the rules of conduct for patrons and the safety and operational standards required of various public-facing facilities.

  • Sections 640 (Penal Code) & 99580 (Public Utilities Code). Patron Conduct on Public Transportation: These sections work together to regulate patron conduct on public transit. Section 640 defines a range of prohibited acts as infractions (e.g., fare evasion, loud music, smoking, vandalism). Section 99580 allows transit agencies to handle these violations through a civil administrative penalty process instead of the criminal infraction process, including setting fines and allowing for community service.

  • Sections 830.31, 830.33, 830.36, 830.37, 831.4 (Penal Code). Peace Officer & Security Officer Duties Towards Patrons: The primary duty of various peace officers and public security officers (including those for LA County, city properties, housing authorities, transit systems, harbors, airports, courts, and firefighter/security guards of the Military Department) includes performing necessary duties with respect to patrons on the properties they administer.

  • Sections 22951 & 22952 (Vehicle Code). Patron Rights in Parking Facilities:

  • An operator of an off-street parking facility is prohibited from parking the vehicle of a patron on any public street or alley.

  • A parking facility operator is guilty of a violation if they tow a vehicle within 24 hours of the parking period expiring or if they tow a vehicle when no attendant was on duty to allow the patron to pay.

  • Sections 115951, 115952, 116064.2 (Health and Safety Code). Patron Safety at Public Swimming Pools: Public pools must be equipped with anti-entrapment devices on drains. Wave pools have additional requirements to protect patrons, including providing lifevests, giving audible warnings, and ensuring sufficient lifeguard coverage.

  • Sections 7914 & 7926 (Labor Code). Patron Safety at Amusement Rides: An operator must report any incident where a patron is injured or falls from a ride. Owners must carry liability insurance of at least $1 million for injuries suffered by patrons.

  • Section 7103 (Revenue and Taxation Code). Patrons at Historic Venues: Creates a fund from sales tax generated from patrons at qualified events to be used for venue upgrades and maintenance.

  • Section 7449 (Business and Professions Code). Patron & Consumer Protections for App-Based Rideshare and Delivery Services: Mandates safety protocols like driver background checks for the benefit of consumers (patrons).

  • Sections 19805, 19860, 19906, 98001, 98004 (Business & Professions Code, Government Code); Sections 336.5, 337j (Penal Code). Patron Safety and Rights in Gambling Establishments: Defines a "player" as a patron participating in a controlled game, and establishes numerous protections for casino patrons, including requirements for local ordinances on patron security, fair play, tribal-state compacts for dispute resolution, and guaranteeing the redemption of gaming chips if an establishment is sold.

  • Section 22707 (Business and Professions Code). Patron Injury Reporting at Tanning Facilities: If a patron is injured and requires medical attention, the facility must report the injury and provide a copy to the injured patron.

  • Section 114337 (Health and Safety Code). Patron Information at Mobile Food Facilities: The name and location of a mobile food facility must be clearly visible to patrons.

  • Section 1812.81 (Civil Code). Health Studio Patrons: Defines a "contract for health studio services" as an agreement for the use of facilities by an individual patron.

  • Section 2527 (Civil Code). Pharmacy Patrons: Defines a prescription drug claims processor as an entity that handles claims submitted by a pharmacy or a patron thereof.

  • Section 68055.1 (Government Code). Patrons of Restaurants and Shopping Centers: Defines "drive-in restaurants" and "shopping centers" in the context of providing parking facilities for the use of patrons.

Part 3: Patron Safety in Personal Care & Health Services

  • Sections 7313, 7351, 7362, 7403.5, 7404 (Business and Professions Code). Health and Safety in Barbering and Cosmetology Establishments: Provides for inspections, allows for the immediate closure of establishments that pose a threat to patron health and safety, and requires amenities like public restrooms for patrons.

  • Section 123471 (Health and Safety Code). Patrons of Family Planning & Pregnancy-Related Facilities: Defines licensed and unlicensed covered facilities in part by whether they advertise or solicit patrons with offers of pregnancy-related services.

Part 4: Patron Rights and Regulation in Wagering

  • Sections 19604, 19610.3, 19610.4, 19610.6 (Business and Professions Code). Patrons of Horse Racing Wagering: Regulates how patrons can place wagers and how a portion of the revenue generated from patrons is distributed for public benefit.

Part 5: Patrons and Alcoholic Beverage Control

  • Sections 25612.5, 25657, 25680 (Business and Professions Code); Section 303a (Penal Code). Patron Safety and Conduct in Bars: Sets operating standards for retailers to ensure patron safety, prohibits anyone from loitering in a bar to solicit a patron or customer to purchase a drink, and mandates Responsible Beverage Service (RBS) training for servers to protect patrons.

  • Section 647e (Penal Code). Open Container Restrictions for Patrons: Local governments can create ordinances making it an infraction for a person to be on the posted premises of an off-sale liquor store with an open alcoholic beverage container. Notices must be clearly visible to patrons.

  • Special Conditions for Venues & Events: Includes provisions for patrons in "entertainment zones," admission of underage patrons to non-alcohol events, rooms for designated patrons, and specific event sponsorships.

Part 6: Patrons of Public and Educational Services

This part addresses the rights and services for patrons of public institutions like libraries and schools.

Patron Privacy and Access at Public Libraries & Agencies

  • Section 7927.105 (Government Code). Confidentiality of Library Patron Records: All "patron use records" (including identifying information and borrowing history) at a public library are confidential.

  • Sections 14900 & 14905 (Government Code). Access to State Publications: Depository libraries must render reasonable service without charge to qualified patrons in the use of state publications.

  • Section 8331 (Government Code). Internet Access: Public libraries are encouraged to provide internet access to their patrons.

  • Sections 19324, 19325, 19325.1 (Education Code). Services for Patrons with Disabilities: The State Librarian is authorized to provide special services to library patrons with print disabilities, including rendering books into braille upon request and providing access to telephonic reading systems.

  • Section 118500 (Health and Safety Code). Restroom Access: Any public agency that maintains restroom facilities for the public must make them available without charge to the patrons, guests, or invitees.

School Cafeteria Patrons

  • Section 38084 (Education Code). Pricing for School Cafeteria Patrons: Food served in school cafeterias shall be sold to the patrons of the cafeterias at a price that covers the cost of maintaining the cafeteria.

Part 7: Patrons in Food, Agriculture, and Wildlife Contexts

This part defines patrons in relation to food service, agricultural fairs, and fish and game regulations.

Food Service and Sales

  • Section 27507 (Food and Agricultural Code). Patrons of Egg Consumers: Defines a "consumer" of eggs to include restaurants, hotels, or other institutions that purchase eggs for serving to guests or patrons.

  • Section 34592 (Food and Agricultural Code). Patrons Served Market Milk: When market milk is served to the public, the individual container cannot be opened except in the presence of the consumer or patron.

  • Section 35016 (Food and Agricultural Code). Patrons of On-Site Dairy Manufacturing: Agencies (like hotels or restaurants) that manufacture frozen dairy products for purchase by any patron, guest, or patient must obtain a permit.

  • Section 114332.3 (Health and Safety Code). Service Animals: Live animals are generally not allowed in food facilities, but this does not prohibit service dogs from being in rooms where food is served to guests or patrons.

  • Section 3212 (Fish and Game Code). Patrons Consuming Domesticated Game: The keeper of a hotel or restaurant may sell portions of domesticated game to a patron for actual consumption on the premises only.

Food Facilities and Storage

  • Sections 112500, 112535, 112575 (Health and Safety Code). Patrons of Frozen Food Locker Plants: Defines a "service locker plant" as one where a patron's foods are prepared or packaged by the operator. Lockers must be thoroughly cleaned before being put into the possession of any patron.

  • Section 114378.2 (Health and Safety Code). Patron Information at Fishermen's Markets: Fishermen's markets must post the name of the fisherman and the type of fish being sold so it is clearly visible to patrons.

Services for Patrons

  • Section 3023 (Food and Agricultural Code). Patrons at Fairs: A fair may issue credential admissions to individuals or groups who render a service to fair patrons.

  • Section 3087 (Fish and Game Code). Patrons of Taxidermy Services: If a taxidermist's patron fails to pay for services, the taxidermist may sell the item only if regulations are adopted that require posting a notice informing patrons of this policy.

  • Section 122384 (Health and Safety Code). Patrons of Pet Boarding Facilities: A pet boarding facility operator must provide written information on their practices and disclose any material deviations from them to the pet's owner or patron.

Part 8: Patron Rights and Non-Discrimination

  • Section 8301.1 (Government Code). Historical Context of Patron Rights: In its findings and declarations regarding reparations, the Legislature acknowledges that California has a history of sanctioning discrimination and enacting predatory laws designed to disrupt "African American businesses and patrons." This section provides important context for the principle of ensuring equal and fair access for all patrons.

Part 9: Patron Rights in Employment and Labor

This part covers laws where the term "patron" defines a relationship that affects employees and public utilities.

  • Sections 350 & 351 (Labor Code). Patron Gratuities:

  • A "gratuity" (or tip) is defined as money paid by a patron over and above the amount due for services rendered or goods sold.

  • Employers are prohibited from taking any portion of a gratuity left for an employee by a patron. Every gratuity is the sole property of the employee. If a patron pays a tip by credit card, the employer must pay the full amount to the employee, without deducting for processing fees.

  • Section 6802 (Labor Code). Patron Rights in Public Utility Regulation: The Public Utilities Commission has the authority to suspend or modify an order from the Division of Occupational Safety and Health if it is found to interfere with a consumer or other patron of a public utility.

Part 10: Patron-Related Criminal Law

This part covers specific sections of the Penal Code that define and regulate conduct related to patrons.

  • Section 334 (Penal Code). Fraudulent Game and Concession Operations: It is illegal for a concession operator to fraudulently obtain money from a patron by using a hidden mechanical device or other means to diminish the patron's chance of winning a prize.

  • Section 648a (Penal Code). Illegal Slugs and Tokens: Establishments that use slugs or tokens must post signs notifying patrons that using them for any monetary purpose outside the premises is prohibited by federal law.

Part 11: Patrons of Public Utilities

This part defines the relationship between public utilities and their patrons, ensuring safety, convenience, and reasonable rates.

  • Section 451 (Public Utilities Code). Utility's Duty to Patrons: Every public utility is required to furnish and maintain adequate, efficient, and reasonable service, equipment, and facilities as are necessary to promote the safety, health, comfort, and convenience of its patrons, employees, and the public.

  • Sections 2901 & 2906 (Public Utilities Code). Regulatory Power over Patron Relationships: A municipal corporation has "powers of control" to supervise and regulate the relationship between public utilities and their present or prospective customers, consumers, or patrons.

  • Sections 99222 & 170052 (Public Utilities Code). Patron Comfort and Convenience in Transit: The Legislature declares that transit development funds should be used for physical improvements to improve the movement of transit vehicles and the comfort of patrons. Likewise, airport authorities, in developing passenger rail access, should endeavor to maximize the convenience of their patrons.
