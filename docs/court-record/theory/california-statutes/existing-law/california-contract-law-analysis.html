Structuring Service-Free Acquisition of Future Artwork in California: A Legal Analysis and Framework

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Part I: Foundational Legal Principles in California

Section 1: The Unilateral Contract as a Mechanism for Public Offers

A unilateral contract represents a distinct form of agreement in which one party, the offeror, makes a promise in exchange for the performance of a specific act by the other party, the offeree.1 Unlike a bilateral contract, which is formed through an exchange of mutual promises, a unilateral contract is only formed and becomes legally binding once the offeree completes the requested act.1 This structure, often seen in public reward offers, provides a potential framework for acquiring goods without entering into a traditional service agreement.

1.1. Formation and Essential Elements: Offer to the Public and Acceptance by Performance

Under California law, the essential elements for any valid contract, including a unilateral one, are enumerated in California Civil Code § 1550: (1) parties capable of contracting, (2) their consent, (3) a lawful object, and (4) a sufficient cause or consideration.4 In the unilateral context, consent is manifested through the offeror's promise and the offeree's subsequent performance.

The offeror is the "master of the offer" and retains the power to dictate the precise terms of the agreement, including the specific conduct that will constitute acceptance.5 For an offer made to the public to be valid, it must be clear, definite, and explicit, leaving nothing open for further negotiation.1 The offeree accepts not by making a return promise, but by fully performing the action specified in the offer.3 For instance, if a company offers a $50 bonus for achieving a sales goal, no contract is formed until an employee actually achieves that goal; the promise to try is not acceptance.3

1.2. Key Precedent: Analysis of Harris v. Time, Inc. and the Nature of Public Offers

The California Court of Appeal's decision in Harris v. Time, Inc. (1987) provides a foundational analysis of what constitutes an offer for a unilateral contract in a commercial context.6 In

Harris, plaintiffs received a direct mailer from Time, Inc. with a see-through window revealing the text: "I'LL GIVE YOU THIS VERSATILE NEW CALCULATOR WATCH FREE Just for, Opening this Envelope".6 The full terms, visible only after opening the envelope, required the recipient to purchase a magazine subscription to receive the watch.6

The court acknowledged that advertisements are typically treated as mere invitations to bargain. However, it applied a critical exception: an advertisement can constitute a binding offer if it calls for the performance of a specific act without further communication and leaves nothing for negotiation.6 The court found that Time's mailer, on its face, was technically an offer for a unilateral contract: the promisor (Time) made a promise (to give a watch) in exchange for the performance of an act by the promisee (opening the envelope).6

Time, Inc. argued that the act of opening an envelope was valueless and thus could not constitute adequate consideration. The court firmly rejected this, affirming a basic tenet of modern contract law: any bargained-for act will constitute adequate consideration, and courts do not inquire into the adequacy or equivalence of the values exchanged.6 From Time's perspective, inducing a recipient to open the envelope and view the sales pitch within was of immense value, making the act sufficient consideration.6

Despite finding that a technical contract was formed upon the opening of the envelope, the court ultimately dismissed the lawsuit. It applied the legal maxim de minimis non curat lex ("the law disregards trifles"), concluding that the plaintiffs had suffered no actual damage and that the litigation was an "absurd waste of resources".6 This case establishes that while the legal threshold for forming a unilateral contract is low, its practical enforceability may be limited where the underlying transaction is trivial.

1.3. Irrevocability upon Commencement of Performance: The Role of Promissory Estoppel and Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 45

A significant evolution in contract law, adopted in California, is the departure from the harsh common law rule that an offer for a unilateral contract could be revoked at any time before the offeree's full performance.7 This modern doctrine is crucial for protecting the offeree who has expended time and effort in reliance on the offeror's promise.

California law embraces the position articulated in the Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 45. This section provides that once an offeree tenders or begins the invited performance, an option contract is created.9 The offeror's main offer becomes irrevocable, and their duty to perform (e.g., to pay the promised amount) is conditional upon the offeree completing the performance as specified in the offer.8 This principle effectively transforms the unilateral offer into a binding option once performance has begun, preventing the offeror from unjustly revoking the offer mid-performance.1 The offeree's part performance or tender is deemed to furnish the consideration for an implied subsidiary promise by the offeror not to revoke.8

1.4. Analysis of Drennan v. Star Paving Co. : Detrimental Reliance as a Substitute for Consideration

The California Supreme Court's landmark decision in Drennan v. Star Paving Co. (1958) further solidified the principle of irrevocability through the doctrine of promissory estoppel, as defined in Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 90.8 This doctrine states that a promise is binding if the promisor should reasonably expect it to induce action or forbearance of a definite and substantial character, and it does induce such action, and injustice can only be avoided by its enforcement.8

In Drennan, a general contractor received a telephoned bid from a subcontractor, Star Paving, for paving work on a school construction project. The general contractor, Drennan, relied on this bid—the lowest received—in calculating and submitting his own master bid for the project.8 After Drennan was awarded the contract, Star Paving attempted to revoke its bid, claiming it had made a mistake.8

The court held that Star Paving's bid was an offer that it should have reasonably expected Drennan to rely upon. Drennan's detrimental reliance—using the bid to secure the prime contract—made the subcontractor's offer irrevocable.8 The court explicitly connected the principles of § 45 (unilateral contracts) and § 90 (promissory estoppel), reasoning that reasonable reliance serves as a substitute for the consideration that would typically be required to make an offer binding.8 The very purpose of § 90 is to make a promise enforceable even when there is no bargained-for exchange.8

The legal mechanism that makes a unilateral offer irrevocable upon the start of performance is the implication of an option contract. The offeree's partial performance or detrimental reliance is treated as the consideration that effectively "purchases" this implied option, binding the offeror to their promise pending the offeree's full performance. This direct link between unilateral contracts and option agreements is fundamental. However, this protective mechanism creates a significant legal challenge for the goal of structuring a service-free agreement. If an artist's commencement of creating a painting makes a buyer's offer to purchase that future painting irrevocable, a binding legal relationship is formed during the creative process. A court, applying the "predominant factor test" (discussed in Section 4), could easily construe this relationship—where one party becomes legally bound to pay based on the other's ongoing labor and reliance—as being predominantly an agreement for the service of creating the painting. The legal doctrine that protects the artist from a capricious buyer simultaneously exposes the buyer to the risk of having the entire transaction re-characterized as an employment or service relationship.

Section 2: The Option Agreement as a Distinct Contractual Right

An option agreement offers an alternative framework that may be more resilient to re-characterization as a service contract. It operates not as a promise for a future performance, but as the present sale of a distinct, enforceable right.

2.1. The Option as an Irrevocable Offer for Future and Intangible Assets

An option contract is a unilateral offer by an "optionor" to sell an asset to an "optionee" under specified terms, which is made irrevocable for a set period of time through the payment of consideration.13 The key distinction is that the optionee is purchasing

time and exclusivity; they are buying the right to make a future purchase decision without the risk of the offer being withdrawn.14 This structure is well-established in California law for a wide range of assets, including real property, tangible goods, and intangible assets.15

The entertainment industry provides a powerful analogue for the acquisition of future creative works. A film producer will frequently purchase an option on a screenplay. For a relatively small, non-refundable "option fee," the producer acquires the exclusive right, for a specified "option period" (e.g., 18 months), to purchase the full rights to the screenplay for a pre-negotiated purchase price.14 During this period, the writer cannot sell the screenplay to anyone else, but the producer has no obligation to actually purchase the rights or make the film. If the producer cannot secure financing, they can let the option expire, losing only the option fee.14 This model is directly applicable to future and intangible assets, including intellectual property that has not yet been created.

2.2. The Doctrine of Consideration: Ensuring Irrevocability

The irrevocability of an option is not automatic; it must be purchased with valid consideration. An option granted without consideration is legally nothing more than a continuing offer, which the offeror can revoke at any time before the offeree accepts it.13

2.2.1. Analysis of Steiner v. Thexton and the Requirement for Independent, Bargained-For Consideration

The California Supreme Court's decision in Steiner v. Thexton (2010) provides a critical lesson on the necessity of independent consideration for option rights. The case involved a real estate purchase agreement that gave the buyer, Steiner, a "free look" period during which he could terminate the agreement for any reason and receive a full refund of his deposit.19 During this period, Steiner incurred significant expense seeking a parcel split for the property.19 The seller, Thexton, then attempted to cancel the deal.

The court held that a purchase agreement containing such a unilateral and unrestricted termination right for the buyer is, in substance, an option agreement.19 Because Steiner could withdraw without forfeiting anything, his promise to purchase was illusory, and there was no mutual obligation. For the seller's promise to keep the offer open to be binding (i.e., for the option to be irrevocable), the buyer must provide separate, bargained-for consideration

specifically for the option right itself.19 A standard purchase deposit that is fully refundable upon termination does not typically satisfy this requirement because the seller does not retain it in exchange for granting the option.19

The precedent from Steiner dictates that to create an enforceable option, the parties must explicitly provide for independent consideration. This is often accomplished by designating a nominal, non-refundable portion of a deposit or a separate payment as the "Independent Consideration" for the grant of the option right.20 This payment is made in exchange for the seller's forbearance from marketing the property to others during the option period and is earned by the seller regardless of whether the final purchase occurs.

2.2.2. Structuring Consideration Where the Artist Has No Obligation to Create

In applying the option model to an artist who has no obligation to create the underlying work, the structure of consideration is paramount. According to California Civil Code § 1605, consideration is a benefit conferred upon the promisor or a prejudice suffered by the promisee, bargained for as an inducement to the promise.22 Critically, past consideration cannot support a new promise.24

In this context, the buyer's payment of the option fee cannot be consideration for the artist's labor or for the creation of the artwork, as the artist is not promising to do either. Instead, the consideration is for a different, legally sufficient bargained-for exchange: the artist's promise to forbear. By accepting the option fee, the artist agrees that if they choose to create an artwork that meets the specified description during the option period, they will sell it exclusively to the buyer at the pre-agreed price. The artist suffers a legal detriment by restricting their future commercial freedom—they are giving up the right to market and sell a potential future work to the highest bidder on the open market. The buyer, in exchange for the fee, receives the benefit of this exclusivity and price certainty for a contingent future asset. This structure ensures that the consideration is valid, present, and bargained-for, supporting the irrevocability of the option without compelling any creative service.

The primary strength of this option model lies in its clear legal and transactional separation. It bifurcates the relationship into two distinct potential events: first, the immediate and completed sale of an intangible right (the option), and second, the potential and contingent future sale of a physical good (the artwork). The initial agreement's "predominant factor" is unequivocally the purchase of a right, for which a fee is paid. The artist's labor is not part of this bargain; the creative process occurs, if at all, entirely outside of any contractual obligation to the buyer. This deliberate lack of obligation is the model's core defense against re-characterization as a service agreement, as it breaks the direct causal link between remuneration and labor that is the trigger for a worker classification analysis.

Part II: Characterization of the Transaction Under Commercial and Labor Law

Section 3: Classifying Commissioned Artwork and Copyright Under the California Commercial Code (UCC)

The classification of the subject matter of the transaction—the artwork and its associated rights—is a critical preliminary step. Whether the transaction is governed by the California Commercial Code or common law has significant implications for the rights and obligations of the parties and for how a court will analyze the nature of the agreement.

3.1. Statutory Definitions: "Goods," "Future Goods," and "Specially Manufactured Goods"

Division 2 of the California Commercial Code governs transactions in "goods." Section 2105 provides the operative definitions. "Goods" are defined as "all things (including specially manufactured goods) which are movable at the time of identification to the contract for sale".25 This definition is broad, encompassing tangible personal property from manufactured items to growing crops.27

The UCC explicitly addresses items that do not yet exist. "Goods which are not both existing and identified are 'future' goods. A purported present sale of future goods or of any interest therein operates as a contract to sell".25 A commissioned piece of artwork, which will be created to a buyer's specifications and does not exist at the time of the agreement, fits squarely within the UCC's definitions of both "

future goods" and "specially manufactured goods".27 This provides a strong statutory basis for treating the physical artwork itself as a "good" subject to the provisions of UCC Division 2.

3.2. Intellectual Property in Commerce: Is a Copyright a "Good" or a "General Intangible"?

While the physical artwork is a "good," the intellectual property rights associated with it are treated differently. The UCC's definition of "goods" expressly excludes "things in action" 25, a category that encompasses intangible rights. Under Division 9 of the UCC, which governs secured transactions, intellectual property rights such as patents, trademarks, and copyrights are consistently classified as "

general intangibles".31 A general intangible is a catch-all category for "any personal property, including things in action, other than" specifically enumerated categories like accounts, goods, and instruments.32

Therefore, the sale of a commissioned artwork involves a transfer of two distinct types of property: the tangible physical object (the painting, sculpture, etc.), which is a "good," and the copyright, which is a "general intangible".31

3.3. Legal Significance of the Distinction: Application of UCC Division 2 vs. Common Law

This dual classification has significant legal consequences. A contract for the sale of "goods" is governed by UCC Division 2, which provides a comprehensive set of rules regarding contract formation (including the statute of frauds), warranties (express and implied), risk of loss, and remedies for breach.27

In contrast, a contract for the transfer of a "general intangible" like a copyright is governed not by UCC Division 2, but by the common law of contracts.27 While

UCC Division 9 provides the rules for creating and perfecting a security interest in a general intangible, it does not govern the outright sale of most general intangibles.31

This means that any agreement for the acquisition of a commissioned artwork is necessarily a hybrid transaction. The transfer of title to the physical object is a sale of goods under the UCC. The assignment of the copyright is a transfer of a general intangible under common law. This hybrid nature demands careful and explicit drafting. A contract that simply states the "sale of artwork" might be legally insufficient to transfer the copyright, potentially leaving the buyer with ownership of the physical canvas but without the legal right to reproduce, display, or create derivative works from the image. The agreement must contain separate and clear language effectuating both the passage of title to the "good" and the assignment of the intellectual property rights. This complexity also feeds directly into the primary challenge of avoiding a service contract classification. A court analyzing the transaction's "essence" may find that the true value lies not in the physical materials but in the creation of the unique copyright, pushing the analysis toward a finding that the contract's predominant purpose was the artist's creative service.

Section 4: The Critical Boundary: Sale of Goods vs. Contract for Services

The distinction between a sale of goods and a contract for services is the legal battleground where the risk of employee misclassification is won or lost. If a transaction is properly characterized as a sale of goods, the strictures of California labor law may not apply. If it is characterized as a contract for services, a rigorous worker classification analysis is triggered.

4.1. The "Predominant Factor Test" in California Courts

When a contract involves both goods and services—a hybrid transaction—California courts apply the "predominant factor test" (also known as the "predominant purpose" or "Bonebrake" test) to determine the agreement's essential character.30 The court must ascertain the "essence of the agreement".30 If the primary purpose of the contract is the rendition of a service, with goods being incidentally involved (e.g., an artist painting a portrait, where the skill and labor are paramount), the contract is treated as one for services and governed by common law.30 Conversely, if the contract is fundamentally for a transaction of sale, with labor being incidentally involved (e.g., the installation of a purchased appliance), it is treated as a sale of goods governed by the UCC.30

Factors courts consider in this analysis include the language of the contract, the method of billing (e.g., lump sum for a product versus hourly rates for labor), and the comparative value of the physical goods versus the service component.35

4.2. Application to Creative Works: Analyzing the "Thrust" of a Commissioned Artwork Transaction

There is a notable absence of controlling California case law that directly applies the predominant factor test to a contract for commissioned art.38 This legal vacuum creates both risk and opportunity. The risk is that a court, as a matter of first impression, could easily conclude that the "thrust" of such an agreement is the artist's unique skill, talent, and creative labor. The paint and canvas are of negligible value compared to the artistic service that transforms them into a valuable work.30 This makes a standard commission agreement highly vulnerable to being classified as a service contract.

The opportunity lies in the ability to shape the outcome through meticulous contract drafting. Because there is no binding precedent, the language chosen by the parties to describe their relationship and the nature of the transaction becomes the most powerful evidence of their intent. A contract that is carefully structured to emphasize the sale of a finished "specially manufactured good" and de-emphasize the process of creation stands a better chance of persuading a court to apply the UCC.

4.3. Statutory Trigger: Defining "Labor or Services for Remuneration"

The entire framework of California's worker classification law, including the ABC test, is activated when one person provides "labor or services for remuneration" to another.39 This phrase, found in

California Labor Code § 2775 and Unemployment Insurance Code § 621(b), is the statutory gateway to an employment analysis.41

The Employment Development Department (EDD) and California courts interpret this trigger broadly. There is a default presumption that any individual providing labor or services in exchange for payment is an employee.41 The term "remuneration" is not limited to hourly wages; it encompasses any form of compensation paid for work performed, including commissions and non-discretionary bonuses.43 The burden then falls squarely on the hiring entity to prove that the relationship is not one of employment by satisfying the stringent requirements of the ABC test or another statutory exception.41

The classification of the transaction under the predominant factor test is therefore determinative. A finding that the agreement is for a "sale of goods" could potentially avoid the "labor or services for remuneration" trigger altogether, rendering the ABC test inapplicable. Conversely, a finding that the agreement is a "contract for services" leads directly into the statutory presumption of employment and places a heavy evidentiary burden on the hiring entity.

Section 5: The Hobbyist Dilemma in California's Worker Classification Framework

The legal status of the artist themselves—whether they are operating as a professional business or engaging in a personal hobby—is a dispositive factor in assessing the risk of misclassification under California law. A failure to appreciate the distinction between tax law definitions and labor law requirements can lead to a catastrophic failure of any service-free contract model.

5.1. Defining the Artist's Status: The IRS Nine-Factor Test for "Hobby" vs. "Business"

For federal and California income tax purposes, the distinction between a business and a hobby hinges on the intent to make a profit.45 An activity engaged in for sport, recreation, or personal enjoyment is a hobby, even if it occasionally generates income. An activity conducted with a genuine profit motive is a business.47

To determine this intent, the IRS and the California Franchise Tax Board (FTB) use a non-exhaustive, nine-factor test. These factors include 45:

  1. Whether the activity is carried on in a businesslike manner (e.g., maintaining complete and accurate books).

  2. The expertise of the taxpayer or their advisors.

  3. The time and effort expended in the activity.

  4. The expectation that assets used in the activity may appreciate in value.

  5. The success of the taxpayer in carrying on other similar or dissimilar activities.

  6. The taxpayer's history of income or losses with respect to the activity (a history of profits suggests a business).

  7. The amount of occasional profits, if any, that are earned.

  8. The financial status of the taxpayer (a taxpayer with substantial income from other sources may be engaging in a hobby).

  9. Elements of personal pleasure or recreation.

No single factor is determinative. An artist who paints for pleasure, does not keep business records, has never made a profit, and does not depend on art for their livelihood would likely be classified as a hobbyist for tax purposes.45

5.2. Intersection with the ABC Test: Can a "Hobbyist" Satisfy Prong C?

California's ABC test, codified in Labor Code § 2775, establishes a presumption of employment. To rebut this presumption, a hiring entity must prove all three of the following prongs 49:

  • (A) The person is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with the performance of the work.

  • (B) The person performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.

  • (C) The person is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as that involved in the work performed.

Prong C is the most critical in this context. The California Supreme Court in Dynamex clarified that to satisfy Prong C, the hiring entity must show that the worker "independently has made the decision to go into business for himself or herself".39 This involves taking the usual steps to establish and promote that business, such as through incorporation, licensure, advertisements, or offering services to the public or a number of clients.39

A fundamental and irreconcilable conflict exists between the legal definition of a "hobby" and the requirements of Prong C. A hobby is, by definition, an activity not engaged in for profit and not conducted in a businesslike manner.46 Prong C requires proof that the individual

is engaged in an "independently established... business".52 It is logically impossible for a hiring entity to argue that an individual whose activity legally qualifies as a hobby is simultaneously engaged in an independently established business.

5.3. Review of FTB and EDD Guidance: The Gap Between Tax Status and Labor Law Classification

An examination of guidance from California's tax and labor agencies reveals that they operate in separate spheres. The FTB's guidance on online activities focuses entirely on the nine-factor test to distinguish a hobby from a business for tax reporting purposes.45 The EDD's guidance focuses squarely on the ABC test and its exceptions for determining employment status for payroll tax purposes.41 There is no official guidance that directly reconciles these two standards or explains how an individual's tax status as a hobbyist should be treated in a labor law analysis.

This gap, however, does not benefit the hiring entity. The burden of proof under the ABC test rests entirely on the hiring entity. In a wage claim proceeding, an artist's own tax returns, where they have declared their art income as hobby income and have not taken business deductions, would serve as powerful evidence against the hiring entity. It would demonstrate that the artist does not consider themselves to be in an independently established business, making it nearly impossible for the hiring entity to meet its burden of proof for Prong C.

Consequently, the artist's status as a "hobbyist" is the single greatest vulnerability in any attempt to structure a service-free acquisition agreement. The legal and factual indicia that define an activity as a hobby for tax law purposes are the very same factors that would cause a hiring entity to fail Prong C of the ABC test. Therefore, a non-negotiable prerequisite for the defensible use of either of the contract models proposed below is that the artist must already be operating as a legitimate business, even if as a sole proprietor, and be able to provide objective evidence of that status.

Part III: Proposed "Service-Free" Contract Models and Defensibility Analysis

Based on the foregoing legal principles, two distinct contract models are proposed. Each is designed to minimize the risk of being re-characterized as a service or employment agreement under California law. The analysis below outlines the framework, essential clauses, and a comparative assessment of the legal defensibility of each model.

Section 6: Model 1 - Unilateral Offer to Purchase Future Artwork

6.1. Framework and Rationale

This model is structured as a standing, irrevocable offer by a buyer to purchase a specific type of good from an artist. It draws directly from the principles of unilateral contract formation, where acceptance is not a promise, but the performance of a specific act.1 Here, the act of acceptance is defined as the artist's tender of a finished artwork that conforms to the specifications laid out in the offer. The artist's creative process is framed not as a service rendered to the buyer, but as the independent act of manufacturing a "specially manufactured good" to meet the offer's pre-existing terms. The goal is to create a relationship where the buyer is passive until a finished product is presented for purchase.

6.2. Essential Terms and Clauses

The following table outlines the critical clauses required to structure this model effectively.

Clause Title Purpose Key Language/Drafting Considerations Primary Legal Risk
**The Offer** To establish a clear, definite, and irrevocable offer to purchase a future good. The document must be titled "Unilateral Offer to Purchase" or similar. It must clearly state: "This is an offer to purchase a good, not a contract for services." The offer must specify the objective characteristics of the desired artwork (e.g., size, medium, subject matter category) with enough clarity that a conforming good can be identified, but without dictating the creative process. **Ambiguity.** If the specifications are too detailed or involve subjective approval, a court could interpret them as "control and direction," implicating Prong A of the ABC test and suggesting a service agreement.
**Acceptance by Performance** To define the sole and exclusive method of acceptance as the tender of a finished good. The clause must explicitly state that the offer can *only* be accepted by the artist's physical tender of a completed, conforming artwork to the buyer. It should further state that no other action, including communication of intent, commencement of work, or partial performance, constitutes acceptance of the offer to purchase. **Re-characterization of Performance.** A court could find that the "performance" is not merely the delivery of the good, but the entire creative process leading to it. This would shift the analysis toward the "predominant factor" being the artist's labor.
**Irrevocability & Implied Option** To acknowledge California law on irrevocability while framing it within a sale-of-goods context. The clause should state that the offer becomes irrevocable for a specified period upon the artist's commencement of substantial work on a conforming artwork, consistent with Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 45. It must frame this as creating an "option to sell a good," not a right to demand payment for services. **The "Service" Nexus.** This is the model's central weakness. Acknowledging that the offer becomes binding *during the creative process* creates a strong nexus between the buyer's payment obligation and the artist's labor, which is the hallmark of a service agreement.8
**Purchase Price & Title Transfer** To set forth the commercial terms of the sale, consistent with UCC Division 2. A fixed purchase price for a conforming artwork must be stated. The clause should detail the process for the transfer of title and risk of loss upon acceptance and payment, using language consistent with the UCC (e.g., referencing "delivery" of "goods"). **Method of Payment.** If payment is structured as progress payments or advances, it will be viewed as compensation for ongoing labor, fatally undermining the model. Payment must be a lump sum upon acceptance of the finished good.
**Disclaimer of Service Relationship** To explicitly disavow any intent to create a service or employment relationship. The contract should include a clear statement that the parties' intent is to establish a buyer-seller relationship for a good, that the artist is not being engaged to provide any labor or services for remuneration, and that the artist is acting as an independent manufacturer of goods for potential sale. **Limited Weight.** While evidence of intent, such a disclaimer will be disregarded by a court if the economic reality of the relationship points toward an employment or service arrangement.

Section 7: Model 2 - Option to Purchase Agreement

7.1. Framework and Rationale

This model is structurally different and legally more robust. It separates the transaction into two distinct components: (1) the immediate sale of an intangible right—an exclusive option—and (2) a potential future sale of a good. The buyer pays a non-refundable option fee. In exchange, the artist agrees that if they create a specified type of artwork during the option period, they will offer it exclusively to the buyer at a predetermined price. The cornerstone of this model is the explicit clause stating the artist is under no obligation whatsoever to create the artwork. This severs the direct link between the buyer's payment and the artist's labor, which is the primary trigger for a service contract analysis.

7.2. Essential Terms and Clauses

The following table details the essential clauses for the Option Agreement model.

Clause Title Purpose Key Language/Drafting Considerations Primary Legal Risk
**Grant of Option** To define the specific, intangible right being sold. The agreement must clearly state that the Artist (Optionor) grants the Buyer (Optionee) an exclusive and irrevocable option to purchase a future, specified artwork, *if and only if* such artwork is created by the Artist during the Option Period. **Illusory Promise.** The grant must be for a sufficiently defined future asset. If the description is too vague, the option could be deemed unenforceable for uncertainty.
**Option Fee as Independent Consideration** To establish valid, bargained-for consideration for the option right itself, ensuring irrevocability per *Steiner v. Thexton*. This clause must state that the Buyer will pay a non-refundable "Option Fee" as "independent and sufficient consideration" for the grant of the option. It must explicitly state that this fee is not an advance or deposit against the future purchase price and is fully earned by the Artist upon execution of the agreement, regardless of whether an artwork is ever created or purchased.19 **Nominal Consideration.** While courts do not typically weigh the adequacy of consideration, an exceptionally low fee (e.g., $1) could be challenged as a sham. The fee should be commercially reasonable for the exclusivity granted.
**No Obligation to Create** To definitively sever the connection between payment and labor, thereby avoiding a service contract characterization. This is the most critical clause. It must state in unambiguous terms: "Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed as imposing any duty or obligation upon the Artist to create, commence, or complete the Artwork. The decision to create the Artwork rests solely and exclusively in the Artist's discretion." **Implied Covenant.** A buyer might argue that the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing creates some obligation on the artist. This clause must be strong enough to override any such potential implication.
**Option Period** To define the duration for which the option is valid. A clear start and end date for the option period must be specified (e.g., 12, 18, or 24 months).14 **Reasonableness.** An excessively long option period for a small fee could be challenged as unconscionable, though this is a high bar to meet.
**Exercise of Option & Purchase Agreement** To provide a clear mechanism for the future sale if the artwork is created. The clause must detail the exact procedure for the Buyer to exercise the option (e.g., written notice upon tender of the artwork by the Artist). The terms of the subsequent purchase (price, transfer of title to the physical good, and assignment of copyright) should be included as an exhibit or within the body of the agreement, to be executed upon exercise.53 **Clarity of Terms.** The terms of the future purchase must be definite enough to be enforceable. An agreement to "mutually agree" on a price later would render the option unenforceable.53

Section 8: Comparative Analysis and Legal Defensibility

8.1. Risk of Re-characterization under the Predominant Factor Test

  • Unilateral Offer Model: High Risk. This model is highly vulnerable under the predominant factor test. The "performance" that forms the contract is, in essence, the artist's labor and creative service. A court is likely to look past the "sale of goods" framing and conclude that the essence of the agreement is to procure the artist's unique skill to create a custom object. The fact that the buyer's obligation becomes binding while the work is in progress further strengthens the argument that the transaction's "thrust" is the service of creation.

  • Option Agreement Model: Low Risk. This model presents a much stronger defense. The initial transaction is not for the artwork, but for the option itself—a distinct intangible right. The "predominant factor" of this executed agreement is the sale of this right for a fee. The artist's labor is not part of the bargain; it is a condition precedent to the second, potential transaction (the purchase). By creating this two-step structure, the model isolates the service component (the act of creation) from the contractual obligation. The buyer pays for exclusivity, not for labor.

8.2. Resilience Against Employee Classification under the ABC Test

Assuming a court finds that either model constitutes a contract for services and applies the ABC test, the analysis is as follows:

  • Prong A (Control): Both models can be drafted to satisfy this prong. By avoiding detailed instructions on the creative process and giving the artist full autonomy over their methods, materials, and schedule (outside of a final deadline), the buyer can demonstrate a lack of control consistent with an independent contractor relationship.

  • Prong B (Outside Usual Course of Business): This prong is entirely dependent on the nature of the buyer's business and cannot be controlled by drafting. If the buyer is an art gallery, publisher, or interior design firm that regularly commissions or sells art, both models will fail this prong. The artist's work would be considered part of the buyer's usual course of business. If the buyer is a tech company acquiring art for its office or a private collector, this prong can likely be satisfied.

  • Prong C (Independently Established Business): This is the critical point of failure for both models if the artist is a hobbyist. As established in Section 5, a hiring entity cannot prove that an individual who operates their creative pursuits as a hobby is "customarily engaged in an independently established... business".39 Both models are equally and fatally vulnerable on this point. However, if the artist does operate as a business (e.g., has a business license, markets their services to others, maintains a professional portfolio), then both models have a strong chance of satisfying Prong C. The Option Agreement may have a slight advantage, as it more closely mirrors a typical business-to-business transaction involving the licensing or sale of contingent assets, a common practice in established creative industries.14

8.3. Strategic Recommendations and Mitigation of Risks

  1. Prefer the Option Agreement Model: The Option Agreement model is legally superior and presents a substantially lower risk of re-characterization as a service contract. Its structural separation of the sale of a right from the potential sale of a good provides a robust defense against the predominant factor test.

  2. Mandatory Due Diligence on Artist Status: The single most important risk mitigation strategy is to avoid engaging with artists who are legally "hobbyists." Before entering into any agreement, the buyer must conduct due diligence to confirm that the artist operates as an independent business. This includes, at a minimum:

  • Requiring the artist to warrant in the contract that they are engaged in an independently established business of creating and selling art.

  • Requesting evidence of this status, such as a business license, a professional website or portfolio, a list of prior sales or commissions, or proof of incorporation or an LLC.

  • Ensuring the artist will be paid through their business entity or as a sole proprietor, not as an individual for whom payroll taxes might be expected.

  1. Meticulous Drafting: Regardless of the model chosen, the language of the agreement is paramount.

  • Consistently use terminology associated with the sale of goods (e.g., "buyer," "seller," "good," "product," "purchase price").

  • Avoid terminology associated with services or employment (e.g., "hire," "commission," "client," "contractor," "fee for services," "compensation").

  • Payment must be structured as a lump sum upon completion and delivery. Avoid any form of advance, draw, or progress payment, as these will be interpreted as wages for ongoing labor.

In conclusion, while no contractual structure can entirely eliminate the risk of a misclassification claim in California's highly regulated environment, the Option to Purchase Agreement, when used with an artist who is verifiably operating an independent business, offers the most legally defensible framework for acquiring future artwork without creating a service or employment relationship. The Unilateral Offer model, due to the legal implications of irrevocability upon part performance, carries a significantly higher risk of being re-characterized as a contract for services.
