Prostitution as Impermissible Expression: A First Amendment Analysis of Speech, Conduct, and Criminality

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Introduction: Framing the Constitutional Inquiry

This report addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of free expression and state police power: To what extent, if any, does the First Amendment of the U.S. Constitution protect the various acts associated with prostitution from criminal prohibition? The inquiry is not merely whether prostitution is "speech," but whether its constituent parts—the communicative acts of solicitation and negotiation, and the physical act of commercial sex—can be classified as either protected expression or expressive conduct. The First Amendment, which states that "Congress shall make no law... abridging the freedom of speech" 1, provides robust, but not absolute, protection for expression.

A proper constitutional analysis requires deconstructing the act of prostitution into its distinct components. The verbal and communicative elements must be analyzed under the doctrines governing unprotected speech categories, specifically commercial speech and speech integral to criminal conduct. The physical, non-communicative element—the act itself—must be analyzed under the framework for expressive conduct established by the Supreme Court in cases such as United States v. O'Brien.3 This bifurcated approach is necessary because the Constitution treats pure speech, expressive conduct, and non-expressive conduct with different levels of scrutiny.

This report will argue that under current First Amendment jurisprudence, neither the speech nor the conduct of prostitution receives constitutional protection. The dispositive factor is the underlying illegality of the commercial sexual exchange, a policy choice made by nearly every state legislature in the exercise of its police powers.5 This criminal status renders the associated speech unprotected as a proposal to engage in an illegal transaction and as speech integral to a crime. Furthermore, the physical conduct lacks the requisite expressive element to qualify for even the marginal protection afforded to some forms of symbolic speech, such as nude dancing. The report will proceed by first establishing the relevant legal doctrines that define the outer limits of First Amendment protection, then applying those doctrines directly to the constituent elements of prostitution, and concluding with an analysis of the substantial state interests that justify its prohibition.

Section I: The Doctrinal Framework of Unprotected Speech

This section will establish the foundational legal principles that define the boundaries of First Amendment protection, creating the doctrinal lens through which prostitution will be analyzed. The core of this framework is the recognition that not all speech is of equal constitutional value, and some categories of expression fall entirely outside the amendment's protective scope.

1.1 Foundational Principles: The "Two-Tier" Theory of Speech

The analysis begins with the Supreme Court's long-standing recognition that the right to free speech is not absolute. In the landmark case Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire (1942), the Court articulated what has come to be known as a "two-tier" theory of speech.7 This theory distinguishes between speech that is socially valuable and contributes to the "exposition of ideas," and certain "well-defined and narrowly limited classes of speech" that are of such "slight social value as a step to truth that any benefit that may be derived from them is clearly outweighed by the social interest in order and morality".7

These categories of unprotected speech, which can be prevented and punished without raising constitutional problems, include obscenity, defamation, fraud, incitement to imminent lawless action, and "fighting words".7 This principle is the bedrock upon which the entire concept of "impermissible expression" rests. It establishes that the government may, consistent with the First Amendment, restrict certain utterances because of the harm they cause, rather than the ideas they contain.

1.2 The Commercial Speech Doctrine: A Lower Tier of Protection

Commercial speech, defined as speech that does no more than propose a commercial transaction, occupies a unique space in First Amendment jurisprudence. While it receives some constitutional protection, it is less than that afforded to political, scientific, or artistic speech.8 The governing standard for assessing regulations on commercial speech is the four-part test articulated in

Central Hudson Gas & Elec. Corp. v. Public Service Comm'n of New York (1980).13

The Threshold Inquiry

Crucially for the analysis of prostitution, for commercial speech to receive any protection under the Central Hudson framework, it must satisfy a threshold inquiry: it "must concern lawful activity and not be misleading".13 This first prong is not part of a balancing test; it is a gateway requirement. Speech that proposes an illegal transaction is categorically excluded from the ambit of First Amendment protection. As the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals explained in a case challenging California's prostitution law, the First Amendment extends no protection to commercial speech that concerns an illegal activity.14 The entire constitutional analysis of the

speech of prostitution is therefore contingent upon the legal status of the conduct of prostitution. The criminalization of the act itself is the legal lynchpin that renders the associated speech unprotected.

Application to "Vices"

The principle that speech proposing illegal activity is unprotected is clear. The Ninth Circuit has noted that while there is no general "vice exception" to commercial speech protections, this applies only to the advertisement of legal vices, such as gambling or alcohol, where regulations must still meet the Central Hudson test.12 For example, truthful advertisements for alcohol can be limited in ways that prevent them from attracting minors, but they cannot be banned outright.12 The advertisement of an activity that is itself illegal, however, falls outside this framework entirely and receives no protection.

1.3 Speech Integral to Criminal Conduct: When Words Become a Weapon

Perhaps the most relevant and powerful doctrine for analyzing the speech of prostitution is the "speech integral to criminal conduct" exception. In Giboney v. Empire Storage & Ice Co. (1949), the Supreme Court held that the First Amendment affords no protection to "speech or writing used as an integral part of conduct in violation of a valid criminal statute".9 This doctrine is essential for understanding how the law treats communication that is not merely about crime, but is the functional mechanism of crime.

Definition and Scope

This "nebulous" but critical exception applies when speech is the very instrument used to effectuate a crime.16 It must be distinguished from the abstract advocacy of lawbreaking, which remains protected speech so long as it is not intended to and likely to provoke immediate unlawful action.9 A general statement that "people should rise up one of these days" is protected advocacy.12 In contrast, speech integral to criminal conduct is "proximately linked to a criminal act".16 A robber's demand at gunpoint, "Hand over your money," is the classic example.9 The words are not an expression of an idea about robbery; they are the

actus reus, the physical act, of the robbery itself. The Supreme Court recently affirmed this principle in U.S. v. Hansen (2023), stating that "Speech intended to bring about a particular unlawful act has no social value; therefore, it is unprotected".16

An "Umbrella Category"

This doctrine can be seen as an "umbrella category" that encompasses other forms of unprotected speech like solicitation, conspiracy, extortion, bribery, and fraud.16 In each case, the speech is not being punished for its communicative content or message, but because it is the functional component of a crime.17

  • Solicitation and Conspiracy: An agreement to perform an illegal act, spoken with the intent to effectuate a crime, constitutes criminal conspiracy and is not protected by the First Amendment.16 Similarly, soliciting another person to commit a crime is a classic example of speech integral to criminal conduct.16

  • Fraud and Extortion: While false speech is generally protected, it becomes unprotected when a speaker tells a lie with the intent to effectuate a fraud, such as to wrongfully obtain money or another material benefit.9 Likewise, the speaking of words intended to threaten a victim to wrongfully obtain something of value is the crime of extortion and is unprotected.16

The critical distinction that emerges from this doctrine is between abstractly advocating for the legalization of prostitution (which is protected speech) and directly soliciting a specific person to engage in an act of prostitution for money (which is unprotected speech). A general statement that "prostitution should be legal" is an expression of a political or social idea. A specific statement to an individual, "I will pay you $100 for sex," is not advocacy but a direct proposal to enter into a specific, illegal transaction. The former is a protected expression of an idea; the latter is unprotected speech integral to a crime.

Section II: The Constitutional Line Between Expression and Conduct

This section explores the legal framework for analyzing conduct that may have an expressive component, a necessary step before evaluating the physical act of prostitution. The First Amendment's protections are not confined to words alone but can extend to actions that are intended to communicate a message.

2.1 Symbolic Speech and the O'Brien Test

The First Amendment's protection extends to "expressive conduct" or "symbolic speech," which includes activities like picketing, wearing armbands, and burning flags.2 However, the Supreme Court has explicitly rejected the "view that an apparently limitless variety of conduct can be labeled 'speech' whenever the person engaging in the conduct intends thereby to express an idea" (

United States v. O'Brien).4 The law therefore requires a method for distinguishing expressive action from simple, non-communicative conduct.

The Spence Test for Expressive Conduct

Before a court applies any level of constitutional scrutiny to a regulation of conduct, it must first determine if the conduct is "inherently expressive".2 Under the two-part test articulated in

Spence v. Washington (1974), this requires a showing of both (1) an "intent to convey a particularized message," and (2) a great likelihood "that the message would be understood by those who viewed it".2 Conduct that is expressive only because the actor accompanies it with an explanation is not considered inherently expressive.2

The Four-Part O'Brien Test

If conduct is deemed expressive, government regulations that incidentally burden that expression are not automatically unconstitutional. They are analyzed under the less-stringent, four-part O'Brien test, which is a form of intermediate scrutiny.2 A government regulation is justified if it meets the following criteria:

  1. It is within the constitutional power of the Government;

  2. It furthers an important or substantial governmental interest;

  3. The governmental interest is unrelated to the suppression of free expression; and

  4. The incidental restriction on alleged First Amendment freedoms is no greater than is essential to the furtherance of that interest.2

Application in O'Brien

The test was established in the case of David O'Brien, who burned his draft card on the steps of a courthouse to protest the Vietnam War.4 The Supreme Court upheld his conviction under a federal law prohibiting the knowing mutilation of draft cards.3 The Court applied its newly minted test and found it satisfied. It concluded that while O'Brien's act was expressive, the government had a substantial, non-speech-related interest in the smooth functioning of the Selective Service system, which required the preservation of draft cards.3 Because the law was aimed at this administrative, "noncommunicative aspect of O'Brien's conduct," it was upheld despite its incidental effect on his protest.3

2.2 The Nude Dancing Precedent—Barnes v. Glen Theatre, Inc. (1991)

The Supreme Court's decision in Barnes v. Glen Theatre, Inc. is the most proximate and instructive precedent for analyzing sexually-themed conduct under the O'Brien framework. In Barnes, the Court confronted the question of whether a state's general public indecency law could be constitutionally applied to prohibit non-obscene, totally nude dancing in adult entertainment establishments.24

Nude Dancing as "Marginally" Protected Expression

A plurality of the Court, in an opinion by Chief Justice Rehnquist, conceded that nude dancing is "expressive conduct within the outer perimeters of the First Amendment, although only marginally so".20 Without engaging in a detailed

Spence analysis, the Court accepted that the erotic performance was intended to convey a message of eroticism and was likely to be understood as such by its audience.20

Upholding Regulation Under O'Brien

Despite finding the conduct expressive, the Court upheld the Indiana law requiring dancers to wear minimal coverings, such as pasties and a G-string.20 It did so by applying the four-part

O'Brien test and finding it satisfied.20

  • Substantial Government Interest: The plurality identified the state's interest in "protecting societal order and morality" as a substantial government interest sufficient to satisfy the second prong of the test.24 In a separate and highly influential concurrence, Justice Souter identified a different interest: combating the "secondary effects" of adult entertainment establishments, such as prostitution, sexual assaults, and other criminal activity.24 This "secondary effects" doctrine provides a powerful tool for the state. It allows a regulation to be justified based on potential negative societal impacts associated with the activity, thereby framing a potentially content-based moral judgment as a content-neutral public safety regulation that is more likely to survive judicial scrutiny.

  • Interest Unrelated to Suppression of Expression: This was the critical step in the analysis. The Court reasoned that the public indecency law was not aimed at the erotic message of the dance, but at the public nudity itself—the non-speech element of the conduct.20 The law's purpose was to ban all public nudity, regardless of whether it was combined with an expressive activity. The plurality stated that an erotic performance could be presented without state interference, so long as the performers wore a scant amount of clothing.26 This conceptual separation of the physical conduct (nudity) from the expressive message (eroticism) was key to finding the state's interest content-neutral and thus satisfying the third prong of the O'Brien test. This legal distinction allows the Court to frame the law as a general prohibition on public nudity that happens to affect an expressive activity, rather than a law targeting the expression itself, thereby avoiding the much stricter standard of review reserved for content-based regulations.

Section III: A First Amendment Analysis of Prostitution

This section applies the legal frameworks established in Sections I and II directly to the communicative and physical acts of prostitution. By dissecting the transaction into its constituent parts, it becomes clear that neither component qualifies for First Amendment protection under existing constitutional doctrine.

3.1 Deconstructing the Transaction: Speech and Conduct Components

The act of prostitution is not a monolithic event but a process that involves distinct elements. A comprehensive legal analysis must address each one separately:

  • (a) The Communicative Acts: This includes the solicitation of a client, the negotiation of terms (services to be rendered, price), and the final agreement to engage in the transaction. These are fundamentally acts of speech.14

  • (b) The Physical Conduct: This is the commercial exchange of a sexual act for money or other consideration. This is the underlying conduct that the speech proposes and facilitates.14

3.2 The Speech of Prostitution: An Unprotected Category

The communicative acts associated with prostitution fail to gain First Amendment protection under at least two independent and dispositive doctrines.

Analysis as Commercial Speech Proposing an Illegal Transaction

As established in Section I, the Central Hudson test for commercial speech contains a critical threshold requirement: the speech must concern "lawful activity".13 Prostitution is illegal in nearly every U.S. jurisdiction, with the limited exception of licensed brothels in some rural counties of Nevada.5 Therefore, speech soliciting or proposing an act of prostitution inherently concerns unlawful activity. This places it squarely outside the scope of protected commercial speech.

The Ninth Circuit's reasoning in Erotic Service Provider Legal, Education & Research Project (ESP) v. GASCON (2018) is directly on point. In that case, the court reviewed a First Amendment challenge to California's law criminalizing prostitution. The court began and ended its commercial speech analysis with the first prong of the Central Hudson test. Because prostitution is an unlawful activity in California, the court held, the speech soliciting it is not protected, and the First Amendment analysis ends there.14 The illegality of the underlying conduct is constitutionally fatal to any claim of protection for the speech that proposes it.

Analysis as Speech Integral to the Crime of Prostitution

Even if viewed outside the commercial speech context, the words of solicitation and agreement are the direct instruments of a crime. In most jurisdictions, the verbal offer and acceptance constitute the crime of solicitation or conspiracy to commit prostitution.14 The speech is not a commentary on prostitution or an abstract discussion of its merits; it is the

commission of a crime related to prostitution. As such, it falls squarely within the "speech integral to criminal conduct" exception and receives no First Amendment protection.9 The words are the means by which the illegal transaction is initiated and formed, making them inseparable from the criminal conduct itself.

3.3 The Conduct of Prostitution: A Lack of Expressive Content

The physical act of commercial sex also fails to qualify for First Amendment protection as expressive conduct. It is legally distinct from other forms of commercialized sexuality, such as nude dancing or pornography, which are considered expressive because they are created for an audience.

Failure to Meet the Spence Test

Unlike nude dancing, which is a performance intended to convey an erotic message to an audience, the act of prostitution is a private, commercial transaction.24 It is not a performance intended to convey a "particularized message" to the public in a way that would satisfy the

Spence test.2 While one could posit that the act expresses ideas about sexuality, commerce, or human relationships, there is no "great likelihood" that such a message would be understood by a viewing public in the way a protest, a play, or a dance is. The conduct is not "inherently expressive" because its primary purpose is transactional, not communicative.2

The Decisive Precedent of Arcara v. Cloud Books, Inc. (1986)

The Supreme Court provided its most direct statement on this matter in Arcara v. Cloud Books, Inc. The case involved the closure of an adult bookstore under a public health nuisance law because solicitation of prostitution and other illicit sexual activities were occurring on the premises.32 The bookstore argued that the closure order, which incidentally burdened its First Amendment right to sell books, should be evaluated under the

O'Brien test.

The Supreme Court emphatically rejected this argument. Writing for the majority, Chief Justice Burger stated that the O'Brien test "has no relevance to a statute directed at imposing sanctions on nonexpressive activity, and the sexual activities carried on in this case manifest absolutely no element of protected expression".32 The Court concluded that "[b]ookselling in an establishment used for prostitution does not confer First Amendment coverage to defeat a valid statute aimed at penalizing and terminating illegal uses of premises".33 This holding represents a categorical denial by the Supreme Court that the conduct of prostitution contains any expressive element worthy of First Amendment consideration.

The legal conclusions regarding the speech and conduct of prostitution are mutually reinforcing. The finding that the conduct is non-expressive and illegal solidifies the conclusion that the speech proposing it is unprotected. Conversely, the fact that the speech constitutes a criminal solicitation reinforces the non-expressive, purely transactional nature of the conduct. This creates a closed legal loop from which prostitution cannot escape under current jurisprudence.

Section IV: Governmental Interests and the Limits of Constitutional Challenge

This section details the justifications for criminalizing prostitution and explains why, given the lack of a First Amendment right, these laws are easily upheld against constitutional challenges. The state's authority to regulate in this area is rooted in its fundamental police power.

4.1 Historical Context of Prostitution Criminalization

The legal status of prostitution in the United States has evolved significantly. Contrary to some modern assumptions, prostitution was not always illegal. It was generally legal and subject to local regulation in many areas, particularly in large cities and the American West, until a wave of social and moral reform in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.5 This movement, often associated with feminist, temperance, and religious groups, viewed prostitution as a threat to public health and morality and an exploitation of women.36

A key turning point was the passage of the federal Mann Act in 1910, which prohibited the interstate transportation of women for "immoral purposes".36 Following this federal action, a nationwide push for criminalization occurred at the state level, and by 1915, nearly all states had passed laws banning brothels and regulating the profits of prostitution.36 This history demonstrates that the current criminalized regime is the result of a specific set of policy choices made a century ago, not an immutable legal tradition extending from the nation's founding.

4.2 The State's Police Power: Substantial Governmental Interests

The criminalization of prostitution is considered a reasonable exercise of the state's inherent police power—the authority to legislate for the protection of public health, safety, welfare, and morals.6 Over the years, courts have recognized numerous substantial governmental interests that justify these laws. While early justifications were heavily centered on morality, modern legal arguments often emphasize public health and safety concerns. This evolution strengthens the state's legal position, as arguments based on preventing violence and trafficking are often seen as more compelling and legally robust than those based purely on subjective moral judgments.

The commonly cited state interests include:

  • Public Health: Preventing the spread of sexually transmitted diseases and infections.35

  • Public Safety and Order: Combating a range of associated crimes, including violence against sex workers, drug use, theft from clients, and the general degradation of public order in areas where street solicitation is common.26

  • Preventing Exploitation and Human Trafficking: A primary modern justification is the view that prostitution is inherently exploitative and inextricably linked to the grave crimes of human trafficking and sexual slavery. This rationale posits that criminalizing the demand for commercial sex is a necessary tool to disrupt trafficking networks and protect vulnerable individuals.37

  • Upholding Public Morals: The traditional interest in discouraging conduct that society deems to be immoral remains a legitimate, though sometimes controversial, basis for legislation. The Supreme Court explicitly recognized this interest as a valid justification for the public indecency law in Barnes.26

As the Ninth Circuit noted in ESP v. GASCON, these interests are considered substantial and rational, and any one of them is typically sufficient to uphold a law against prostitution.14

4.3 The Standard of Review: Rational Basis

Because courts have consistently found no fundamental right to engage in prostitution—whether under the First Amendment's protection of expression or the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantees of privacy and liberty—laws criminalizing it are subject only to the most deferential standard of judicial review: the rational basis test.14

To pass this test, the state does not need to prove that its law is the best or most effective policy. It need only show that the law is rationally related to a legitimate government interest. Given the long list of substantial interests associated with regulating prostitution, from public health to preventing human trafficking, anti-prostitution statutes easily survive this low level of constitutional scrutiny.

Section V: Synthesis and Concluding Analysis

This report has conducted a systematic analysis of prostitution through the lens of First Amendment jurisprudence. By deconstructing the act into its communicative and physical components and applying the relevant legal doctrines, a clear and consistent conclusion emerges.

5.1 Summary of Findings

Under a systematic application of established First Amendment principles, prostitution is an impermissible expression. Its communicative elements—the acts of solicitation and negotiation—constitute both unprotected commercial speech proposing an illegal transaction and speech integral to criminal conduct. Its physical element—the commercial exchange of sex—constitutes non-expressive conduct that falls entirely outside the scope of First Amendment protection, as explicitly stated by the Supreme Court in Arcara v. Cloud Books, Inc..32 Consequently, the criminalization of prostitution and its associated activities does not violate the freedom of speech.

5.2 The Decisive Role of Criminalization

The conclusion is inescapable: the status of prostitution as a criminal act is the determinative factor in the First Amendment analysis. The entire doctrinal framework, from the commercial speech test to the speech-integral-to-crime exception, hinges on the predicate illegality of the underlying conduct. Were the underlying conduct to be decriminalized by a state legislature, the entire constitutional landscape would shift dramatically. The solicitation of prostitution would become commercial speech concerning a lawful activity. As such, any state regulations on advertising or solicitation would then be subject to intermediate scrutiny under the Central Hudson test, a much more difficult standard for the state to meet. This illustrates that the boundary of First Amendment protection in this context is not drawn by judges based on the nature of the speech itself, but by legislatures based on their policy decision to criminalize the underlying act.

5.3 Distinguishing Prostitution from Protected Erotic Expression

It is crucial to reiterate the fine but firm lines drawn by constitutional law in the realm of commercialized sexuality. The First Amendment robustly protects the creation and distribution of non-obscene pornography and the performance of non-obscene erotic dance precisely because they are legally deemed to be expressive acts intended for an audience. Prostitution is denied protection because it is legally defined as a private, commercial, non-expressive, and criminal act.
