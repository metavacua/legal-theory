The Legal Status of Bodily Autonomy in the United States: A Post-Dobbs Jurisdictional Analysis

By Ian D.L.N. McLean —

Section I: The Federal Framework and the Primacy of State Law

The legal architecture governing the right to bodily autonomy in the United States is a complex and contested domain, fundamentally reshaped by the U.S. Supreme Court's 2022 decision in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization. Historically, the concept of bodily autonomy—the right of an individual to govern their own body and make decisions about their physical self without external coercion—has not been an explicitly enumerated right within the U.S. Constitution.1 Instead, rights related to bodily integrity and personal decision-making have been constructed through decades of judicial interpretation, primarily rooted in the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, which prohibits states from depriving "any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law".3 This jurisprudential approach created a "zone of privacy," not textually explicit but inferred from the "penumbras" of other constitutional protections, that shielded certain personal decisions from government intrusion.6

The high-water mark for this federally protected, unenumerated right came during the latter half of the 20th century. Landmark cases such as Griswold v. Connecticut (1965), which struck down a state law banning contraceptives for married couples, and Roe v. Wade (1973), which established a constitutional right to abortion, were foundational.3 These decisions interpreted the "liberty" protected by the Fourteenth Amendment to include a sphere of personal life into which the government could not intrude. The 1992 decision in

Planned Parenthood v. Casey reaffirmed this principle, explicitly linking the right to terminate a pregnancy to the concepts of "liberty or personal autonomy".3 For nearly five decades, this line of reasoning stood for the proposition that an individual's right to make deeply personal decisions about their own body could, in certain circumstances, outweigh the state's interest in regulating those choices.

This entire legal foundation was dismantled by the Dobbs decision, which not only overturned Roe and Casey but also instituted a profoundly different and more restrictive test for the recognition of unenumerated rights.1 The Court's ruling had two immediate and transformative effects. First, it held that the U.S. Constitution does not confer a right to abortion, thereby returning the authority to regulate or prohibit the procedure entirely to the individual states.10 Second, and more broadly, the majority established a new standard for any right not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution: it must be "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition" to be recognized.1 This test is structurally hostile to the recognition of autonomy in areas that have a history of criminalization rather than protection. As the

Dobbs majority argued, because abortion was criminalized in many states when the Fourteenth Amendment was ratified, a right to it could not be found in the nation's history.3

The Dobbs decision did not merely return a single issue to the states; it fundamentally altered the nature of American federalism regarding personal liberties, creating a legal landscape where a right as fundamental as bodily autonomy is no longer universal but geographically contingent. The removal of a federal constitutional floor has led to a "balkanization" of rights, with states moving in sharply divergent directions. While some states have moved to enact near-total bans on abortion, others have responded by passing new statutes and even constitutional amendments to create explicit, durable protections for reproductive freedom.10 This creates a system where American citizenship no longer carries with it a consistent set of rights regarding personal autonomy; these rights now depend entirely on one's state of residence, leading to profound inequalities in liberty.

Furthermore, the "history and tradition" test serves as a formidable strategic roadblock for future federal litigation. It effectively forecloses many potential claims for bodily autonomy before they can even be argued, as practices such as modern sex work, non-normative body modification, and even access to certain forms of contraception lack a "deeply rooted" history of legal protection and were, in fact, often subject to criminalization.3 This legal reality has precipitated a strategic pivot in legal advocacy. With federal courts now structurally hostile to unenumerated rights, the focus of civil liberties litigation has necessarily shifted to the 50 state supreme courts and state-level political processes, which have become the primary and, in many cases, only viable arenas for affirming and defending the right to bodily autonomy.17 Consequently, a comprehensive assessment of the right to bodily autonomy in the United States is no longer possible through an analysis of federal law alone; it requires a detailed, state-by-state jurisdictional analysis.

Section II: Explicit Affirmations in State Constitutions

In the post-Dobbs legal environment, the most powerful and durable affirmations of bodily autonomy are those enshrined within state constitutions. These protections arise from direct constitutional amendments passed by voters, a trend that has accelerated significantly since 2022. This exercise of direct democracy represents the strongest possible affirmation of bodily autonomy at the state level. Since June 2022, voters in eleven states have approved such measures:

  • California (2022): Proposition 1 amended the state constitution to explicitly establish a "fundamental right to choose to have an abortion" and a "fundamental right to choose or refuse contraceptives".15

  • Michigan (2022): Proposal 3 established a "fundamental right to reproductive freedom," which is broadly defined to include decisions about prenatal care, childbirth, postpartum care, contraception, sterilization, abortion care, miscarriage management, and infertility care.15

  • Vermont (2022): Article 22 was added to the state constitution, establishing a right to "personal reproductive autonomy".15

  • Ohio (2023): Issue 1 amended the constitution to protect an individual's right to "make and carry out one's own reproductive decisions," including but not limited to decisions about abortion, contraception, fertility treatment, and miscarriage care.16

  • Arizona (2024): Proposition 139 established a "fundamental right to an abortion" up to the point of fetal viability and thereafter when necessary to protect the life or health of the pregnant person.16

  • Colorado (2024): Amendment 89 recognized a "right to abortion" in the state constitution and repealed a previous constitutional provision that prohibited the use of state funds for abortion coverage.16

  • Maryland (2024): Question 1 guaranteed a "fundamental right to reproductive freedom," including the ability to prevent, continue, or end one's own pregnancy.16

  • Missouri (2024): Amendment 3 protected "reproductive freedom" and the ability to make and effectuate decisions about reproductive health, including abortion up to viability.16

  • Montana (2024): CI-128 affirmed a "right to make and carry out decisions about one's own pregnancy, including the right to abortion".16

  • Nevada (2024): Question 6 established a "fundamental right to abortion" until fetal viability. Under Nevada law, this measure must be approved by voters again in the 2026 general election before the amendment is added to the constitution.16

  • New York (2024): Proposal 1 amended the state's equal rights provision to prohibit discrimination based on "pregnancy, pregnancy outcomes, and reproductive healthcare and autonomy".16

The varied language used in these amendments is not merely semantic; it reflects a deliberate and forward-looking legal strategy. The choice of broader terms like "reproductive freedom" (Michigan, Maryland, Missouri) and "reproductive autonomy" (Vermont, New York) over the more specific "right to abortion" creates a more flexible and powerful constitutional foundation. A right to "reproductive autonomy," for instance, can be litigated to protect not only abortion but also access to contraception, fertility treatments like in-vitro fertilization (IVF), and gender-affirming care, thereby preempting future legislative attacks on a wider range of bodily autonomy issues.4 This suggests that the legal battles of the next decade in these states will focus on defining the full scope and application of these new, broadly-worded rights.

Section III: Statutory and Code-Based Affirmations of Autonomy

Beyond the foundational protections of state constitutions, the right to bodily autonomy is also explicitly affirmed through various state statutes and legal codes. These laws, while generally less durable than constitutional provisions, provide critical, context-specific protections in the domains of medical decision-making, sexual consent, and reproductive health care. They codify the principle that individuals have the right to control what is done to their bodies and to be free from unwanted physical intrusion.

The Right to Self-Determination in Medical Consent

One of the most widespread and fundamental statutory affirmations of bodily autonomy is the legal doctrine of informed consent. This principle, codified in the laws of every state, is based on the right of every individual to determine what shall be done to their own body in the context of medical treatment.27 The doctrine requires a process of communication between a patient and a physician that results in the patient's explicit authorization for a specific medical intervention, thereby giving legal force to the patient's self-determination.28 Analysis of specific state statutes reveals clear language affirming this right:

  • California: The California Code of Regulations (Tit. 9, § 784.29) provides an unambiguous affirmation of patient control. The text states, "The client has the right to accept or refuse the proposed treatment, and if he or she consents, has the right to revoke his or her consent for any reason at any time".29 This language codifies not only the initial right to decide but also the ongoing right to withdraw consent, reinforcing the principle of continuous personal sovereignty.

  • New York: The state's Public Health Law (§ 2805-D) frames the right through the lens of malpractice, defining a "lack of informed consent" as the failure to disclose information in a manner "permitting the patient to make a knowledgeable evaluation".30 The statute also explicitly recognizes a patient's right to control the flow of information about their own body by providing a legal defense if "the patient assured the... practitioner that he did not want to be informed".30 This acknowledges the patient's autonomy extends even to the decision of whether to participate in the informed consent process itself.

  • Texas: The Texas Health and Safety Code (Ch. 313) addresses situations of patient incapacity by establishing a framework for surrogate decision-making. Critically, the law mandates that any decision made by a surrogate "must be based on knowledge of what the patient would desire, if known".31 This provision centers the patient's own values and previously expressed wishes, ensuring that their autonomy is respected to the greatest extent possible even when they cannot communicate for themselves.

  • Massachusetts: The state's General Laws (Ch. 112, § 12R) require the "written informed consent of the patient seeking an abortion." For minors, the law provides a detailed judicial bypass procedure that respects their autonomy if a judge determines they are mature and capable of giving informed consent, thereby recognizing that age alone is not an absolute barrier to self-determination.32

The Affirmative Consent Standard in Sexual Assault Law

A powerful, context-specific affirmation of bodily autonomy is found in the criminal codes of states that have adopted an "affirmative consent" standard for sexual activity. This represents a significant evolution from the historical legal model for sexual assault, which was coercion-based. In a coercion-based framework, the central legal question is whether the perpetrator used "forcible compulsion" to overcome the victim's will.1 This model does not positively affirm a right to be free from unwanted sexual contact in the absence of a specific level of violence.

In contrast, the modern consent-based model, particularly the "only yes means yes" standard of affirmative consent, reframes the crime entirely. It defines sexual assault by the absence of consent, not necessarily the presence of force.1 Affirmative consent is statutorily defined as a knowing, voluntary, and mutual decision, communicated through unambiguous words or actions, to engage in a specific sexual act.1 These statutes explicitly state that silence, passivity, or the absence of physical resistance does not constitute consent.1 This legal standard is a direct affirmation of bodily autonomy because it recognizes each person's default state of self-sovereignty and places the legal responsibility on the person initiating a sexual act to secure positive, unambiguous permission.

This modern legal framework creates a stark contradiction with the standards for non-sexual social interactions. For general social touching, such as incidental contact in a crowded public space, the law relies on the pragmatic, objective standard of "implied consent" to facilitate the functioning of society.33 This "Consent Paradox" reveals that bodily autonomy is not an absolute principle but a context-dependent one. The law grants it the highest level of protection in the sexual sphere, recognizing the unique and profound harms of sexual violation, while significantly lowering that protection in other areas of physical contact for reasons of social utility.

Reproductive Health Acts and Interstate Shield Laws

In addition to medical and criminal codes, several states have enacted broad statutory schemes that positively affirm reproductive autonomy. These fall short of constitutional amendments but represent significant legislative protections.

  • Reproductive Health Acts: States including Illinois (Reproductive Health Act of 2019) and New Jersey (Freedom of Reproductive Choice Act of 2022) have passed comprehensive statutes that explicitly declare reproductive health care, including abortion and contraception, to be a "fundamental right".15 These acts codify protections in state law, creating a statutory bulwark against judicial or executive actions that might seek to limit access.

  • Interstate Shield Laws: A direct response to the post-Dobbs landscape has been the enactment of "shield laws" in numerous states, including New Jersey, Illinois, and California.15 These statutes are designed to protect in-state medical providers, patients who travel for care, and individuals who assist them from out-of-state investigations, subpoenas, and extradition requests related to the provision of reproductive health services that are legal within the state's borders. These laws function as an affirmation of the state's sovereign authority to protect the bodily autonomy of individuals within its jurisdiction from the legal overreach of other states.

A state like California exemplifies a multi-domain approach to protecting bodily autonomy. It has a post-Dobbs constitutional amendment for reproductive freedom, a pre-existing explicit right to privacy in its constitution that courts have interpreted to protect medical decisions, a robust medical informed consent statute, and a criminal code with a clear affirmative consent standard for sexual assault.1 This layered approach demonstrates that a comprehensive legal framework for bodily autonomy is not monolithic. The strength of the right in any given state is a function of how many of these distinct legal domains contain positive, explicit affirmations of the principle of self-sovereignty.

Section IV: The Negative Legal Landscape: Autonomy by Implication or Absence

To fully comprehend the states that positively affirm bodily autonomy, it is essential to analyze the legal frameworks in jurisdictions that lack such explicit protections. In these states, the right to bodily autonomy is at its most precarious. The absence of affirmative legal language, combined with the persistence of archaic legal doctrines, creates a negative legal landscape where autonomy is significantly diminished or functionally absent. This analysis validates the core assumption that, post-Dobbs, the absence of explicit state-level affirmation often implies an absence of the right itself.

Coercion-Based Criminal Statutes: The Failure to Affirm Autonomy

The most significant area where the law fails to affirm bodily autonomy is in states that retain a coercion-based model for sexual assault crimes. In jurisdictions such as Alabama, Arkansas, and Massachusetts, the legal definition of sexual assault is not centered on the victim's lack of consent but on the perpetrator's use of "forcible compulsion".1 The central legal question is not "Did the individual consent?" but "Did the perpetrator use a sufficient level of force or threat of violence to overcome the victim's will?".1

This legal structure, by its very design, creates a category of sexual interaction that is factually non-consensual but not criminally illegal. If a statute requires the element of "forcible compulsion," a sexual act that occurs without consent but also without the statutorily defined level of force does not meet the legal definition of the crime. In such a scenario, even if a victim is passive, silent, or verbally says "no" without physically resisting, the absence of the force element means a key component of the crime is missing.1 This is not a loophole but the intended function of a coercion-based legal framework, which permits certain non-consensual sexual acts to occur without criminal liability. These statutes fail to positively affirm a right to be free from unwanted sexual contact, instead offering protection only when that contact is accompanied by a specific degree of violence.

The Diminishment of Autonomy in Marital Law

A particularly stark example of where state law explicitly curtails bodily autonomy is found in the persistent statutory loopholes related to marital rape. While marital rape is now a crime in all 50 states, the legacy of the common law doctrine that a husband could not rape his wife—based on the theory that a wife gave irrevocable consent through the marriage contract—endures in the legal codes of several states.42 These de jure loopholes create a system where the legal significance of a spouse's non-consent is diminished under specific circumstances.

  • Incapacity Exemptions: The most egregious of these loopholes exist in states like Michigan and Mississippi. Their laws create an explicit exemption for non-forcible sex with a spouse who is "mentally incapable, or mentally incapacitated".1 This means that an act which would be a felony if committed against an unmarried person—sex with someone unable to consent due to impairment—is legally permissible within a marriage. This legal distinction codifies the principle that the marriage contract itself diminishes the right to bodily autonomy, sanctioning an act based not on the presence of consent in the moment, but on the legal status of the relationship.

  • Differential Standards: In South Carolina, the law creates a separate and lesser crime of "spousal sexual battery." To be convicted of this crime, the state must prove the use of "aggravated force," a higher standard than for non-spousal rape. Furthermore, the maximum penalty is 10 years, compared to 30 years for non-spousal rape, and a 30-day reporting time limit applies if the couple lives together.1 This statutory scheme explicitly creates a lower tier of bodily autonomy for married individuals.

  • Other Jurisdictional Loopholes: Numerous other states retain some form of marital rape loophole, often related to incapacity or different standards of force. These states include Connecticut, Idaho, Iowa, Minnesota, Nevada, Ohio, Oklahoma, and Rhode Island.1

The persistence of these exemptions is not merely an archaic oversight; it is an active legal statement about the nature of consent and autonomy within marriage. The specific focus on incapacity reveals a deeper legal principle at play: the law in these states treats the marriage contract as a form of standing, residual consent to sexual access that is not fully nullified by a spouse's temporary incapacity. This stands in sharp contrast to the law for unmarried persons, where incapacity is an absolute bar to consent. It is a direct legal inheritance of the common law doctrine of coverture, where a wife's legal identity was subsumed by her husband's, and it demonstrates a clear instance where the state actively and explicitly curtails an individual's baseline right to bodily autonomy based on their marital status.

Section V: Synthesis and Jurisdictional Overview

The preceding analysis demonstrates that the legal right to bodily autonomy in the United States is no longer a matter of settled federal doctrine but a dynamic and fragmented issue being forged in the 50 distinct legal laboratories of the states. The existence, scope, and durability of this right are now entirely dependent on a complex interplay of state constitutions and legislative action. To synthesize these findings, the states can be categorized into a tiered model of protection, revealing a stark jurisdictional divide in the affirmation of this fundamental human right.

A Tiered Model of Protection

  • Tier 1: Robust & Durable Protection: This tier includes states that have the strongest and most durable legal protections for bodily autonomy. These are the jurisdictions that, since 2022, have amended their state constitutions through direct voter initiatives to explicitly guarantee a right to reproductive freedom or autonomy. This category includes California, Michigan, Vermont, Ohio, Arizona, Colorado, Maryland, Missouri, Montana, Nevada, and New York. The protections in these states are the most secure because they are enshrined in the foundational legal document and can only be removed by another, often difficult, constitutional amendment process.

  • Tier 2: Context-Specific Statutory Protection: This tier includes states that lack broad constitutional protections but have affirmed autonomy in specific statutory domains. This includes states with strong affirmative consent laws for sexual assault (such as Washington and Wisconsin, if they lack constitutional protections) or comprehensive Reproductive Health Acts (such as New Jersey). These protections are significant but are limited to the specific context of the statute and are more easily repealed or amended by a simple legislative majority compared to constitutional rights.

  • Tier 3: Precarious or Absent Protection: This tier includes states that lack any significant positive affirmations of bodily autonomy. These are jurisdictions that continue to rely on coercion-based criminal laws for sexual assault and/or retain significant legal loopholes that diminish autonomy, such as marital rape exemptions. States like Alabama, Mississippi, and South Carolina fall into this category. In these states, the right to bodily autonomy is, as the user's query presumed, functionally absent in key areas of the law.

Final Conclusion

In the wake of Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization, the United States no longer has a unified legal standard for bodily autonomy. The right has been effectively returned to the states, resulting in a balkanized system where the ability of an individual to control their own body is contingent upon their zip code. A small but growing number of states have responded by erecting powerful legal shields through constitutional amendments and robust statutes that explicitly affirm the right to self-determination in medical, reproductive, and sexual contexts. In these jurisdictions, bodily autonomy is a positively asserted right. However, in a significant portion of the country, the absence of such affirmations, coupled with the persistence of archaic legal frameworks that prioritize force over consent and marital status over individual sovereignty, means that the right to bodily autonomy remains conditional, precarious, and, in many critical respects, absent from the law. The struggle to define, defend, and secure this fundamental right has now become a central and enduring feature of American state-level legislation and politics.
