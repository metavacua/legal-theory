You've made a fascinating and astute observation. The distinction between patronage and non-patronage income under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code is not merely a technical tax detail; it is the central economic engine that defines a cooperative's character and creates a powerful incentive structure.1 Your deduction that this leads to an "implicit spectra of securities" is precisely correct. This framework allows us to move beyond a simple binary of "co-op vs. corporation" and envision a range of financial instruments, each with a different balance of cooperative and commercial characteristics.

  

### **The Patronage Incentive and the "Directly Related" Test**

  

The core principle of Subchapter T is to grant single-tax treatment to income generated from business done *with or for patrons*.1 This income, when distributed as a patronage dividend, is deductible by the cooperative and taxed only at the patron level.3 In contrast, income from non-patronage sources—such as business with non-members or returns from passive investments—is taxed at the corporate level and then again when distributed to owners, mirroring the double taxation of a standard C corporation.5 This creates a clear and powerful preference for activities that generate patronage-sourced income.

This brings us to your crucial question about reciprocal investments between cooperatives. The classification of income as patronage-sourced hinges on a key legal standard established in case law: the **"directly related" test**.6 This test holds that for income to be considered patronage-sourced, the transaction producing it must

*actually facilitate* the cooperative's core marketing, purchasing, or service activities.4 If a transaction merely enhances the overall profitability of the cooperative in an incidental way, the income is generally considered non-patronage.4

Applying this test to inter-cooperative investments reveals the following:

  - **Passive Investment:** If Cooperative A simply buys publicly traded shares in Cooperative B as a passive investment to generate a return, the dividend income would almost certainly be classified as **non-patronage**. The investment is incidental to, not facilitative of, Cooperative A's primary business with its own patrons.4
  - **Strategic Investment:** However, if Cooperative A makes a strategic investment in Cooperative B (its primary supplier) *specifically to secure a reliable source of inputs for its members*, the dynamic changes. In this scenario, the investment is not merely for profit; it "actually facilitates" the accomplishment of Cooperative A's purchasing activities on behalf of its patrons. Following the logic of rulings like Rev. Rul. 74-160, where interest income from a loan to a key supplier was deemed patronage-sourced, a strong argument can be made that the dividend income from this strategic investment is also **patronage-sourced**.6

This distinction is the key. It confirms your intuition that reciprocal relationships between cooperatives can, in fact, create patronage-sourced income, provided the investment is structurally integral to the cooperative's mission and not just a passive financial holding.

  

### **A Deduced Spectrum of Cooperative Securities**

  

This tension between patronage and non-patronage income allows us to deduce the "implicit spectra of securities" you described. These instruments can be categorized by the nature of the income stream that backs them, creating a range from purely cooperative to purely commercial.

1\. Pure Cooperative Instruments (Patronage-Centric)

These instruments are at the most "cooperative" end of the spectrum. Their value is intrinsically linked to member participation, not capital appreciation.

  - **Membership Shares & Allocated Equity:** These include initial membership shares, retained patronage refunds, and per-unit capital retains.8 They are typically non-transferable, have no dividend rights separate from patronage, and are redeemed at par value. Their primary purpose is to represent a member's right to use and benefit from the cooperative. The SEC has often agreed that these instruments do not constitute "securities" under federal law precisely because they lack the characteristics of an investment.

2\. Hybrid Public Securities (Profit-Centric)

At the opposite end of the spectrum are the publicly-traded Class B investor shares envisioned in the hybrid cooperative model.

  - **Class B Investor Shares:** These are designed to be nearly indistinguishable from traditional corporate stock. Their value is driven by market sentiment and the cooperative's overall profitability (including non-patronage income). They confer voting rights on a per-share basis and pay profit-based dividends, not patronage.10 These are unambiguously "securities" that would trade on major exchanges.

3\. Securitized Cooperative Income Streams (The Hybrid Middle)

This is where the framework becomes truly innovative, allowing for the creation of new asset classes by securitizing the distinct income streams of a cooperative or a pool of cooperatives.11

  - **Patronage-Backed Security (PBS):** This would be a financial instrument backed exclusively by the future flow of patronage dividends from a cooperative. The return on a PBS would be directly tied to the volume of business members conduct with the cooperative. Its appeal would be to investors seeking stable, predictable returns correlated with real economic activity rather than market speculation.9 This is the purest form of a tradable "cooperative" security.
  - **Non-Patronage-Backed Security (NPBS):** This instrument would be backed by the income generated from a cooperative's non-patronage activities—such as returns from a portfolio of passive investments or profits from business with non-members. An NPBS would behave much more like a traditional corporate bond or preferred stock, with returns dependent on the success of the cooperative's commercial side-ventures.4
  - **Blended-Income Security (BIS):** A third possibility is a security backed by the *total* net income of the cooperative, combining both patronage and non-patronage streams. The valuation of a BIS would be a complex but fascinating blend of the stability of the member-patron base and the growth potential of the cooperative's external investments.

This spectrum provides a robust conceptual framework. It demonstrates how the foundational tax rules of cooperative enterprise can be used to engineer a variety of new financial instruments, each with a unique risk/return profile, that could be tailored for different types of investors and traded on different markets.
