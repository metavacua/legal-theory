# **The Constitutional Paradox of Section 230: A Legal Analysis of Intermediary Immunity and the Digital Public Sphere**

  
  

## **Introduction: The Twenty-Six Words That Shaped the Internet and the Constitutional Challenges They Provoke**

  

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act of 1996, often referred to as "the twenty-six words that created the internet," stands as the foundational legal pillar of the modern, user-centric digital world.1 Enacted when the commercial internet was in its infancy, this provision provides broad immunity to online platforms from liability for content generated by their users.4 This legal shield was not merely a technical adjustment to tort law; it was a deliberate policy choice that constructed an architecture of power, enabling the explosive growth of "interactive computer services" and the rise of dominant private platforms that now function as the de facto "modern public square".5

This report provides a comprehensive legal analysis of the multifaceted and often paradoxical constitutional questions that arise from Section 230's existence and its profound impact on public discourse. The central thesis of this analysis is that the primary constitutional battleground is not the text of Section 230 itself, but rather the legitimacy of state-level legislative attempts to regulate the powerful private actors the statute has empowered. This dynamic has created a profound legal inversion: Section 230, a law designed to foster a vibrant and competitive free market of ideas unfettered by government regulation, has inadvertently armed private platforms with the ability to wield the First Amendment as a shield to defend their own editorial discretion—a discretion that the statute itself incentivized.9

The resulting conflict is not a single, straightforward legal question but a complex landscape of competing constitutional claims. This report will map the full terrain of this conflict, beginning with the statutory and juridical architecture of Section 230 to establish its legislative purpose and judicial interpretation. It will then delve into the primary First Amendment collision, analyzing the Supreme Court's landmark decisions in *Moody v. NetChoice* and *NetChoice v. Paxton*, which have fundamentally reshaped the debate around content moderation and state power. The analysis will critically evaluate the legal theories that seek to re-characterize platforms as "common carriers" or "public forums" to justify regulation. Finally, the report will explore novel constitutional and statutory challenges, including arguments based on the Fifth Amendment's Takings Clause and an innovative theory rooted in California labor law that seeks to dismantle Section 230's protections by re-characterizing the factual relationship between platforms and their users. Through this exhaustive analysis, the report will illuminate the central constitutional paradox of Section 230: that a law intended to promote free expression has become the catalyst for a new and defining struggle over the constitutional limits of both corporate and state power in the digital age.

  

## **Part I: The Statutory and Juridical Architecture of Intermediary Immunity**

  

To comprehend the constitutional challenges surrounding Section 230, it is essential to first establish a definitive understanding of its legal framework, the historical context of its enactment, and the key judicial interpretations that have defined its scope. The statute was not created in a vacuum but as a direct legislative response to a specific and paradoxical legal problem that threatened to stifle the growth of the nascent internet.

  

### **Section 1.1: Legislative Intent and the "Moderator's Dilemma"**

  

In the early 1990s, the legal landscape for online intermediaries was governed by traditional common law principles of publisher liability, which proved ill-suited for the new digital environment.13 Under these principles, liability for defamatory or otherwise unlawful content depended on whether an entity was classified as a "publisher" or a "distributor." A publisher, like a newspaper, which exercises editorial control over its content, could be held strictly liable for republishing defamatory material. A distributor, like a newsstand or library, which merely circulates content created by others without review, could only be held liable if it knew or had reason to know of the content's unlawful nature.13

This distinction created a critical legal contradiction for online platforms, a problem that came to be known as the "moderator's dilemma," crystallized by two seminal court cases. The first, *Cubby, Inc. v. CompuServe Inc.* (1991), established that a hands-off approach to content provided a legal shield. The court found that CompuServe, which hosted a third-party forum without exercising editorial control, was a mere distributor and thus could not be held liable for defamatory content of which it was unaware.4 This ruling created a powerful incentive for platforms to adopt a completely passive role and avoid any form of content moderation.

The second and more consequential case, *Stratton Oakmont, Inc. v. Prodigy Services Co.* (1995), created the opposite incentive. Prodigy marketed itself as a family-friendly service and actively used screening software and human moderators to enforce content guidelines.15 A New York court ruled that by engaging in this moderation, Prodigy had assumed the role of a publisher and was therefore liable for defamatory statements posted by a user.15 The combined effect of

*Cubby* and *Stratton Oakmont* was a perverse legal trap: platforms that did nothing to police their content were protected, while those that made a good-faith effort to create a safer environment were exposed to greater liability.11

Congress recognized this "moderator's dilemma" as a significant threat to the internet's development. The legislative history of Section 230 makes clear that its primary purpose was to overturn the *Stratton Oakmont* decision and "remove disincentives for the development and utilization of blocking and filtering technologies".10 The goal was to encourage platforms to self-regulate and moderate objectionable content without fearing that such actions would transform them into publishers liable for all user-generated content.17

  

### **Section 1.2: Deconstructing the Immunity Shield**

  

Section 230 resolved the moderator's dilemma by creating a broad statutory immunity for online intermediaries. The law's core provisions are found in subsection (c).9

The first and most powerful provision, §230(c)(1), establishes the primary immunity shield. It states, "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider".12 This language effectively severs the link between a platform's role as a host and traditional publisher liability for third-party content.

The second provision, §230(c)(2), is the "Good Samaritan" clause, which more directly addresses the act of moderation. It protects platforms from liability for "any action voluntarily taken in good faith to restrict access to or availability of material that the provider or user considers to be obscene, lewd, lascivious, filthy, excessively violent, harassing, or otherwise objectionable, whether or not such material is constitutionally protected".4

The scope of this immunity is defined by two key statutory terms. An **"interactive computer service" (ICS)** is defined broadly as "any information service, system, or access software provider that provides or enables computer access by multiple users to a computer server".9 Courts have interpreted this definition expansively to cover not only social media giants like Meta and Google but also web hosting services, internet service providers, and even entities like libraries or employers that provide internet access.12 An

**"information content provider" (ICP)** is defined as any entity that is "responsible, in whole or in part, for the creation or development of information".12 The statute's central function is to immunize the ICS from liability for content created by the ICP.

The judiciary's interpretation of these provisions has been crucial in defining their power. The landmark ruling came in *Zeran v. America Online, Inc.* (1997), the first federal appellate court decision to interpret the law.17 In

*Zeran*, the Fourth Circuit held that §230(c)(1) provided sweeping immunity, barring a defamation claim against AOL even though the plaintiff had notified the company of the false and malicious content posted by a third party. The court ruled that the statute forbids "lawsuits seeking to hold a service provider liable for its exercise of a publisher's traditional editorial functions—such as deciding whether to publish, withdraw, postpone or alter content".12 This expansive interpretation established that the immunity was not contingent on a platform's ignorance of harmful content and effectively shielded the core editorial decisions of online intermediaries from most forms of civil liability.

  

### **Section 1.3: Judicial Limits on Immunity - The Material Contribution Test**

  

While *Zeran* established a broad shield, subsequent judicial decisions have carved out important, albeit narrow, limitations. The most significant of these is the "material contribution" test, which emerged from the Ninth Circuit's en banc decision in *Fair Housing Council of San Fernando Valley v. Roommates.com, LLC* (2008).22

In this case, the plaintiffs alleged that Roommates.com violated the federal Fair Housing Act (FHA) by requiring users to answer a questionnaire that included discriminatory preferences regarding sex, sexual orientation, and familial status.22 The platform argued that it was immune under Section 230 because the profiles were created by its users. The Ninth Circuit disagreed, creating a crucial distinction. The court found that while Roommates.com was immune for content posted by users in an open-ended "additional comments" section, it was

*not* immune for the content generated through its mandatory, multiple-choice questionnaire.22

The court's reasoning was that by designing a questionnaire with discriminatory options and requiring users to select from them, Roommates.com was not merely a passive host of third-party content. Instead, it had become "responsible, in whole or in part, for the creation or development" of the unlawful information, thereby making it an information content provider for that specific content.22 This established the "material contribution" test: a platform loses its Section 230 immunity if it "materially contribute\[s\] to \[the\] alleged unlawfulness" of the content it hosts.23 The

*Roommates.com* decision demonstrated that a single platform can simultaneously function as an immune ICS for some content (user-generated comments) and a liable ICP for other content (its own structured questionnaire).22 This judicial refinement created a critical safety valve, allowing courts to hold platforms accountable for their own architectural and design choices that facilitate illegality, without dismantling the broader immunity for hosting user speech that

*Zeran* had established.

## **Part II: The First Amendment Collision - Editorial Discretion vs. State Regulation**

  

The broad immunity granted by Section 230 fostered an environment where online platforms could develop and enforce their own content moderation policies without fear of being held liable as publishers. This, in turn, has given rise to the central constitutional conflict of the modern internet: the clash between the First Amendment rights of these private platforms to curate their content and the attempts by state governments to regulate that curation.

  

### **Section 2.1: Content Moderation as Constitutionally Protected Expressive Conduct**

  

The foundational principle undergirding the platforms' defense against state regulation is the First Amendment's protection of editorial discretion.11 The Supreme Court has long held that the act of publishing is an expressive activity. This includes not only the creation of original speech but also the selection, arrangement, and presentation of speech created by others. This right of editorial judgment protects a private entity's freedom to decide what speech to publish, what to prioritize, and what to remove.

Crucially, this protection extends to the right *not* to speak. The government generally cannot force a private actor to host or disseminate a message with which it disagrees.28 This principle of "compelled speech" is the core constitutional shield that social media platforms now wield against state laws that seek to dictate their content moderation policies.

  

### **Section 2.2: The** ***NetChoice*** **and** ***Moody*** **Revolution**

  

This constitutional conflict came to a head in a pair of cases challenging state laws in Texas and Florida. These laws, Texas's H.B. 20 and Florida's S.B. 7072, were enacted in response to allegations of anti-conservative bias and sought to prohibit large social media platforms from engaging in what the states termed "viewpoint-based censorship".30 The laws created a direct clash between the states' asserted power to ensure open discourse and the platforms' asserted First Amendment right to moderate their own sites.

The legal battle produced starkly conflicting rulings from the federal circuit courts. In *Moody v. NetChoice*, the Eleventh Circuit enjoined Florida's law, holding that it likely violated the First Amendment by restricting the platforms' exercise of "editorial judgment".33 In

*NetChoice v. Paxton*, the Fifth Circuit reached the opposite conclusion, upholding the Texas law. The court reasoned that platforms do not possess a "freewheeling" First Amendment right to censor what people say and that their content moderation activities constituted regulable "conduct," not protected "speech".29

In a unanimous 2024 ruling, the Supreme Court resolved this circuit split, fundamentally reshaping the legal landscape. Justice Elena Kagan's majority opinion did not decide the ultimate constitutionality of the laws but instead established the controlling legal principles for how such challenges must be analyzed. The Court's key holdings were twofold:

1.  **Content Moderation is Expressive Activity:** The Court decisively rejected the Fifth Circuit's reasoning and affirmed that content moderation is a form of expressive conduct protected by the First Amendment. Justice Kagan wrote that when a private entity engages in expressive activity, including "compiling and curating others' speech," government interference with that activity implicates the First Amendment.28 The Court explicitly stated that these principles "likely apply to the content moderation practices of social media platforms like Facebook's News Feed," providing a strong signal that the core functions of these platforms are constitutionally protected.28
2.  **A High Bar for Facial Challenges:** Despite this substantive victory for the platforms, the Court vacated and remanded both cases on procedural grounds. It held that the lower courts had failed to conduct a proper analysis of a *facial* challenge to the laws.29 A facial challenge, which seeks to invalidate a law in its entirety, is "disfavored" and requires the challenger to show that "a substantial number of \[the law's\] applications are unconstitutional" when measured against its legitimate applications.29 The Court found that the laws applied to a wide range of services beyond just curated news feeds—such as direct messaging, email, and e-commerce—some of which may not involve the same level of editorial discretion.36 This procedural ruling effectively shifts the legal battleground from broad facial challenges to more fact-intensive, "as-applied" challenges, where courts will have to analyze the constitutionality of the laws as they apply to specific platform functions one by one.35

This outcome represents a procedural victory for the states but a substantive one for the platforms. While the laws were not struck down, the Court's strong language affirming content moderation as protected expression provides a clear roadmap for how lower courts should analyze future as-applied challenges. This makes it highly probable that the core provisions of these state laws, as they apply to services like curated news feeds and recommendation algorithms, will ultimately be found unconstitutional.

  

### **Section 2.3: The Specter of Viewpoint Discrimination**

  

A central argument against the Texas and Florida laws is that they represent an unconstitutional form of viewpoint discrimination by the government itself. The First Amendment is particularly hostile to laws that regulate speech based on its message or perspective.38 Viewpoint discrimination occurs when the government does not just regulate a subject matter but targets "particular views taken by speakers on a subject" for disfavored treatment.38 The Supreme Court has called this an "egregious form of content discrimination" that is presumptively unconstitutional.38

The legislative history and stated purpose of the Texas and Florida laws reveal a clear intent to protect conservative viewpoints from what lawmakers perceived as biased moderation by "Big Tech".33 By enacting laws that prohibit platforms from removing content based on viewpoint, the states are not acting as neutral arbiters of a forum; they are actively taking sides in a political debate. They are using their regulatory power to compel private speakers (the platforms) to host and disseminate messages that align with the government's preferred ideology. This attempt to engineer a "balanced" marketplace of ideas by forcing private actors to carry speech they would otherwise reject is a form of government-mandated viewpoint expression that runs directly counter to core First Amendment principles.

## **Part III: Re-characterizing the Platform - The "Public Square" and "Common Carrier" Theories**

  

To justify state regulation of private platforms, proponents of laws like those in Texas and Florida have advanced legal theories designed to re-characterize these entities as something other than purely private speakers. The goal of these arguments is to circumvent the First Amendment's state action doctrine, which holds that constitutional speech protections apply to government action, not the decisions of private companies. If a platform can be legally redefined as a quasi-public entity, it can be subjected to regulations that would otherwise be unconstitutional.

  

### **Section 3.1: The "Modern Public Square" Analogy**

  

A common rhetorical and legal argument is that dominant social media platforms have become the "modern public square" and should therefore be treated as public forums, bound by First Amendment constraints against content discrimination.5 This analogy gained prominence after the Supreme Court, in

*Packingham v. North Carolina*, used the phrase to describe the internet's importance for civic discourse.

However, this argument is legally tenuous. While the Court has used the "public square" metaphor to emphasize the internet's role in speech, it has explicitly rejected the legal conclusion that private ownership of a forum for public speech transforms that entity into a state actor.8 In

*Manhattan Community Access Corp. v. Halleck*, the Court held that "merely hosting speech by others is not a traditional, exclusive public function and does not alone transform private entities into state actors subject to First Amendment constraints." The operation of a social media platform, no matter how large or influential, is not a power traditionally and exclusively reserved to the state. Therefore, under current doctrine, platforms remain private actors whose editorial decisions are protected *by* the First Amendment, not constrained by it.7

  

### **Section 3.2: The Common Carrier Doctrine**

  

A more legally sophisticated argument for regulating platforms is the common carrier doctrine. Historically, this doctrine has been applied to essential services that hold themselves out to the public, such as telegraphs, railroads, and telephone companies, subjecting them to duties of non-discrimination and open access.27 The theory, most notably advanced by Justice Clarence Thomas in a series of statements and concurrences, is that some digital platforms have achieved a level of market dominance and public necessity that makes them "sufficiently akin to common carriers".2 If so, they could be regulated to ensure they carry all lawful content without discrimination, much like a phone company must connect calls regardless of the speakers' viewpoints. The Fifth Circuit explicitly adopted this reasoning in its

*Paxton* decision, finding that social media platforms are communications firms that hold themselves out to serve the public and have an "effective monopoly" over their niche, justifying their regulation as common carriers.30

The counterarguments to this theory are substantial. First, platforms are not passive conduits like telephone lines; they are active curators of speech, constantly making editorial decisions about what to feature, prioritize, and recommend.33 This active curation distinguishes them from traditional utilities. Second, the common carrier doctrine historically applied to the

*transmission* of information from point A to point B, not to the *publication and arrangement* of content for a mass audience. Applying this doctrine to platforms would represent a significant expansion of its traditional scope.

  

### **Section 3.3: Constitutional Counterarguments - Compelled Speech**

  

Even if a court were to accept the common carrier analogy, imposing "must-carry" obligations on platforms would face a powerful First Amendment challenge under the compelled speech doctrine.28 The Supreme Court has repeatedly struck down laws that force private speakers to carry messages they wish to exclude.

In *Miami Herald Publishing Co. v. Tornillo*, the Court invalidated a Florida law that required newspapers to grant political candidates a "right of reply" to critical editorials, holding that any choice of material to be printed constitutes the exercise of editorial judgment, and the government cannot compel a newspaper to publish that which it believes should not be published.33 Similarly, in

*Hurley v. Irish-American Gay, Lesbian and Bisexual Group of Boston*, the Court ruled that parade organizers could not be forced to include a group whose message they did not wish to convey, as this would "alter the expressive content of the\[ \] parade".29 These cases stand for the core principle that the state cannot commandeer a private expressive enterprise to broadcast the government's preferred messages. A law forcing a social media platform to host all viewpoints would directly contravene this principle by altering the expressive compilation of speech that the platform chooses to present to its users.28

  

## **Part IV: Alternative Constitutional and Statutory Challenges**

  

While the First Amendment debate over content moderation has dominated the legal discourse, other constitutional and statutory theories offer alternative avenues for challenging platform power and, by extension, the scope of Section 230's immunity.

  

### **Section 4.1: The Fifth Amendment Front - Takings and Due Process**

  
  

#### **Sub-section 4.1.1: The Takings Clause**

  

A novel but potent constitutional argument against state "must-carry" laws can be found in the Fifth Amendment's Takings Clause, which states that private property shall not "be taken for public use, without just compensation".43 While typically applied to physical property, the doctrine of "regulatory takings" recognizes that a government regulation can go "too far" and effectively appropriate the value or use of property, thus requiring compensation.44

This theory can be applied to social media platforms by conceptualizing their digital infrastructure—their servers, proprietary code, and curated user interface—as a form of private property. A state law that compels a platform to host and disseminate unwanted content can be framed as a government-authorized physical invasion of that private digital property.46 The state is, in effect, commandeering the platform's servers and bandwidth to serve a public purpose: promoting a wider range of speech. Under this theory, such a regulatory mandate could constitute a per se compensable taking, reframing the debate from a violation of the platform's expressive rights to a seizure of its property rights.47 This provides an alternative constitutional basis for challenging state content moderation laws, one that is distinct from the First Amendment analysis.

  

#### **Sub-section 4.1.2: Procedural Due Process**

  

Another potential constitutional avenue is the Due Process Clause, which prohibits the government from depriving a person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. Users who are de-platformed or have their content removed might argue that they have a property or liberty interest in their social media accounts and that platforms, by acting as the modern public square, are quasi-state actors who must provide notice and an opportunity to be heard before terminating an account.

However, this line of argument faces the same significant hurdle as the "public square" theory: the state action doctrine.12 The Due Process Clause, like the First Amendment, applies to government action, not the decisions of private companies.4 As established in

*Halleck*, platforms are not state actors, and their moderation decisions are private actions not subject to constitutional due process requirements. While some legislative proposals, like the PACT Act, seek to impose procedural requirements on platforms as a condition of Section 230 immunity, a direct constitutional claim based on due process is a much weaker legal strategy.

  

### **Section 4.2: A Novel Statutory Attack - Misclassification and the Demolition of the Neutral Intermediary**

  

Perhaps the most innovative and potentially disruptive challenge to the current platform liability regime comes not from constitutional law, but from state-level labor and employment law.51 This theory, particularly potent under California's stringent worker classification statutes, seeks to render Section 230 inapplicable on purely factual grounds, thereby circumventing the entire constitutional debate.51

The legal argument proceeds through a series of logical steps rooted in the operational reality of many social media platforms:

1.  **Deputizing Moderators:** Platforms like Reddit and Facebook rely on a vast workforce of volunteer users to act as community moderators. These moderators are granted significant authority to enforce platform rules, remove content, and discipline or ban other users within their communities.51
2.  **Re-characterization as Supervisors:** Under California's rigid "ABC test" for worker classification, these moderators could be legally re-characterized as misclassified employees or, more specifically, as supervisors. A supervisor is defined as an individual with the authority to "discharge, assign, reward, or discipline other employees" or the "responsibility to direct them".51 A moderator who bans a user is functionally exercising the power to "discharge" them from the digital workspace. By approving or removing posts, they are "assigning" acceptable work and "directing" the creative output of others.51
3.  **The Platform as Active Manager:** If the moderators are deemed agents or employees of the platform, the platform's legal status fundamentally changes. It is no longer a passive, "neutral intermediary" merely hosting content from "another" information content provider. It becomes an "active manager of a digital workspace," using its own deputized workforce to curate and control the content environment.51
4.  **Dissolving Section 230 Immunity:** This factual re-characterization demolishes the legal predicate for Section 230 immunity. The shield protects an ICS from liability for content provided by *another* ICP. However, if the moderation and curation of content are being performed by the platform's own agents (the misclassified moderators), then the platform itself becomes "responsible, in whole or in part, for the... development" of the content on its site. It is no longer a neutral host but an active participant in content creation and development, stripping it of Section 230's protection in those contexts.51

This "bottom-up" statutory attack represents a paradigm shift. Instead of challenging the constitutionality of Section 230, it uses state law to alter the factual reality to which the federal statute applies. It is a potentially more disruptive legal strategy because it bypasses the high bars of constitutional litigation and focuses instead on the economic realities of platform labor—an area where platforms, especially those reliant on uncompensated user moderation, are highly vulnerable.

  

## **Part V: Synthesis, Conclusion, and Strategic Recommendations**

  

The legal and constitutional landscape surrounding Section 230 is defined by a series of complex and often paradoxical tensions. The statute, designed to foster a free and open internet, has instead become a catalyst for profound constitutional conflicts over the nature of speech, property, and power in the digital age. Synthesizing the preceding analysis reveals the core dynamics of this conflict and points toward the future trajectory of platform regulation.

  

### **Section 5.1: The Unconstitutionality Paradox**

  

The central paradox of the Section 230 debate is this: while the federal statute itself is likely constitutional, state laws designed to counteract its effects by regulating platform content moderation are themselves likely *unconstitutional*. The First Amendment, as interpreted by the Supreme Court, protects the editorial discretion of private platforms. State laws that attempt to compel these platforms to host speech against their will, particularly when motivated by viewpoint-based considerations, are an infringement on this protected expressive activity. Therefore, Section 230 is not, in itself, unconstitutional; rather, it is a *catalyst* for constitutional conflict, having created a class of powerful private speakers whose subsequent exercise of their First Amendment rights is now being challenged by state governments.

  

### **Section 5.2: The Future of Platform Regulation After** ***NetChoice***

  

The Supreme Court's landmark ruling in *Moody v. NetChoice* and *NetChoice v. Paxton* has set the course for the next phase of platform regulation. By setting a high bar for facial challenges while strongly affirming that content moderation is expressive conduct, the Court has shifted the legal battleground. The future will likely see less emphasis on broad, sweeping attempts to invalidate state laws in their entirety and more on targeted, "as-applied" litigation.35 These cases will force courts to conduct a granular analysis of specific platform functions, grappling with difficult questions about which activities—from curated news feeds and recommendation algorithms to direct messaging and e-commerce listings—constitute protected expression and which might be subject to regulation as non-expressive conduct.

In the legislative arena, the constitutional constraints clarified by the Court will likely push lawmakers away from content-based mandates. Instead, reform efforts may coalesce around process- and transparency-oriented proposals, such as the Platform Accountability and Consumer Transparency (PACT) Act. Such legislation seeks to condition Section 230 immunity not on the substance of moderation decisions, but on requirements for platforms to provide clear terms of service, implement fair and transparent appeals processes, and report on their moderation activities.52 This approach represents a potential middle ground that respects the platforms' editorial rights while increasing their accountability to users.

  

### **Section 5.3: Strategic Considerations for Stakeholders**

  

The evolving legal landscape presents distinct strategic imperatives for the key actors involved in the Section 230 debate.

  - **For Policymakers:** The *NetChoice* decision serves as a clear constitutional warning. Future legislative efforts to regulate platforms should pivot away from attempts to control protected *expression* (i.e., content moderation decisions) and focus instead on regulating unprotected *conduct*. More constitutionally durable avenues for regulation include strengthening consumer protection laws, enhancing data privacy requirements, and applying antitrust principles to address the market power of dominant platforms. The PACT Act's model of conditioning immunity on procedural transparency, rather than content mandates, offers a more viable path forward.54
  - **For Platforms:** The primary legal strategy must now be twofold. First, platforms must prepare for a new wave of as-applied First Amendment litigation by developing a robust factual and legal record to defend the expressive nature of their various services and functions. Second, they must address the significant latent risk posed by the worker misclassification theory. This requires a thorough review of their relationships with volunteer moderators and other user contributors, particularly in states with stringent labor laws like California, to mitigate the risk of a legal re-characterization that could undermine their Section 230 protections.
  - **For Civil Liberties Advocates:** The current legal environment requires a continued nuanced and sophisticated approach. Organizations like the ACLU have defended Section 230 as a vital shield for user speech and a bulwark against government censorship, while simultaneously challenging platform business practices that lead to unlawful discrimination.55 This dual strategy remains the most effective path. It involves defending the core immunity for hosting third-party speech against unconstitutional state intrusion while supporting targeted regulations aimed at harmful platform conduct—such as discriminatory ad-targeting or anticompetitive behavior—that falls outside the scope of protected editorial expression.
