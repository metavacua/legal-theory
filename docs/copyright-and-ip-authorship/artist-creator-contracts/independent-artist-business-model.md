# **A Minimalist, Legally Defensible Model for the Patron-Artist Relationship in California**

  
  

## **Introduction**

  

This report constructs a legally compliant, low-overhead model for a patron-artist relationship in the state of California. The framework is specifically designed for the scenario of an individual patron commissioning an unincorporated artist who operates as a sole proprietor from a residential location. The primary objective is to delineate the absolute minimum requirements necessary for the artist to be correctly classified as an independent contractor, thereby navigating the state's complex and stringent employment laws.

The central legal challenge addressed herein is California Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5), a landmark piece of legislation that established a default presumption of employee status for all workers. This presumption imposes significant administrative and financial burdens—such as payroll taxes, workers' compensation insurance, and wage-and-hour compliance—that are often untenable for the small-scale, project-based engagements characteristic of the arts and culture sector.1 The ambiguity and perceived complexity of these regulations have created considerable friction and uncertainty for both independent creators and the patrons who wish to support them.

The solution presented in this report is a multi-layered strategic model built upon three core components. First, it leverages the specific "fine artist" exemption codified in Assembly Bill 2257 (AB 2257), which provides a crucial pathway to bypass the rigid ABC test in favor of the more flexible, multi-factor *Borello* standard for determining worker classification.2 Second, it implements a robust yet streamlined contract architecture, utilizing a Master Service Agreement (MSA) and Statement of Work (SOW) system to establish and maintain a clear, defensible independent contractor relationship.3 Third, it provides an actionable checklist of non-negotiable administrative and compliance steps that, while minimal, are essential for the legal integrity of the model. By adhering to this framework, patrons and artists can engage in creative commissions with legal clarity, mutual protection, and minimal administrative overhead.

  

## **Section 1: The Foundation of the Agreement: Contract Law in California**

  

Before addressing the complexities of employment classification, any patron-artist relationship must be established upon the bedrock of a valid, enforceable contract. California law, while generally permissive of various contract forms, imposes specific requirements that make a formal written agreement not merely a best practice but a legal necessity for the model in question. The convergence of foundational contract principles, the state's Statute of Frauds, and new freelance-specific legislation creates a clear mandate for a documented agreement.

  

### **Subsection 1.1: The Four Pillars of a Valid Contract**

  

Under the California Civil Code, the existence of any valid contract hinges on the presence of four essential elements.4 The proposed model is architected to ensure each of these pillars is firmly established.

1.  **Parties Capable of Contracting (Capacity):** The law requires that all parties to a contract have the legal capacity to enter into an agreement. This generally means they are of legal age and sound mind.3 The Master Service Agreement (MSA) component of this model explicitly addresses this by identifying the full legal names of the patron and the artist and affirming that both are legally capable of entering the binding relationship.
2.  **Consent (Mutual Assent):** A contract requires a "meeting of the minds," where both parties knowingly and willingly agree to the same terms.6 The MSA serves to document this mutual assent to the overarching legal framework of the relationship, while each individual Statement of Work (SOW) documents consent to the specific terms of a particular commission.3 The act of signing both documents provides clear, irrefutable evidence of this consent.
3.  **A Lawful Object:** The subject of the contract must be legal. A contract to create an illegal item or perform an illegal service is void.5 In the context of artistic commissions, the "lawful object" is the artwork itself. The SOW provides a detailed description of the commissioned piece, ensuring its legality. Furthermore, as analyzed in a subsequent section, a contract to create a work of "fine art" is, by its nature, a contract for an object with "serious artistic value," a standard that inherently protects it from being classified as obscene and thus ensures its status as a lawful object under the  
    *Miller v. California* test.7
4.  **A Sufficient Cause or Consideration:** A contract must involve an exchange of value between the parties. This does not have to be monetary; an exchange of promises or services can suffice.6 The SOW meticulously defines the consideration: for the artist, it is their labor, skill, and the intellectual property embodied in the final artwork; for the patron, it is the payment of fees and any other agreed-upon compensation.3

By structuring the relationship around an MSA and SOW, all four pillars of a valid California contract are systematically and explicitly satisfied, creating a legally sound foundation before any work begins.8

  

### **Subsection 1.2: The Statute of Frauds and the Imperative of a Written Agreement**

  

While many oral contracts can be enforceable in California, the state's Statute of Frauds, codified in Civil Code § 1624, renders certain types of agreements invalid unless they are memorialized in writing and signed by the party against whom enforcement is sought.10 This statute exists to prevent fraudulent claims based on purported oral agreements in high-stakes situations.11

Two provisions are particularly relevant to artistic commissions. First, the statute applies to any "agreement that by its terms is not to be performed within a year from the making thereof".10 While a single, short-term art commission might be completed in less than a year, establishing an ongoing patron-artist relationship or commissioning a complex piece could easily extend beyond this timeframe, triggering the writing requirement. Relying on an oral agreement is legally perilous, as its terms are notoriously difficult to prove in court.13

Second, and more directly applicable, is the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), which has been adopted into California law. The UCC's own statute of frauds requires a written contract for the sale of "goods" for a price of $500 or more.16 A commissioned piece of tangible art, such as a painting or sculpture, is legally considered a "good".18 Therefore, any commission valued at $500 or more falls squarely under this rule and mandates a written agreement to be enforceable. Given the value of an artist's labor and materials, the vast majority of non-trivial commissions will exceed this threshold.

  

### **Subsection 1.3: The Freelance Worker Protection Act (SB 988): A New Mandate**

  

Any remaining ambiguity regarding the necessity of a written contract is eliminated by the Freelance Worker Protection Act (FWPA), a new law effective January 1, 2025.19 This legislation provides broad new protections for independent contractors and imposes clear, non-negotiable requirements on hiring entities.

The FWPA mandates a written contract for any freelance engagement where the services are valued at $250 or more, either in a single project or in aggregate over the preceding 120 days.21 This low threshold effectively makes written agreements a legal requirement for nearly all professional artistic commissions in California.

Furthermore, the law specifies the minimum terms that must be included in the contract 20:

  - The name and address of both the freelance worker and the hiring party.
  - An itemized list of the services to be provided.
  - The rate and method of compensation.
  - The date on which payment is due, or the mechanism by which the due date will be determined.

The law also stipulates that payment must be made by the date specified in the contract or, if no date is specified, within 30 days of the completion of services.20 These requirements align perfectly with the proposed SOW structure, which is designed to capture precisely this information for each project. The combined effect of the common law contract pillars, the Statute of Frauds, the UCC, and the FWPA creates a "triple mandate" for a written agreement. It is no longer a matter of best practice but an absolute legal prerequisite for establishing a defensible patron-artist relationship in California.

  

## **Section 2: Navigating Employment Classification: The AB 5, AB 2257, and Borello Framework**

  

The most significant legal hurdle for any entity engaging an individual for services in California is the state's stringent worker classification law. Assembly Bill 5 (AB 5), and its subsequent clarifying legislation, AB 2257, established a new paradigm that presumes all workers are employees. Understanding how to navigate this framework is the central strategic challenge for creating a minimalist and legally compliant patron-artist model. The goal is not to circumvent the law, but to understand and meet the specific criteria the law provides for a legitimate independent contractor relationship.

  

### **Subsection 2.1: The Default Presumption: Understanding the Strict ABC Test**

  

Effective January 1, 2020, AB 5 codified the California Supreme Court's decision in *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court*, establishing the "ABC test" as the default standard for determining worker classification for most purposes under the California Labor Code and Unemployment Insurance Code.23 This test begins with the strong legal presumption that a worker is an employee.23

To rebut this presumption and classify a worker as an independent contractor, the hiring entity bears the burden of proving that **all three** of the following conditions are met 23:

  - **(A) The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity** in connection with the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact.
  - **(B) The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business.**
  - **(C) The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business** of the same nature as the work performed.

The failure to satisfy even one of these prongs results in the worker being classified as an employee by default.25 For many patrons or arts organizations, prong (B) presents an insurmountable obstacle. If a patron's "business" is collecting art, or an arts nonprofit's mission is to foster new art, then the work performed by a commissioned artist could be construed as being within the "usual course" of that business, causing the test to fail. This strict, all-or-nothing standard makes it imperative to find a statutory exemption.

  

### **Subsection 2.2: The "Fine Artist" Exemption: The Primary Pathway to Independent Contractor Status**

  

In response to concerns from numerous industries, including the arts, the legislature passed AB 2257, which amended AB 5 and expanded the list of occupations and professional services that are exempt from the ABC test. For these exempt categories, worker classification is instead determined by the more flexible, multi-factor test established in *S.G. Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations* (the "*Borello* test").2

Among the "professional services" exemptions is a specific carve-out for a **"fine artist"**. AB 2257 provides a statutory definition for this term, clarifying who may qualify for the exemption:

A "fine artist" is an individual who creates works of art to be appreciated primarily or solely for their imaginative, aesthetic, or intellectual content, including drawings, paintings, sculptures, mosaics, works of calligraphy, works of graphic art, crafts, or mixed media.

This definition is broad enough to cover the majority of artists engaged in typical commission scenarios, making it the primary legal pathway for the proposed model.

  

### **Subsection 2.3: Meeting the Bare Minimum: The Six Conditions for the Exemption**

  

Crucially, the "fine artist" exemption is not automatic. To qualify for the exemption and have the relationship evaluated under the *Borello* test, the hiring entity must demonstrate that the artist meets a series of six specific conditions. These conditions represent the "bare minimum" requirements that the artist must satisfy to operate as a legitimate independent business in the eyes of the state.

|  |  |
| :-: | :-: |
| Requirement | Actionable Step for the Artist |
| 1\\. \*\*Maintains a business location separate from the hiring entity.\*\* | The artist's home studio, even if in a parent's house, satisfies this requirement. The location must be distinct from the patron's location. |
| 2\\. \*\*Has a business license, in addition to any required professional licenses or permits.\*\* | The artist must apply for and obtain a business license if one is required by their local jurisdiction (e.g., the City of Chico). This is a non-negotiable prerequisite. |
| 3\\. \*\*Has the ability to set or negotiate their own rates for the services performed.\*\* | The artist must have the freedom to determine their own pricing structure, which should be reflected in the SOW for each commission. |
| 4\\. \*\*Has the ability to set their own hours, outside of project completion dates and reasonable business hours.\*\* | The artist must control their own work schedule, free from a mandated 9-to-5-style requirement from the patron. |
| 5\\. \*\*Is customarily engaged in an independently established business of the same nature as the work performed.\*\* | The artist must hold themselves out to the public as being in the business of creating art. This can be demonstrated through a portfolio, website, or by contracting with other clients. |
| 6\\. \*\*Customarily and regularly exercises discretion and independent judgment in the performance of the services.\*\* | The artist must have creative control over their work, consistent with the nature of being a skilled professional, rather than simply following rote instructions. |

The user's initial premise of engaging an artist without a business license is fundamentally incompatible with the legal structure that provides the desired flexibility. The business license is not a superfluous piece of bureaucracy to be sidestepped; it is the statutory key that unlocks the "fine artist" exemption. Without satisfying this and the other five conditions, the relationship defaults back to the strict ABC test, which it would almost certainly fail, resulting in worker misclassification and significant legal liability for the patron. Therefore, the "bare minimum" for this model is not to do nothing, but to do precisely what the exemption statute demands.

  

### **Subsection 2.4: Application of the Borello Test: A Multi-Factor Analysis**

  

Successfully meeting the six conditions for the "fine artist" exemption does not automatically classify the artist as an independent contractor. Instead, it earns the patron and artist the right to have their relationship evaluated under the older, more holistic *Borello* standard.2

Unlike the rigid, pass/fail nature of the ABC test, the *Borello* test is a multi-factor "economic realities" test that weighs numerous considerations to determine the true nature of the working relationship.27 No single factor is controlling; rather, a court or agency looks at the totality of the circumstances.24 The most significant factor is whether the hiring entity has the

**right to control the manner and means** of accomplishing the desired result, not just the result itself.29

Other key factors considered under *Borello* include:

  - Whether the worker is engaged in a distinct occupation or business.
  - Whether the worker or the hiring entity supplies the instrumentalities, tools, and the place of work.
  - The worker's investment in the equipment or materials required for their task.
  - The special skill required in the occupation.
  - The worker's opportunity for profit or loss depending on their managerial skill.
  - The degree of permanence of the working relationship.
  - The method of payment (by time or by the job).
  - The belief of the parties regarding the nature of the relationship.

The strategic goal of the entire model is to structure the relationship in such a way that it clearly satisfies the six prerequisites for the exemption and then overwhelmingly weighs in favor of independent contractor status under the *Borello* factors.

## **Section 3: A Practical Contract Architecture: The MSA and SOW**

  

With a clear understanding of California's employment classification laws, the next step is to build a contractual framework that is both efficient for small-scale commissions and legally robust. A Master Service Agreement (MSA) combined with a Statement of Work (SOW) for each project provides the ideal architecture. This structure separates the overarching legal relationship from the specifics of individual projects, creating clarity, reducing administrative friction, and proactively building a defensible record of the artist's independent status.

  

### **Subsection 3.1: The Master Service Agreement (MSA): Establishing the Independent Relationship**

  

The MSA is the foundational document that governs the long-term relationship between the patron and the artist. Its primary purpose is not to detail any specific artwork but to establish the "rules of engagement" and memorialize the artist's status as an independent contractor.31

The MSA is a strategic tool for satisfying the *Borello* test. It should contain explicit clauses that mirror the *Borello* factors, creating a primary piece of evidence to defend the artist's classification. Key clauses in the MSA should state that the artist 31:

  - **Is an Independent Contractor:** An explicit declaration that the relationship is that of a hiring entity and an independent contractor, not an employer and employee.
  - **Controls Manner and Means:** A statement that the artist retains full control over the manner and means by which the services are performed and the artwork is created.
  - **Supplies Own Tools and Workspace:** An acknowledgment that the artist will use their own tools, equipment, and supplies, and will perform the work at their own location (e.g., their home studio).
  - **Is Responsible for Taxes:** A clause making it clear that the artist is solely responsible for paying all their own federal and state income taxes, self-employment taxes, and that the patron will not withhold any taxes from payments.
  - **Is Free to Work for Others:** A non-exclusivity clause confirming that the artist is free to offer their services to other clients and is not restricted from working for competitors.
  - **Bears Risk of Profit and Loss:** A statement that the artist is responsible for their own business expenses and bears the risk of profit or loss associated with their enterprise.

By signing an MSA with these terms, both parties formally acknowledge and agree to a relationship structure that aligns with the legal requirements for an independent contractor under *Borello*.

  

### **Subsection 3.2: The Statement of Work (SOW): Defining Each Commission**

  

While the MSA governs the relationship, the SOW governs the project. For each new commission, the parties will execute a simple, project-specific SOW that incorporates the terms of the MSA by reference.31 This document contains the negotiable, dynamic details of the work.

An SOW for an artistic commission should include the following essential elements 31:

  - **Reference to MSA:** A clear statement that the SOW is governed by the terms of the pre-existing and executed MSA.
  - **Description of Work (The Lawful Object):** A detailed description of the artwork to be created, including subject matter, medium, dimensions, style, and any other relevant creative specifications.
  - **Deliverables and Timeline:** A clear outline of the final deliverables, key milestones, and the final delivery date.
  - **Compensation (The Consideration):** The total fee for the project, the payment schedule (e.g., 50% upfront, 50% on completion), and any provisions for expense reimbursement. This section must also comply with the new Freelance Worker Protection Act by specifying a payment due date.20
  - **Intellectual Property Terms:** The specific terms governing copyright for the project, explicitly referencing the chosen model (e.g., Limited License or Option-to-Purchase).

  

### **Subsection 3.3: Ensuring Clarity and Managing Risk**

  

The MSA/SOW structure provides significant advantages for a recurring patron-artist relationship. Once the MSA is negotiated and signed, initiating new projects becomes highly efficient; the parties only need to agree on a brief SOW without revisiting the core legal terms.31 This reduces legal friction and costs, making it ideal for the low-overhead model requested.

To manage potential inconsistencies, the MSA should include a "conflict" or "order of precedence" clause. This clause typically states that if there is a conflict between the terms of the MSA and a specific SOW, the terms of the SOW will prevail for that particular project.31 This allows for necessary project-specific flexibility without undermining the foundational legal relationship established in the MSA.

  

## **Section 4: A Strategic Approach to Intellectual Property**

  

The management of intellectual property (IP) is a critical component of any artistic commission. The default rules under U.S. copyright law, combined with the specific hazards of California's labor laws, necessitate a deliberate and strategic approach. This section outlines three models for handling IP, recommending one as the standard for its legal soundness and mutual protection, and issuing a strong warning against a commonly used but legally perilous alternative.

  

### **Subsection 4.1: The Default Rule and the Limited License (Model 1)**

  

Under the U.S. Copyright Act, the creator of an original work is the initial owner of the copyright from the moment the work is fixed in a tangible medium.33 The act of commissioning a work does not automatically transfer copyright ownership to the patron. In the absence of a written agreement stating otherwise, the artist retains full copyright to the commissioned piece.35

The simplest IP model, therefore, is to adhere to this default and formalize it with a **Limited License**. In this scenario, the artist retains full copyright ownership and grants the patron a narrow, non-exclusive license for a specific, defined use. For example, the SOW could grant the patron a license "for personal, non-commercial display within a private residence only".3 This model is ideal for initial, low-stakes "probe" commissions where the patron wishes to assess an artist's talent with minimal financial and legal commitment.38

  

### **Subsection 4.2: The Option-to-Purchase Agreement (Model 2 - Recommended Standard)**

  

For commissions where the patron's goal is to acquire full ownership of both the physical artwork and its underlying copyright, the **Option-to-Purchase Agreement** is the most legally sophisticated and recommended model.3 This structure, adapted from best practices in the film and entertainment industries, provides a secure and enforceable mechanism for transferring rights to an asset that does not yet exist.40

The agreement is deconstructed into two parts within a single SOW 40:

1.  **The Option:** The patron makes an initial, non-refundable payment known as the **Option Fee**. This fee is not a deposit on the artwork itself; it is the legal consideration paid in exchange for securing an exclusive and irrevocable right—the option—to purchase the full copyright of the completed artwork at a pre-agreed price.40 This fee typically represents 25-50% of the total project cost. The SOW defines the  
    **Option Period**, which is the timeframe during which the artist will create the work and the patron holds this exclusive right.3
2.  **The Purchase:** The pre-agreed total cost for the artwork and its copyright is defined as the **Purchase Price**. The patron **exercises the option** by formally accepting the delivered artwork and paying the remaining balance of the Purchase Price (i.e., Purchase Price minus the Option Fee). This action triggers the "purchase" part of the agreement, and a pre-written copyright assignment clause within the SOW becomes effective at that moment.40

This structure is legally superior because the initial contract is for a present, enforceable right (the option), not for a future, non-existent asset (the copyright). It also provides mutual protection:

  - **Patron Protection:** If the final artwork is unsatisfactory, the patron can decline to exercise the option and is not obligated to make the final payment. Their only loss is the Option Fee.3
  - **Artist Protection:** If the patron declines the work, the artist retains the non-refundable Option Fee as compensation for their labor and materials up to that point. This functions as a built-in **"kill fee"**.42 The artist also retains the artwork and all rights to it, free to sell it elsewhere.

  

### **Subsection 4.3: The Work-for-Hire Trap (Model 3 - A Cautionary Analysis)**

  

A "Work Made for Hire" (WFH) agreement is a common but profoundly misunderstood and misused tool in creative commissions. Under federal copyright law, a work can only be a WFH under two very specific conditions: (1) it is created by an employee within the scope of their employment, or (2) it is a specially commissioned work that falls into one of **nine narrow statutory categories** and is accompanied by a written agreement signed by both parties stating it is a WFH.36

The nine categories are: a contribution to a collective work, part of a motion picture or other audiovisual work, a translation, a supplementary work, a compilation, an instructional text, a test, answer material for a test, or an atlas.36 A typical commissioned painting, drawing, or sculpture

**does not fit into any of these categories**. Consequently, a WFH clause in a contract for such a work is legally ineffective under federal law for its intended purpose of transferring copyright. The correct legal mechanism is a **copyright assignment**, as utilized in the Option-to-Purchase model.

More dangerously, in California, using a WFH agreement with an individual creator triggers a specific and perilous legal trap. California Unemployment Insurance Code §686 and Labor Code §3351.5(c) create a legal presumption that any individual who signs a WFH agreement is a **statutory employee** for purposes of workers' compensation and unemployment insurance.47 This means that by using a WFH contract, a patron inadvertently reclassifies the artist as an employee, triggering all the attendant legal obligations and liabilities that this entire model is designed to avoid. Using a WFH agreement with an individual artist in California is not only legally incorrect for copyright purposes but is also a direct path to worker misclassification.

  

### **Subsection 4.4: Moral Rights and the Optional Waiver**

  

Separate from the economic rights of copyright are an artist's **Moral Rights**, which protect the artist's personal connection to their work. These are primarily established in California by the California Art Preservation Act (CAPA) and at the federal level by the Visual Artists Rights Act (VARA).49 They consist of:

  - **The Right of Attribution:** The right to be identified as the creator of the work, or to disclaim authorship if the work is distorted.
  - **The Right of Integrity:** The right to prevent any intentional defacement, mutilation, alteration, or destruction of the work.

These rights are retained by the artist even after the physical artwork is sold and the copyright is assigned.52 However, unlike in many other countries, moral rights in the United States can be waived through an express written instrument signed by the artist.54 For patrons who require absolute control over the physical artwork—for example, if it is to be integrated into a building where it might one day need to be altered or removed—it is advisable to include a clear, explicit, and separate

**Waiver of Moral Rights** clause in the SOW.

| :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| \*\*IP Management Model\*\* | \*\*Copyright Owner\*\* | \*\*Best Use Case\*\* | \*\*Risk to Patron\*\* | \*\*Risk to Artist\*\* | \*\*California Legal Risk\*\* |
| \*\*Limited License\*\* | Artist | Initial "probe" commissions; non-commercial use. | Low. Cannot commercially exploit the work. | Low. Retains full control of IP for future licensing. | \*\*Minimal.\*\* |
| \*\*Option-to-Purchase / Assignment\*\* | Artist initially, transfers to Patron upon final payment. | Acquiring full commercial rights to a new, custom artwork. | Low. Can reject final work, losing only the option fee. | Low. Guaranteed compensation (kill fee) if work is rejected. | \*\*Minimal.\*\* This is the legally correct and safest method. |

## **Section 5: Minimum Viable Compliance: Administrative and Logistical Imperatives**

  

Beyond the contract, maintaining a legally defensible independent contractor relationship requires adherence to certain non-negotiable administrative and logistical steps. These actions are not optional bureaucratic hurdles; they are essential components of the evidence needed to prove that the artist is, in fact, operating an independent business. This section details the minimum viable compliance actions related to business licensing, tax reporting, and record keeping.

  

### **Subsection 5.1: The Business License: A Non-Negotiable Prerequisite**

  

As established in Section 2, a core requirement of the AB 2257 "fine artist" exemption is that the artist "has a business license" if one is required by their local jurisdiction. For an artist operating from a residence in Chico, California, this involves a two-step process.

1.  **Home Occupation Permit (HOP):** The first step is to apply for a HOP with the City of Chico Planning Division. A home occupation is defined as a limited business activity in a residential zone that is secondary to the residential use.56 The application process requires the artist to agree to a set of operating standards, such as limiting signage, traffic, and noise, and ensuring the business does not alter the residential character of the property.57 A fee is required for the permit application.59
2.  **City Business License:** A City of Chico Business License cannot be issued for a home-based business until the HOP application has been reviewed and approved by the Planning Division.56 Once the HOP is cleared, the artist must apply for the business license through the Finance Department.

A potential point of confusion arises from the City of Chico's business license tax application, which states that if a business's estimated annual gross receipts are $5,000 or less, the business is not required to pay a license tax, and the City will not *issue* a license.60 This presents a paradox: how can an artist obtain a license that the city will not issue, yet is required for the AB 2257 exemption? The solution lies in the act of compliance itself. The artist

*must* still complete and submit the Home Occupation Permit and Business License applications. The official response from the city—whether it is an issued license or a formal determination that no license is required due to the low income threshold—serves as the necessary proof. This documented interaction demonstrates that the artist has complied with the jurisdiction's rules, thereby satisfying the "has a business license" condition of the state exemption law.

  

### **Subsection 5.2: The Administrative Threshold: "Substantial" vs. "Insubstantial" Commissions**

  

A key innovation for a minimalist model is to classify commissions based on whether they trigger mandatory government reporting. This creates a practical distinction between "substantial" and "insubstantial" engagements, allowing the patron to apply administrative rigor only when legally required.3

The defining line is the IRS reporting threshold for Form 1099-NEC, "Nonemployee Compensation." A commission becomes **substantial** when the total payments from a single patron to a single artist meet or exceed this threshold in a calendar year.

  - **For tax year 2025:** The threshold is **$600**.61
  - **Effective January 1, 2026:** The threshold will increase significantly to **$2,000** and will be indexed for inflation in subsequent years.61

When a relationship crosses into "substantial" territory, the patron has a legal obligation to:

1.  Obtain a completed Form W-9, "Request for Taxpayer Identification Number and Certification," from the artist before making payment.
2.  Issue a Form 1099-NEC to the artist by January 31 of the following year, reporting the total payments made.
3.  File a copy of the Form 1099-NEC with the IRS.

For **insubstantial** commissions (total payments below the threshold), these reporting requirements do not apply, reducing the administrative burden. However, it is critical for both parties to track all payments regardless of amount for their own tax and accounting purposes.63 The act of correctly issuing a 1099-NEC is powerful evidence that the patron is treating the artist as a separate business entity, reinforcing the independent contractor classification under both federal tax law and the state-level

*Borello* analysis.

  

### **Subsection 5.3: Essential Record Keeping for Maintaining Independent Status**

  

To substantiate their status as an independent business, the artist must maintain basic but consistent records. This is not only crucial for tax purposes but also serves as evidence for the "independently established business" prong of both the ABC and *Borello* tests.24 A minimalist yet effective record-keeping system includes:

  - **A Separate Business Bank Account:** All business-related income and expenses should flow through a dedicated bank account, separate from personal finances. This is the simplest and most effective way to demonstrate a distinct business operation.64
  - **Income and Expense Tracking:** Maintain a simple ledger or spreadsheet to track all income from commissions and all business-related expenses (e.g., art supplies, software, marketing costs, business license fees).66 Digital receipts should be saved in organized folders.64
  - **Contract and Invoice File:** Keep digital or physical copies of all signed MSAs, SOWs, and invoices issued to clients. This documentation is the primary record of the business's professional engagements.68
  - **Public-Facing Portfolio:** Maintain an online portfolio (e.g., a simple website or a professional social media profile) that showcases their work and advertises their services to the public. This is direct evidence that the artist is "holding themselves out" as an independent business available to multiple potential clients, a key factor in the *Borello* analysis.24

These simple administrative actions, when performed consistently, create a coherent and defensible narrative. The local business license establishes the business's legitimacy under state law, the patron's adherence to federal 1099 rules affirms their treatment of the artist as a contractor, and the artist's own record keeping provides the final, crucial evidence of an independently operating enterprise.

  

## **Section 6: The Complete Model: A Step-by-Step Implementation Guide**

  

This section synthesizes the legal principles and practical requirements detailed in this report into a single, chronological workflow. It provides a step-by-step guide for a patron to follow when commissioning an individual artist in California under this minimalist, legally defensible model.

Step 1: Initial Vetting and Qualification

Before engaging the artist, the patron should conduct informal due diligence to confirm the artist is positioned to qualify for the "fine artist" exemption under AB 2257. This involves verifying that the artist:

  - Operates from a distinct business location (e.g., a home studio).
  - Possesses, or is in the process of obtaining, a local business license and/or Home Occupation Permit as required by their city or county.
  - Maintains a public-facing portfolio (website, social media) that demonstrates they are "customarily engaged" in the business of creating art for the public.

Step 2: Execute the Master Service Agreement (MSA)

Once the artist is vetted, the first formal action is for both parties to sign the MSA. This foundational document establishes the overarching independent contractor relationship. It should contain clauses explicitly addressing the Borello factors, such as the artist's control over their work, responsibility for their own tools and taxes, and freedom to work for other clients. This agreement is signed once and governs all future projects.

Step 3: Define the Project in a Statement of Work (SOW)

For the specific commission, the patron and artist collaborate on and sign a project-specific SOW. This document should:

  - Explicitly state that it is governed by the terms of the signed MSA.
  - Detail the scope of the artwork (subject, size, medium).
  - Outline the timeline and deliverables.
  - Structure the IP transfer using the **Option-to-Purchase model**, defining the non-refundable Option Fee and the total Purchase Price.
  - Specify the payment schedule and due dates in compliance with the Freelance Worker Protection Act.
  - Include an optional Waiver of Moral Rights clause if needed.

Step 4: Initial Payment (The Option Fee)

In accordance with the SOW, the patron makes the initial, non-refundable payment of the Option Fee. This payment secures the patron's exclusive right to purchase the final artwork and its copyright upon completion.

Step 5: The Creation Period

The artist proceeds with creating the artwork, maintaining creative control over the "manner and means" of their process. The artist may provide progress updates as mutually agreed upon in the SOW.

Step 6: Delivery and Review

The artist delivers the completed artwork to the patron for final review, as stipulated in the SOW.

Step 7: Final Payment (Exercising the Option)

Upon satisfaction with the final piece, the patron formally accepts the work and makes the final payment (the Purchase Price minus the already-paid Option Fee). This act constitutes the "exercising of the option."

Step 8: Automatic Transfer of Intellectual Property

As per the terms of the Option-to-Purchase agreement in the SOW, the receipt of the final payment by the artist automatically triggers the assignment of the full copyright to the patron. No separate assignment document is needed, as the clause is pre-written into the SOW. Any waived moral rights also become effective at this time.

Step 9: Year-End Administrative Compliance

The patron must track all payments made to the artist throughout the calendar year. If the total payments meet or exceed the IRS 1099-NEC reporting threshold ($600 for 2025; $2,000 for 2026 and beyond), the patron must:

  - Ensure they have a valid Form W-9 on file from the artist.
  - Issue a Form 1099-NEC to the artist by January 31 of the following year.
  - File the form with the IRS.

By following these nine steps, the parties create a comprehensive paper trail that documents a professional, compliant, and legally defensible independent contractor relationship from inception to completion.

  

## **Conclusion**

  

This report has constructed a minimalist yet legally robust model for the patron-artist relationship in California, tailored specifically for unincorporated artists operating on a small scale. The framework is designed to navigate the state's complex legal environment by providing a clear, actionable, and low-overhead path to compliance.

The core tenets of this model are built upon a series of non-negotiable legal and administrative imperatives. First, the relationship must be grounded in a clear, comprehensive written contract. The convergence of the California Civil Code, the Uniform Commercial Code, and the new Freelance Worker Protection Act makes oral or informal agreements legally untenable for nearly all professional commissions. The recommended MSA/SOW architecture provides an efficient and strategically sound method for satisfying this requirement.

Second, the artist's classification as an independent contractor hinges on proactively qualifying for a statutory exemption under AB 2257. This report demonstrates that the "fine artist" exemption is the most direct route, but it is not automatic. The artist **must** take affirmative steps to operate as a legitimate business, the most critical of which is obtaining a local business license or permit where required. This action is not a bureaucratic burden to be avoided but is the essential key that unlocks the more flexible *Borello* standard of review.

Finally, the model emphasizes the correct legal mechanisms for intellectual property transfer. It strongly advises against the use of "Work-for-Hire" agreements for individual fine artists in California, highlighting their ineffectiveness for copyright transfer and the significant legal risk of triggering a statutory presumption of employment. Instead, the Option-to-Purchase structure, culminating in a formal copyright assignment, is presented as the superior method, offering legal soundness and mutual protection for both parties.

By adhering to this framework—embracing written contracts, satisfying the prerequisites for the fine artist exemption, and utilizing the correct IP transfer mechanisms—patrons and artists can foster creative collaborations with confidence. This model provides the legal clarity and minimal administrative structure necessary to support a vibrant arts ecosystem at all levels, ensuring that even the smallest-scale engagements are built on a foundation of legal integrity.
