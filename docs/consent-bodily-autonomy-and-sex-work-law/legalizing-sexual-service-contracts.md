# **Beyond Obscenity: The Legal and Public Policy Case for the Enforceability of Private Sexual Service Contracts**

  
  

## **Introduction: Re-characterizing Prostitution Statutes as Obscenity Regulation**

  

The legal framework governing the commercial exchange of sexual services in the United States, and specifically within California, rests upon a foundational premise that has largely escaped rigorous constitutional scrutiny. Statutes prohibiting "prostitution" are typically viewed and enforced as straightforward regulations of commerce or public order. This report advances a contrary thesis: that California Penal Code § 647(b) and analogous federal statutes are not, in their legal essence, neutral regulations of a commercial activity. Rather, they are content-based regulations of sexual conduct deemed "lewd" or "dissolute," placing them squarely within the constitutional domain of obscenity law. This re-characterization is not a matter of semantic preference; it is a constitutional necessity that subjects these statutes to the exacting standards of First Amendment jurisprudence, as articulated by the United States Supreme Court. The failure to apply this proper analytical framework has resulted in the unconstitutional proscription of private, consensual conduct that, when correctly evaluated, falls outside the narrow category of expression the state is permitted to regulate.

The textual basis for this argument is explicit within the controlling California statute. California Penal Code § 647(b) criminalizes the act of soliciting, agreeing to engage in, or engaging in an "act of prostitution".1 While the statute appears broad, its definitional provision reveals its core concern. As defined in Penal Code § 647, "'prostitution' includes any lewd act between persons for money or other consideration".4 This language is the cornerstone of the present analysis. By defining the prohibited commercial act in terms of its "lewd" character, the legislature has shifted the statute's focus from the mere fact of a commercial exchange to the moral and social content of the act itself. The term "lewd" is not a descriptor of a commercial transaction; it is a judgment on the nature of the sexual conduct.

This legislative focus on "lewdness" is further illuminated by the statute's immediate proximity and parallel structure to Penal Code § 647(a), which prohibits engaging in "lewd or dissolute conduct in any public place or in any place open to the public or exposed to public view".3 The operative language—"lewd conduct"—is nearly identical. The primary distinction between subsection (a) and subsection (b) is not the intrinsic nature of the act, but its context: § 647(a) addresses public lewdness, while § 647(b) addresses commercial lewdness. This statutory architecture strongly indicates a legislative intent to regulate a category of conduct deemed morally offensive, a quintessential function of obscenity and public morals legislation. The state's interest is not in the economic transaction per se, but in the perceived obscenity of exchanging a "lewd act" for compensation.

This underlying concern with the morality of the sexual conduct is also reflected in federal law. While federal statutes often anchor their jurisdiction in interstate commerce, such as the Mann Act and 18 U.S.C. § 2421A (Promotion or facilitation of prostitution), they frequently employ language that targets the nature of the sexual activity itself.5 For instance, 18 U.S.C. § 2423, which governs the transportation of minors, refers to the intent to engage in "prostitution, or in any sexual activity for which any person can be charged with a criminal offense" and "illicit sexual conduct".7 This terminology reveals that the federal interest is not merely in the regulation of movement across state lines, but in the prevention of sexual conduct deemed "illicit." Even the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines' semantic shift from "prostitution" to "commercial sex acts" does not alter the fundamental analysis, as the severity of the offense remains tied to the nature and circumstances of the sexual act involved.8

The statutory focus on "lewd acts" transforms Penal Code § 647(b) from a presumably neutral commercial regulation into a content-based restriction on a specific type of human interaction. The state is not prohibiting all personal services for hire, but rather a specific service defined by its sexual and "lewd" content. This is, by definition, a content-based distinction. The Supreme Court has long held that content-based regulations of speech are presumptively unconstitutional and subject to the most exacting judicial scrutiny.9 While the state has broad power to regulate conduct, the First Amendment's protections are triggered when that conduct contains significant expressive elements.11 Private, consensual sexual activity is an inherently expressive and associative act, a fundamental form of human communication and relationship. Therefore, a law that prohibits this activity based on its "lewd" content must be analyzed not under the deferential rational basis review typically applied to economic regulations, but as a content-based regulation of expressive conduct. The specific and exclusive form of judicial scrutiny developed by the Supreme Court for regulating sexually explicit content is the test articulated in

*Miller v. California*.

  

## **Part I: The Constitutional Limits of Regulating Private Sexual Conduct**

  
  

### **A. The Doctrinal Ascendancy of the Miller Test as the Exclusive Standard for Obscenity**

  

The regulation of sexually explicit expression has been one of the most fraught and challenging areas of First Amendment jurisprudence. For decades, the Supreme Court struggled to formulate a coherent and constitutionally sound standard for distinguishing protected speech from proscribable obscenity. Early attempts, such as the standard articulated in *Roth v. United States*, which defined obscenity as material that is "utterly without redeeming social importance," proved to be unworkable and led to legal confusion.13 This standard, further complicated by the plurality opinion in

*Memoirs v. Massachusetts*, created an exceedingly difficult burden for prosecutors and resulted in a period of doctrinal instability, characterized by ad hoc, case-by-case reversals of obscenity convictions.16

In its landmark 1973 decision in *Miller v. California*, the Supreme Court decisively addressed this doctrinal morass by establishing a new, definitive three-prong test for obscenity.16 The

*Miller* test was explicitly designed to provide clear, constitutionally grounded guidelines for triers of fact and to confine the scope of regulation to what the Court termed "patently offensive 'hard core' sexual conduct".18 The Court acknowledged the "inherent dangers of undertaking to regulate any form of expression" and mandated that state statutes must be "carefully limited" to this new standard.18 This decision marked a pivotal moment, replacing the vague "utterly without redeeming social value" formulation with a more structured and specific inquiry.

The three prongs of the *Miller* test, each of which must be met for material to be deemed obscene, are:

1.  Whether "the average person, applying contemporary community standards" would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest.16
2.  Whether the work depicts or describes, in a patently offensive way, sexual conduct specifically defined by the applicable state law.16
3.  Whether the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value (the "SLAPS" test).16

The establishment of this test was a constitutional demarcation. It represents the Supreme Court's definitive statement on the outer limits of the state's power to regulate expression based on its sexual content. Any state action that seeks to prohibit expression on the grounds of its obscenity or lewdness must, as a matter of constitutional law, satisfy all three prongs of the *Miller* test.

  

### **B. The Application of Obscenity Standards to Live Conduct as a Form of Expression and Association**

  

The constitutional protections for expression are not limited to the written or spoken word. The Supreme Court has long recognized that the First Amendment also protects "expressive conduct"—actions that are "sufficiently imbued with elements of communication".11 The act of burning a flag in protest, as in

*Texas v. Johnson*, or wearing a black armband to protest a war, as in *Tinker v. Des Moines*, are classic examples of conduct that is protected as symbolic speech because it is intended to convey a particular message that is likely to be understood by those who view it.12 Private sexual conduct between consenting adults is a fundamental form of human interaction, association, and expression. It is a profound mode of communication that conveys intimacy, affection, and desire. When the state seeks to regulate this conduct based on its "lewd" character, it is targeting the expressive content of the act itself.

The fundamental constitutional error in the enforcement of modern prostitution statutes is the failure to subject them to the *Miller* analysis. These laws criminalize private, consensual conduct based on a legislative judgment that it is "lewd," yet they bypass the very constitutional test designed to validate such a judgment. If the state's justification for prohibiting the commercial exchange of sex is the prevention of "obscenity" or "lewdness," it cannot circumvent the First Amendment by simply labeling the statute a regulation of commerce. The content-based nature of the prohibition—targeting "lewd" acts—demands a First Amendment analysis.

The constitutional principles that animate the *Miller* test are not confined to a specific medium, such as books or films; they apply to the *content* of the expression itself. The state's interest in regulating obscenity is predicated on the perceived harms stemming from the content's message and its effect on the audience—its appeal to a prurient interest, its patent offensiveness, and its lack of redeeming social value.13 These are concerns about the substance of the expression, not the form in which it is delivered. Because the First Amendment protects expression that takes the form of conduct, there is no principled constitutional basis for granting the state greater power to regulate live, private expressive conduct than it has to regulate a film depicting the exact same conduct.

Indeed, the state's regulatory interest is arguably weaker in the context of private conduct. The "patently offensive" prong of the *Miller* test is deeply concerned with protecting an unwilling public from offensive displays.19 In a private setting with consenting participants, this concern is entirely absent. To allow the state to ban a live, private act that it could not constitutionally ban if it were recorded and distributed on film would create an illogical and untenable loophole in First Amendment protection. It would suggest that the physical presence of the expressive actors somehow strips them of constitutional rights that an absent filmmaker retains. Such a conclusion is inconsistent with the core principles of expressive freedom. Therefore, the

*Miller* test, as the definitive constitutional safeguard against the overbroad regulation of sexual content, must apply to any statute that criminalizes private, consensual sexual conduct on the basis of its "lewd" or "obscene" nature.

  

## **Part II: Proposed Findings of Fact: Private, Consensual Sexual Services Are Not Obscene**

  

A systematic application of the three-prong *Miller* test to the act of providing compensated sexual services between consenting adults in a private setting demonstrates that such conduct does not meet the constitutional definition of obscenity. Because a work or act must fail all three prongs to be deemed obscene, the fact that private sexual services satisfy at least one—and arguably all three—prongs means that a blanket statutory prohibition on such services is constitutionally overbroad and impermissible.

  

### **A. Finding: The act does not appeal to a "prurient interest" as defined by Supreme Court jurisprudence**

  

The first prong of the *Miller* test requires a finding that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the "prurient interest." It is critical to understand that "prurient interest" is a legal term of art, not a synonym for a healthy, normal, or even robust sexual desire. The Supreme Court, referencing its earlier decision in *Roth*, has defined material appealing to a prurient interest as that which has a "tendency to excite lustful thoughts" in a manner that reflects a "shameful or morbid interest in nudity, sex, or excretion".15 The term incites "lasciviousness or lust" in a way that is considered unwholesome, excessive, or illicit.26 It is the opposite of a "wholesome" interest in sex.

A contract for private sexual services between consenting adults is a direct, transactional arrangement for the fulfillment of a sexual desire, need, or curiosity. It is not an "appeal" to a shameful or morbid interest in the abstract; it is the consensual act itself. The commercial nature of the transaction grounds it in a rational, albeit intimate, exchange of a service for compensation. This is fundamentally different from the kind of detached, obsessive, or unhealthy preoccupation with sexual themes that the "prurient interest" prong was designed to target. The purpose of the transaction, from the client's perspective, is typically sexual satisfaction, companionship, education, or a therapeutic outcome—not the stimulation of "lustful thoughts" divorced from an actual, consensual human interaction. The act is the subject of the agreement, not a medium designed to incite a morbid interest in others. Therefore, it does not satisfy the first prong of the *Miller* test.

  

### **B. Finding: The act, when conducted in private, is not "patently offensive" under contemporary community standards**

  

The second prong of the *Miller* test requires that the work depict or describe, in a "patently offensive way," sexual conduct specifically defined by state law.18 The historical and legal context of this prong is crucial. It originates from the "fighting words" doctrine articulated in

*Chaplinsky v. New Hampshire*, which dealt with words that "by their very utterance inflict injury or tend to incite an immediate breach of the peace".14 The core concern is the protection of an unwilling audience from offensive and injurious expression. The

*Miller* court explicitly echoed this concern, framing its decision in a case involving "sexually explicit materials" that had been "thrust by aggressive sales action upon unwilling recipients".19 The concept of "patent offensiveness" is thus inextricably linked to an assault on public sensibility and the privacy of non-consenting individuals.

Private, consensual sexual conduct, by its very definition, involves no unwilling audience. The participants have explicitly and mutually consented to the activity. The act occurs behind closed doors, with no public display, no aggressive solicitation of the unwilling, and no affront to the general community's standards of public decency. The entire rationale for the "patently offensive" prong—the protection of the public from unwanted exposure to sexually explicit conduct—is rendered moot. Because there is no non-consenting party to be offended, the conduct cannot be "patently offensive" in the constitutional sense intended by the Supreme Court. To apply this prong to private conduct is to fundamentally misinterpret its purpose, twisting a shield for public decency into a sword against private liberty.

  

### **C. Finding: The act does not lack "serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" (SLAPS) under the required "reasonable person" standard**

  

The third prong of the *Miller* test, the SLAPS test, is perhaps the most significant barrier to the prohibition of sexual services. A work is only obscene if it "lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value".22 Crucially, the Supreme Court held in

*Pope v. Illinois* that this prong is not to be judged by local "community standards." Instead, the proper inquiry is "whether a reasonable person would find such value in the material, taken as a whole".23 This objective, national standard was deliberately chosen to protect expression that may be unpopular or offensive to a local majority but is nonetheless valued by a reasonable minority.30 A blanket prohibition on private sexual services cannot survive this objective analysis, as there is overwhelming evidence of their serious value in multiple domains.

  - **Serious Scientific and Therapeutic Value:** The most compelling evidence of SLAPS value comes from the established fields of surrogate partner therapy and sexological bodywork. These are recognized therapeutic modalities that utilize hands-on, intimate, and sometimes explicitly sexual touch as part of a structured treatment plan supervised by a licensed therapist.31 Surrogate partner therapy has been shown to be effective in treating a range of diagnosed sexual dysfunctions, including anorgasmia, erectile dysfunction, and vaginismus.31 It is also used to help survivors of sexual assault and trauma reclaim their bodies and differentiate between safe, consensual touch and harmful touch.31 Furthermore, these services provide a vital pathway to intimacy and sexual experience for individuals with disabilities, traumatic brain injuries, or conditions like autism.31 A reasonable person, presented with evidence of these therapeutic applications and outcomes, could not possibly conclude that the entire category of sexual services "lacks serious scientific value."
  - **Serious Artistic Value:** Sexual services can be understood as a form of performance art, requiring significant skill, emotional intelligence, and the ability to create a specific experience for the client. The widely recognized concept of the "girlfriend experience," for example, involves the crafting of a narrative of intimacy and emotional connection, which is a fundamentally artistic endeavor.38 The service is not merely a physical act but the co-creation of an experience, which has clear artistic dimensions.
  - **Serious Political Value:** The act of engaging in sex work, and the advocacy surrounding it, is an inherently political statement. It challenges deeply entrenched societal norms regarding sexuality, commerce, gender roles, and bodily autonomy. By choosing to engage in this work, providers assert economic independence and control over their own bodies, acts that have profound political implications in a society that continues to debate these issues. A reasonable person would recognize the serious political value in this form of expression and advocacy.
  - **Serious Literary and Relational Value:** For many individuals, particularly those who are lonely, socially isolated, or inexperienced, a sexual service provider offers more than a physical act; they provide companionship, a non-judgmental space for human connection, and a safe environment to explore intimacy. These functions—providing comfort, connection, and a narrative of human interaction—are central themes of literature and the humanities. The service can fulfill a deep-seated human need for intimacy that might otherwise go unmet, an outcome with serious and positive value.

The Supreme Court's decision in *Pope v. Illinois* to apply an objective, "reasonable person" standard to the SLAPS test, while leaving the "prurient interest" and "patent offensiveness" prongs to subjective "community standards," creates a fatal constitutional flaw in blanket prostitution bans. A work is only obscene if it fails *all three* prongs of the *Miller* test.22 A prosecutor in a socially conservative jurisdiction might successfully argue to a local jury that commercial sex appeals to a prurient interest and is patently offensive according to that community's standards. However, the third prong is not subject to that same local sentiment. The SLAPS test is governed by a national, objective standard.30 The existence of demonstrable, serious therapeutic, artistic, and political value in sexual services is an objective fact. A reasonable person, informed of the documented benefits of surrogate partner therapy for trauma survivors or individuals with disabilities, could not conclude that the entire category of conduct is "valueless".31 Because some forms of compensated sexual services possess serious value, a law that prohibits

*all* such services is unconstitutionally overbroad. It sweeps within its prohibition constitutionally protected, non-obscene expressive conduct, and therefore cannot stand.

  

## **Part III: The Enforceability of Sexual Service Contracts Under California Law**

  
  

### **A. The Doctrine of Illegality: A Contract is Void Only if its Object is Unlawful**

  

Under fundamental principles of California contract law, a valid and enforceable contract requires several key elements: mutual agreement, an offer and acceptance, consideration, and capacity of the parties.39 Critically, the agreement must also have a "legal purpose." A court will not enforce a contract whose object is to break the law; such contracts are considered void and unenforceable from their inception.39 For example, a contract for the sale of illegal narcotics would be void because its central purpose is the commission of a crime.

In the context of sexual services, the sole basis for declaring a contract "illegal" is the existence of criminal statutes, principally California Penal Code § 647(b). There is no independent common law doctrine in California that renders the private, consensual exchange of sex for money inherently illegal absent this statutory prohibition. The entire legal edifice of unenforceability rests on the presumption that P.C. § 647(b) is a valid and constitutional exercise of state power.

  

### **B. Conclusion of Law: A Contract for Non-Obscene Sexual Services Has a Lawful Object**

  

The analysis presented in Parts I and II of this report leads to an inescapable legal conclusion. If, as argued, California Penal Code § 647(b) is a content-based regulation of expressive conduct that fails the constitutional test for obscenity established in *Miller v. California*, then the statute is unconstitutional. An unconstitutional statute is void and cannot serve as the legal basis for declaring the object of a contract to be "unlawful."

Once the unconstitutional statutory prohibition is removed from consideration, a contract for sexual services stands as a simple agreement for the provision of a personal service in exchange for valuable consideration. It contains all the necessary elements of a valid contract and no longer has an illegal object. Therefore, such contracts must be deemed legally valid and enforceable in a court of law. The state's power to interfere with private contracts is contingent on a legitimate exercise of its police power; an unconstitutional statute provides no such legitimacy.

  

### **C. The California Constitutional Right to Privacy as a Shield for Private Consensual Contracts**

  

The argument for the enforceability of sexual service contracts is further fortified by California's robust, explicit, and independent constitutional right to privacy. This right, enshrined in Article I, Section 1 of the California Constitution, provides broader protection for individual autonomy than its federal counterpart. It shields Californians from unwarranted government intrusion into deeply personal matters, including decisions about their bodies, relationships, and private transactions.41

A private contract for sexual services between consenting adults falls squarely within this protected zone of privacy. It involves intimate association, personal expression, and private financial arrangements. For the state to justify its intrusion into this sphere, it must demonstrate a compelling state interest. While the state may have a compelling interest in preventing harm—such as coercion, exploitation, public disorder, or the involvement of minors—its interest in prohibiting a private, consensual act between adults based solely on moral disapproval is significantly weaker.

Having established that the act itself is not constitutionally obscene under the *Miller* standard, the state's primary justification for the prohibition evaporates. The state cannot demonstrate a compelling interest in criminalizing non-obscene, private expressive conduct. In the balance between the profound privacy interests of the contracting individuals and the state's attenuated interest in regulating non-harmful, non-obscene private behavior, the constitutional right to privacy must prevail. This right serves as a powerful shield, rendering the state's interference impermissible and reinforcing the legal conclusion that the underlying contracts are valid and enforceable.

A critical re-evaluation of the "public policy" doctrine is necessary. Historically, courts have reflexively voided contracts for sexual services as being "against public policy," with that policy being defined almost exclusively by the existence of the criminal statutes themselves.40 This reasoning is circular: the contract is against public policy because it is illegal, and it is illegal because it is against public policy. This report argues for a complete inversion of this logic. The true public interest should be determined not by referencing a constitutionally dubious statute, but by examining the real-world consequences of the legal regime that statute creates.

A vast body of empirical evidence demonstrates that the *criminalization* of consensual sex work is the policy that actively harms the public. Criminalization increases violence against sex workers by driving them into isolated and dangerous situations and making them afraid to report crimes to the police.45 It elevates public health risks by impeding access to healthcare and discouraging safe sex practices.45 It fosters stigma, which creates barriers to housing, alternative employment, and social integration.46 Finally, it incurs enormous public expense through the costs of law enforcement, prosecution, and incarceration, with little to no evidence of deterring the activity itself.46

Conversely, regulatory and decriminalization models have been shown to produce positive public policy outcomes. They improve worker safety, reduce STI rates, enhance access to justice, and generate significant tax revenue while reducing state expenditures.47 Therefore, the actual public policy interests of California—in public health, public safety, economic efficiency, and individual liberty—are far better served by a legal framework that recognizes and enforces private, consensual sexual service contracts. A court, when asked to consider the public policy implications of such a contract, should find that it is the criminal statute, not the private agreement, that stands in opposition to the public good. Enforcing the contract is the position that truly advances sound public policy.

  

## **Part IV: The Laudable Public Policy Imperative for Recognizing Sexual Service Contracts**

  

Moving beyond the question of mere legal permissibility, a comprehensive analysis reveals that the legal recognition of sexual service contracts is a positive social good. A regulated and professionalized market for sexual services offers demonstrable benefits in public health, worker safety, economic efficiency, and individual well-being that are impossible to achieve under a regime of criminalization. The policy of prohibition, far from protecting society, actively creates and exacerbates the very harms it purports to prevent.

  

### **A. Enhancing Public Health and Worker Safety: A Comparative Analysis of Regulatory Models**

  

The most compelling argument for a new legal framework is grounded in the empirical outcomes of different regulatory approaches to sex work worldwide. The evidence overwhelmingly indicates that treating sex work as a crime is a catastrophic public policy failure, while models based on regulation and decriminalization produce vastly superior results for workers and the community at large.

The harms of the current criminalization model in the United States are well-documented. Criminalization drives the industry underground, forcing workers into isolated and unsafe environments where they are vulnerable to violence from clients, managers, and even law enforcement.45 The fear of arrest prevents workers from reporting crimes committed against them, creating a climate of impunity for perpetrators.46 Furthermore, criminalization directly harms public health. Police have historically used the possession of condoms as evidence of intent to commit prostitution, discouraging workers from carrying and using them, which leads to higher rates of sexually transmitted infections (STIs).45 The stigma associated with a criminal record creates lifelong barriers to housing, education, and alternative employment, trapping individuals in cycles of poverty and marginalization.46

In stark contrast, jurisdictions that have moved away from criminalization have seen dramatic improvements in health and safety. In Nevada, the only U.S. state with a system of legal, regulated brothels, the environment is structured to maximize safety. Brothels are equipped with security measures like panic buttons and have on-site personnel, leading to very low rates of reported client-perpetrated violence.50 The state mandates regular health checks for all legal sex workers, including weekly testing for gonorrhea and chlamydia and monthly testing for HIV and syphilis, a policy that protects both workers and clients.51 This system of public scrutiny and official regulation transforms a dangerous, clandestine activity into a managed and safer profession.55

New Zealand's Prostitution Reform Act of 2003, which fully decriminalized sex work, provides another powerful model. Research on the impacts of the Act found that it significantly empowered sex workers, strengthening their ability to refuse clients and negotiate safe sex practices.53 Decriminalization improved workers' relationships with the police, making them more willing to report violence and exploitation.53 By treating sex work as legitimate labor, the law granted workers access to the legal system to address grievances, such as sexual harassment by brothel operators, and to enforce their rights.53 The overarching goals of the Act—to safeguard human rights, protect workers from exploitation, and promote their welfare and occupational health—have been largely realized, providing a clear example of a successful, health- and rights-based approach.58

### **B. The Economic Benefits of Regulation Over Criminalization**

  

The policy of criminalization is not only a failure in terms of health and safety; it is also an immense and unjustifiable drain on public resources. American cities spend millions of dollars annually on the enforcement of prostitution laws, including costly police stings and court-appointed diversion programs that have proven to be largely ineffective at addressing the root causes of why people engage in sex work.46 These expenditures represent a significant opportunity cost, diverting funds that could be invested in community-based services such as housing, healthcare, and job training—interventions that actually reduce vulnerability. Furthermore, the creation of criminal records erects substantial barriers to future employment, often increasing individuals' long-term reliance on public assistance programs.46

A regulated market, by contrast, transforms this economic liability into a public asset. In the rural counties of Nevada where brothels are legal, the industry is a significant source of public revenue. Brothels pay substantial quarterly licensing fees, worker registration fees, and other local taxes that directly fund essential public services.62 Nye County, for example, collected over $141,000 in a single fiscal year, while Lyon County brings in nearly $400,000 annually from its four establishments.64 While proposals to enact a statewide excise tax on prostitution services have so far failed, estimates suggest such a tax could bring in millions more in revenue.64 An economic analysis of potential decriminalization in Washington, D.C., projected that the city would not only save over $20,000 per worker in criminal legal system costs but would also gain over $5,000 per worker in new income tax revenue.49 Legally recognizing sexual service contracts allows the state to tax the resulting income and business activity, creating a new and sustainable revenue stream to fund public priorities.

  

### **C. The Social and Therapeutic Value of Professionalized Sexual Services**

  

The legal recognition of sexual service contracts is a prerequisite for the professionalization of the industry, a development that would unlock immense social and therapeutic value. A legal framework allows for the establishment of professional standards, ethical codes of conduct, and the open development of specialized services that cater to profound human needs.

Models for this professionalization already exist. The Association of Certified Sexological Bodyworkers, for instance, has a detailed code of ethics that governs its members' practice. This code emphasizes principles of informed consent, client safety, clear professional boundaries, and a commitment to the client's well-being and growth.65 Similarly, the International Professional Surrogates Association provides a framework for ethical, therapeutically-guided work.34 These models provide a clear roadmap for how a legalized sex industry could develop and enforce professional standards that protect both providers and clients. Legalization would also enable sex workers to unionize and advocate for labor rights, such as minimum pay, workplace safety standards, and benefits, just like workers in any other industry.61

Beyond the formal therapeutic context of surrogate partner therapy, professional sexual services provide invaluable benefits to society. They offer a source of companionship and human touch for the lonely and isolated, a safe and educational environment for the sexually inexperienced to explore their identity, and a means of achieving physical intimacy for individuals with disabilities or other physical limitations that may make traditional partnering difficult.31 These services contribute directly to individual happiness, mental health, and overall well-being. By decriminalizing and professionalizing this work, society can openly acknowledge and meet these legitimate human needs. Finally, recognizing sexual services as legitimate work is the most powerful tool for combating the stigma that is a primary source of harm for those in the industry.46 When sex work is no longer a crime, workers are more likely to be seen as valued members of society, with a corresponding increase in their ability to access healthcare, housing, and justice without fear of judgment or discrimination.

  

## **Conclusion: A Proposed Framework for the Legal Recognition of Sexual Service Contracts**

  

The legal prohibition of prostitution in California and across the United States is built on a constitutionally flawed foundation. This report has presented a series of proposed legal findings demonstrating that these statutes, by targeting "lewd" conduct, function as content-based regulations of expressive activity. As such, they must be subjected to the strictures of the First Amendment's obscenity jurisprudence, as defined by the Supreme Court in *Miller v. California*.

The analysis yields four primary conclusions. First, prostitution laws like California Penal Code § 647(b) are, in substance, unconstitutional regulations of obscenity, not neutral commercial statutes. Second, private, consensual sexual services between adults do not meet the constitutional definition of obscenity under the three-prong *Miller* test, most critically because they possess serious therapeutic, artistic, and political value under the objective "reasonable person" standard. Third, because the underlying conduct is not constitutionally proscribable, contracts for such services have a lawful object and are legally enforceable under California law, a conclusion buttressed by the state's constitutional right to privacy. Fourth, an examination of empirical evidence reveals that public policy overwhelmingly favors the regulation and decriminalization of sex work over the current regime of criminalization, which demonstrably fails to achieve its objectives while actively causing significant social and public health harms.

Based on these findings, this report advocates for a new legal framework that abandons the failed and unconstitutional approach of criminalization. Contracts for private sexual services between consenting adults should be recognized as valid and enforceable commercial agreements. This would remove the industry from the purview of the criminal justice system and place it under the regulatory authority of standard business, labor, and public health codes. Such a framework would empower the state to implement sensible regulations, such as licensing, health and safety standards, and taxation, while respecting the fundamental rights of individual autonomy, privacy, and expression. This approach is not only compelled by a correct application of constitutional law but is also the most effective path toward maximizing public health, enhancing worker safety, and promoting the economic well-being of the community.
